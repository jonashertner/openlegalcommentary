# Art. 17 FC — Freedom of the Media

## Doctrine

### 1. Legislative History

**N. 1** The predecessor of today's freedom of the media is freedom of the press, which was enshrined in Switzerland for the first time in the Helvetic Constitution of 12 April 1798. Following its brief suspension by the Helvetic Government, the cantons reintroduced freedom of the press in their constitutions from the period of Regeneration (1830–1848) onwards. At the federal level, it was incorporated in 1848 as a «fundamental right of the first hour» (Biaggini, BV, Art. 17 N. 1) in Art. 45 FC 1848 (resp. Art. 55 FC 1874); the motivation was distrust of conservative cantons that restricted the free press (Piolino/Cueni, Onlinekommentar Art. 17 BV, N. 2). Radio and television were not enshrined as independent freedoms in the old Federal Constitution; Art. 55bis FC 1874 (inserted in 1984) merely referred to «autonomy in programme design» and «independence».

**N. 2** In the course of the total revision of the Federal Constitution, the draft constitution of 1996 (Art. 14 DC 1996) still defined «freedom of the press, radio and television» as a component of a unified freedom of opinion, information and media. The express inclusion of radio and television was based on the case-law of the ECtHR on Art. 10 ECHR, according to which broadcasting also enjoys fundamental rights protection (Federal Council Message on the FC, BBl 1997 I 157). Parliament then created, with Art. 17 FC, a self-standing provision for freedom of the media, supplemented by the prohibition of censorship (para. 2) and the editorial privilege (para. 3) — both introduced on parliamentary initiative (Piolino/Cueni, Onlinekommentar Art. 17 BV, N. 3). At the same time, the scope of protection was kept technologically neutral for new forms of communication through the general clause «other forms of public telecommunication of programmes and information».

**N. 3** The Federal Council's Message on the new Federal Constitution (BBl 1997 I 157 f.) stated that freedom of the media occupies a special position alongside general freedom of opinion (Art. 16 FC) because the media, in a democracy, function as a link between the state and the public and exercise an indispensable control function. This functional rationale continues to shape the interpretation of the provision to this day.

---

### 2. Systematic Classification

**N. 4** Art. 17 FC belongs to the catalogue of fundamental rights in the second chapter of the Federal Constitution (Art. 7–36 FC) and is closely systematically related to Art. 16 FC (freedom of opinion and information) and Art. 93 FC (radio and television). ↔ Art. 16 FC protects individual communication; Art. 17 FC, by contrast, grants specifically enhanced protection for mass communication that is technically disseminated and directed at an indeterminate audience. This distinction has practical consequences: restrictions on freedom of the media are subject to a stricter proportionality standard than restrictions on general freedom of opinion (**BGE 141 I 211** E. 3.1; **BGE 143 I 194** E. 3.1).

**N. 5** → Art. 36 FC applies as the general restriction clause for all paragraphs of Art. 17 FC. Restrictions require a legal basis, must be justified by a public interest or by the need to protect the fundamental rights of others, and must remain proportionate. → Art. 93 FC (radio and television) supplements Art. 17 FC in the area of broadcasting with a specific competence provision and contains the public service mandate (Art. 93 para. 2 FC). Questions of universal service and media diversity in broadcasting are primarily to be located under Art. 93 FC, but always touch upon the protective content of Art. 17 FC as well.

**N. 6** At the level of international law, freedom of the media is guaranteed by Art. 10 ECHR, albeit not as an independent guarantee but as part of the broader protection of freedom of expression. In Swiss case-law, Art. 10 ECHR and the case-law of the ECtHR carry considerable weight; the Federal Supreme Court regularly refers to Strasbourg case-law when interpreting Art. 17 FC (**BGE 141 I 211** E. 3.1; Piolino/Cueni, Onlinekommentar Art. 17 BV, N. 6). Art. 19 UN Covenant II provides an additional parallel guarantee under international law.

### 3. Elements of the Offence / Normative Content

#### 3.1 Material Scope of Protection (para. 1)

**N. 7** Art. 17 para. 1 FC protects «freedom of the press and of radio and television and of other forms of public telecommunication of programmes and information». According to Federal Supreme Court practice, the normative concept of «media» encompasses public, technically disseminated statements of an ideational nature directed at an indeterminate audience (Müller/Schefer/Zeller, Medienfreiheit, p. 441 ff.; **BGE 128 IV 53** E. 5c). Individual communication via messenger services or email, by contrast, falls under Art. 16 FC and Art. 13 para. 1 FC (privacy of correspondence), not under Art. 17 FC.

**N. 8** Freedom of the media protects both the *content* and the *institutional* framework conditions of media activity. In terms of content, all phases of the journalistic process are protected: research, editorial work, production and dissemination of media products (**BGE 137 I 8** E. 2.5; **BGE 141 I 211** E. 3.1). The institutional dimension protects the organisational structures necessary for effective media activity, in particular the requirement of independence from the state (Piolino, Staatsunabhängigkeit, Zurich 2021, p. 90). The distinction is doctrinally relevant: institutional restrictions typically have an indirect effect on content; their constitutional assessment requires consideration of the potential «chilling effect» (ECtHR, Goodwin v. United Kingdom, no. 17488/90, 20.3.1996, N. 39).

**N. 9** Holders of the fundamental right are all natural and legal persons involved in researching, producing and disseminating media content, including media professionals, media enterprises and their auxiliary persons (Zeller/Kiener, BSK BV, Art. 17 N. 18; SGK-Errass/Rechsteiner, Art. 17 BV N. 65 f.). The Federal Supreme Court now affirms that the Swiss Broadcasting Corporation (SRG SSR) also holds this fundamental right when fulfilling its public service mandate (**BGE 137 I 8** E. 2.4; **BGE 137 I 340** E. 3.3). The audience does not enjoy direct protection under Art. 17 FC, but may invoke the functionally related freedom of information under Art. 16 para. 3 FC.

**N. 10** The provision is framed in a technologically neutral manner: the catch-all clause «other forms of public telecommunication of programmes and information» opens the scope of protection to new media forms, in particular online media and internet publications (Piolino/Cueni, Onlinekommentar Art. 17 BV, N. 14; Zeller/Kiener, BSK BV, Art. 17 N. 15). According to Federal Supreme Court practice, primarily commercial content is not protected under Art. 17 FC but under the freedom of economic activity (Art. 27 FC); this distinction is contested in academic writing (→ N. 22 below).

#### 3.2 Prohibition of Censorship (para. 2)

**N. 11** Art. 17 para. 2 FC declares censorship to be prohibited. The prohibition of censorship forms the core content of freedom of the media and, according to the consistent view of academic writing and case-law, is to be understood as an absolute limit — i.e. as content that cannot be restricted by way of Art. 36 FC (**BGE 138 I 274** E. 2.2; Schefer, Kerngehalte, p. 463 f.; Müller/Schefer, Grundrechte in der Schweiz, 4th ed. 2008, p. 352 f.). Censorship in the constitutional sense means exclusively *preventive and systematic* state content control. Subsequent sanctions for unlawful statements do not constitute censorship within the meaning of Art. 17 para. 2 FC.

**N. 12** To be distinguished from the absolutely prohibited systematic prior censorship are individual preventive interventions in specific cases (e.g. a judicial publication ban based on Art. 261 ff. CPC in cases of threatened violation of personality rights). According to the prevailing view, these do not affect the core content, but they regularly constitute serious restrictions that require a clear formal-statutory basis, a weighty public interest, and a strict proportionality assessment (Müller/Schefer, Grundrechte in der Schweiz, p. 354 f.; Zeller/Kiener, BSK BV, Art. 17 N. 40). The prohibition of censorship also covers state control of media activity through organisational measures (*requirement of state independence*); «general censorship» through institutional dominance is equally prohibited as targeted content control (Piolino, Staatsunabhängigkeit, p. 221 ff.).

#### 3.3 Editorial Privilege (para. 3)

**N. 13** Art. 17 para. 3 FC guarantees the editorial privilege. It protects internal editorial processes and information, and in particular the identity of journalistic sources (Schmohl, Redaktionsgeheimnis, Zurich 2013, p. 4 ff.; Zeller/Kiener, BSK BV, Art. 17 N. 44). The scope of protection is broad: it encompasses the right to refuse to testify, the right to refuse to produce documents, protection against searches and seizures, and protection against targeted criminal proceedings as attempts to circumvent source protection (ECtHR, Tillack v. Belgium, no. 20477/05, 27.11.2007, N. 60 ff.).

**N. 14** The editorial privilege is not absolute. Restrictions are permissible under Art. 36 FC, but only for the protection of high-ranking legal interests and following careful case-by-case proportionality assessment (**BGE 132 I 181** E. 2.1–2.3). The Federal Supreme Court recognised source protection as part of press freedom for the first time in 1996, after the ECtHR in *Goodwin v. United Kingdom* (20.3.1996) had derived such protection from Art. 10 ECHR (**BGE 123 IV 236** E. 8a/bb). Art. 28a SCC and Art. 172 CrimPC give concrete expression to the editorial privilege for criminal proceedings, but define the scope of protection more narrowly than Art. 17 para. 3 FC; the constitutional guarantee also applies in civil and administrative proceedings as well as in preliminary police investigations (Piolino/Cueni, Onlinekommentar Art. 17 BV, N. 93; Zeller/Kiener, BSK BV, Art. 17 N. 53).

**N. 15** The scope of the prohibition on seizure is determined by **BGE 140 IV 108** E. 6: the prohibition on seizure pursuant to Art. 264 para. 1 lit. c CrimPC covers «regardless of location» not only documents held by the media professional, but also those held by the accused or by third parties. The Court bases this on the clear wording of the statute, the legislative history (parliamentary deliberations 2006/2007) and the purpose of source protection as a «cornerstone of press freedom» (E. 6.8).

### 4. Legal Consequences

**N. 16** In its *subjective-law* dimension, Art. 17 FC establishes a constitutionally enforceable defensive right against state interference with freedom of the media. The fundamental right protects against publication bans, criminal prosecution for journalistic activity, denial of access and disclosure of journalistic sources. State interventions that impair these legal positions are permissible only under the cumulative conditions of Art. 36 FC (**BGE 141 I 211** E. 3.2; **BGE 143 I 194** E. 3.2).

**N. 17** In its *objective-law* dimension, Art. 17 FC obliges the state to create and maintain the conditions for effective media activity (Art. 35 para. 1 FC). These include: the design of media criminal law in conformity with fundamental rights (Art. 28, 28a SCC), the protection of personality rights (Art. 28 ff. CC, Art. 266 CPC), the rules on seizure in criminal procedure (Art. 172, 264 CrimPC), and the safeguarding of media diversity as an institutional prerequisite for the free formation of opinion (Müller/Schefer/Zeller, Medienfreiheit, p. 475 f.; Piolino/Cueni, Onlinekommentar Art. 17 BV, N. 26–28).

**N. 18** Restrictions on freedom of the media require a legal basis pursuant to Art. 36 para. 1 FC, whereby the higher the requirements on the level and density of the norm, the more serious the restriction. A *serious* restriction — in particular a complete exclusion of media professionals from a public court hearing — requires a clear and express basis in a formal act of parliament (**BGE 143 I 194** E. 3.3; **BGE 141 I 211** E. 3.2). A *minor* restriction may be based on an act in the material sense. Proportionality (suitability, necessity, reasonableness) must always be assessed on a case-by-case basis.

**N. 19** Media professionals do not enjoy a general criminal law justification for constituent-element-fulfilling methods of information gathering. Freedom of the media justifies neither incitement to breach of official secrecy (**BGE 127 IV 122** E. 5b) nor unlawful entry for the purpose of gathering information (**BGE 127 IV 166** E. 3), where the principle of proportionality and the absence of other suitable means are not satisfied. Only where the conduct fulfilling the relevant offence constitutes the sole possible and appropriate means of pursuing a legitimate journalistic interest can a justification be considered.

### 5. Contested Issues

#### 5.1 The Concept of Media and the Protection of Non-Journalistic Actors

**N. 20** There is a contested issue between academic writing and case-law as to how far the concept of media in Art. 17 FC extends and whether non-journalistic actors (NGOs, bloggers, social media users) fall within its scope of protection.

Zeller/Kiener (BSK BV, Art. 17 N. 9, 17 and 26) argue that the enhanced protection of Art. 17 FC should be limited to journalistic activity; non-journalistic contributions by actors with a function essential to democracy (such as NGOs) may in specific instances enjoy comparable protection within the framework of Art. 16 FC. Piolino/Cueni (Onlinekommentar Art. 17 BV, N. 11) agree with this view in principle, but emphasise that the criterion of journalistic diligence — not the organisational form — is determinative.

Cottier (CR CO, Art. 17 Cst. N. 11) differentiates according to the intensity of editorial activity: online platforms may qualify as media depending on the degree of control exercised over the selection and presentation of content. In its more recent practice, the Federal Supreme Court tends towards a narrower concept of media for Art. 17 FC in questions of access rights and justification, but allows a broader concept in media criminal law (BGer 6B_440/2019 of 18.11.2020 E. 5.4.2 ff.).

#### 5.2 Scope of the Prohibition of Censorship in Digital Communication

**N. 21** Müller/Schefer (Grundrechte in der Schweiz, p. 355 f.), as well as Zeller/Kiener (BSK BV, Art. 17 N. 41) and Schefer (Kerngehalte, p. 465 f.), propose that in the digital age, systematic post-publication censorship occurring immediately after publication (immediate website blocking) should also be qualified as absolutely prohibited censorship, since in its effect it is equivalent to prior censorship. This extension of the prohibition of censorship is largely accepted in academic writing, but has not yet been expressly confirmed by the Federal Supreme Court.

It remains unresolved whether and to what extent state-motivated systematic content control by private platform operators («collateral censorship») conflicts with the prohibition of censorship. Schefer/Cueni (Öffentlichkeit im Wandel, BAKOM study 2020, p. 53 ff.) advocate for a state duty to protect against arbitrary deletion decisions by opinion-powerful service providers.

#### 5.3 Protection of Commercial Content

**N. 22** The Federal Supreme Court protects primarily commercial content not under Art. 17 FC but under freedom of economic activity (Art. 27 FC), which means, inter alia, that systematic prior control of pharmaceutical advertising is constitutionally permissible (**BGE 128 I 295** E. 5a). Part of academic writing (Barrelet/Werly, Droit de la communication, 2nd ed. 2011, Rz. 72 f.; SGK-Errass/Rechsteiner, Art. 17 BV N. 36) considers this distinction too narrow, because advertising is indispensable for the economic viability of media and sometimes also takes up political or cultural themes. Piolino/Cueni (Onlinekommentar Art. 17 BV, N. 10) follow the Federal Supreme Court's distinction in its result, but advocate a constitutionally conforming interpretation of advertising restriction rules.

#### 5.4 Source Protection and the Catalogue of Exceptions in Criminal Procedure

**N. 23** It is contested whether media professionals are always obliged to testify where an offence listed in the catalogue of exceptions in Art. 28a para. 2 SCC (resp. Art. 172 para. 2 CrimPC) is present, or whether an individual proportionality assessment must also take place in these cases.

The Federal Supreme Court — and in its wake the academic view of Werly (Secret rédactionnel, p. 238 f.) — has clarified that even within the scope of the catalogue of exceptions, a case-by-case proportionality assessment must be conducted: the mere presence of a catalogued offence does not suffice; rather, the testimony must be «absolutely indispensable» for solving the offence (**BGE 132 I 181** E. 2.3 and 4.2). The ECtHR in *Jecker v. Switzerland* (no. 35449/14, 6.10.2020, N. 41 f.) criticised Swiss practice as insufficiently case-specific and demanded a more extensive individual balancing exercise.

### 6. Practical Notes

**N. 24** **Court reporting**: Media professionals enjoy extended rights of access to criminal proceedings closed to the public, based on Art. 70 para. 3 CrimPC. The exclusion of accredited court reporters from an appeal hearing is permissible only where there are overriding competing interests (in particular the protection of children, young persons or victims), and where less far-reaching measures (such as anonymised reporting) have proved insufficient (**BGE 143 I 194** E. 3.6). A complete exclusion from both the hearing *and* the pronouncement of judgment regularly violates Art. 17 FC as well as Art. 30 para. 3 FC and Art. 6 para. 1 ECHR.

**N. 25** **Publication bans**: A judicial ban on publishing certain information about an accused person always requires a sufficient legal basis in a formal act of parliament. In the absence thereof, the ban is impermissible, regardless of whether the restriction is serious or minor (**BGE 141 I 211** E. 3.4–3.5). Art. 70 para. 3 CrimPC allows conditions to be imposed on court reporters only where the public has been formally excluded; without such exclusion, there is no basis for content-related publication bans.

**N. 26** **Source protection and seizure**: The prohibition on seizing documents from communications between accused persons and media professionals (Art. 264 para. 1 lit. c CrimPC) applies regardless of location — that is, not only to documents held by the journalist but also to those held by the accused or by third parties (**BGE 140 IV 108** E. 6.5, 6.10). In practice, early application for sealing (Art. 248 CrimPC) is recommended as a protective measure.

**N. 27** **Journalistic justification**: Freedom of the media operates as a ground of justification only where the conduct fulfilling the relevant offence is the sole appropriate means of exposing a significant grievance, the legitimate interest in information clearly outweighs the opposing interest in confidentiality, and journalistic diligence has been observed (**BGE 127 IV 122** E. 5b–c; **BGE 127 IV 166**). Journalists should avoid unlawful methods of information gathering and always examine whether the same objective can be achieved by lawful means.

**N. 28** **Freedom of Information Act and the media**: Art. 10 para. 4 lit. a FOIA and Art. 15 para. 4 OFIA provide for simplified access requests and fee reductions for media professionals. This reflects the special constitutional position of the press as an intermediary between authorities and the public (**BGE 142 II 313** E. 3.3 f.; **BGE 139 I 114** E. 4.3).
