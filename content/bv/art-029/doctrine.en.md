# Art. 29 BV

## Doctrine

### 1. Legislative History

**N. 1** Art. 29 BV consolidates the general procedural guarantees that were previously scattered across various provisions of the old Federal Constitution. The Message on a new Federal Constitution of 20 November 1996 (BBl 1997 I 1, 230 ff.) emphasized the need to systematically unite these fundamental procedural rights in one place. The constitutional legislator was strongly guided by Federal Supreme Court case law and the guarantees of the ECHR.

**N. 2** The provision codifies three central procedural guarantees: the right to fair treatment and judgment within a reasonable time (para. 1), the right to be heard (para. 2) as well as free legal proceedings and legal assistance for those in need (para. 3). While the right to be heard was already recognized as unwritten constitutional law under the old Federal Constitution, the claims to reasonable procedural duration and free legal proceedings were explicitly anchored in the Constitution for the first time (BBl 1997 I 232).

### 2. Systematic Classification

**N. 3** Art. 29 BV stands in the context of general procedural guarantees (Art. 29–32 BV) and supplements the specific guarantees of court proceedings (→ Art. 30 BV) as well as criminal proceedings (→ Art. 31–32 BV). The provision applies to all proceedings before state authorities, regardless of whether they are judicial or administrative authorities. This distinguishes Art. 29 BV from Art. 6 ECHR, which primarily focuses on judicial proceedings (Müller/Schefer, Grundrechte in der Schweiz, 4th ed. 2008, p. 892 ff.).

**N. 4** The procedural guarantees of Art. 29 BV are closely linked to the rule of law principle (→ Art. 5 BV) and the prohibition of arbitrariness (→ Art. 9 BV). They concretize the rule of law requirements for state decision-making procedures and simultaneously serve to realize legal equality (↔ Art. 8 BV) in procedural law.

### 3. Elements of the Offense / Normative Content

#### a) Right to Equal and Fair Treatment (para. 1)

**N. 5** The right to equal and fair treatment encompasses the right to a fair procedure (*fair trial*). Häfelin/Haller/Keller/Thurnherr (Schweizerisches Bundesstaatsrecht, 10th ed. 2020, N 831) emphasize that this right encompasses both formal and substantive aspects. The authority must decide impartially, treat all parties equally and comply with procedural rules.

**N. 6** Judgment within a reasonable time requires that proceedings be conducted without unjustified delays. What is considered «reasonable» depends on the complexity of the case, the behavior of the parties and the significance of the matter in dispute (Rhinow/Schefer/Uebersax, Schweizerisches Verfassungsrecht, 3rd ed. 2016, N 2986).

#### b) Right to be Heard (para. 2)

**N. 7** According to the teaching of Tschannen/Zimmerli/Müller (Allgemeines Verwaltungsrecht, 4th ed. 2014, § 30 N 1 ff.), the right to be heard comprises the following partial contents:
- Right to prior hearing
- Right to access to files
- Right to participate in evidence proceedings
- Right to reasoning of the decision
- Right of reply

**N. 8** The Federal Supreme Court clarified in **BGE 137 I 195** that the right of reply requires service of all submissions by the parties to the proceedings. Mere access to files is not sufficient. Violation of the right to be heard leads in principle to annulment of the contested decision, unless the violation is incurable (formal character) and a remand would lead to a formalistic empty exercise.

#### c) Free Legal Proceedings and Legal Assistance (para. 3)

**N. 9** The right to free legal proceedings requires two cumulative conditions: indigence of the applicant and lack of hopelessness of the legal claim. Waldmann/Belser/Epiney (BSK BV, 2nd ed. 2024, Art. 29 N 45) point out that indigence exists when the person cannot raise the procedural costs without impairing the maintenance necessary for them and their family.

**N. 10** The right to free legal counsel additionally depends on legal representation being necessary to safeguard rights. According to **BGE 128 I 225**, this is the case with complex factual or legal questions or with serious interventions in personal freedom.

### 4. Legal Consequences

**N. 11** Violation of the procedural guarantees of Art. 29 BV leads in principle to annulment of the contested decision. For violations of the right to be heard, case law distinguishes between curable and incurable violations. Violations of the right to be heard are curable if the affected person can still present their arguments in the legal remedy proceedings and the legal remedy instance has full cognition.

**N. 12** In case of undue procedural duration, the affected person may claim damages and satisfaction. The Federal Supreme Court has recognized compensation claims for excessively long proceedings in its case law on Art. 29 para. 1 BV in conjunction with Art. 6 no. 1 ECHR.

### 5. Disputed Points

**N. 13** In doctrine, it is disputed to what extent free legal proceedings also allows partial granting. Ehrenzeller/Schindler/Schweizer/Vallender (St. Galler Kommentar BV, 4th ed. 2023, Art. 29 N 62) take the position that a gradation according to the degree of indigence must be possible. In contrast, part of the doctrine advocates for an all-or-nothing solution. The Federal Supreme Court affirmed the admissibility of partial granting in **BGE 141 III 369**.

**N. 14** The question of whether Art. 29 para. 2 BV guarantees a right to oral proceedings is also controversially discussed. While Kiener/Kälin/Wyttenbach (Grundrechte, 3rd ed. 2018, § 32 N 45) derive such a right from the right to be heard, the prevailing doctrine rejects this and refers to the special regulation in Art. 6 no. 1 ECHR for civil claims and criminal matters.

### 6. Practical Guidelines

**N. 15** When asserting violations of the right to be heard, it should be noted that these must in principle be objected to immediately after becoming aware of them. A belated assertion may be interpreted as a waiver of asserting the violation, provided the party was represented by legal counsel.

**N. 16** For the practice of free legal proceedings, it is relevant that the application must be filed in a timely manner, that is, before expiry of the relevant procedural deadline. Subsequent granting to restore missed deadlines is only possible in exceptional cases. The indigence examination is based on the concrete financial circumstances, taking into account the subsistence minimum calculation under debt enforcement law.
