## Doctrine

### 1. Legislative History

**N. 1** Art. 36 BV dates back to the total revision of the Federal Constitution and was adopted by the Swiss people on 18 April 1999. The provision consolidates the doctrine of limitations on fundamental rights that had already been developed under the old Federal Constitution of 1874 (BBl 1997 I 1, 163ff.). The constitutional legislator thereby elevated the Federal Supreme Court jurisprudence and legal doctrine on the unwritten prerequisites for fundamental rights interferences to constitutional rank.

**N. 2** The Federal Council's message emphasized that Art. 36 BV codifies «the doctrine on limitations of fundamental rights developed over decades by jurisprudence and legal doctrine» (BBl 1997 I 163). The emergency clause in para. 1 sentence 3 was already recognized under the old Federal Constitution, but was explicitly incorporated into constitutional law for the first time. The core content protection (para. 4) also represented a transposition of judge-made principles.

### 2. Systematic Classification

**N. 3** As the «general limitation clause for fundamental rights limitations», Art. 36 BV forms the cornerstone of the fundamental rights protection system. The provision stands at the end of the catalogue of fundamental rights (Art. 7–35 BV) and applies to all fundamental rights, unless more specific limitation provisions exist (→ Art. 10 para. 2 BV for personal liberty; → Art. 31 BV for deprivation of liberty).

**N. 4** The provision is closely linked to Art. 5 BV (principles of rule of law conduct). The principle of legality (Art. 5 para. 1 BV) is concretized in Art. 36 para. 1 BV, while the principle of proportionality (Art. 5 para. 2 BV) is given a fundamental rights-specific form in Art. 36 para. 3 BV. Moreover, there is a systematic connection to Art. 35 BV (realization of fundamental rights), since both articles regulate the state's obligation to respect fundamental rights.

**N. 5** In relation to international law, Art. 36 BV must be interpreted in light of international human rights guarantees (→ Art. 190 BV). The ECHR and UN covenants contain similar, but sometimes more strictly formulated limitation provisions, which must be taken into account when interpreting Art. 36 BV (**BGE 139 I 16 E. 3**).

### 3. Elements / Normative Content

#### Legal Basis (Para. 1)

**N. 6** The requirement of a legal basis demands that every fundamental rights interference be based on a legal norm. The requirements for the normative level depend on the severity of the interference: minor interferences may be based on regulatory law, while serious interferences must be provided for in formal law itself (Häfelin/Haller/Keller/Thurnherr, Schweizerisches Bundesstaatsrecht, 10th ed. 2020, N 332).

**N. 7** The principle of legal certainty requires that the legal basis be formulated with such precision that those subject to the law can orient their conduct accordingly and foresee the consequences of certain behavior with a degree of certainty appropriate to the circumstances (**BGE 136 I 87 E. 3.1**). In police law, the requirements for certainty are reduced due to the unpredictability of the regulatory situations (Rhinow/Schefer/Uebersax, Schweizerisches Verfassungsrecht, 3rd ed. 2016, § 11 N 49).

**N. 8** The emergency clause exceptionally permits fundamental rights interferences without a legal basis in cases of «serious, imminent and otherwise unavoidable danger». These requirements are cumulative and must be interpreted restrictively (**BGE 130 I 65 E. 3.1**). Police emergency situations justify only temporary measures; a legal basis must be created as soon as possible (Müller/Schefer, Grundrechte in der Schweiz, 4th ed. 2008, p. 127).

#### Public Interest (Para. 2)

**N. 9** Fundamental rights restrictions must be justified by a public interest or the protection of third parties' fundamental rights. All public welfare concerns qualify as public interests: security, order, public health, but also the functional capacity of state institutions or rule of law procedural guarantees (**BGE 129 I 232 E. 3**).

**N. 10** The protection of third parties' fundamental rights constitutes an independent justification ground. The state also has a duty to protect fundamental rights, which can justify interferences with other fundamental rights positions (Tschannen/Zimmerli/Müller, Allgemeines Verwaltungsrecht, 4th ed. 2014, § 51 N 11). In cases of conflicting fundamental rights, practical concordance must be established (**BGE 144 I 170 E. 3**).

#### Proportionality (Para. 3)

**N. 11** The principle of proportionality requires a three-part test: the measure must be suitable, necessary and reasonable (proportionate in the narrow sense) (**BGE 145 IV 263 E. 3.4**). These test steps must be worked through sequentially; if the measure fails on one element, further examination becomes unnecessary.

**N. 12** Suitability requires that the measure be capable of achieving or at least promoting the intended goal. Necessity requires that no milder, equally effective means be available (**BGE 142 I 121 E. 6**). Reasonableness requires a balancing between the severity of the interference and the weight of the public interests justifying it (**BGE 140 I 2 E. 6.3**).

#### Core Content (Para. 4)

**N. 13** The core content encompasses the inviolable minimum of a fundamental right, without which it would lose its character (**BGE 147 I 372 E. 2.3**). Legal doctrine disagrees whether the core content should be understood as absolute (every fundamental right has a fixed inviolable core) or relative (the core is determined by circumstances). The Federal Supreme Court tends toward a relative understanding, but has left the question open (Ehrenzeller/Schindler/Schweizer/Vallender, St. Galler Kommentar BV, 4th ed. 2023, Art. 36 N 44).

### 4. Legal Consequences

**N. 14** If any of the requirements of Art. 36 BV is missing, the fundamental rights interference is unconstitutional. In concrete judicial review proceedings, this leads to non-application of the unlawful norm in the individual case. In abstract judicial review, the norm is invalidated.

**N. 15** In cases of factual acts, those affected have a claim to omission or elimination of the unconstitutional state of affairs. If damage was caused by the unlawful interference, a state liability claim exists (→ Art. 146 BV). In cases of serious personality violations, satisfaction may also be owed.

### 5. Areas of Controversy

**N. 16** **Normative Level Theory**: Kiener/Kälin/Wyttenbach (Grundrechte, 3rd ed. 2018, § 6 N 51ff.) advocate a differentiated normative level theory, according to which the required normative level depends on the intensity of interference. In contrast, Schefer (in: Waldmann/Belser/Epiney, BSK BV, 2nd ed. 2024, Art. 36 N 18) demands a formal statutory basis for all substantial interferences, regardless of intensity.

**N. 17** **Core Content Theory**: The absolute core content theory is advocated by Rhinow/Schefer/Uebersax (Schweizerisches Verfassungsrecht, 3rd ed. 2016, § 11 N 64), who attribute an inviolable essential content to every fundamental right. Müller/Schefer (Grundrechte in der Schweiz, 4th ed. 2008, pp. 134f.) argue for a relative understanding, where the core content is determined by concrete circumstances. The Federal Supreme Court has deliberately left this controversy open (**BGE 129 I 173 E. 4**).

**N. 18** **Emergency Doctrine**: Tschannen (Staatsrecht der Schweizerischen Eidgenossenschaft, 5th ed. 2021, § 7 N 38) interprets the emergency clause narrowly and permits it only for short-term police measures. Häfelin/Haller/Keller/Thurnherr (Schweizerisches Bundesstaatsrecht, 10th ed. 2020, N 340) also recognize longer-term emergency measures, provided creating a legal basis is objectively impossible.

### 6. Practice Notes

**N. 19** When examining fundamental rights interferences, the following procedure is recommended: First, identify the affected fundamental right and determine the scope of protection. If an interference exists, examine the four requirements of Art. 36 BV in sequence. Special attention should be paid to the proportionality test, which often determines the outcome.

**N. 20** In legal application, distinctions must be made between different types of fundamental rights: liberty rights have a broad scope of protection with limitation reservations, while procedural fundamental rights (→ Art. 29–32 BV) are often guaranteed without reservation. For social rights (→ Art. 12 BV), the justiciable core content must be distinguished from programmatic content.

**N. 21** In legislation, Art. 36 BV must be observed preventively: new enactments should be formulated to meet the requirements of legal certainty and normative level. The legislative materials should document the public interest and proportionality considerations to facilitate later judicial review.
