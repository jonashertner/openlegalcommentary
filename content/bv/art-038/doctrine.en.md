# Art. 38 FC — Acquisition and Loss of Citizenship

## Doctrine

### 1. Legislative History

**N. 1** Art. 38 FC traces back to Art. 44 of the Federal Constitution of 1874 (aFC), which assigned to the Confederation legislative competence over the acquisition and loss of Swiss citizenship. The 1874 revision established for the first time a central federal competence, while reserving to the cantons and communes the actual naturalisation decision. This federalist basic structure was carried over into the FC 1999.

**N. 2** The Federal Council's Dispatch on the FC 1999 (BBl 1997 I 1 ff., esp. 159 f.) emphasised that Art. 38 FC represented no substantive change from Art. 44 aFC, but rather consolidated the existing allocation of competences at the constitutional level. Art. 38 para. 1 FC codifies the exclusive federal competence for acquisition and loss by descent, marriage and adoption, as well as for readmission to citizenship; Art. 38 para. 2 FC limits federal competence in the case of ordinary naturalisation by the cantons to the enactment of minimum requirements and the granting of the federal naturalisation permit.

**N. 3** Para. 3 was adopted in the popular vote of 12 February 2017 and entered into force on the same day (Federal Decree of 30 September 2016, Federal Council Decree of 13 April 2017, AS 2017 2). The constitutional mandate was based on the Federal Council's Dispatch of 4 March 2011 on the total revision of the Citizenship Act (BBl 2011 2825) and a parliamentary constitutional amendment proposal that anchored at the constitutional level the facilitation of naturalisation for persons of the third generation of foreign nationals as well as for stateless children. The Dispatch for the 2016 popular vote emphasised that the descendants of the second generation of immigrants who had grown up in Switzerland were in fact integrated, but that formal naturalisation was disproportionately difficult (BBl 2016 7097 ff.).

---

### 2. Systematic Classification

**N. 4** Art. 38 FC is a competence norm, not a fundamental rights norm. It confers no constitutional entitlement to naturalisation on the individual; the individual may rely only on ordinary statutory law — in particular the Citizenship Act of 20 June 2014 (CitA; SR 141.0) — and on constitutional fundamental rights guarantees (Art. 8, 9, 29 FC) (Achermann/von Rütte, BSK BV, Art. 38 N. 3 f.; Rhinow/Schefer/Uebersax, Schweizerisches Verfassungsrecht, 3rd ed. 2016, para. 300 ff.).

**N. 5** The provision is connected with Art. 37 FC (↔ Art. 37 FC, three-part citizenship: federal, cantonal and communal citizenship) and Art. 39 FC (→ Art. 39 FC, exercise of political rights). The division of responsibilities between the Confederation, cantons and communes in ordinary naturalisation is anchored at the constitutional level in Art. 38 para. 2 FC and is given concrete form in ordinary legislation by the CitA. The permissibility of cantonal and communal requirements must always be assessed against Art. 38 para. 2 FC as well as → Art. 8 and → Art. 9 FC.

**N. 6** Art. 38 FC contains no directly applicable norm in favour of private individuals, but rather a legislative mandate to the Confederation. The Federal Supreme Court no longer qualifies naturalisation decisions as political acts outside the rule of law, but as sovereign acts to which the principles of the rule of law apply without restriction (**BGE 149 I 91** E. 2.6; Gutzwiller, CR CO/Cst., Art. 38 N. 4).

### 3. Elements of the Provision / Normative Content

#### 3.1 Para. 1: Exclusive Federal Competence

**N. 7** Para. 1 sentence 1 transfers to the Confederation the exclusive regulatory competence for the acquisition and loss of citizenship by descent (*ius sanguinis*), marriage and adoption. The competence is exhaustive — the cantons have no regulatory discretion in this area. The current CitA of 20 June 2014 gives concrete form to these areas in Art. 1 ff. (descent), Art. 21 ff. (facilitated naturalisation of spouses of Swiss citizens) and Art. 7 f. (adoption).

**N. 8** Para. 1 sentence 2 additionally transfers to the Confederation the regulatory competence for the loss of Swiss citizenship on other grounds (in particular release and revocation pursuant to Art. 42 ff. CitA) as well as for readmission to citizenship (Art. 51 ff. CitA). Readmission to citizenship must be distinguished from ordinary naturalisation: it presupposes previous possession of Swiss citizenship.

#### 3.2 Para. 2: Minimum Requirements and Naturalisation Permit

**N. 9** Para. 2 contains a concurrent legislative competence: the Confederation enacts minimum requirements (*prescriptions minimales*) governing the naturalisation of foreign nationals by the cantons. The use of the term «minimum requirements» means that the cantons may impose stricter, but not less demanding, requirements (Achermann/von Rütte, BSK BV, Art. 38 N. 37 f.; Gutzwiller, CR Cst., Art. 38 N. 36 f.; **BGE 148 I 271** E. 4.2; **BGE 138 I 305** E. 1.4.3). Where federal law exhaustively defines integration criteria, cantonal derogations to the detriment of applicants are unconstitutional (**BGE 148 I 271** E. 5.5).

**N. 10** In addition, the Confederation — acting through the State Secretariat for Migration (SEM) — grants the naturalisation permit. This federal naturalisation permit is a prerequisite for every ordinary naturalisation (Art. 13 CitA). The SEM examines compliance with the federal minimum requirements; once these are satisfied, case law holds that there is no discretion to refuse the permit (**BGE 149 I 91** E. 2.6; **BGE 146 I 49** E. 2.7). An appeal on points of public law to the Federal Supreme Court lies against decisions of the Federal Administrative Court on the federal naturalisation permit (**BGE 149 I 91** E. 2.7).

**N. 11** The naturalisation procedure is three-tiered: (1) review and grant of the permit by the SEM at the federal level; (2) decision by the competent cantonal authority; (3) decision by the commune. The division of responsibilities means that no substantive double examination takes place — the SEM focuses on the criteria of public security and order, while the canton and commune assess local integration (**BGE 149 I 91** E. 2.8).

#### 3.3 Para. 3: Facilitated Naturalisation for Specific Groups of Persons

**N. 12** Para. 3 lit. a enshrines a constitutional mandate to facilitate the naturalisation of persons of the third generation of foreign nationals. This covers foreign nationals whose parents and at least one grandparent have already lived in Switzerland and who have themselves grown up in Switzerland. The constitutional anchoring of this facilitation goes back to the 2016 constitutional amendment proposal and is given concrete form in ordinary legislation by the CitA (Art. 24a ff.).

**N. 13** Para. 3 lit. b obliges the Confederation to facilitate the naturalisation of stateless children (children without any nationality). The background is the International Convention on the Reduction of Statelessness (SR 0.141.12). The facilitation is intended to prevent statelessness from being perpetuated in practice. The CitA provides for a simplified naturalisation of stateless children in Art. 31.

**N. 14** Para. 3 contains a direct legislative mandate to the Confederation. It establishes neither a subjective right to naturalisation nor a directly applicable norm; implementation is a matter for the ordinary legislature (Achermann/von Rütte, BSK BV, Art. 38 N. 52 f.).

### 4. Legal Consequences

**N. 15** The allocation of competences established by Art. 38 FC has the following legal consequences: In the domain of para. 1, federal law applies exclusively; cantonal law is not permissible (→ Art. 49 para. 1 FC, supremacy of federal law). In the domain of para. 2, federal law applies as a minimum standard; cantonal requirements are permissible provided they are constitutionally compliant and do not render naturalisation excessively difficult.

**N. 16** If a person satisfies all federal and cantonal naturalisation requirements, a refusal of naturalisation would be arbitrary (Art. 9 FC) and would violate the principle of equality before the law (Art. 8 para. 1 FC). The legal situation in such a case approaches an entitlement, without a formal right to naturalisation existing (**BGE 146 I 49** E. 2.7; Rhinow/Schefer/Uebersax, Schweizerisches Verfassungsrecht, 3rd ed. 2016, para. 300 ff.; Achermann/von Rütte, BSK BV, Art. 38 N. 35).

**N. 17** Integration requirements must on the whole be proportionate and free from discrimination. Focusing on a single criterion is impermissible; a holistic assessment of all relevant aspects is required (**BGE 146 I 49** E. 4.4; **BGE 138 I 242** E. 5.3). Excessive language proficiency requirements or the non-recognition of equivalent proof of language skills violate Art. 38 para. 2 FC as well as Art. 9 FC (**BGE 148 I 271** E. 5.11). Discriminatory refusals of naturalisation — for example on grounds of intellectual disability — must be assessed against Art. 8 para. 2 FC; discrimination may be overcome by qualified justification, but not by blanket group exclusion (**BGE 139 I 169** E. 7.3.1).

**N. 18** The federal naturalisation permit has the legal character of a final decision amenable to appeal. If the SEM refuses the permit, the dispute is resolved on the merits; the canton cannot naturalise without the federal permit (**BGE 149 I 91** E. 2.7). The SEM's decision is an individual examination and attribution of the statutory requirements; collective liability by attributing the security risk posed by family members is impermissible (**BGE 149 I 91** E. 4.2).

### 5. Contested Issues

**N. 19** **Entitlement to naturalisation:** It is controversially debated whether, once all requirements are met, a legal entitlement to naturalisation exists. The Federal Supreme Court denies a formal entitlement, but acknowledges that «no discretion» remains to refuse (**BGE 138 I 305** E. 1.4.5; **BGE 146 I 49** E. 2.7). Rhinow/Schefer/Uebersax (Schweizerisches Verfassungsrecht, 3rd ed. 2016, para. 300 ff.) describe the situation as an «approximation to an entitlement situation». Achermann/von Rütte (BSK BV, Art. 38 N. 35) concur with this assessment. Gutzwiller (Droit de la nationalité suisse, 2016, pp. 39 f.) highlights the associated tension with the classical concept of sovereignty, according to which naturalisation constitutes a political sovereign act that should be exempt from judicial review — a view that the Federal Supreme Court abandoned from **BGE 130 I 140** onwards and increasingly so from **BGE 138 I 305**.

**N. 20** **Scope of the cantonal minimum requirements competence:** Contentious is the reach of the concept of «further integration criteria» within the meaning of Art. 12 para. 3 CitA: Achermann/von Rütte (BSK BV, Art. 38 N. 37 f.) and Gutzwiller (CR Cst., Art. 38 N. 36 f.) take the view that the cantons may give concrete form to the federal criteria, but may not alter them in substance. Von Rütte (Das neue Bürgerrechtsgesetz und dessen Umsetzung in den Kantonen, Jahrbuch für Migrationsrecht 2017/2018, p. 77) points out that «further» criteria are to be understood additively and do not permit modification of the federal requirements. The Federal Supreme Court left this question open in **BGE 148 I 271** E. 4.3, but held that cantonal language requirements that undermine the federal proof of language skills may come into conflict with the supremacy of federal law (→ Art. 49 FC).

**N. 21** **Communal autonomy vs. minimum requirements:** Legal scholarship is divided on how far communal autonomy (→ Art. 50 para. 1 FC) extends in naturalisation matters. The Federal Supreme Court held in **BGE 146 I 83** E. 2.3 that communes cannot derive an independent autonomy from federal law; their room for manoeuvre derives exclusively from cantonal law. Raselli (Die Einbürgerung zwischen Politik und Justiz, ZBl 112/2011, pp. 578 ff.) emphasises that the historically deep-rooted communal naturalisation authority constitutes a cultural core that, even after **BGE 130 I 140**, has not been fully subjected to judicial review. Uebersax (Das Bundesgericht und das Bürgerrechtsgesetz, BJM 2016, pp. 177 ff.), by contrast, sees the development as consistent: once the naturalisation decision was recognised as a sovereign act, the rule-of-law guarantees became fully applicable.

### 6. Practical Notes

**N. 22** **Three-tier examination structure:** Practitioners must observe the three-tier allocation of competences (Confederation/SEM, canton, commune) in proceedings. The decision on the federal naturalisation permit (SEM → Federal Administrative Court → Federal Supreme Court) and the cantonal naturalisation decision (cantonal appeals authority → Federal Supreme Court by way of subsidiary constitutional complaint, Art. 113 ff. SCA) are procedurally independent of each other.

**N. 23** **Integration requirements:** Integration requirements must always be assessed on the basis of a holistic evaluation. Individual shortcomings — for example in geographical or cultural knowledge — may be offset by strengths in other criteria (**BGE 146 I 49** E. 4.4). Requirements may not exceed what could reasonably be expected of an average Swiss citizen resident in the commune (**BGE 146 I 49** E. 4.3).

**N. 24** **Language skills:** Proof of language skills must be based on a standardised, quality-assured procedure. Equivalent officially recognised qualifications — in particular sufficient Federal Maturity Certificate grades in the local language — must be accepted as proof of language skills; a double validation by the SEM is excessively formalistic (**BGE 148 I 271** E. 5.9–5.11). Local-language requirements must be proportionate and must respect the territoriality principle of language law (↔ Art. 70 FC).

**N. 25** **Security criteria:** The criterion of not posing a threat to public security must be assessed individually. Family relationships alone with persons posing a security risk are not sufficient to refuse naturalisation; what is required is the applicant's own contribution or a conscious, material profiting by the applicant (**BGE 149 I 91** E. 4.2). Internet sources from media reports that are used as decisive evidence in proceedings must be presented to the persons concerned in advance for comment (→ Art. 29 para. 2 FC; **BGE 149 I 91** E. 3.4).

**N. 26** **Special groups of persons:** In the naturalisation of persons with an intellectual disability, a lack of capacity to act must not lead to a blanket exclusion from naturalisation. What is determinative is the presumed will of the person concerned as well as an assessment of the other naturalisation requirements tailored to the specific abilities of that person (**BGE 139 I 169** E. 7.3.2). Art. 33 f. CitA expressly provides for the representation of persons lacking capacity to act in naturalisation proceedings.

**N. 27** **Constitutional mandate para. 3:** For the practical application of the facilitated naturalisation of persons of the third generation of foreign nationals (Art. 38 para. 3 lit. a FC in conjunction with Art. 24a ff. CitA), the causality between the long-standing residence of the preceding generations and the integration of the applicant is determinative. The entitlement to facilitated naturalisation derives solely from the CitA; Art. 38 para. 3 FC is formulated as a constitutional mandate and does not establish a subjective right ↔ Art. 37 FC.
