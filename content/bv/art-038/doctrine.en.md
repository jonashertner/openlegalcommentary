# Art. 38 BV

## Doctrine

### 1. Legislative History

**N. 1** Art. 38 BV was created within the framework of the total revision of the Federal Constitution in 1999 and unites federal competences in the area of citizenship that were previously distributed across various articles. The provision continues the tradition of Art. 43 and 44 of the old Constitution, whereby the wording was modernised and formulated in gender-neutral terms (BBl 1997 I 270 ff.).

**N. 2** The most significant change occurred through the popular vote of 12 February 2017, when para. 3 was added. This extension obliges the Confederation to facilitate the naturalisation of third-generation foreigners and stateless children (BBl 2014 2883, BBl 2015 769). The new paragraph reflects Switzerland's demographic reality with a high proportion of third-generation foreigners.

### 2. Systematic Classification

**N. 3** Art. 38 BV stands in Title 2 «Citizenship and Political Rights» and together with Art. 37 BV (citizenship) forms the constitutional foundation of Swiss nationality law. The norm is a **competence norm** that transfers comprehensive legislative powers to the Confederation in the area of citizenship.

**N. 4** Systematically, Art. 38 BV is closely linked with:
- → Art. 37 BV (dual citizenship and three-tier structure of citizenship)
- → Art. 5 BV (rule of law principle, particularly in naturalisation procedures)
- → Art. 8 BV (equality before the law and prohibition of discrimination)
- → Art. 9 BV (prohibition of arbitrary action)
- → Art. 29 BV (general procedural guarantees)
- → Art. 50 BV (municipal autonomy)

**N. 5** The system of Swiss citizenship is based on the three-tier principle: municipal, cantonal and federal citizenship are inseparably linked (Art. 37 para. 1 BV). This connection shapes the division of competences in ordinary naturalisation (**BGE 146 I 49** E. 2.1).

### 3. Elements of the Offence / Content of the Norm

**N. 6** **Paragraph 1** encompasses three areas of regulation:
- **Acquisition by descent** (ius sanguinis): Swiss citizenship is primarily acquired through descent from a Swiss parent
- **Acquisition by marriage**: Today concerns only the facilitated naturalisation of spouses
- **Acquisition by adoption**: Minor children acquire citizenship upon adoption by Swiss parents

**N. 7** The **loss of citizenship** «for other reasons» encompasses according to doctrine (Ehrenzeller/Schindler/Schweizer/Vallender, SGK BV, Art. 38 N 12; Waldmann/Belser/Epiney, BSK BV, Art. 38 N 8):
- Release upon request
- Declaration of nullity of naturalisation
- Withdrawal from dual citizens (under strict conditions)

**N. 8** **Paragraph 2** establishes a dualistic system of ordinary naturalisation:
- The Confederation issues **minimum requirements** as a federal framework
- The cantons (and municipalities) regulate naturalisation within the framework of these provisions
- The Confederation grants the **federal naturalisation permit** as a formal act

**N. 9** The «minimum requirements» must be proportionate according to **BGE 148 I 271** E. 5.2 and may not undermine cantonal competence. They typically include residence periods, integration criteria and procedural guarantees.

**N. 10** **Paragraph 3** (since 2017) obliges the Confederation to create facilitated naturalisation procedures for:
- **Third-generation foreigners**: Grandchildren of immigrants whose parents were already born or raised in Switzerland
- **Stateless children**: Minors without nationality

### 4. Legal Consequences

**N. 11** Art. 38 BV establishes **comprehensive federal competence** in the area of citizenship. The Confederation has exercised this competence through the Swiss Citizenship Act (BüG) of 20 June 2014 (in force since 1.1.2018).

**N. 12** For **ordinary naturalisation**, para. 2 results in shared competence:
- Federal level: Minimum requirements and granting of the federal permit
- Cantons: Specification of naturalisation requirements insofar as they go beyond federal provisions
- Municipalities: Decision on municipal citizenship within the framework of their autonomy (**BGE 146 I 83**)

**N. 13** Naturalisation does not establish **a subjective legal claim**. However, there is a right to treatment free from arbitrary action and discrimination (**BGE 138 I 305** E. 5.8). Where all requirements are fulfilled, a refusal would be arbitrary (**BGE 146 I 49** E. 2.3).

**N. 14** Facilitated naturalisation according to para. 3 is a **purely federal competence**. Cantons and municipalities have no say here, which represents a systemic break with the three-tier citizenship principle (critically: Rhinow/Schefer/Uebersax, Schweizerisches Verfassungsrecht, 3rd ed. 2016, para. 3985).

### 5. Disputed Issues

**N. 15** **Scope of municipal autonomy**: The extent to which municipal autonomy reaches in ordinary naturalisation is disputed. While Tschannen (Tschannen/Zimmerli/Müller, Allgemeines Verwaltungsrecht, 4th ed. 2014, § 15 N 28) advocates for broad autonomy, Kiener (Kiener/Kälin/Wyttenbach, Grundrechte, 3rd ed. 2018, § 10 N 15) emphasises the binding effect of fundamental rights.

**N. 16** **Relationship to Art. 8 ECHR**: Whether Art. 8 ECHR can establish a right to naturalisation is controversially discussed. The prevailing doctrine rejects this (Müller/Schefer, Grundrechte in der Schweiz, 4th ed. 2008, p. 896; different view Thurnherr, ZSR 2018, 5 ff., who affirms a claim in extreme cases).

**N. 17** **Integration criteria**: The requirements for integration are disputed. While Waldmann (BSK BV, Art. 38 N 14) advocates for objectively measurable criteria, Biaggini (BV Kommentar, 2nd ed. 2017, Art. 38 N 5) defends the discretionary scope of naturalisation authorities. The Federal Court has described excessive requirements as unconstitutional (**BGE 148 I 271**).

**N. 18** **Dual citizenship**: The automatic acceptance of dual citizenship since 1992 is criticised. Schindler (SGK BV, Art. 38 N 18) sees this as a weakening of the loyalty principle, while Kälin (Kälin/Kühler, in: Kaufmann/Krauthammer, Staatsbürgerschaft und Teilhabe, 2020, p. 45) defends this as adaptation to global mobility.

### 6. Practice Notes

**N. 19** In **ordinary naturalisation**, the three-tier examination must be observed:
1. Fulfilment of federal minimum requirements (Art. 9–11 BüG)
2. Fulfilment of additional cantonal requirements
3. Consent of the municipality of residence (municipal autonomy)

**N. 20** **Procedural guarantees**: The naturalisation procedure is subject to general procedural guarantees (→ Art. 29 BV). In particular, there is a right to be heard and to receive reasons for rejections (**BGE 149 I 91** E. 4).

**N. 21** **Legal remedies**: Against negative naturalisation decisions, cantonal legal remedies are available and ultimately appeal to the Federal Court. However, cognition is limited to arbitrary action and fundamental rights violations (**BGE 138 I 305**).

**N. 22** **Language requirements** must be proportionate and oriented to the concrete circumstances. The non-recognition of adequate school certificates as proof of language skills was qualified as excessive formalism (**BGE 148 I 271** E. 6.4).

**N. 23** **Facilitated naturalisation** according to para. 3 takes place in a purely federal procedure at the State Secretariat for Migration (SEM). The material requirements are regulated in Art. 24–26 BüG (third generation) and Art. 23 BüG (stateless persons).
