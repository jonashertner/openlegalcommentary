## Doctrine

### 1. Legislative History

**N. 1** Art. 35 FC was introduced with the total revision of the Federal Constitution in 1999. The Federal Council's Message of 20 November 1996 (FG 1997 I 1, 154 ff.) designated this provision as a "key article" for the understanding of fundamental rights under the new Constitution. The provision combines three central principles: the objective legal dimension of fundamental rights (para. 1), comprehensive fundamental rights obligations in the performance of state tasks (para. 2) and the third-party effect of fundamental rights (para. 3).

**N. 2** The constitutional legislator sought to expressly anchor the principles developed in case law and doctrine. In particular, the extension of fundamental rights obligations to all entities carrying out state tasks (para. 2) went beyond previous practice, which primarily focused on formal state affiliation. The constitutional anchoring of third-party effect (para. 3) was intended to constitutionally secure the indirect third-party effect already recognised under the old Federal Constitution (FG 1997 I 154).

### 2. Systematic Classification

**N. 3** Art. 35 FC, together with Art. 36 FC (restrictions on fundamental rights), forms the centrepiece of the general fundamental rights provisions. The provision is deliberately placed at the end of the fundamental rights catalogue and before the restriction provisions to emphasise its validity for all preceding fundamental rights (Art. 7–34 FC).

**N. 4** The provision corresponds with → Art. 5 FC (principles of rule of law conduct), which establishes the binding of state action to law and the constitution. While Art. 5 FC regulates formal legal binding, Art. 35 FC specifies the material fundamental rights binding. Central cross-references exist to → Art. 8 FC (equality before the law), → Art. 9 FC (protection from arbitrariness) and ↔ Art. 36 FC (restrictions on fundamental rights).

### 3. Elements of the Offence / Content of the Norm

#### 3.1 Validity in the Entire Legal Order (Para. 1)

**N. 5** Art. 35 para. 1 FC anchors the objective legal dimension of fundamental rights. Müller/Schefer (Grundrechte in der Schweiz, 4th ed. 2008, 54 f.) emphasise that fundamental rights are not only subjective defensive rights, but also objective principle norms that permeate the entire legal order. This radiating effect encompasses all areas of law — from constitutional law through administrative law to civil and criminal law.

**N. 6** According to Kiener/Kälin/Wyttenbach (Grundrechte, 3rd ed. 2018, § 4 N. 14) this specifically means: legislators and law-applying authorities must consider fundamental rights as an interpretive maxim when interpreting indeterminate legal concepts and in discretionary decisions. The Federal Supreme Court consistently applies this principle, for instance in the fundamental rights-compliant interpretation of general clauses in civil law (**BGE 129 III 35**).

#### 3.2 Binding in State Tasks (Para. 2)

**N. 7** The functional concept of tasks in para. 2 considerably extends fundamental rights obligations. According to Häfelin/Haller/Keller/Thurnherr (Schweizerisches Bundesstaatsrecht, 10th ed. 2020, N. 329), this covers:
- Authorities at all state levels (Confederation, cantons, municipalities)
- Public law corporations and institutions
- Mixed-economy enterprises in fulfilling public tasks
- Private entities in exercising delegated state tasks

**N. 8** Case law has specified this obligation: the SBB are bound by fundamental rights in railway station management (**BGE 138 I 274**), as is the SRG in advertising (**BGE 139 I 306**) and on digital platforms (**BGE 149 I 2**). The functional perspective is decisive: whoever performs state tasks is bound by fundamental rights regardless of legal form.

#### 3.3 Third-Party Effect of Fundamental Rights (Para. 3)

**N. 9** According to Rhinow/Schefer/Uebersax (Schweizerisches Verfassungsrecht, 3rd ed. 2016, N. 1087), third-party effect encompasses two aspects: the state's protective duty when fundamental rights are endangered by private parties and the indirect third-party effect through general clauses and indeterminate legal concepts.

**N. 10** Biaggini (BV-Kommentar, 2nd ed. 2017, Art. 35 N. 11) distinguishes three intensity levels of third-party effect:
- Direct third-party effect with explicit legal provision (e.g. protection against dismissal)
- Indirect third-party effect through civil law general clauses
- Protective duties in cases of serious fundamental rights endangerment

### 4. Legal Consequences

**N. 11** Art. 35 FC gives rise to different legal consequences according to paragraph:

Para. 1 establishes the duty of fundamental rights-compliant interpretation of the entire legal order. Norms must be interpreted in light of fundamental rights; where several interpretive variants exist, the one that best corresponds to fundamental rights must be chosen (Tschannen, in: St. Galler Kommentar BV, 4th ed. 2023, Art. 35 N. 5).

**N. 12** Para. 2 leads to direct fundamental rights obligations for all state task bearers. Violations can be challenged with constitutional procedural legal remedies. Fundamental rights obligations apply regardless of the chosen form of action — even private law action by state task bearers is subject to fundamental rights (**BGE 129 III 35**).

**N. 13** Para. 3 obliges the legislature to enact fundamental rights-protecting norms. The justiciability of protective duties is disputed; the Federal Supreme Court recognises broad legislative discretion and intervenes only in cases of evident protection gaps (Waldmann, BSK BV, 2nd ed. 2024, Art. 35 N. 42).

### 5. Points of Contention

**N. 14** The scope of fundamental rights obligations for mixed-economy enterprises is doctrinally disputed. While Häfelin/Müller/Uhlmann (Allgemeines Verwaltungsrecht, 8th ed. 2020, N. 1845) advocate comprehensive obligations in case of any state participation, Tschannen (St. Galler Kommentar BV, Art. 35 N. 18) argues for differentiated consideration according to task areas.

**N. 15** Regarding third-party effect, positions diverge on the intensity of state protective duties. Schweizer (in: St. Galler Kommentar BV, Art. 35 N. 25) demands active fundamental rights protection through legislation, while Kiener/Kälin/Wyttenbach (Grundrechte, § 4 N. 45) focus primarily on indirect third-party effect through civil law general clauses.

**N. 16** The question of negative protective duties is also controversial: must private parties be protected from excessive state fundamental rights protection? Biaggini (BV-Kommentar, Art. 35 N. 14) affirms this with reference to economic freedom, while Rhinow/Schefer/Uebersax (Verfassungsrecht, N. 1095) reject it.

### 6. Practice Notes

**N. 17** When examining fundamental rights obligations, a functional approach must always be taken: what is decisive is not the legal form of the actor, but the performance of a state task. Indicators include legal foundations, state financing or control, and monopoly-like positions.

**N. 18** Fundamental rights-compliant interpretation (para. 1) must be distinguished from constitution-compliant interpretation. While the latter only applies in cases of norm conflicts, the former is a general interpretive maxim. In private law disputes, fundamental rights must be given effect through general clauses such as Art. 2 CC or Art. 28 CC.

**N. 19** Different instruments are available for enforcing third-party effect (para. 3): in cases of legislative gaps, constitutional appeal for violation of protective duties can be raised. In individual cases, indirect third-party effect through civil law general clauses enables fundamental rights protection between private parties.
