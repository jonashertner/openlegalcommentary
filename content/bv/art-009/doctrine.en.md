# Art. 9 FC — Protection against Arbitrariness and Preservation of Good Faith

## Doctrine

### 1. Legislative History

**N. 1** Art. 9 FC codifies two principles that the Federal Supreme Court had already developed under Art. 4 of the former Federal Constitution (aFC) as unwritten constitutional guarantees. The Federal Council's message on the new Federal Constitution of 20 November 1996 expressly states that these principles «had already previously been derived from Art. 4 FC» and that the consolidation does not bring about any substantive changes (BBl 1997 I 181 f.). Their inclusion in the catalogue of fundamental rights was intended to make the guarantees more visible and to anchor them as independent constitutional rights.

**N. 2** During the parliamentary deliberations, the central question was whether the express entrenchment of the prohibition of arbitrariness as an independent fundamental right would compel a change in the Federal Supreme Court's case law on standing to appeal. The rapporteur of the Council of States committee, Councillor of States Inderkum, expressed the expectation that the Federal Supreme Court would abandon its restrictive practice on standing in complaints of arbitrariness (AB 1998 S 39). The Council of States adopted the provision without further discussion; the National Council did likewise (AB 1998 N 681).

**N. 3** The Federal Supreme Court, in **BGE 126 I 81 E. 3–6** — the first leading decision after the entry into force of the new Federal Constitution on 1 January 2000 — held that it would maintain its existing case law. It justified this on the basis of the absence of a clear parliamentary intention to change the law and on grounds of practical coherence; a change of system was to be reserved for a comprehensive reform of the system of legal remedies. At the same time, it held that Art. 9 FC does not alter the existing concept of arbitrariness in terms of substance: «The concept of arbitrariness derived from Art. 4 aFC has not changed in substance as a result of Art. 9 FC, which entered into force on 1 January 2000» (**BGE 127 I 38 E. 2**).

---

### 2. Systematic Classification

**N. 4** Art. 9 FC is located in the second chapter of the second title of the Federal Constitution («Fundamental Rights», Art. 7–36 FC). The provision is a genuine fundamental right with subjective content: «Every person has the right to …». This right is directed exclusively against state authorities (→ Art. 35 FC); Art. 9 FC does not apply directly between private individuals, but may produce indirect effects through the interpretation of general clauses.

**N. 5** Art. 9 FC contains two substantively independent guarantees: the **prohibition of arbitrariness** and the **principle of good faith** (protection of legitimate expectations). Both guarantees are combined as equals within a single provision, but must be distinguished doctrinally. The prohibition of arbitrariness protects against manifestly untenable state acts; the principle of good faith protects the legitimate trust of the citizen in the conduct of state authorities. Häfelin/Haller/Keller/Thurnherr, Schweizerisches Bundesstaatsrecht, 10th ed. 2020, N 807 ff. treat both guarantees as expressions of the general principle of fairness in the conduct of state authorities.

**N. 6** Art. 9 FC is closely linked to ↔ Art. 8 FC (equality before the law), since arbitrariness frequently constitutes unequal treatment at the same time; a legal act may simultaneously violate both provisions (**BGE 129 I 1 E. 3**). The protection against arbitrariness must further be distinguished from → Art. 29 FC (general procedural guarantees) and → Art. 5 para. 3 FC (principle of proportionality): outside the context of interferences with fundamental rights, the Federal Supreme Court reviews proportionality only from the perspective of arbitrariness (**BGE 134 I 153 E. 4**). In criminal proceedings, Art. 9 FC complements the more specific guarantee of the presumption of innocence under → Art. 32 para. 1 FC (**BGE 127 I 38 E. 2**).

**N. 7** Restrictions on the protection against arbitrariness and on good faith are permissible pursuant to → Art. 36 FC, provided they have a legal basis, are justified by a public interest and are proportionate. Their inviolable core (→ Art. 36 para. 4 FC) is, however, rarely discussed independently, since Art. 9 FC, as a relational guarantee — i.e. in relation to state acts — does not possess an essential content that is difficult to withdraw in the classical sense.

### 3. Normative Content

#### 3.1 Prohibition of Arbitrariness

**N. 8** The prohibition of arbitrariness protects against manifestly inappropriate state conduct. The Federal Supreme Court defines arbitrariness in consistent case law as follows: a state act is arbitrary if it is manifestly untenable, in clear contradiction with the actual situation, grossly violates a legal norm or an uncontested legal principle, or is shockingly contrary to the sense of justice. The Federal Supreme Court will, however, only quash a decision if not merely the reasoning, but also the **result** is untenable (Judgment 2C_752/2013 of 2 May 2014 E. 2.1; **BGE 129 I 8 E. 2.1**). A solution that merely appears suboptimal or inexpedient is not, in itself, arbitrary.

**N. 9** The prohibition of arbitrariness applies to all areas of state activity: legislation (law-making), application of the law, and actual administrative conduct. In the area of **law-making**, a legal act is arbitrary if it cannot be supported by serious objective reasons or if it is purposeless or pointless (**BGE 129 I 1 E. 3**). In the area of **application of the law**, the prohibition of arbitrariness applies in particular to the interpretation and application of cantonal law, to the establishment of facts and the assessment of evidence: arbitrariness exists where the judge manifestly fails to understand evidence that is material to the decision, disregards it without serious reason, or where the judge's inferences from the evidence gathered simply cannot be sustained (**BGE 129 I 8 E. 2.1**).

**N. 10** The prohibition of arbitrariness is addressed to all **state authorities** within the meaning of Art. 35 para. 2 FC, i.e. the Confederation, the cantons and the communes, as well as to private individuals insofar as they perform public tasks. Court decisions, administrative rulings, legislative acts and actual conduct are all equally covered.

#### 3.2 Principle of Good Faith (Protection of Legitimate Expectations)

**N. 11** The principle of good faith protects the legitimate trust of the citizen in state conduct. It obliges authorities to act consistently and coherently, and precludes a venire contra factum proprium. Rhinow/Schefer/Uebersax, Schweizerisches Verfassungsrecht, 3rd ed. 2016, N 2845 distinguish two components: the prohibition of contradictory conduct (requirement of coherence) and the protection of legitimate expectations in the narrow sense (protection of trust placed in concrete dispositions).

**N. 12** The protection of legitimate expectations in the narrow sense requires, according to consistent case law, five cumulatively satisfied conditions (**BGE 137 I 69 E. 2.5.1**; **BGE 117 Ia 285 E. 2**):
1. A basis for legitimate expectations: a specific, unambiguous representation made by a competent authority (assurance, information, ruling, or conduct);
2. Legitimate reliance: the person concerned was entitled to regard the basis for trust as authoritative, without culpably causing an error;
3. Detrimental reliance: the person acted in reliance on the basis for trust and thereby took dispositions;
4. A causal nexus between the basis for trust and the disposition;
5. No overriding public interest that takes precedence over the protection of legitimate expectations.

**N. 13** In tax law, a particular standard applies: protection of legitimate expectations in an official representation does not require an additional balancing of interests in that domain, provided the other conditions are clearly fulfilled. The particular importance of the principle of legality (→ Art. 127 para. 1 FC) is, however, limited to facts that operate to the detriment of citizens (**BGE 150 I 1 E. 4.1–4.3**).

**N. 14** The protection of legitimate expectations also protects reliance on rulings. In cases of **revocation of rulings**, the interest in the correct application of objective law (principle of legality) is weighed against the interest in the protection of legitimate expectations; the authority must carry out a balancing of interests. Where the interest in the protection of legitimate expectations prevails, revocation is in principle impermissible (**BGE 137 I 69 E. 2.3 and 2.6.3**).

### 4. Legal Consequences

**N. 15** An established violation of Art. 9 FC leads to the annulment of the contested act (cassation) or — where permissible — to a reformatory amendment by the appellate court. In the case of legislative acts, the upholding of a complaint of arbitrariness generally results in the annulment of the contested provision; in the case of administrative decisions and judgments, the matter is referred back to the lower instance for a new decision.

**N. 16** Art. 9 FC does not, on its own, confer **standing to appeal** within the meaning of Art. 115 lit. b of the Federal Supreme Court Act (BGG). The appellant must demonstrate a legally protected interest deriving from a statutory provision or a more specific fundamental right; the prohibition of arbitrariness alone is insufficient (**BGE 126 I 81 E. 3–6**; **BGE 133 I 185 E. 2.2**; **BGE 136 I 229 E. 3.2**). Exceptionally, a provision conferring bound discretion on the authority may give rise to a sufficiently defined right that confers standing to raise a complaint of arbitrariness (**BGE 138 I 305 E. 1.4.5**).

**N. 17** When raising complaints of arbitrariness before the Federal Supreme Court, a **qualified duty to state grounds** applies (Art. 106 para. 2 BGG): the appellant must clearly and substantively set out in what respect the contested decision violates Art. 9 FC. Merely appellate criticism of the lower court's judgment does not suffice (**BGE 133 II 249 E. 1.4.2**; **BGE 136 I 229 E. 4.1**).

### 5. Contested Issues

#### 5.1 Standing to Raise Complaints of Arbitrariness

**N. 18** The question of whether Art. 9 FC, as an independent fundamental right, confers standing to appeal on its own is controversial. The **prevailing academic opinion** advocates for broad standing: Auer/Malinverni/Hottelier, Droit constitutionnel suisse II, 3rd ed. 2013, N 1111 f. argue that, with its express entrenchment as a fundamental right, Art. 9 FC by itself confers a protected legal position. Kälin, Die Bedeutung der neuen Bundesverfassung für das öffentliche Verfahrensrecht, in: Berner Tage für die juristische Praxis 2000, p. 274 ff., distinguishes between direct addressees (for whom Art. 9 FC alone suffices) and third parties (for whom an additional protective provision remains necessary). Müller/Schefer, Grundrechte in der Schweiz, 4th ed. 2008, p. 4 ff. criticise the restrictive practice as conceptually inconsistent, since it renders a fundamental right nugatory through procedural law.

**N. 19** The **Federal Supreme Court**, by contrast, holds in consistent case law that Art. 9 FC does not relax the requirements for standing (**BGE 126 I 81 E. 4–6**). It bases this position on three arguments: (i) the Federal Council did not announce any change in standing practice in its message (BBl 1997 I 146 f.); (ii) a broadening of standing would have required an express statutory provision; (iii) grounds of practical coherence require a comprehensive reform of the system of federal judicial administration. This tension between academic opinion and Federal Supreme Court practice persists and has not been fully resolved by the introduction of the subsidiary constitutional complaint (Art. 113 ff. BGG).

#### 5.2 Arbitrariness in the Result or in the Reasoning

**N. 20** The scope of the so-called result criterion is contested. The Federal Supreme Court will only quash a decision if not merely the reasoning, but also the result is untenable. Rhinow/Schefer/Uebersax, op. cit., N 2823 criticise this standard as too narrow: even untenable reasoning that legally supports the result may violate the prohibition of arbitrariness, if the reasoning itself is not legally sustainable. The Federal Supreme Court has not adopted this criticism to date and continues to adhere to the result criterion (Judgment 5D_165/2019 of 12 November 2019 E. 2.1).

#### 5.3 Relationship between Arbitrariness and Legislative Freedom

**N. 21** In the area of cantonal legislation, heightened respect for the legislator's freedom to shape the law applies. Häfelin/Haller/Keller/Thurnherr, op. cit., N 816, distinguish between a narrower review of arbitrariness in the application of the law and a broader standard of review in law-making: the legislator acts arbitrarily only when the rule cannot be supported by any serious objective reasons and is purposeless or pointless. By contrast, Müller/Schefer, op. cit., p. 12 f., point out that the legislator too is bound by Art. 9 FC in many situations, and that overly broad discretion removes cases from the scope of arbitrariness review.

#### 5.4 Protection of Legitimate Expectations in Tax Law

**N. 22** In tax law, the Federal Supreme Court introduced a special rule in **BGE 150 I 1 E. 4.1–4.3**: the protection of legitimate expectations in an official representation does not, in tax law — unlike in other areas of law — require an additional balancing of interests. This departure from the general five-condition model has been criticised in some academic writing as contrary to the system, since on the one hand it facilitates protection of legitimate expectations in tax law, while on the other it restricts the particular importance of the principle of legality (→ Art. 127 para. 1 FC).

### 6. Practical Notes

**N. 23** **Complaints of arbitrariness** before the Federal Supreme Court must be substantively reasoned (qualified duty to state grounds, Art. 106 para. 2 BGG). The appeal must: (i) identify the provision that is allegedly applied in an arbitrary manner; (ii) set out why the application of the law or the establishment of facts is manifestly untenable; (iii) demonstrate that the result — not merely the reasoning — is untenable. An appellant who merely presents their own differing solution as preferable does not meet the requirements for stating grounds.

**N. 24** For a successful **complaint of violation of legitimate expectations**, all five conditions (→ N. 12) must be substantively demonstrated. Particular weight is given to the proof of detrimental reliance: the person concerned must specifically demonstrate what measures they took in reliance on the basis for trust and why those measures can no longer be undone (**BGE 137 I 69 E. 2.5.3**). Oral representations by authorities are more difficult to prove; documentation (file note, written confirmation) is strongly recommended.

**N. 25** In connection with **standing** for the subsidiary constitutional complaint (Art. 113 ff. BGG), it should be noted: the mere assertion of a violation of arbitrariness does not in itself confer standing under Art. 115 lit. b BGG. The appellant must first identify a statutory provision that confers a subjective right or protects their interests, and then demonstrate that this provision was applied in an arbitrary manner. Where no such provision exists, a specific fundamental right — but not Art. 9 FC alone — may exceptionally confer standing (**BGE 136 I 229 E. 3.2–3.3**).

**N. 26** In **administrative practice**, the principle of good faith obliges authorities in particular to: (i) act consistently over time; (ii) provide accurate and complete information in response to enquiries from citizens; (iii) draw attention to identified errors in prior official decisions before imposing a disadvantageous legal consequence (**BGE 137 I 69 E. 2.5.2**). In procedural terms, Art. 9 FC also protects against decisions that take parties by surprise and against the use of the authority's own procedural errors to the detriment of the parties (**BGE 133 I 201 E. 4**).

**N. 27** The scope of protection of Art. 9 FC extends — since the wording reads «every person» — to natural and legal persons under private law as well as to public entities acting as holders of private rights. Foreign nationals without a special residence status are in principle protected, even if their standing to raise complaints of arbitrariness may be restricted owing to the absence of a legal entitlement to a permit (**BGE 126 I 81 E. 3**).
