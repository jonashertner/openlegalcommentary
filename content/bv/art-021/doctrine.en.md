## Doctrine

### 1. Legislative History

**N. 1** Freedom of art was first enshrined as an independent fundamental right in the Federal Constitution of 1999. Under the old Federal Constitution of 1874, freedom of art was not expressly mentioned but was recognized as an unwritten fundamental right derived from other civil liberties, particularly freedom of expression (Müller/Schefer, Grundrechte in der Schweiz, 4th ed. 2008, p. 401).

**N. 2** The Federal Council message on the new Federal Constitution of 20 November 1996 (BBl 1997 I 1, p. 177) stated that freedom of art "belongs to the most important and characteristic civil liberties of a free and democratic state." The constitutional legislator wanted to emphasize the special significance of artistic freedom of expression for a pluralistic society through its explicit inclusion in Art. 21 FC.

### 2. Systematic Classification

**N. 3** Art. 21 FC is located in the section on fundamental rights (Arts. 7–36 FC) and belongs to the communication fundamental rights. Freedom of art has close connections to other civil liberties:
- → Art. 16 FC (freedom of opinion and information): common root of communication freedoms
- → Art. 20 FC (freedom of science): related protected areas in scientific-artistic works
- ↔ Art. 22 FC (freedom of assembly): overlaps in performances and artistic demonstrations

**N. 4** As a fundamental right guaranteed without reservation, freedom of art is nevertheless subject to the general limitations according to → Art. 36 FC. Any restriction requires a legal basis, must serve the public interest or the protection of third parties' fundamental rights, and must be proportionate (Rhinow/Schefer/Uebersax, Schweizerisches Verfassungsrecht, 3rd ed. 2016, N 2147).

### 3. Elements of the Offense / Normative Content

**N. 5** The **concept of art** must be interpreted broadly under constitutional law. The Federal Supreme Court refrains from a conclusive definition and follows an open, formal concept of art (Ehrenzeller/Schindler/Schweizer/Vallender, St. Galler Kommentar BV, 4th ed. 2023, Art. 21 N 8). All forms of artistic creation and artistic communication are protected, regardless of their aesthetic quality or social acceptance.

**N. 6** The **personal scope of protection** includes:
- Artists (creators)
- Art mediators (galleries, theaters, publishers)
- Art consumers (public)
- Legal entities insofar as they participate in the artistic process

**N. 7** The **material scope of protection** covers three dimensions (Waldmann/Belser/Epiney, BSK BV, 2nd ed. 2024, Art. 21 N 12):
1. **Creative sphere**: The artistic creation itself
2. **Impact sphere**: The dissemination and presentation of art
3. **Reception sphere**: Access to artistic works

### 4. Legal Consequences

**N. 8** Freedom of art primarily establishes a **defensive right** against state interference. The state may neither intervene in a censorious manner in the artistic process nor discriminate against certain art forms (**BGE 127 I 145 E. 4b**). Prior censorship is fundamentally inadmissible.

**N. 9** There is generally no **entitlement to benefits** for state art funding. However, freedom of art obliges the state to neutral and non-discriminatory funding practices when it decides to support art (Häfelin/Haller/Keller/Thurnherr, Schweizerisches Bundesstaatsrecht, 10th ed. 2020, N 473).

**N. 10** In case of **collisions with other fundamental rights**, a balancing of interests must be undertaken. Conflicts are particularly frequent with:
- Personality rights (→ Art. 28 CC): **BGE 135 III 145**
- Youth protection: **BGE 131 IV 64**
- Religious freedom and religious feelings

### 5. Contentious Issues

**N. 11** **Concept of art**: While Müller/Schefer (Grundrechte, p. 403) advocate for a broad, evolutionary concept of art that follows social developments, Kley (in: Ehrenzeller et al., St. Galler Kommentar, Art. 21 N 9) represents a more formal approach that focuses on the creator's intention.

**N. 12** **Satire privilege**: The scope of special protection for satirical art forms is disputed. Steinauer (Le droit au respect de la vie privée, 1990, N 445) favors enhanced protection for satire as a socially critical art form. Riemer (Personenrecht, 3rd ed. 2000, N 276) urges restraint and emphasizes limits in cases of personality violations.

**N. 13** **Pornography and art**: In distinguishing between punishable pornography and protected art, Stratenwerth/Wohlers (Schweizerisches Strafrecht BT I, 5th ed. 2019, § 8 N 21) take a restrictive line: artistic value must clearly outweigh pornographic depiction. Trechsel/Lieber (in: Trechsel/Pieth, StGB Praxiskommentar, 4th ed. 2021, Art. 197 N 8) advocate for a more generous assessment in favor of freedom of art.

### 6. Practice Notes

**N. 14** When **evaluating works of art**, the following must be observed:
- No substantive evaluation by state organs
- Consideration of the overall context of a work
- Attention to art-specific forms of expression (alienation, exaggeration, provocation)

**N. 15** **Procedurally**, the following applies:
- Special restraint is required for interim measures against artworks
- The proportionality test must consider chilling effects on freedom of art
- Expert opinions may be sought for classification as artwork

**N. 16** For **artists**, the following is recommended:
- For personality depictions: sufficient alienation or fictionalization
- For socially critical art: clear labeling as satire or fiction
- Documentation of artistic intention for evidence preservation
