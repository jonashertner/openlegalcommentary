# Art. 29a BV

## Doctrine

### 1. Legislative History

**N. 1** The guarantee of the right to recourse was incorporated into the Federal Constitution as part of the judicial reform and entered into force on 1 January 2007. The Dispatch on the Complete Revision of Federal Judicial Organisation of 28 February 2001 emphasised the necessity of comprehensive constitutional anchoring of access to a court (BBl 2001 4202, 4391 ff.). The constitutional legislator wanted to elevate the guarantees already enshrined in the ECHR (Art. 6 para. 1) and the UN Covenant II (Art. 14) to constitutional level and systematically ensure legal protection against state acts.

**N. 2** The introduction of Art. 29a BV took place simultaneously with the creation of unified federal administrative jurisdiction and the new Federal Supreme Court Act. This coordinated reform was intended to close gaps in legal protection and ensure coherent legal protection at all state levels (BBl 2001 4202, 4395). The possibility of statutory exceptions in para. 2 was deliberately provided to take account of federalist particularities and existing traditions.

### 2. Systematic Classification

**N. 3** Art. 29a BV stands systematically between the general procedural guarantees (Art. 29 BV) and the special procedural guarantees in court proceedings (Art. 30 BV). This classification underlines the bridging function of the guarantee of the right to recourse: it ensures access to the court (→ Art. 29 BV) and thereby enables the application of specific judicial procedural guarantees (→ Art. 30 BV).

**N. 4** The guarantee of the right to recourse is closely connected with the principle of the rule of law (→ Art. 5 BV) and the prohibition of arbitrariness (→ Art. 9 BV). It concretises the rule-of-law postulate of judicial control of state action. At the same time, it supplements the system of restrictions on fundamental rights (→ Art. 36 BV) by ensuring their procedural enforcement. In the context of judicial independence (→ Art. 191c BV), Art. 29a BV guarantees the institutional prerequisite for effective legal protection.

### 3. Elements of the Offence / Normative Content

**N. 5** The concept of **«legal dispute»** is to be interpreted broadly. According to the case law of the Federal Supreme Court, it encompasses all disputes about rights and obligations, regardless of whether these are of a public-law or private-law nature (**BGE 135 I 6** E. 2.1). Kradolfer (OnlineKommentar, Art. 29a BV N. 8) emphasises that disputes about the existence or non-existence of legal relationships are also covered. However, purely political questions or discretionary decisions without legal entitlement are not covered.

**N. 6** The **«judicial authority»** must exhibit the characteristics of a court in the material sense. According to **BGE 136 I 229** E. 5.4, these are: institutional independence, full cognition in factual and legal terms, binding decision-making competence and procedural guarantees according to Art. 29 ff. BV. Waldmann (BSK BV, Art. 29a N. 18) specifies that specialised administrative courts can also fulfil these requirements.

**N. 7** The **«right to adjudication»** guarantees full review of legal and factual questions. The court must be empowered to review the contested act in all relevant points (**BGE 134 I 199** E. 2.2). Mere legal control or limited cognition does not satisfy the requirements of Art. 29a BV, as Kiener/Kälin/Wyttenbach (§ 42 N. 7) emphasise.

### 4. Legal Consequences

**N. 8** The guarantee of the right to recourse obligates the Confederation and cantons to provide a judicial remedy for all legal disputes. If such a remedy is lacking, there is formal denial of justice, as the Federal Supreme Court held in **BGE 148 I 104** E. 4.2. The cantons must organise their court organisation such that at least one judicial instance is available (**BGE 135 I 265** E. 2.1).

**N. 9** In case of violation of the guarantee of the right to recourse, the Federal Supreme Court can refer the matter back to the competent judicial authority for reconsideration. If no such authority exists, it can only establish the violation of rights without being able to remedy it (→ Art. 190 BV). This was exemplified in **BGE 137 I 128**, where the Federal Supreme Court identified a protection gap in asylum law but could not act itself due to lack of competence.

### 5. Disputed Issues

**N. 10** The **scope of permissible exceptions** under para. 2 is disputed. Rhinow/Schefer/Uebersax (N. 2841) advocate a restrictive interpretation: only overriding public interests and the absence of individual interests worthy of protection would justify exceptions. In contrast, Kley (SGK BV, Art. 29a N. 12) argues for more flexibility with traditionally political decisions. The Federal Supreme Court follows a mediating line and recognises exceptions where the matter has a predominantly political character (**BGE 141 I 172** E. 4.3).

**N. 11** The **applicability to provisional measures** is controversially discussed. Grodecki (CR BV, Art. 29a N. 15) demands unrestricted application of the guarantee of the right to recourse even for provisional orders. Waldmann (BSK BV, Art. 29a N. 24) differentiates according to the scope of the measure: in case of significant interventions in fundamental rights positions, the guarantee of the right to recourse should be applicable without restriction. Federal Supreme Court practice tends towards the differentiating solution (**BGE 146 I 62** E. 3.2).

**N. 12** The **scope in social law** is also disputed. While Kälin (Rechtsweggarantie, p. 67) argues for comprehensive application in all benefit disputes, Biaggini (Art. 29a BV N. 9) represents a more restrictive position for mere discretionary decisions without legal entitlement. The Federal Supreme Court differentiates according to the nature of the disputed claim and denies applicability for pure discretionary decisions without individual legal position (**BGE 133 I 185** E. 6.2).

### 6. Practice Notes

**N. 13** In **organising cantonal legal remedy procedures**, the cantons must ensure that at least one instance has a court with full cognition deciding. This can also be a specialised administrative court, provided it fulfils the independence requirements. The mere designation as «appeals commission» does not suffice if the material prerequisites are lacking.

**N. 14** For **appellants**, it should be noted that the guarantee of the right to recourse does not guarantee a hierarchy of instances. It only ensures access to at least one court. Cantonal procedural autonomy remains preserved within the framework of federal legal requirements. In case of negative conflicts of competence between authorities, clarification of jurisdiction should be sought early to avoid formal denial of justice.

**N. 15** **Legislators** must observe proportionality when making exceptions under para. 2 and clearly justify the exception. The dispatch must explain why judicial adjudication is excluded and what alternative legal protection possibilities exist. Blanket exclusions of entire areas of law are impermissible. Every exception requires case-specific justification taking into account the affected individual interests.
