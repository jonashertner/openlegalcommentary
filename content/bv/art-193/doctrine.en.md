## Doctrine

### 1. Legislative History

**N. 1** Art. 193 BV has its roots in Art. 119 and 120 of the Federal Constitution of 1874. The current version was adopted without material changes as part of the constitutional reform of 1999 (BBl 1997 I 422). The Federal Council's message emphasised that total revision is conceived as the «ultima ratio» of constitutional amendment and therefore features high procedural hurdles (BBl 1997 I 431).

**N. 2** The restriction of peremptory provisions of international law (para. 4) was only inserted in 1999. The constituent power wanted to clarify that even sovereign constituent power is bound by ius cogens (BBl 1997 I 432). This addition was made in parallel to the introduction of the same restriction for partial revision (Art. 139 para. 3 and Art. 194 para. 2 BV).

### 2. Systematic Classification

**N. 3** Art. 193 BV forms, together with Art. 192 (principle) and Art. 194 (partial revision), the constitutional revision law in the narrow sense. The provision stands in Title 6 «Revision of the Federal Constitution and Transitional Provisions» and is systematically conceived as a counterpart to the more frequently used partial revision.

**N. 4** The provision has close connections to the following provisions:
- → Art. 138 BV (popular initiative for total revision)
- → Art. 139 BV (popular initiative for partial revision)
- → Art. 140 para. 1 lit. a BV (mandatory referendum)
- → Art. 163 para. 1 BV (form of Federal Assembly enactments)
- → Art. 193 para. 4 BV refers to Art. 139 para. 3 and Art. 194 para. 2 BV (ius cogens restriction)

### 3. Elements of the Provision / Normative Content

**N. 5** **Right of initiative (para. 1):** Total revision can be initiated by three actors:
- By the people through popular initiative according to Art. 138 BV (100,000 eligible voters)
- By one of the two chambers through simple federal decree
- By the Federal Assembly through concordant resolution of both chambers

Ehrenzeller/Schindler/Schweizer/Vallender (St. Galler Kommentar BV, 4th ed. 2023, Art. 193 N. 3) emphasise that the initiative of one chamber represents a parliamentary peculiarity, as normally both chambers must concur.

**N. 6** **Referendum in principle (para. 2):** In case of popular initiative or disagreement between the chambers, the people must decide on the implementation. This vote concerns only the «whether» of total revision, not its content (Häfelin/Haller/Keller/Thurnherr, Schweizerisches Bundesstaatsrecht, 10th ed. 2020, N. 1856).

**N. 7** **New election of chambers (para. 3):** The constituent power is renewed through new election of both chambers. Rhinow/Schefer/Uebersax (Schweizerisches Verfassungsrecht, 3rd ed. 2016, N. 3586) see in this the democratic legitimation for comprehensive constitutional amendment.

**N. 8** **Ius cogens restriction (para. 4):** Peremptory provisions include in particular:
- Prohibition of torture
- Prohibition of genocide
- Prohibition of slavery
- Principle of non-refoulement
- Core content of non-derogable guarantees

### 4. Legal Consequences

**N. 9** Acceptance of the referendum in principle by the people triggers the following mandatory legal consequences:
1. Immediate dissolution of both chambers and new election
2. The newly elected chambers function as constituent assembly
3. The constitutional draft is subject to mandatory referendum (people and cantons)

**N. 10** Total revision replaces the entire Federal Constitution. Unlike partial revision, there is no right to retain individual provisions (Tschannen, in: Waldmann/Belser/Epiney, BSK BV, 2nd ed. 2024, Art. 193 N. 12).

### 5. Controversies

**N. 11** **Scope of ius cogens restriction:** Müller/Schefer (Grundrechte in der Schweiz, 4th ed. 2008, p. 1124) advocate a narrow interpretation, limited to the norms mentioned in Art. 53 of the Vienna Convention. Kälin/Künzli (Universeller Menschenrechtsschutz, 4th ed. 2019, N. 175) argue for a dynamic interpretation including new ius cogens norms.

**N. 12** **Binding of constituent power:** Malinverni/Hottelier/Hertig Randall/Flückiger (Droit constitutionnel suisse, 4th ed. 2021, N. 1847) see the newly elected chambers as completely free in designing the new constitution. Ehrenzeller (St. Galler Kommentar BV, Art. 193 N. 15) conversely argues that certain structural principles (federalism, democracy) are factually unalterable.

**N. 13** **Procedure according to para. 1, 3rd alternative:** It is unclear whether a resolution of the Federal Assembly leads directly to new election or also requires a popular vote. Aubert/Mahon (Petit commentaire de la Constitution, 2003, Art. 193 N. 8) favour direct new election, while Hangartner/Kley (Die demokratischen Rechte in Bund und Kantonen, 2000, N. 2682) demand analogous application of para. 2.

### 6. Practical Notes

**N. 14** A total revision has never been carried out since 1874. The constitutional reform of 1999 was deliberately conceived as partial revision to avoid the cumbersome procedure (BBl 1997 I 16).

**N. 15** Popular initiatives for total revision are subject to the same grounds for invalidity as partial revisions. The Federal Assembly examines unity of form, unity of subject matter, feasibility and compatibility with ius cogens (→ Art. 139 para. 3 BV).

**N. 16** The practical significance lies primarily in the distinction from partial revision. As a rule of thumb: amendments affecting more than half of the constitutional articles or changing the basic structure of the constitution require the total revision procedure (Tschannen, BSK BV, Art. 193 N. 18).
