# Art. 186 FC — Doctrine

## 1. Legislative History

**N. 1** Art. 186 FC was created as part of the total revision of the Federal Constitution of 1999. The provision consolidates the tasks of the Federal Council in the federal system, which were previously scattered across various articles of the old Federal Constitution of 1874 (Federal Gazette 1997 I 1, 447). The constitutional commission emphasised that the cultivation of relationships with the cantons (para. 1) and federal supervision (para. 4) should be explicitly enshrined in the new constitution as central tasks of the Federal Council.

**N. 2** The requirement for approval of cantonal enactments (para. 2) corresponds to Art. 85 no. 5 old FC, but was formulated more precisely: approval is only required «where the implementation of federal law so requires». The right to object to inter-cantonal agreements (para. 3) adopted Art. 85 no. 5 and Art. 7 para. 2 old FC. Federal supervision (para. 4) derives from Art. 102 no. 2 old FC (Federal Gazette 1997 I 447f.).

## 2. Systematic Classification

**N. 3** Art. 186 FC is located in Title 3 (Confederation, Cantons and Communes), Chapter 2 (Powers), Section 2 (Federal Council) of the Federal Constitution. The provision specifies the general tasks of the Federal Council as the supreme executive authority described in Art. 182 FC. It must be read in the context of cooperative federalism, which characterises the relations between the Confederation and the cantons (→ Art. 44 FC).

**N. 4** The provision is closely linked to Art. 49 FC (primacy and compliance with federal law), which forms the substantive basis for federal supervision. While Art. 49 para. 2 FC generally obligates the Confederation to exercise supervision, Art. 186 para. 4 FC specifically assigns this task to the Federal Council. Other important cross-references: → Art. 172 FC (relations between the Confederation and cantons as a task of the Federal Assembly), → Art. 5 FC (rule of law principle).

## 3. Elements of the Offence / Content of the Norm

### 3.1 Cultivation of Relations and Cooperation (para. 1)

**N. 5** The Federal Council is obligated to actively cultivate relations with the cantons. This includes regular consultations, dialogue in conferences of cantonal governments, and informal contacts. The duty to cooperate gives concrete form to the principle of federal loyalty enshrined in Art. 44 FC (Häfelin/Haller/Keller/Thurnherr, Federal Constitutional Law, N 1124).

**N. 6** The provision does not establish enforceable rights of the cantons, but rather stipulates a behavioural obligation of the Federal Council. It requires partnership-based cooperation within the framework of implementation federalism, where the cantons implement federal law (Biaggini, FC Commentary, Art. 186 N 3).

### 3.2 Approval of Cantonal Enactments (para. 2)

**N. 7** The requirement for approval exists only «where the implementation of federal law so requires». This must be interpreted restrictively: approval is only required if federal law expressly provides for this or if uniform implementation of federal law would be jeopardised without approval (Ehrenzeller/Schindler/Schweizer/Vallender, St. Gallen Commentary, Art. 186 N 11).

**N. 8** Examples of cantonal enactments requiring approval can be found in tax law (Art. 129 para. 2 FC), healthcare, or spatial planning. Approval is a supervisory instrument of a preventive nature that takes effect before the cantonal enactment enters into force (Tschannen/Zimmerli/Müller, Administrative Law, § 57 N 23).

### 3.3 Objection to Treaties (para. 3)

**N. 9** The right of objection covers both inter-cantonal concordats and agreements between cantons and foreign countries. It is a preventive supervisory instrument that must be exercised before conclusion or ratification of the treaty. An objection must be raised if the treaty violates federal law or endangers interests protected by federal law (Waldmann/Belser/Epiney, BSK FC, Art. 186 N 18).

**N. 10** In practice, this instrument is rarely used. The Federal Council exercises restraint in order to respect cantonal autonomy. For cross-border treaties, it examines in particular compatibility with the Confederation's foreign policy (→ Art. 54 FC).

### 3.4 Federal Supervision (para. 4)

**N. 11** Federal supervision is not merely a power but an obligation of the Federal Council (**JAAC 69.1**). It serves to ensure uniform and correct application of federal law and extends to both the autonomous and delegated spheres of competence of the cantons (Rhinow/Schefer/Uebersax, Constitutional Law, N 3682).

**N. 12** The Federal Council has a broad spectrum of supervisory instruments that have developed in practice: information, recommendation, warning, instruction, substitute performance, and as a last resort federal intervention under Art. 52 FC. These instruments generally do not require a special legal basis but are based directly on Art. 186 para. 4 FC (**JAAC 69.1**).

## 4. Legal Consequences

**N. 13** Violation of the duty to cooperate (para. 1) may have political but not legal consequences. Judicial enforcement is not possible as this is a programmatic norm.

**N. 14** Refusal of approval (para. 2) means that the cantonal enactment cannot enter into force. The Federal Council's decision is subject to the possibility of appeal to the Federal Supreme Court under Art. 82 lit. b FSCA.

**N. 15** A successful objection (para. 3) prevents the contested treaty from entering into force. The cantons may challenge the objection before the Federal Supreme Court (Art. 82 lit. b FSCA).

**N. 16** Within the framework of federal supervision (para. 4), the Federal Council may issue binding instructions and take substitute measures in case of non-compliance. Federal intervention is available as a last resort. However, supervisory measures must always be proportionate (**BGE 148 II 369**).

## 5. Disputed Issues

**N. 17** The scope of federal supervision over cantonal courts is disputed. The prevailing doctrine and case law take the view that federal supervision cannot generally be used to overturn cantonal court judgments, as this would violate the separation of powers (**Decision 2C_681/2023**). However, Tschannen/Zimmerli/Müller (Administrative Law, § 57 N 31) emphasise that in extreme cases of systematic denial of justice, judicial decisions could also be subject to supervision.

**N. 18** The question of whether supervisory instruments require a legal basis is also controversially discussed. While Häfelin/Müller/Uhlmann (Administrative Law, N 1489) consider direct reliance on Art. 186 para. 4 FC sufficient, other authors such as Moor/Poltier (Administrative Law, Volume II, p. 234) call for a special legal basis for intrusive measures.

## 6. Practical Notes

**N. 19** Cantons should coordinate planned enactments with the competent federal authorities at an early stage in order to avoid subsequent refusal of approval. The consultation procedure provides a suitable framework for this.

**N. 20** For inter-cantonal agreements, prior informal consultation with the Federal Council is recommended, particularly when foreign policy aspects are involved. This applies especially to cross-border cooperation.

**N. 21** Against supervisory measures by the Federal Council, the cantons have recourse to the Federal Supreme Court (Art. 82 lit. b FSCA). However, the appeal has no suspensive effect unless the Federal Council expressly waives this.
