# Art. 31 FC — Deprivation of Liberty

## Doctrine

### 1. Legislative History

**N. 1** The Federal Constitution of 1874 contained no express guarantee of the right concerning deprivation of liberty; corresponding guarantees fell within cantonal competence. The right to personal liberty was recognised by the Federal Supreme Court from the 1960s onward as unwritten constitutional law (BGE 90 I 29; cf. Häfelin/Haller/Keller/Thurnherr, Schweizerisches Bundesstaatsrecht, 10th ed. 2020, N 371). With the total revision of the Federal Constitution, which entered into force on 1 January 2000, Art. 31 FC was codified as a standalone provision, thereby expressly transferring the unwritten judge-made law into the constitutional text.

**N. 2** The dispatch on the new Federal Constitution of 20 November 1996 (BBl 1997 I 1, 190 ff.) records that Art. 31 FC consolidates the existing constitutional and international law guarantees and develops them further at the statutory level. Para. 1 is directly modelled on Art. 5 para. 1 ECHR; paras. 2–4 draw on various preliminary drafts for the total revision as well as on Art. 9 UN Covenant II (ratified by Switzerland in 1992). The wording of the provision has remained unchanged since its entry into force.

**N. 3** With the creation of the federal Swiss Code of Criminal Procedure (CrimPC, in force since 1 January 2011), the law on detention was unified and Art. 31 FC was given statutory expression (Arts. 217–233 CrimPC). The dispatch on the CrimPC (BBl 2006 1057, 1130) emphasises that detention matters must be treated as a priority in view of the requirement of expedition under Art. 31 para. 3 FC and Art. 5 No. 3 ECHR. This principle was confirmed and further developed by the Federal Supreme Court in **BGE 133 I 270 E. 1.2.2**, holding that the statutory suspension of time limits does not apply in cases of pre-trial detention under criminal procedure law.

---

### 2. Systematic Classification

**N. 4** Art. 31 FC is situated in the Second Chapter of the Federal Constitution (Fundamental Rights, Arts. 7–36 FC) and, together with Art. 10 para. 2 FC (personal liberty) and Art. 36 FC (restriction of fundamental rights), forms a regulatory cluster. Whereas Art. 10 para. 2 FC protects personal liberty in its comprehensive sense — including physical integrity and freedom of movement — Art. 31 FC specifically governs the most intensive form of restriction of liberty: deprivation of liberty. Art. 31 FC is to be read as a concretisation and complement to Art. 10 para. 2 FC; → Art. 10 para. 2 FC, ↔ Art. 36 FC.

**N. 5** The provision fulfils a dual function: para. 1 establishes the substantive requirements for any deprivation of liberty (principle of legality), while paras. 2–4 guarantee specific procedural rights to persons in detention. Para. 3 occupies a special position, as it relates exclusively to pre-trial detention; the remaining paragraphs apply to all forms of deprivation of liberty. Closely connected in systematic terms are → Art. 29 FC (general procedural guarantees), → Art. 29a FC (guarantee of access to the courts), → Art. 30 FC (judicial guarantees), and → Art. 32 FC (criminal procedure guarantees), as well as → Art. 5 ECHR and → Art. 9 UN Covenant II.

**N. 6** Art. 31 FC is a directly applicable fundamental right with the character of a defensive right. It is directed primarily against state deprivations of liberty — irrespective of whether they are criminal, administrative, or civil in nature. The fundamental right applies to all natural persons regardless of nationality, age, and residence status (Vest, in: Ehrenzeller/Schindler/Schweizer/Vallender [eds.], St. Galler Kommentar, 4th ed. 2023, Art. 31 N. 10; Müller/Schefer, Grundrechte in der Schweiz, 4th ed. 2008, pp. 37 f.).

### 3. Elements of the Provision / Substantive Content

#### 3.1 Concept of Deprivation of Liberty (Para. 1)

**N. 7** The concept of «deprivation of liberty» within the meaning of Art. 31 FC and Art. 5 ECHR is not confined to detention in the narrow sense, but equally does not encompass every restriction of liberty. The decisive criterion is the distinction based on the type, duration, extent, and intensity of the restriction; the overall effects of the measure are determinative. Typical examples are pre-trial detention, security detention, and extradition detention, as well as involuntary placement (**BGE 134 I 140 E. 3.2**). The detention of a person for several days in an airport transit zone may likewise constitute a deprivation of liberty (**BGE 123 II 193 E. 3c**).

**N. 8** Geographic exclusion orders and contact bans within the meaning of domestic violence protection measures do not, as a rule, fall under the concept of deprivation of liberty due to insufficient intensity (**BGE 134 I 140 E. 3.3**: «Diese Art der Freiheitsbeschränkung ist in ihrem Ausmass und in ihrer Intensität nicht vergleichbar mit einer Festhaltung an einem bestimmten Ort»). Such measures are governed by the guarantees of Art. 10 para. 2 and Art. 36 FC; → Art. 36 FC.

**N. 9** The principle of legality under para. 1 entails two requirements: first, an express statutory basis for the deprivation of liberty («in the cases provided for by law and in accordance with the procedures prescribed by law»); and second, compliance with the procedure prescribed by law. The ECtHR requires a particular normative density for the statutory basis: the law must be accessible, foreseeable, and precise (Ajil/Mühlemann, Kommentierung zu Art. 31 BV, in: Schlegel/Ammann [eds.], Onlinekommentar zur Bundesverfassung, N. 30). The permissible grounds for detention are exhaustively enumerated in Art. 5 para. 1 lit. a–f ECHR; para. 1 FC must be interpreted in conformity with international law.

**N. 10** The principle of proportionality (→ Art. 36 para. 3 FC) requires that the deprivation of liberty be suitable, necessary, and reasonable. In the field of criminal procedure, this means: pre-trial detention may only be ordered as a measure of last resort; less restrictive substitution measures (Art. 237 CrimPC) must be examined as a matter of priority (**BGE 133 I 270 E. 3.3**). A violation of the principle of proportionality arises in particular where the duration of detention approaches the probable length of the sentence to be imposed (**BGE 133 I 168 E. 4.1**; **BGE 132 I 21 E. 4.1**).

#### 3.2 Rights to Information and Notification (Para. 2)

**N. 11** Para. 2 contains three interrelated rights: the right to be informed promptly in a language that the person understands of the grounds for detention and of their rights; the right to actually assert those rights; and the right to have their next of kin notified. The right to information comes into effect at the moment the deprivation of liberty becomes operative — already before any initial questioning (Ajil/Mühlemann, loc. cit., N. 56 f.).

**N. 12** «Promptly» does not mean compliance with a rigid time limit, but timely notification so that the person is able to assert their rights effectively. The information must be communicated in a language that the person concerned understands; translation must be provided where necessary. It must be formulated in generally comprehensible terms — not in technical legal language. In criminal proceedings, Art. 219 CrimPC is the statutory concretisation of this right (Vest, SGK, Art. 31 N. 30 ff.).

**N. 13** The right to have next of kin notified is not limited to the immediate family circle, but encompasses any person with whom the detained individual maintains a personal, professional, or other relationship. Art. 214 CrimPC implements this right at the statutory level and provides, in particular circumstances, for a deferral of notification to the extent that this is indispensable for the effectiveness of the investigation (Ajil/Mühlemann, loc. cit., N. 63).

#### 3.3 Pre-Trial Detention and Judicial Hearing (Para. 3)

**N. 14** Para. 3 sentence 1 guarantees the person taken into pre-trial detention the right to be brought before a judge without delay. «Without delay» corresponds to Art. 5 No. 3 ECHR; according to ECtHR case law, a period of 48 hours may not in principle be exceeded. In the CrimPC, this is implemented through the 48-hour time limit for the prosecuting authority's application for detention and the judicial detention decision (Art. 224 para. 2, Art. 226 para. 1 CrimPC).

**N. 15** The judge decides whether the person is to remain in detention or to be released. This decision-making authority lies with the compulsory measures courts (CMC; Art. 18 CrimPC), which were established as an «indispensable counterbalance to the powers of the police and the prosecution» (BBl 2006 1057, 1130). The judge is obliged to fully examine all aspects of the detention, its conditions, and proportionality; a blanket refusal of such examination violates Art. 29 para. 2 FC (**BGE 133 I 270 E. 3.2.2**).

**N. 16** The right to judgment within a reasonable time (para. 3 sentence 2) codifies the requirement of expedition in pre-trial detention (→ Art. 29 para. 1 FC). There is no statutory maximum duration; reasonableness is assessed according to the circumstances of the individual case, in particular the complexity of the case and the gravity of the expected sentence (Art. 212 para. 3 CrimPC; **BGE 133 I 270 E. 3.4.2**; **BGE 132 I 21 E. 4.1**). The Federal Supreme Court holds that detention must not approach the probable length of the sentence to be imposed; in cases of anticipatory enforcement of a sentence, the resocialisation principles under Arts. 74 f. SCC must also be taken into account (**BGE 133 I 270 E. 3.2.2**).

**N. 17** The grounds for pre-trial detention — risk of collusion, risk of flight, risk of offending, and risk of reoffending — are governed at the statutory level by Art. 221 CrimPC. Specific indications are required for each ground for detention; a merely theoretical possibility does not suffice (**BGE 132 I 21 E. 3.2**). After completion of the criminal investigation and the filing of charges, the ground of risk of collusion in particular requires especially careful scrutiny (**BGE 132 I 21 E. 3.2.2**). The ground of risk of offending presupposes the threat of a «serious offence»; misdemeanours do not suffice (**BGE 137 IV 122 E. 5.2–5.3**).

#### 3.4 Habeas Corpus (Para. 4)

**N. 18** Para. 4 enshrines the right of habeas corpus: any person deprived of their liberty by a non-judicial authority may at any time apply to a court, which shall decide on the lawfulness of the deprivation of liberty as quickly as possible. Unlike the specific review under para. 3 (automatic in pre-trial detention), this right is of general application and must be actively invoked (**BGE 142 I 135 E. 3.1–3.2**).

**N. 19** «As quickly as possible» means, according to Federal Supreme Court case law and the ECtHR, that detention review proceedings must always be treated as a matter of priority («dans un minimum de temps»); the specific time limit depends on the complexity of the case and the conduct of the parties (**BGE 142 I 135 E. 3.1** with reference to ECtHR judgments Mooren v. Germany and M.B. v. Switzerland). In Dublin detention cases, Art. 31 para. 4 FC requires an initial judicial review within a period analogous to the general 96-hour time limit under Art. 80 para. 2 FNIA; the eight-day time limit for applications for release (Art. 80a para. 4 FNIA) does not apply to the initial detention review (**BGE 142 I 135 E. 3.3**).

**N. 20** The reviewing court examines the lawfulness of the deprivation of liberty comprehensively — both on the merits and in procedural terms — and must ensure adversarial proceedings with equality of arms (Ajil/Mühlemann, loc. cit., N. 74). It must be possible to apply for renewed review at reasonable intervals; a one-month bar period (Art. 228 para. 5 CrimPC) is permissible, whereas a two-month period is disproportionate (Müller/Schefer, Grundrechte in der Schweiz, 4th ed. 2008, pp. 88 ff.).

### 4. Legal Consequences

**N. 21** A deprivation of liberty ordered without a statutory basis or in violation of the procedure prescribed by law violates Art. 31 para. 1 FC and is unlawful; the person concerned must be released without delay. Violation of para. 2 (right to information) regularly leads, in criminal proceedings, to the inadmissibility of evidence (Art. 158 para. 2 in conjunction with Art. 141 para. 1 CrimPC).

**N. 22** Violations of the requirement of expedition (para. 3 sentence 2) may in principle justify release from detention where a serious violation has occurred and the authorities are unable or unwilling to advance the proceedings. In less serious cases, the violation must be taken into account in sentencing (**BGE 133 I 270 E. 3.4.2**).

**N. 23** Where the court seised under para. 4 concludes that the deprivation of liberty is unlawful, it shall order the release of the detained person. If the person has been released in the meantime, the Federal Supreme Court may nonetheless adjudicate the appeal on the merits where there is a particular interest — notably in respect of ECHR rights or a matter of fundamental importance (**BGE 136 I 274 E. 1.3**; **BGE 142 I 135 E. 1.3.1**). Art. 5 para. 5 ECHR provides for a right to compensation in the event of a deprivation of liberty in breach of the Convention (Arts. 429, 431 CrimPC).

### 5. Contested Issues

**N. 24** **Distinction between deprivation of liberty and restriction of liberty**: According to the prevailing doctrine and practice, the difference is one of degree, not of kind: the criterion is the intensity, duration, and spatial extent of the restriction (Vest, SGK, Art. 31 N. 15 ff.; Ajil/Mühlemann, loc. cit., N. 22 f.). The classification of house arrest and electronic monitoring is disputed: part of the doctrine qualifies house arrest as a deprivation of liberty where it reaches a sufficient degree of intensity; ECtHR practice requires an overall assessment of the concrete situation.

**N. 25** **Maximum duration of pre-trial detention**: It is disputed whether the absence of a statutory maximum duration is compatible with the constitutional guarantees. Ajil/Mühlemann (loc. cit., N. 69 ff.) criticise the fact that the Federal Supreme Court permits detention of up to three-quarters of the expected sentence — a threshold that already exceeds the two-thirds threshold applicable for conditional release. By contrast, Vest (SGK, Art. 31 N. 52) takes the view that case-by-case proportionality review provides adequate protection. The Federal Supreme Court maintains its case-by-case approach and shows no inclination towards rigid time limits (**BGE 133 I 168**; **BGE 132 I 21 E. 4.1**).

**N. 26** **Effectiveness of the compulsory measures courts**: Ajil/Mühlemann (loc. cit., N. 67 f.) point to an empirical deficit: CMCs rarely reject prosecutorial applications for detention in practice, thereby effectively weakening the constitutionally required counterbalance. Häfelin/Haller/Keller/Thurnherr (loc. cit., N 395) welcome the creation of the CMCs as a structural achievement, but note that practical implementation remains inconsistent across cantons. The Federal Supreme Court recognises the CMCs' duty to give reasons as a minimum constitutional requirement (**BGE 133 I 270 E. 3.5.1**).

**N. 27** **Administrative detention in immigration law**: Whether administrative detention under aliens law fully satisfies the constitutional requirements of Art. 31 FC is disputed in legal scholarship. Ajil/Mühlemann (loc. cit., N. 45–49) criticise the fact that persons in administrative detention are structurally housed in facilities of a prison character, which is difficult to reconcile with the non-punitive nature of such detention. The Federal Supreme Court emphasises the requirements relating to the statutory grounds for detention: the mere fact that a person has lodged an asylum application in another Dublin State does not justify detention (**BGE 142 I 135 E. 4.1–4.2**).

**N. 28** **Relationship to Art. 5 ECHR**: It is broadly accepted that Art. 31 FC and Art. 5 ECHR are substantially congruent, with Art. 5 ECHR exhaustively enumerating the permissible grounds for detention through its case law (lit. a–f). Art. 31 FC contains no such exhaustive enumeration, but must be interpreted in conformity with the Convention. Müller/Schefer (loc. cit., pp. 36 ff.) emphasise that Art. 31 FC may go beyond the minimum standard of Art. 5 ECHR in certain respects — notably with regard to the habeas corpus right under para. 4.

### 6. Practical Notes

**N. 29** Any restriction of liberty must first be characterised under Art. 31 para. 1 FC and Art. 5 ECHR: does it in fact constitute a deprivation of liberty (as opposed to a mere restriction of liberty)? In making this assessment, the type, duration, intensity, and modalities of the measure must be weighed cumulatively (**BGE 134 I 140 E. 3.2**).

**N. 30** Appeals against detention orders must be treated with particular urgency; the suspension of time limits under Art. 46 para. 1 BGG does not apply in cases of pre-trial detention under criminal procedure law (**BGE 133 I 270 E. 1.2.2**). The 30-day appeal period begins upon service of the complete decision (Art. 100 para. 1 BGG). In cases of administrative detention under aliens law, the suspension of time limits continues to apply in principle, except in Dublin detention cases, where the question remains open following **BGE 142 I 135 E. 1.2.3**.

**N. 31** The detention judge or CMC is obliged to conduct a comprehensive investigation of the facts and may not rely on rudimentary information supplied by the prosecution (**BGE 133 I 270 E. 3.5.1**). In particular, where detention has been prolonged, the court must examine proportionality on the basis of the complete investigation file and provide a comprehensible statement of reasons; a mere reference to earlier decisions does not suffice (**BGE 133 I 270 E. 3.4.3**; **BGE 132 I 21 E. 3.2.1**).

**N. 32** In the case of pre-trial detention on the ground of risk of collusion, a particularly strict standard must be applied after charges have been filed; abstract possibilities of collusion do not suffice (**BGE 132 I 21 E. 3.2.2**). Substitution measures (Art. 237 CrimPC) must always be examined before ordering or extending detention; geographic restriction is particularly appropriate in cases of risk of flight, while in cases of risk of collusion a prohibition on making contact often constitutes a less restrictive measure (**BGE 137 IV 122 E. 6.4**).

**N. 33** The right to prompt notification under para. 2 must be ensured by the authorities ex officio; the detained person need not actively insist upon it. In criminal proceedings, the notification must be given before any questioning takes place (Art. 158 para. 1 CrimPC). The information must be provided in generally comprehensible language and, where necessary, with the assistance of an interpreter.

**N. 34** For habeas corpus proceedings under para. 4, the requirement to decide «as quickly as possible» applies. The 96-hour time limit under Art. 80 para. 2 FNIA serves as a reference point (by analogy). An initial detention review conducted after almost two weeks violates para. 4 and Art. 5 No. 4 ECHR (**BGE 142 I 135 E. 3.4**). The person concerned retains a legitimate interest in a judicial declaration of a Convention violation even after subsequent release from detention (**BGE 136 I 274 E. 1.3**; **BGE 139 I 206 E. 1.2.1**).
