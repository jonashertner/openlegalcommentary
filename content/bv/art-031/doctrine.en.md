## Doctrine

### 1. Legislative History

**N. 1** Art. 31 Federal Constitution was newly introduced as part of the total revision of the Federal Constitution in 1999. The old Federal Constitution of 1874 contained no explicit guarantee for protection against deprivation of liberty. Constitutional protection was only derived from the unwritten fundamental right of personal liberty and from Art. 5 ECHR.

**N. 2** The Federal Council's Message on the new Federal Constitution emphasized the necessity of an independent constitutional provision: «The proposed provision concretizes the fundamental right of personal liberty in cases of deprivation of liberty and closely follows Article 5 ECHR as well as Article 9 of the UN Covenant II» (BBl 1997 I 1, 165). The constitutional legislator thereby sought to anchor the protection already guaranteed by the ECHR at the constitutional level and partially strengthen it.

### 2. Systematic Classification

**N. 3** Art. 31 Federal Constitution is systematically placed between the general guarantee of personal liberty (Art. 10 Federal Constitution) and the procedural fundamental rights (Art. 29-32 Federal Constitution). This position underscores its dual character: On one hand, it concretizes personal liberty for the specific case of deprivation of liberty (→ Art. 10 para. 2 Federal Constitution), on the other hand, it contains specific procedural guarantees that go beyond the general procedural rights.

**N. 4** The provision is closely related to other constitutional guarantees: The principle of legality (→ Art. 5 para. 1 Federal Constitution) is specified in para. 1 for deprivation of liberty. The procedural guarantees of paras. 2-4 supplement the general procedural fundamental rights (→ Art. 29 Federal Constitution). In cases of fundamental rights restrictions, Art. 36 Federal Constitution (Restriction of fundamental rights) must always be observed.

### 3. Elements of the Offence / Normative Content

#### 3.1 Concept of Deprivation of Liberty

**N. 5** The concept of deprivation of liberty encompasses not only classical detention, but any state measure that prevents a person from moving freely and determining their place of residence themselves. The Federal Supreme Court held in **BGE 134 I 140**: «The concept of ‹deprivation of liberty› within the meaning of Art. 5 ECHR and Art. 31 Federal Constitution is not to be understood merely as detention in the narrow sense. Conversely, not every type of restriction of liberty falls under this guarantee, but only restrictions of liberty of a certain extent and intensity.»

**N. 6** The distinction between deprivation of liberty and mere restriction of liberty is made according to objective criteria: decisive are the type, duration, effects and implementation modalities of the measure (Müller/Schefer, Grundrechte in der Schweiz, 4th ed. 2008, pp. 173 f.). The following are considered deprivation of liberty in particular:
- Criminal and investigative detention
- Security detention and early commencement of sentence
- Deportation and enforcement detention
- Welfare-related deprivation of liberty
- Administrative detention in military service

#### 3.2 Principle of Legality (Para. 1)

**N. 7** Art. 31 para. 1 Federal Constitution requires a formal legal basis for any deprivation of liberty. The requirements go beyond the general principle of legality: the deprivation of liberty must occur «in the cases provided for by law itself». A mere ordinance basis does not suffice (Rhinow/Schefer/Uebersax, Schweizerisches Verfassungsrecht, 3rd ed. 2016, N 2854).

**N. 8** The law must also regulate the form of deprivation of liberty («in the manner prescribed by law»). This includes the applicable procedure, the competent authorities and the modalities of enforcement. Delegation to the ordinance legislator is only permissible for subordinate implementing provisions.

#### 3.3 Information and Notification Rights (Para. 2)

**N. 9** The immediate information about the reasons for deprivation of liberty and the rights of the affected person must take place «in a language understandable to them». This does not necessarily require the mother tongue, but a language that the person demonstrably understands (Tschannen/Zimmerli/Müller, Allgemeines Verwaltungsrecht, 4th ed. 2014, § 52 N 14).

**N. 10** The right to notify relatives is not absolute. It can be restricted if overriding interests of criminal prosecution (in particular risk of collusion) are opposed. However, the restriction must be proportionate and must be lifted as soon as the reasons cease to exist.

#### 3.4 Judicial Appearance in Investigative Detention (Para. 3)

**N. 11** The «prompt» appearance before a judge means according to the case law of the Federal Supreme Court and the ECtHR generally within 48 hours, in exceptional cases maximum 96 hours (Häfelin/Haller/Keller/Thurnherr, Schweizerisches Bundesstaatsrecht, 10th ed. 2020, N 825).

**N. 12** The claim to a «judgment within reasonable time» concretizes the acceleration requirement for criminal proceedings with detained persons. Reasonableness is assessed according to the complexity of the case, the behaviour of the parties and the significance of the matter for the affected person (**BGE 133 I 168**).

#### 3.5 Habeas Corpus Guarantee (Para. 4)

**N. 13** Art. 31 para. 4 Federal Constitution grants the right to call upon a court «at any time». This means that no deadlines or other formal obstacles may block access to the court (**BGE 137 I 23**). The court must decide «as quickly as possible» – in case of first appeal generally within a few days.

**N. 14** The guarantee applies to all non-judicial deprivations of liberty, i.e. in particular to police arrests, administrative detention under foreign nationals law and welfare-related deprivations of liberty. In case of judicially ordered detention, the ordinary legal remedy procedure applies primarily.

### 4. Legal Consequences

**N. 15** Violations of Art. 31 Federal Constitution lead to the unlawfulness of the deprivation of liberty. The affected person must be released immediately if:
- no sufficient legal basis exists
- the prescribed form was not observed
- the information obligations were violated
- judicial review is refused or delayed

**N. 16** In case of unlawful deprivation of liberty, there exists a claim to just satisfaction according to Art. 5 No. 5 ECHR. The amount depends on the duration and circumstances of the unlawful detention. Additionally, damage compensation claims according to cantonal state liability law may exist.

### 5. Disputes

**N. 17** **Disputed scope for short-term detention**: Kiener (BSK BV, Art. 31 N 15) takes the position that even police detentions of several hours can fall under Art. 31 Federal Constitution. In contrast, the prevailing doctrine (Müller/Schefer, p. 174; Rhinow/Schefer/Uebersax, N 2851) requires a minimum duration of 24 hours. The Federal Supreme Court has left this question open so far.

**N. 18** **Notification right in case of risk of collusion**: While part of the doctrine (Vest/Höhener, BSK StPO, Art. 214 N 8) considers a complete refusal of relative notification in case of serious risk of collusion to be permissible, the opposing view (Donatsch/Hansjakob/Lieber, Kommentar StPO, Art. 214 N 5) advocates for a differentiated solution with selective notification of trustworthy persons.

**N. 19** **Applicability to private law deprivations of liberty**: Schweizer/Mohler (St. Galler Kommentar BV, Art. 31 N 12) affirm a direct third-party effect of Art. 31 Federal Constitution for private security services that perform sovereign tasks. The opposing view (Biaggini, BV-Kommentar, Art. 31 N 4) limits the application to state deprivations of liberty and refers to the criminal law protective norms.

### 6. Practice Notes

**N. 20** **Documentation obligations**: Every deprivation of liberty must be documented comprehensively from the beginning. This includes time, legal basis, implementing authority, instructions given and notifications made. Missing documentation can lead to the inadmissibility of evidence.

**N. 21** **Instruction in understandable language**: For foreign-language persons, it must be ensured that the information is actually understood. In case of doubt, an interpreter must be consulted. Standard forms in common languages should be kept available, but do not replace the examination of actual understanding.

**N. 22** **Time calculation for weekends**: The 48-hour deadline for judicial appearance also runs on weekends and holidays. The cantons must ensure a standby service. In case of foreseeable bottlenecks (e.g. major events), preventive measures must be taken.

**N. 23** **Proportionality examination**: Before every deprivation of liberty, it must be examined whether milder measures (reporting obligations, bail, electronic surveillance) are sufficient. The proportionality examination must be documented and must withstand subsequent judicial review.
