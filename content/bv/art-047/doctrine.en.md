# Art. 47 FC — Autonomy of the Cantons

## Doctrine

### 1. Legislative History

**N. 1** Art. 47 FC is an innovation of the totally revised Federal Constitution of 1999. The predecessor constitution of 1874 contained no corresponding express duty of the Confederation to protect the cantons; the autonomy of the cantons was protected only indirectly through Art. 3 of the former FC (sovereignty clause) and the catalogues of competences. In the course of the constitutional reform, the absence of a positive guarantee was perceived as a normative deficit.

**N. 2** In its dispatch of 20 November 1996 (BBl 1997 I 1), the Federal Council justified the inclusion of Art. 47 FC by stating that the provision should oblige the Confederation to actively preserve cantonal independence in its legislative and enforcement activities. The dispatch notes that the article does not constitute an independent competence provision, but rather concretises the federal principle underlying the Swiss federal state as a directive for interpretation binding on the Confederation (BBl 1997 I 223 f.). The parliamentary deliberations proceeded without any fundamental controversy.

**N. 3** Paragraph 2 in its current form was only adopted by the popular vote of 28 November 2004 on the redesign of fiscal equalisation and the allocation of tasks between the Confederation and the cantons (NFA) and entered into force on 1 January 2008 (AS 2007 5765; BBl 2002 2291). The original constitutional text of 1999 contained in paragraph 2 only the guarantee of autonomy with respect to tasks and organisation; the financial guarantees were added by the NFA reform. The dispatch on the NFA (BBl 2002 2291 ff.) emphasises that the addition was intended to secure for the cantons a sufficient financial base for the independent discharge of their tasks and at the same time to mitigate the vertical imbalance between Confederation and cantonal finances (BBl 2002 2308 f.).

**N. 4** In the dispatch on the NFA, the Federal Council stressed that the financial component of Art. 47 para. 2 FC does not aim to weaken the cantons' own responsibility, but to secure their structural foundation (BBl 2002 2309). The scope of the Confederation's duty of assistance was a matter of particular controversy during the parliamentary process; the wording of the second sentence of paragraph 2, as finally adopted, states that the Confederation merely «contributes» to ensuring that the cantons have the necessary resources — a deliberately restrained formulation (AB 2003 S 592; AB 2003 N 1482).

### 2. Systematic Classification

**N. 5** Art. 47 FC is located in Chapter 3 («Confederation and Cantons») of Title 2 of the Federal Constitution and constitutes the federalist-protective counterpart to the supremacy of federal law under → Art. 49 FC. The provision supplements and concretises the cantonal sovereignty clause of → Art. 3 FC and the implementation autonomy under → Art. 46 paras. 2 and 3 FC. It is closely connected with → Art. 50 FC (communal autonomy), which extends the same protective idea to the communal level, as well as with → Art. 43a para. 1 FC (principle of subsidiarity).

**N. 6** Art. 47 FC is to be classified systematically as a **principle norm** and a constitutional mandate: it is addressed primarily to the Confederation and establishes an institutional duty of protection, not justiciable subjective rights of the cantons (→ N. 17). It thus differs structurally from the fundamental rights guarantees (Art. 7–36 FC), which establish directly enforceable defensive rights. As a norm type, Art. 47 FC is situated between programmatic state objective provisions and objective constitutional law; its normative substance unfolds primarily as a directive for interpretation and as a limit on the exercise of competences (Ehrenzeller/Schindler/Schweizer/Vallender [eds.], St. Galler Kommentar zur Schweizerischen Bundesverfassung, 4th ed. 2023 [hereinafter: SGK], Art. 47 N. 3 f.).

**N. 7** The relationship to → Art. 5 FC (rule of law principle) and → Art. 5a FC (principle of subsidiarity) is close: Art. 47 FC operationalises the principle of subsidiarity at the federal level by obliging the Confederation to leave the cantons sufficient scope for the independent discharge of their tasks. Encroachments on cantonal autonomy are to be measured against the principle of proportionality (→ Art. 36 para. 3 FC by analogy) (Häfelin/Haller/Keller/Thurnherr, Schweizerisches Bundesstaatsrecht, 10th ed. 2020, N 1040 ff.).

**N. 8** In the field of tax law, Art. 47 para. 2 FC is to be read in conjunction with → Art. 128 FC (income and profit tax), → Art. 129 FC (tax harmonisation), → Art. 135 FC (national fiscal equalisation, NFA) and ↔ Art. 127 para. 3 FC (prohibition of intercantonal double taxation). The Federal Supreme Court has held that the restriction of cantonal sovereignty (Art. 3 FC) and of regulatory autonomy in financial matters (Art. 47 para. 2 FC) that goes hand in hand with a uniform intercantonal tax allocation must be accepted where this is necessary to avoid double taxation and to ensure taxation in accordance with economic capacity (**BGE 150 II 527** E. 6.2).

### 3. Elements of the Provision / Normative Content

#### 3.1 Para. 1: Duty of Protection of the Confederation

**N. 9** «The Confederation shall preserve the autonomy of the cantons.» The concept of **autonomy** is to be understood broadly: it encompasses legal independence (own legal order, own constitution, → Art. 51 FC), organisational independence (independent authority structures, → Art. 47 para. 2 sentence 1 FC) and financial independence (own sources of revenue and resources for the discharge of tasks, → Art. 47 para. 2 sentence 2 FC). The Federal Supreme Court derives from Art. 47 FC the **cantons' organisational sovereignty** as a central element of Swiss federalism (**BGE 128 I 254** E. 3.8.2).

**N. 10** The word «preserves» indicates an active duty of protection: the Confederation may not merely refrain from violating cantonal autonomy (negative prohibition), but is also required to take it into positive account and to respect it in the exercise of its competences. In practice, this duty is concretised primarily as a standard for the constitutionally conforming interpretation of federal law (Rhinow/Schefer/Uebersax, Schweizerisches Verfassungsrecht, 3rd ed. 2016, N 2410 ff.).

#### 3.2 Para. 2 Sentence 1: Autonomy over Tasks and Organisation

**N. 11** **«Sufficient tasks of their own»**: The Confederation must leave the cantons a substantial sphere of tasks of their own. This prohibits a gradual hollowing-out of the cantonal sphere of tasks through excessive centralisation, but does not call into question every expansion of federal competence. The guarantee with respect to tasks has constitutional rank and is thus binding even on the constituent power (SGK, Art. 47 N. 12).

**N. 12** **Organisational autonomy**: The cantons are free to organise their authorities, courts and procedures in accordance with their own law. The Confederation may encroach on this autonomy only where this is **necessary** and **proportionate** for the correct and uniform implementation of federal law (**BGE 128 I 254** E. 3.8.2; **BGE 144 IV 240** E. 2.1). In **BGE 128 I 254** E. 3.8.3, the Federal Supreme Court clarified that encroachments on cantonal organisational autonomy may also be permissible even in the context of framework or principles-based legislative competences, where important enforcement concerns so require. In **BGE 148 I 33** E. 7.4, the diversity of cantonal measures is recognised as a «consequence of federalism» (Art. 46 para. 3 and Art. 47 FC); cantonal authorities may, on objectively tenable grounds, carry out an independent risk assessment and thereby adopt stricter measures than other cantons or the Confederation.

**N. 13** Organisational autonomy protects in particular the freedom of cantons to delegate tasks to municipalities or to decentralise them to the district or regional level. Where federal law restricts this, the restriction must be express and proportionate (Müller/Schefer, Grundrechte in der Schweiz, 4th ed. 2008, pp. 716 ff.). In **BGE 144 IV 240** E. 2.5, the Federal Supreme Court confirmed that Art. 47 FC protects cantonal freedom of organisation, but that this finds its limit where federal law has enacted an exhaustive regulation.

#### 3.3 Para. 2 Sentence 2: Financial Autonomy

**N. 14** Art. 47 para. 2 sentence 2 contains two sub-sentences: the **first sub-sentence** obliges the Confederation to leave the cantons «adequate sources of funding». The **second sub-sentence** establishes a duty of assistance: the Confederation «contributes» to ensuring that the cantons have the necessary financial resources to discharge their tasks. This deliberately graduated formulation protects financial autonomy in structural terms, without establishing an unconditional entitlement to benefits.

**N. 15** **«Adequate sources of funding»** (para. 2 sentence 2 first sub-sentence): The provision requires that the cantons retain sufficient sources of tax revenue and income of their own to be able to finance from their own resources the tasks assigned to them by constitutional law. Closely related to this guarantee is the **communal financial autonomy** enshrined in cantonal constitutions: in **BGE 147 I 136** E. 2.3.4 f., the Federal Supreme Court held, with respect to the Lucerne cantonal constitution (§ 78 para. 2 KV/LU), that a canton undermines the financial autonomy of its municipalities if it does not allow them to set their own tax rate; the cantonal constitutional obligation to leave municipalities «adequate sources of funding» consciously mirrors the wording of Art. 47 para. 2 FC. Although **BGE 147 I 136** relies primarily on Art. 50 para. 1 FC and cantonal constitutional law, it illustrates the normative parallel between the financial guarantee of the Confederation vis-à-vis the cantons (Art. 47 para. 2 FC) and that of the cantons vis-à-vis the municipalities.

**N. 16** **«Necessary financial resources»** (para. 2 sentence 2 second sub-sentence): Where cantonal own revenues are insufficient, the Confederation is obliged to contribute to ensuring that the cantons have the resources necessary for the discharge of their tasks. This occurs primarily through the NFA (→ Art. 135 FC), which compensates resource-weaker cantons. This sub-sentence does not establish an entitlement to specific federal contributions; it merely obliges the Confederation to participate systemically in shaping a functional financial order (SGK, Art. 47 N. 24 f.; Häfelin/Haller/Keller/Thurnherr, N 1052).

### 4. Legal Consequences

**N. 17** Art. 47 FC is an **objective-law principle norm**. It produces binding effects on the Confederation as legislator and as enforcement authority. Where doubt exists, federal statutes are to be interpreted in such a way as to preserve cantonal autonomy to the greatest possible extent (constitutionally conforming interpretation).

**N. 18** **Absence of individual justiciability**: Art. 47 FC establishes **no subjective rights** of the cantons that could be claimed before the Federal Supreme Court in individual cases. The provision is not a cause of action on which a canton or a private individual could directly rely in appellate proceedings in order to challenge a federal statute as unconstitutional. The Federal Supreme Court applies Art. 47 FC as an aid to interpretation and as a standard governing the exercise of competences, not as an independent ground of appeal (**BGE 144 IV 240** E. 2.5; **BGE 128 I 254** E. 3.8.2). Pursuant to → Art. 190 FC, federal statutes remain binding on the Federal Supreme Court even if they should conflict with Art. 47 FC.

**N. 19** **Effect as a directive for interpretation**: In practice, Art. 47 FC produces its most important effect as a standard of interpretation. Where federal law leaves room for discretion, preference is to be given to the interpretation that best protects cantonal autonomy (→ Art. 46 paras. 2 and 3 FC). The Federal Supreme Court explicitly confirmed this principle in **BGE 128 I 254** E. 3.8.1 f. Differing cantonal regulations that respond differently to the same federal framework are regarded as constitutionally permissible «consequences of federalism» (**BGE 148 I 33** E. 7.4; **BGE 148 I 19** E. 6.3.4).

**N. 20** **Limit on the exercise of federal competences**: Art. 47 FC operates as an immanent limit on the exercise of federal competences: encroachments by the Confederation on cantonal organisational and task autonomy must be necessary and proportionate. Disproportionate encroachments violate Art. 47 FC, even if the underlying federal competence is undisputed (**BGE 128 I 254** E. 3.8.2). In the area of intercantonal tax allocation, the Federal Supreme Court accepts a restriction of cantonal financial autonomy (Art. 47 para. 2 FC) to the extent that this is required for the realisation of the prohibition of double taxation (Art. 127 para. 3 FC) and taxation in accordance with economic capacity (**BGE 150 II 527** E. 6.2).

### 5. Contested Issues

**N. 21** **Legal nature of Art. 47 FC: Programmatic provision or constitutional guarantee?** It is disputed whether Art. 47 FC constitutes merely a programmatic objective provision or an institutional constitutional guarantee for the cantons. The prevailing doctrine qualifies Art. 47 FC as an institutional guarantee without an individual right of action: see Ehrenzeller/Schindler/Schweizer/Vallender, SGK, Art. 47 N. 3 f.; likewise Häfelin/Haller/Keller/Thurnherr, Schweizerisches Bundesstaatsrecht, 10th ed. 2020, N 1049. A contrary position, which would attribute to the article a certain subjective protective effect for the cantons, is held by a minority (see in principle Rhinow/Schefer/Uebersax, Schweizerisches Verfassungsrecht, 3rd ed. 2016, N 2415), but has found no reflection in the case law of the Federal Supreme Court.

**N. 22** **Scope of organisational autonomy vis-à-vis federal law**: It is controversial how far federal legislation may encroach on cantonal organisational autonomy. The Federal Supreme Court held in **BGE 128 I 254** E. 3.8.3 that encroachments are permissible even in the context of the Confederation's framework competences where «particularly important enforcement concerns» so require. By contrast, part of the doctrine emphasises that proportionality must be examined more strictly: encroachments on organisational autonomy require a compelling federal enforcement justification (Müller/Schefer, Grundrechte in der Schweiz, 4th ed. 2008, pp. 718 f.). In **BGE 148 I 33** E. 7.4, the Federal Supreme Court recognised cantonal diversity as a positive federalist value and expressly referred to Art. 47 FC; this strengthens the position of those who advocate restraint in federal intervention in organisational autonomy. The tension is particularly noticeable in practice in the implementation of federal law in the areas of the Code of Criminal Procedure (CCP) and spatial planning law (SPA).

**N. 23** **Financial autonomy: Entitlement or design guarantee?** With respect to para. 2 sentence 2, it is disputed whether the cantons can rely on Art. 47 FC to assert an entitlement to the maintenance of existing sources of funding or to a specific flow of funds from the NFA. The prevailing doctrine answers in the negative: the financial clause is to be understood as a constitutional obligation on the Confederation when designing fiscal equalisation, but does not establish an enforceable claim by individual cantons (SGK, Art. 47 N. 24; Häfelin/Haller/Keller/Thurnherr, N 1052). A dissenting view, according to which the provision guarantees at least a minimum standard of financial autonomy that would be violated if the Confederation were to assume cantonal tasks without financial compensation, is advanced by Rhinow/Schefer/Uebersax, N 2420. **BGE 147 I 136** shows, in the communal parallel (§ 78 para. 2 KV/LU), that the financial guarantee has at least a normative core that also protects against legislative encroachments — this tends to support the position of Rhinow/Schefer/Uebersax. In the intercantonal context, the Federal Supreme Court in **BGE 150 II 527** E. 6.2 accepted restrictions on cantonal financial regulatory autonomy (Art. 47 para. 2 FC) where overriding coordination requirements (Art. 127 para. 3 FC) so demand.

**N. 24** **Relationship to communal autonomy (Art. 50 FC)**: In academic writing, there is disagreement as to whether Art. 47 FC also indirectly protects communal autonomy. The Federal Supreme Court held in **BGE 135 I 28** E. 5.4 that constitutional sovereignty (Art. 3 FC), organisational autonomy (Art. 47 FC) and cantonal own interests alone do not provide a sufficient basis for derogating from federal law (OPA); the decision in question concerned specifically the affiliation of municipalities with pension institutions in the area of occupational pension provision. Protection of the communal level occurs primarily through → Art. 50 FC and not indirectly through Art. 47 FC, as **BGE 147 I 136** E. 2.1 f. confirms by deriving communal autonomy in the setting of tax rates exclusively from Art. 50 para. 1 FC and cantonal constitutional law.

### 6. Practical Notes

**N. 25** **Interpretation of federal law**: When interpreting federal statutes that impose organisational requirements on the cantons, it is always necessary to examine whether the encroachment on cantonal organisational autonomy is proportionate. Art. 47 FC requires, in cases of doubt, the interpretation most favourable to the cantons (**BGE 128 I 254** E. 3.8.1 f.). This is of particular practical importance in connection with the delegation of federal enforcement tasks and the determination of procedural and jurisdictional provisions in federal statutes (e.g. Art. 25 para. 2 SPA, Art. 104 para. 2 CCP).

**N. 26** **Cantonal freedom of implementation within federal framework conditions**: Cantons implementing federal law (→ Art. 46 para. 2 FC) may not use their organisational and enforcement freedom to circumvent federal requirements. Organisational autonomy ends where the federal legislator has enacted an exhaustive regulation (**BGE 144 IV 240** E. 2.3.2 in conjunction with → Art. 49 para. 1 FC). Within the federal framework, however, cantons may choose independent solutions, even if these are stricter or different from those of other cantons or the Confederation itself — this applies in particular also in crisis situations (**BGE 148 I 19** E. 6.3.4: cantonal restriction of participants in public assemblies to 300 persons as a permissible federalist measure; **BGE 148 I 33** E. 7.4: differing cantonal risk assessments as a «consequence of federalism» within the meaning of Art. 46 para. 3 and Art. 47 FC).

**N. 27** **NFA connection**: The financial component of Art. 47 para. 2 sentence 2 FC is to be assessed in the context of the Federal Council's quadrennial NFA report. The effectiveness review of the NFA (Art. 135 para. 4 FC) serves as an institutional instrument for examining whether the Confederation has complied with its duty of assistance. Appeals against specific aspects of the NFA design may rely on → Art. 135 FC, but not directly on Art. 47 para. 2 FC.

**N. 28** **Procedural implications**: Since Art. 47 FC does not establish subjective rights (→ N. 18), it is excluded as an independent ground of appeal before the Federal Supreme Court. In the statement of grounds of appeal, it must regularly be combined with objections under → Art. 49 para. 1 FC (supremacy of federal law), → Art. 9 FC (prohibition of arbitrariness) or → Art. 29 FC (procedural guarantees). A violation of Art. 47 FC by federal statutes cannot, pursuant to → Art. 190 FC, be made the subject of judicial review of legislation.

**N. 29** **Intercantonal cooperation and tax allocation**: Art. 47 FC does not preclude intercantonal concordats that exercise cantonal autonomy in a coordinated manner (→ Art. 48 FC). Conflicts between cantonal autonomy and intercantonal coordination duties are to be resolved in accordance with the principles set out in **BGE 148 I 65** E. 3–4. The most recent leading decision **BGE 150 II 527** E. 6.2 (2024) confirmed that the Federal Supreme Court accepts the restriction of cantonal regulatory autonomy in financial matters (Art. 47 para. 2 FC) associated with a uniform intercantonal tax allocation where this is necessary for the realisation of the prohibition of double taxation (Art. 127 para. 3 FC). Cantonal tax sovereignty must accordingly be exercised in such a way as to comply with the constitutional requirements for the avoidance of double taxation — this illustrates the tension between cantonal autonomy and federal coordination duties.
