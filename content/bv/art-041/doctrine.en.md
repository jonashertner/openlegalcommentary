# Art. 41 FC — Social Goals

## Doctrine

### 1. Legislative History

**N. 1** Art. 41 FC derives from the reform process of the total revision of the Federal Constitution, concluded by the Federal Decree on a New Federal Constitution of 18 April 1999. The constituent legislature faced the conceptual question of how to enshrine social state goals in constitutional law without creating an uncontrolled inflation of entitlements. In its dispatch of 20 November 1996, the Federal Council described the social goals as «mandates to the Confederation and the cantons», categorially distinct from directly enforceable fundamental rights (BBl 1997 I 193 f.). It expressly stated that the social goals were not intended to give rise to justiciable claims to state benefits (BBl 1997 I 194).

**N. 2** The distinction between «social goals» (Art. 41 FC), «social fundamental rights» in the strict sense (Art. 12 FC: right to assistance in situations of need; Art. 19 FC: right to basic education) and «social participation rights» derives from more recent cantonal constitutions that served as models for the constituent legislature. The Federal Supreme Court expressly confirmed this distinction — based on the legislative materials — in **BGE 129 I 12 E. 4.3** and pointed to Art. 41 para. 4 FC as the decisive demarcation criterion. The dispatch notes that Art. 36 FC (restriction of fundamental rights) does not apply to the social goals of Art. 41 FC, since the latter do not establish subjective defensive rights (BBl 1997 I 194 f.).

**N. 3** Para. 1 let. g — the protection and promotion of the development of children and young persons and their integration and health promotion — was inserted by the revision of 27 September 2009 (AS 2010 3853). The constituent legislature thereby responded to socio-political developments in the field of child and youth promotion; the provision nonetheless remains programmatic in nature (BBl 2008 8577).

---

### 2. Systematic Classification

**N. 4** Art. 41 FC is situated in the third chapter («Social Goals») of the second title («Fundamental Rights, Civil Rights and Social Goals») of the Federal Constitution. Its placement after the fundamental rights articles (Art. 7–36 FC) and the civil rights provisions (Art. 37–40 FC) is not accidental: it expresses the fact that the social goals constitute an autonomous category of norms, assignable neither to the freedom rights nor to the directly justiciable benefit rights. They are **state objective provisions** (Rhinow/Schefer/Uebersax, Schweizerisches Verfassungsrecht, 3rd ed. 2016, N. 1736) or **programme norms** (Häfelin/Haller/Keller/Thurnherr, Schweizerisches Bundesstaatsrecht, 10th ed. 2020, N. 287).

**N. 5** The demarcation from other constitutional provisions is central to the application of the law:

- ↔ **Art. 12 FC** (right to assistance in situations of need): a justiciable social fundamental right granting a judicially enforceable minimum entitlement to a basic subsistence — in sharp contrast to the non-justiciable social goal provision of Art. 41 para. 1 let. a FC.
- ↔ **Art. 19 FC** (right to basic education): a justiciable social fundamental right conferring an individual entitlement that takes precedence over Art. 41 para. 1 let. f FC (**BGE 129 I 12 E. 4.1**; **BGE 133 I 156 E. 3.6.4**).
- → **Art. 2 FC** (purpose article): The promotion of the «common welfare» enshrined in Art. 2 para. 2 FC constitutes the general constitutional backdrop against which Art. 41 FC is to be read.
- → **Art. 36 FC** (restriction of fundamental rights): Not applicable to the social goals (BBl 1997 I 194 f.).
- → **Art. 190 FC** (applicable law): Federal statutes and international law that give concrete expression to the social goals (e.g. the AHVG, KVG, ArG) are binding on the Federal Supreme Court and other law-applying authorities.

**N. 6** The social goals must furthermore be distinguished from the **jurisdictional articles** that confer upon the Confederation the authority to legislate in specific social domains. Art. 41 FC does not establish any competences; these derive from other constitutional provisions (Art. 112 ff. FC: social insurance; Art. 116 FC: family allowances; Art. 117 FC: health insurance).

### 3. Elements / Normative Content

#### 3.1 Subjects of the Constitutional Mandate (Para. 1 Introductory Sentence)

**N. 7** «The Confederation and the cantons» are jointly the addressees of the constitutional mandate under Art. 41 para. 1 FC. The provision thus contains a **shared state objective** for both levels of government. This does not explicitly include the communes, but these are made subject to the mandate through cantonal law. The allocation of tasks between the Confederation and the cantons must be read in a differentiated manner according to their respective constitutional competences (Art. 41 para. 3 FC; → N. 13).

**N. 8** The social goals are subsidiary to «personal responsibility and private initiative». The introductory sentence of Art. 41 para. 1 FC qualifies state engagement as supplementary («in supplement to»). This constitutionally anchors the **principle of subsidiarity** (→ Art. 5a FC) in the social domain. Müller/Schefer emphasise that this subsidiarity reservation is normatively significant: it not only prohibits the state from remaining inactive, but also prescribes a particular order of priority — individual self-responsibility first, state intervention second (Müller/Schefer, Grundrechte in der Schweiz, 4th ed. 2008, p. 784 f.).

#### 3.2 Individual Social Goals (Para. 1 Let. a–g)

**N. 9** **Let. a** (social security): The social goal of participation in social security is the most comprehensive. It corresponds to the specific jurisdictional norms of the Confederation for social insurance (Art. 112–114 FC). Legislative implementation is effected in particular by the AHVG, IVG, ALV, ELG and AVIG. The social goal does not confer an entitlement to a particular level of benefits, but does impose on the legislature a mandate to close gaps (Häfelin/Haller/Keller/Thurnherr, op. cit., N. 289).

**N. 10** **Let. b** (health care): The social goal of health care encompasses the care «necessary for health» — i.e. medically indicated care. It forms the constitutional backdrop of the KVG without, however, establishing concrete entitlements to specific treatments. In **BGE 127 I 6**, the Federal Supreme Court made clear that the social goal of Art. 41 para. 1 let. b FC does not constitute a basis for interference with other fundamental rights (in particular personal freedom).

**N. 11** **Let. c** (protection of the family): The social goal of protection of the family covers «families as communities of adults and children». The wording is deliberately broad and includes unmarried cohabiting partnerships with children (Rhinow/Schefer/Uebersax, op. cit., N. 1741). The Federal Supreme Court invoked Art. 41 para. 1 let. c FC in **BGE 140 V 449 E. 4.1** as an aid to interpretation in matters of family allowance law, without deriving independent entitlements therefrom.

**N. 12** **Let. d–f** (work, housing, education): These three social goals form the core of economic and social participation. «Appropriate conditions» (let. d), «affordable conditions» (let. e) and «according to their abilities» (let. f) are indeterminate legal concepts that leave the legislature broad latitude for concrete implementation. The education goal in let. f is, as the Federal Supreme Court held in **BGE 133 I 156 E. 3.6.4**, to be strictly distinguished from Art. 19 FC: «This applies a fortiori with regard to Art. 41 para. 1 let. f FC, from which provision (as a social goal) no direct entitlements can in any event be derived.»

**N. 13** **Let. g** (promotion of children and young persons): The provision inserted by the 2009 constitutional revision comprises three elements: (1) promotion of independence and social responsibility; (2) support of social, cultural and political integration; (3) health promotion. The provision is closely connected with → Art. 11 FC (protection of children and young persons), which — in contrast to Art. 41 let. g — contains a justiciable fundamental right.

#### 3.3 Protection against the Economic Consequences of Social Risks (Para. 2)

**N. 14** Art. 41 para. 2 FC contains a separate social goal mandate: protection against the economic consequences of **old age, invalidity, illness, accident, unemployment, maternity, being orphaned and widowhood**. These eight «insured events» (catalogue of risks) largely correspond to the classic catalogue of social insurance. The wording «is covered» — as distinct from «shall endeavour to ensure» in para. 1 — might suggest a higher density of obligation; however, according to the prevailing doctrine, para. 2 likewise does not establish a subjective entitlement (Häfelin/Haller/Keller/Thurnherr, op. cit., N. 293; Rhinow/Schefer/Uebersax, op. cit., N. 1744).

#### 3.4 Resources Reservation (Para. 3)

**N. 15** The Confederation and the cantons «shall endeavour to achieve the social goals within the framework of their constitutional competences and their available resources». This twofold reservation — competence and resources — qualifies the state obligation as an **obligation of endeavour**, not an obligation of result. The competence clause is of particular importance in the federal system: it does not prohibit the federal legislature from giving concrete expression to the social goals (insofar as it has a competence to do so), but prohibits it from relying on Art. 41 FC alone as a jurisdictional basis (→ Art. 3 FC). The resources reservation («available resources») is to be understood as the constitutional anchoring of fiscal pragmatism.

#### 3.5 Non-Justiciability (Para. 4)

**N. 16** Art. 41 para. 4 FC is the central provision of the article: «No direct rights to state benefits may be derived from the social goals.» The Federal Supreme Court has treated this as an **absolute rule**: even where a social goal comes thematically close to a justiciable fundamental right, it does not in itself give rise to a judicially enforceable entitlement (**BGE 129 I 12 E. 4.3**). Non-justiciability applies equally to all seven subparagraphs of para. 1 and to para. 2. The word «direct» in para. 4 is not to be read as an opening clause: there are no «indirect» entitlements derived from Art. 41 FC.

### 4. Legal Consequences

**N. 17** The social goals produce their normative effects on three levels:

**(1) Legislative mandate**: Art. 41 FC obliges the Confederation and the cantons to actively legislate in the social domain. This mandate is politically, not judicially, enforceable. A permanent legislative standstill in one of the areas mentioned would be constitutionally problematic, but cannot be sanctioned by the courts (Müller/Schefer, op. cit., p. 786).

**(2) Interpretive directive**: The social goals serve as **aids to interpretation** in the concretisation of other constitutional provisions and statutory norms. The Federal Supreme Court applies this approach in particular in the field of social insurance law: Judgment 9C_736/2011 of 7.2.2012; see also the reference to Art. 41 FC as interpretive context in **BGE 140 V 449 E. 4.1**. The social goals may not, however, lead to an expansion of the scope of protection of another fundamental right (**BGE 133 I 156 E. 3.6.4**).

**(3) Standard of review in respect of state action**: In the context of the proportionality review of state measures, social goals may be taken into account as public interests in the balancing exercise. This has become apparent in case law on taxation: in **BGE 133 I 206 E. 7.4**, the Federal Supreme Court invoked the social goal of solidarity — following the commentary by Bigler-Eggenberger (St. Galler Kommentar, N. 10 f. on Art. 41 FC) — as a normative context for the interpretation of the ability-to-pay principle (Art. 127 para. 2 FC).

**N. 18** What the social goals do **not** achieve is equally significant from a normative standpoint: they do not establish subjective rights, direct claims to benefits, standing before the Federal Supreme Court, or a basis for the annulment of conflicting legislation. No constitutional legal claim can be constructed from the absence of legislative implementation.

### 5. Contested Issues

#### 5.1 Normative Significance of Non-Justiciability

**N. 19** It is disputed whether Art. 41 para. 4 FC has a purely declaratory or a constitutive significance. **Rhinow/Schefer/Uebersax** (op. cit., N. 1750 f.) take the view that non-justiciability excludes only **direct** enforceability, but not every indirect effect. The social goals are to be understood as the «soft law» of the Federal Constitution, normatively structuring the decision-making latitude of law-applying authorities without establishing entitlements.

**N. 20** **Müller/Schefer** (op. cit., p. 787 f.) distinguish between the rejection of benefit claims and the recognition of a «minimum protection requirement» (*Untermassverbot*): where the legislature completely ignores a social goal or drastically reduces an already existing level of protection without objective justification, this could be characterised as an unconstitutional interference with the state's protective mandate. This minority view has not yet prevailed in the case law of the Federal Supreme Court.

**N. 21** **Häfelin/Haller/Keller/Thurnherr** (op. cit., N. 295) reject both justiciability and a minimum protection requirement in the sense of Müller/Schefer. In their view, the social goals are conceived as pure state objective provisions whose normative effect operates exclusively in the political process and not in the legal process. The Federal Supreme Court's case law on Art. 41 para. 4 FC is closely aligned with this position (**BGE 129 I 12 E. 4.3**).

#### 5.2 Relationship between Social Goals and Social Fundamental Rights

**N. 22** An unresolved dispute concerns interpretive methodology when social goals and social fundamental rights run thematically in parallel (e.g. Art. 41 para. 1 let. f FC and Art. 19 FC in the field of education). The Federal Supreme Court held in **BGE 133 I 156 E. 3.5.2** that legal scholarship largely insists on interpreting Art. 19 FC in the light of existing case law, without Art. 41 para. 1 let. f FC leading to any expansion. **Plotke** (Schweizerisches Schulrecht, 2nd ed. 2003, p. 185 ff.), by contrast, has advocated a teleological interpretation that uses the social goals as a compass for an evolutive reading of the social fundamental rights — a position expressly not adopted by the Federal Supreme Court in **BGE 133 I 156**.

#### 5.3 Scope of the Subsidiarity Reservation

**N. 23** Whether the subsidiarity reservation («in supplement to personal responsibility and private initiative», Art. 41 para. 1 introductory sentence) is to be read as a restriction of the social state mandate or merely as a declaratory reinforcement of liberal constitutional values is disputed in legal scholarship. **Bigler-Eggenberger** (St. Galler Kommentar zur BV, N. 10 ff. on Art. 41 FC) emphasises that the subsidiarity clause does not produce an absolute blocking effect but rather enshrines the primacy of the individual over the state, without restricting the social state mandate. **Uebersax** (in: Rhinow/Schefer/Uebersax, op. cit., N. 1737), by contrast, sees in the subsidiarity clause an indication that the constituent legislature deliberately refrained from establishing an extensive state responsibility.

### 6. Practical Notes

**N. 24** In legal remedies and actions based on an alleged violation of Art. 41 FC, the Federal Supreme Court will decline to enter into the merits or dismiss the ground of appeal. Art. 41 para. 4 FC categorically excludes the derivation of a «constitutional right» within the meaning of Art. 116 para. 1 let. a BGG from Art. 41 FC. Appeals based solely on Art. 41 FC are inadmissible.

**N. 25** By contrast, **invoking Art. 41 FC as an aid to interpretation** of other, justiciable constitutional provisions or of statutory norms is procedurally permissible and legally conducive. A party asserting a violation of Art. 19 FC (right to basic education), Art. 12 FC (right to assistance in situations of need), Art. 8 FC (equality) or Art. 13 FC (protection of privacy) may use Art. 41 FC in support of a teleological line of argument.

**N. 26** In **legislative proceedings**, the social goals constitute a normative standard for assessing the conformity of social legislation with the Constitution. Legislative projects that reduce benefits in the social domain (e.g. cuts to social insurance benefits) must be measured against the framework of the social mandate of Art. 41 FC — not in the sense of annulment, but in the sense of a political obligation to justify such measures in relation to the constitutional mandate.

**N. 27** In **cantonal law**, the social goals may be «upgraded» by cantons into genuine social rights where cantonal constitutions — as do, for instance, Art. 29 para. 2 of the KV/BE or Art. 24 and 25 of the KV/AR — contain corresponding justiciable provisions. The Federal Supreme Court recognised this in **BGE 129 I 12 E. 4.4** as a permissible further development of the Federal Constitution through cantonal law. The non-justiciability under federal constitutional law does not preclude such a cantonal upgrade; conversely, federal law imposes no obligation to configure social goals as justiciable rights at cantonal level.

**N. 28** In the field of **social insurance**, it should be noted that the Federal Supreme Court draws on the social goals as a constitutional interpretive context when construing benefit entitlements (Judgment 9C_736/2011 of 7.2.2012 E. 4). This does not mean that a right to a particular level of benefits can be derived from Art. 41 FC; it does mean, however, that when interpreting indeterminate legal concepts in ordinary social insurance law (e.g. «appropriate insurance benefit»), the social goals must be taken into account as a normative backdrop.

**N. 29** The **demarcation between para. 1 and para. 2** has practical significance: Art. 41 para. 2 FC contains a narrower, specifically social-insurance-law mandate (risk protection), while para. 1 encompasses a broad spectrum of social state objectives. In legislative practice, para. 2 is typically invoked as the constitutional point of reference for the classic branches of social insurance (AHV, IV, UV, KV, ALV).
