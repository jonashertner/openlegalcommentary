# Art. 34 FC – Political Rights

## Doctrine

---

### 1. Legislative History

**N. 1** Art. 34 FC codifies a guarantee that the Federal Supreme Court had developed since the 1940s as unwritten federal constitutional law. Originally, the Court derived the freedom to vote and to participate in elections from Art. 4 of the former FC (principle of equality) and applied it exclusively to cantonal and communal suffrage matters (**BGE 121 I 138** cons. 3 p. 141). The total revision of the Federal Constitution of 1999 provided this case law with an explicit constitutional basis.

**N. 2** The Federal Council's Dispatch of 20 November 1996 (BBl 1997 I 1, 175 f.) stated that Art. 34 FC encompasses, in addition to active and passive voting and electoral rights, the right to vote and the right to participate in popular referendums. Para. 2 was formulated as a substantive description of the protected content: the provision protects «the free formation of opinion» in the run-up to a ballot as well as «the unaltered expression of the vote» on polling day itself (BBl 1997 I 176).

**N. 3** Before the judicial reform of 2007 entered into force, judicial protection in federal suffrage matters was limited: voting complaints were adjudicated by the Federal Council and election complaints by the National Council, each as first and final instances. Art. 189 para. 1 let. f FC and Art. 82 let. c BGG transferred final judicial protection at the federal level to the Federal Supreme Court, thereby giving effect to the claim to judicial review implicitly contained in Art. 34 para. 1 FC (**BGE 138 I 61** cons. 3.2 p. 69). The guarantee of political rights has since been subject to comprehensive judicial protection, subject to Art. 189 para. 4 FC with respect to Federal Council acts.

---

### 2. Systematic Classification

**N. 4** Art. 34 FC is situated in the third chapter of the Federal Constitution («Fundamental Rights», Art. 7–36 FC). Systematically, the provision constitutes a political fundamental right (right to participation) and thereby differs from the classic defensive rights (Art. 10 ff. FC): it protects not only against state interference but also constitutes a positive state duty to establish correct ballot and election procedures (→ Art. 39 FC for the conditions for exercising voting rights).

**N. 5** The right to judicial protection (↔ Art. 29a FC) is included within Art. 34 para. 1 FC for political rights; **BGE 138 I 61** cons. 3.2 confirmed that Art. 34 in conjunction with Art. 29a FC establishes a constitutional right to judicial review of popular votes.

**N. 6** Para. 1 guarantees political rights as an institutional guarantee (the fundamental rights institution of «direct democracy»); para. 2 gives concrete form to the individual protective content through two teleologically interrelated sub-elements: «free formation of opinion» (the communicative process prior to the ballot) and «unaltered expression of the vote» (the technical and procedural execution). Both elements are interdependent and together constitute the so-called «principle of voting freedom» (**BGE 130 I 290** cons. 3 p. 293).

**N. 7** Art. 34 FC applies at all levels — Confederation, cantons and communes — and also bears on popular rights established under cantonal law (→ Art. 39 para. 1 FC). The substantive requirements that the Federal Supreme Court has developed from Art. 34 para. 2 FC are equally binding on all public bodies (**BGE 138 I 61** cons. 6.3 p. 83; Steinmann, in: St. Galler Kommentar BV, 4th ed. 2023, Art. 34 N. 1).

---

### 3. Elements of the Provision / Normative Content

#### 3.1 Para. 1: Institutional Guarantee

**N. 8** Art. 34 para. 1 FC guarantees «political rights» as a whole. The concept encompasses: active and passive voting and electoral rights (Art. 136, 143 FC), the right to popular initiative (Art. 138 f. FC), the right to referendum (Art. 140 f. FC), and the right of petition (Art. 33 FC). The catalogue is not exhaustive. Steinmann (St. Galler Kommentar BV, 4th ed. 2023, Art. 34 N. 6) and Müller/Schefer (Grundrechte in der Schweiz, 4th ed. 2008, pp. 604 ff.) emphasise that the guarantee establishes a procedural minimum standard for all direct-democratic popular rights.

**N. 9** According to the case law, the guarantee also encompasses the right to an independent judicial remedy in suffrage matters. This follows from Art. 34 para. 1 in conjunction with Art. 29a FC and in particular grounds the suffrage complaint pursuant to Art. 82 let. c BGG (**BGE 138 I 61** cons. 3.2 pp. 68 f.).

#### 3.2 Para. 2: Free Formation of Opinion

**N. 10** «Free formation of opinion» protects the communicative process that eligible voters undergo before reaching their decision. Specifically, it guarantees the right «not to be subjected to pressure or to undue influence either in forming or in expressing one's political will» (**BGE 145 I 1** cons. 4.1 p. 5 with references). The freedom to vote safeguards the «openness of debate that is necessary for the democratic process and the legitimacy of direct-democratic decisions» (**BGE 143 I 78** cons. 4.3 p. 82).

**N. 11** From the duty to secure free formation of opinion, the Federal Supreme Court derives an **obligation of authorities to provide information**: explanatory statements on ballots and elections must be objective and balanced. An authority breaches this obligation if it «provides incorrect information about the purpose and scope of a proposal» or «suppresses elements important for the decision of eligible voters in the voting explanations» (**BGE 130 I 290** cons. 3.2 p. 294; **BGE 138 I 61** cons. 6.2 pp. 82 f.). The authority is not obliged to remain neutral — it may issue a voting recommendation — but it is required to act objectively (Tschannen, Staatsrecht der Schweizerischen Eidgenossenschaft, 4th ed. 2016, § 48 N. 29).

**N. 12** The standard for the precision of official information is measured against the «normally informed, averagely attentive, and politically interested eligible voter» (**BGE 130 I 290** cons. 4.11 pp. 302 f.). Not every inaccuracy or omission of a single detail violates the freedom to vote; what is determinative is whether the misinformation «could have materially influenced or distorted the outcome of the vote» (**BGE 130 I 290** cons. 3.4 pp. 295 f.).

**N. 13** A fundamental special case of a violation of the free formation of opinion was recognised in **BGE 138 I 61**: the absence of essential informational elements — in particular the indication of the inestimable financial consequences of a proposal — violates Art. 34 para. 2 FC, even where no actively false statements were made (cons. 8.6 p. 93).

**N. 14** Forecasts in voting explanations do not violate the freedom to vote solely because they subsequently prove to be inaccurate. What is decisive is whether they were «prepared with care and with a commitment to objectivity» and whether their uncertainty was indicated (**BGE 138 I 61** cons. 8.4 pp. 91 f.).

#### 3.3 Para. 2: Unaltered Expression of the Vote

**N. 15** «Unaltered expression of the vote» protects the technical process of the ballot and its result. The guarantee means «that no voting or election result may be recognised that does not reliably and without distortion give expression to the free will of the voters» (**BGE 135 I 19** cons. 2 p. 23; **BGE 129 I 185** cons. 5 p. 192).

**N. 16** Specifically, the guarantee covers: correct verification of ballots, proper counting, prevention of double voting, and fair delimitation of electoral districts in proportional elections. In cantonal parliamentary elections held under the proportional representation system, Art. 34 para. 2 FC requires that equality of voting power (equality of electoral rights) be maintained within the bounds of constitutional possibility (**BGE 131 I 74** cons. 3 pp. 78 f.; **BGE 136 I 352** cons. 2 pp. 356 ff.).

**N. 17** In the case of very close results, there is no direct right to a recount under Art. 34 para. 2 FC. Such a recount may, however, prove to be factually required where it is procedurally possible (**BGE 141 II 297** cons. 5.3). If a recount is precluded because a significant proportion of the ballots have been destroyed, Art. 34 para. 2 FC requires that the vote be repeated (**BGE 138 I 171** cons. 5.3 p. 183).

#### 3.4 Unity of Subject Matter

**N. 18** From Art. 34 para. 2 FC, the Federal Supreme Court derives the requirement of unity of subject matter in popular initiatives: eligible voters must be able to answer «yes» or «no» to each question without being compelled to enter into a «package deal» (**BGE 129 I 366** cons. 2 pp. 370 ff.). The requirement applies to cantonal popular initiatives and ensures that the expressed will of the eligible voters corresponds to their actual will.

#### 3.5 Official Interventions in Referendum Campaigns

**N. 19** Art. 34 para. 2 FC also entails a restriction on official interventions in referendum campaigns. For votes within their **own public body**, authorities fulfil an advisory function; a voting recommendation is permissible provided it is made in an objective manner (**BGE 130 I 290** cons. 3.2 p. 294; **BGE 143 I 78** cons. 4.4 pp. 82 f.).

**N. 20** A stricter standard applies to interventions in referendum campaigns of **other public bodies**: communes may intervene in cantonal votes only if they have «a direct and particular interest that far exceeds that of the other communes of the canton» (**BGE 108 Ia 155** cons. 4b p. 159; confirmed in **BGE 143 I 78** cons. 4.4 p. 83). The same applies to cantons in federal votes (**BGE 143 I 78** cons. 4.6 pp. 85 f.).

**N. 21** In **BGE 145 I 1**, the Federal Supreme Court clarified: where the outcome of a federal vote significantly affects several or all cantons, the Conference of Cantonal Governments (KdK) may make public statements and issue a voting recommendation, provided the criteria of objectivity, proportionality, and transparency are observed (cons. 6.5.2 pp. 18 f.). Interventions by specialist directors' conferences remain excluded (cons. 6.5.2 p. 19).

---

### 4. Legal Consequences

**N. 22** If the Federal Supreme Court finds a violation of Art. 34 FC, it annuls the election or vote concerned where «the irregularities complained of are substantial and could have influenced the outcome». Appellants are not required to prove a causal effect; it suffices that «on the basis of the established facts, such an effect is within the realm of possibility» (**BGE 130 I 290** cons. 3.4 pp. 295 f.; **BGE 143 I 78** cons. 7.1 p. 90).

**N. 23** In proportional representation systems, a declaration of unconstitutionality is frequently issued, combined with an order to redesign the electoral system in conformity with the constitution for future elections; annulment of the election already held is withheld on grounds of legal certainty (**BGE 131 I 74** cons. 6 p. 91; **BGE 136 I 352** cons. 7 pp. 369 f.).

**N. 24** Subsequent judicial protection is also possible: where massive irregularities come to light only after the fact, they may be made the subject of a revision-like procedure pursuant to Art. 29 para. 1 in conjunction with Art. 29a FC; however, a constitutional right to annulment of the vote exists only where the result has not already produced far-reaching legal effects that cannot be reversed (**BGE 138 I 61** cons. 8.7 pp. 94 ff.).

**N. 25** Standing to appeal is granted by Art. 89 para. 3 BGG to any person entitled to vote in the matter concerned; proof of particular personal interest is not required — in contrast to the ordinary constitutional complaint (**BGE 130 I 290** cons. 1.2–1.3 pp. 292 f.).

### 5. Contested Issues

#### 5.1 Scope of the Freedom to Vote in Relation to Private Actors

**N. 26** It is disputed to what extent Art. 34 para. 2 FC also constrains private campaign actors (companies, associations). Müller/Schefer (Grundrechte in der Schweiz, 4th ed. 2008, pp. 625 ff.) argue that private influence on the freedom to vote does not fundamentally impair the constitutional openness of the process; the limit lies at the «use of reprehensible means» (financial overwhelming, deception). Hangartner/Kley (Die demokratischen Rechte in Bund und Kantonen, 2000, N. 2633), by contrast, emphasise a stronger indirect horizontal effect to the detriment of large private financial power in referendum campaigns. The Federal Supreme Court has thus far left open whether excessive use of resources by private parties alone violates Art. 34 para. 2 FC; the standard is the requirement of a fair process of debate (**BGE 135 I 292** cons. 2 pp. 293 f.).

#### 5.2 Cantonal Interventions in Federal Votes

**N. 27** Whether cantonal governments are in principle entitled to intervene in federal referendum campaigns was long disputed. Widmer (Wahl- und Abstimmungsfreiheit, 1989, pp. 191 f.) and Corti (RDAT 1992 II pp. 365 f.) denied this categorically; Auer (Staatsrecht der schweizerischen Kantone, 2016, N. 1210), Besson (Behördliche Information vor Volksabstimmungen, 2003, pp. 342 ff.), Müller/Schefer (op. cit., pp. 629 f.) and Tschannen (Staatsrecht, 2016, p. 689) permit intervention where there is particular concern. The Federal Supreme Court followed the latter view in **BGE 143 I 78** cons. 4.6 p. 85; in **BGE 145 I 1** cons. 6.5.2 pp. 18 f., it extended the permissibility also to cases where all or several cantons are significantly affected, without requiring relative particular concern. Langer (ZBl 118/2017 pp. 183 ff.) and Christoph Auer (ZBl 118/2017 pp. 227 ff.) advocate a further relaxation: objective, proportionate, and transparent positions taken by cantonal governments should be permissible without any requirement of particular concern.

#### 5.3 Judicial Reviewability of Federal Council Voting Explanations

**N. 28** Art. 189 para. 4 FC excludes Federal Council acts from challenge before the Federal Supreme Court. Whether official voting explanations issued by the Confederation are nonetheless indirectly subject to judicial review is doctrinally contested. Steinmann (BSK BGG, 2nd ed. 2011, Art. 82 N. 95), Tschannen (Staatsrecht, § 48 N. 41), and Rhinow/Koller/Kiss/Thurnherr/Brühl-Moser (Öffentliches Prozessrecht, 2nd ed. 2010, N. 1898) consider the explanations to be non-challengeable. **BGE 138 I 61** cons. 7.4 pp. 86 f. developed a middle position: while the explanations as such are not challengeable, the *state of information in the run-up to* a vote may be the subject of a suffrage complaint, by means of which the constitutionality of the explanations is reviewed indirectly.

#### 5.4 Equality of Electoral Rights in Proportional Elections

**N. 29** Art. 34 FC in conjunction with Art. 8 para. 1 FC gives rise, for cantonal proportional elections, to the requirement of equality of electoral rights (equality of the counting value and of the success value of votes). It remains controversial how narrowly the tolerance thresholds for electoral district sizes are to be drawn. Following **BGE 131 I 74**, the Federal Supreme Court developed detailed case law on minimum sizes for proportional electoral districts, which is assessed in the literature as either too strict (**BGE 140 I 394** cons. 3.4) or as a necessary democratic safeguard (Hangartner/Kley, op. cit., N. 2568 ff.).

### 6. Practical Notes

**N. 30** **Suffrage complaint (Art. 82 let. c BGG):** Complaints concerning violations of political rights in federal votes must be filed with the cantonal government within three days of the discovery of the grounds for complaint (Art. 77 para. 2 BPR), and thereafter within five days before the Federal Supreme Court (Art. 100 para. 3 let. b BGG). The time limit is a preclusive deadline. Defects in the preparation of votes must be «raised immediately and before the ballot is held» (**BGE 147 I 194** cons. 3 p. 199).

**N. 31** **Requirements for voting documentation:** Authorities must provide objective information in voting explanations and may include evaluative statements provided these are «objectively tenable». Explanations that are one-sided or that suppress elements important for the formation of opinion constitute a challengeable violation of Art. 34 para. 2 FC, provided that «the irregularity could have materially influenced the outcome of the vote» (**BGE 130 I 290** cons. 3.4 pp. 295 f.). Information gaps regarding consequences that cannot be estimated may also violate the freedom to vote (**BGE 138 I 61** cons. 8.6 p. 93).

**N. 32** **Cantonal electoral systems:** Cantonal proportional electoral systems must necessarily be measured against Art. 34 FC. Electoral districts that systematically distort the success value of votes of different parties are unconstitutional. The Federal Supreme Court measures corresponding cantonal constitutional provisions against the further developed constitutional case law in concrete cases, even where the cantonal constitution has been guaranteed by the Federal Assembly (**BGE 140 I 394** cons. 3.4 p. 399).

**N. 33** **Official interventions:** Authorities that become active in a referendum campaign must examine: (1) whether they have competence for the proposal (their own or another public body), (2) whether a particular concern exists, and (3) whether the requirements of objectivity, proportionality, and transparency are met (**BGE 145 I 1** cons. 5.2.1 pp. 9 f.; **BGE 143 I 78** cons. 4.4 pp. 82 f.). Interventions by specialist directors' conferences in the run-up to federal votes are generally impermissible (**BGE 145 I 1** cons. 6.5.2 p. 19).

**N. 34** **Subsequent judicial protection:** Massive irregularities discovered only after the fact may be made the subject of a revision-like procedure pursuant to Art. 29 para. 1 in conjunction with Art. 29a FC. The threshold is high; legal certainty and the protection of legitimate expectations may militate against annulment even where a violation has been established (**BGE 138 I 61** cons. 8.7 pp. 94 ff.).
