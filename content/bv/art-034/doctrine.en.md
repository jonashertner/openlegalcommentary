# Doctrine

## 1. Legislative History

**N. 1** Art. 34 Cst. was incorporated as a central guarantee of political rights during the total revision of the Federal Constitution in 1999. The provision consolidates previously scattered guarantees and codifies the Federal Supreme Court's case law on political rights (BBl 1997 I 1, 163f.). With Art. 34 Cst., the constitutional legislator sought to create a comprehensive guarantee that protects both the exercise of political rights and their substantive content.

**N. 2** The formulation of para. 2 is based on the established case law of the Federal Supreme Court, which had developed free will formation and unvitiated voting as central elements of political rights. The Federal Council's message emphasises that this guarantee is the «cornerstone of the democratic order» and ensures the credibility of democratic decisions (BBl 1997 I 163).

## 2. Systematic Classification

**N. 3** Art. 34 Cst. is systematically placed in Title 2 on fundamental rights, civil rights and social objectives. The norm stands at the transition between classical fundamental rights (Art. 7-33 Cst.) and civil rights (Art. 37-40 Cst.). This intermediate position reflects the dual character of political rights as individual freedom rights and as institutional guarantees of the democratic order.

**N. 4** The provision is closely related to:
- → Art. 136 Cst. (political rights at federal level)
- → Art. 39 Cst. (exercise of political rights)
- → Art. 51 Cst. (cantonal constitutions)
- ↔ Art. 8 Cst. (equality before the law, particularly in elections)
- → Art. 29 Cst. (general procedural guarantees)

**N. 5** At the international level, Art. 34 Cst. corresponds to Art. 25 ICCPR and Art. 3 of the 1st Additional Protocol ECHR. These international law guarantees must be considered in interpretation, but go less far than the Swiss constitutional guarantee (Müller/Schefer, Grundrechte, 4th ed. 2008, p. 659).

## 3. Elements of the Offence / Normative Content

### 3.1 Guarantee of Political Rights (Para. 1)

**N. 6** The «political rights» comprise according to prevailing doctrine (Tschannen, St. Galler Kommentar BV, Art. 34 N. 5; Steinmann, BSK BV, Art. 34 N. 8):
- the active and passive right to vote
- the right to vote on matters of substance
- the right of initiative and referendum
- the right to participate in assemblies

**N. 7** The guarantee is to be understood comprehensively. It protects not only formal exercise, but also the material prerequisites for effective participation in the democratic process (Häfelin/Haller/Keller/Thurnherr, Bundesstaatsrecht, 10th ed. 2020, N. 798). Whether a right to information also exists is controversially discussed (in favour: Schefer; against: Biaggini).

### 3.2 Free Will Formation (Para. 2)

**N. 8** Free will formation requires that those entitled to vote can make their decision without inadmissible influence. The Federal Supreme Court has specified that the authorities are obligated to provide objective and balanced information (**BGE 143 I 78 E. 3.2**). Private actors are not subject to comparable restrictions, insofar as they do not employ unfair means.

**N. 9** The scope of official neutrality obligations is disputed. While Rhinow/Schefer (Verfassungsrecht, 3rd ed. 2016, N. 3104) demand strict restraint, more recent doctrine (Tschannen, St. Galler Kommentar BV, Art. 34 N. 18) permits official statements when a special public interest exists.

### 3.3 Unvitiated Voting (Para. 2)

**N. 10** Unvitiated voting guarantees that the expressed will is correctly recorded and implemented. According to consistent case law (**BGE 135 I 19 E. 3.1**), this includes:
- correct counting of votes
- mathematically correct seat allocation in elections
- faithful implementation of popular decisions

**N. 11** Doctrine is divided on the scope of recount obligations for close results. Waldmann (BSK BV, Art. 34 N. 25) favours automatic recounting for differences under 0.5%, while the Federal Supreme Court recognises no general obligation (**BGE 141 II 297**).

## 4. Legal Consequences

**N. 12** Violation of Art. 34 Cst. can entail various legal consequences:
- Invalidity of elections or votes
- Repetition of the poll
- Adaptation of procedural provisions
- State liability for culpable violation

**N. 13** Restraint is required when declaring popular initiatives invalid. Partial invalidity only comes into question if the remaining part corresponds to the initiators' will (**BGE 134 I 172 E. 4.3**). This restrictive practice is criticised by parts of doctrine (Hangartner/Kley, Demokratische Rechte, 2000, N. 2154).

## 5. Controversial Issues

**N. 14** **Official intervention in voting campaigns**: The admissibility of official statements is hotly disputed. Biaggini (Kommentar BV, Art. 34 N. 15) takes a liberal position and permits interventions for justified interests. In contrast, Müller/Schefer (Grundrechte, p. 668) demand strict restraint. The Federal Supreme Court has taken a middle path (**BGE 143 I 78**).

**N. 15** **E-Voting**: The introduction of electronic voting raises new questions. While Serdült (in: Glaser/Langer, E-Voting, 2012, pp. 45ff.) emphasises the opportunities, critics like Braun Binder (AJP 2021, 456) warn of security risks. Prevailing doctrine demands that e-voting systems must guarantee at least the same security level as postal voting.

**N. 16** **Electoral district geometry**: The permissible deviations in electoral district division are controversial. Garrone (BSK BV, Art. 34 N. 31) accepts deviations up to 10% as a «natural quorum». Poledna (Wahlrechtsgrundsätze, 1988, p. 234) demands strict mathematical equality instead. The Federal Supreme Court tolerates larger deviations only for compelling factual reasons (**BGE 129 I 185 E. 7**).

## 6. Practice Guidelines

**N. 17** When contesting elections and votes, the short complaint deadlines must be observed (generally 3 days after discovery of grounds for complaint, Art. 77 para. 2 LPA). Defects in preparation must be objected to before the poll (**BGE 147 I 194 E. 2.4**).

**N. 18** For practice, the distinction is significant between:
- Violations that lead to annulment (e.g. erroneous vote counting)
- Violations without impact on the result (e.g. isolated formal errors)
- Curable defects (e.g. subsequent correction of counting errors)

**N. 19** For close results, preventive retention of ballot papers beyond the statutory period is recommended. Cantonal retention periods vary between 30 days and 6 months. A precautionary recount can avoid later proceedings, but is not legally enforceable (**BGE 141 II 297 E. 4**).
