# Art. 34 FC — Political Rights

## Doctrine

### 1. Legislative History

**N. 1** Art. 34 FC codifies an unwritten federal constitutional right that the Federal Supreme Court had already developed under the former Federal Constitution of 1874 from Art. 4 aFC and recognised as freedom of election and voting (*Wahl- und Abstimmungsfreiheit*) (**BGE 121 I 138** E. 3). With the total revision of the Federal Constitution of 1999, this judge-made guarantee was expressly incorporated into the Constitution. The Federal Council's dispatch on the new Federal Constitution of 20 November 1996 (BBl 1997 I 189 ff.) describes Art. 34 FC as a codification of the unwritten constitutional law recognised in case law, and emphasises that para. 2 includes in particular the obligation of the authorities to provide correct and restrained information in advance of votes.

**N. 2** Para. 1 takes over the function of the former voting rights complaint (*Stimmrechtsbeschwerde*) under Art. 85 lit. a OJ. Para. 2 gives the protective content of freedom of voting an explicit constitutional basis in the form of a dual structure (free formation of opinion / unfalsiified expression of vote). The parliamentary deliberations did not substantially modify the Federal Council's draft; the wording of para. 2 essentially corresponds to the formula used in Federal Supreme Court practice since the 1970s (**BGE 138 I 61** E. 3.1).

---

### 2. Systematic Classification

**N. 3** Art. 34 FC belongs to the second chapter of the second title of the Federal Constitution (Fundamental Rights, Art. 7–36 FC). The provision is an **institutional fundamental rights guarantee**: it does not primarily protect individual exercise of freedom, but rather safeguards the integrity of the democratic decision-making process as a precondition for legitimate direct democracy. In this function, Art. 34 FC is to be distinguished from other rights of political participation, which are governed in the third title (Art. 136–142 FC).

**N. 4** The material scope of Art. 34 para. 1 FC extends to all cantonal and communal political rights, insofar as these are conferred by cantonal law (→ Art. 39 para. 1 FC). At the federal level, political rights are primarily governed by the Federal Act on Political Rights (APR, SR 161.1). Art. 34 FC serves as the constitutional standard for the design and application of these statutory provisions.

**N. 5** Restrictions on political rights are governed by Art. 36 FC: they require a statutory basis, a public interest or the protection of a fundamental right of a third party, and proportionality. The core content (→ Art. 36 para. 4 FC) is absolutely protected; it consists in the right that the democratic decision reflects the free and unfalsified will of the voters (**BGE 135 I 19** E. 2). ↔ Art. 8 FC (equality before the law in electoral law), ↔ Art. 29 FC (procedural guarantees in voting law), → Art. 136 ff. FC (design of federal popular rights).

### 3. Normative Content

#### 3.1 Para. 1: Guarantee of Political Rights

**N. 6** Art. 34 para. 1 FC guarantees political rights as such. This guarantee covers all rights relating to political participation in democratic decisions: active and passive voting rights, rights of initiative and referendum, and the right to a voting rights complaint. Insofar as cantonal law confers political rights, Art. 34 para. 1 FC also protects their proper exercise (**BGE 130 I 290** E. 3.1). A violation occurs when an election or vote is conducted in such a way that the democratic process of forming the will of the people cannot proceed freely and without falsification.

**N. 7** Art. 34 para. 1 FC, in conjunction with Art. 29a FC, also establishes a right to judicial review in voting rights matters. The 2007 judicial reform transferred ultimate judicial protection in federal voting rights matters to the Federal Supreme Court (Art. 189 para. 1 lit. f FC; Art. 82 lit. c SCA). For cantonal and communal voting rights matters, the Federal Supreme Court already had jurisdiction beforehand (**BGE 138 I 61** E. 3.2). In contrast to cantonal votes, acts of the Federal Council such as validation decisions (*Erwahrungsbeschlüsse*) and explanatory booklets (*Abstimmungserläuterungen*) are subject to only limited judicial challenge under Art. 189 para. 4 FC; the Federal Supreme Court's review takes place within the framework of the overall information situation from the perspective of freedom of voting (**BGE 138 I 61** E. 7.4).

#### 3.2 Para. 2: Free Formation of Opinion and Unfalsified Expression of Vote

**N. 8** Art. 34 para. 2 FC contains the substantive core of the guarantee. The provision protects two complementary elements: first, the **free formation of opinion** — the process in which voters form their political views on the basis of accurate and balanced information —, and second, the **unfalsiified expression of vote** — the correspondence between the will formed and its expression in the vote result. The Federal Supreme Court encapsulates this guarantee in a guiding formula: no voting or election result may be accepted that does not reliably and without falsification reflect the free will of the voters (**BGE 129 I 185** E. 7.2; **BGE 135 I 19** E. 2; **BGE 135 I 292** E. 2).

**N. 9** **Duty of the authorities to provide information**: Art. 34 para. 2 FC gives rise to an obligation on the authorities to provide correct and restrained information in advance of votes (**BGE 130 I 290** E. 3.2; **BGE 143 I 78** E. 4.4). Official explanatory booklets are permissible and are not in principle required to be neutral — a voting recommendation is permitted — but they are required to be objective. The authorities violate freedom of voting if they provide incorrect information about the purpose and scope of a proposal, suppress elements that are material to the decision, or misrepresent the arguments of the opposing side (**BGE 138 I 61** E. 6.2; **BGE 130 I 290** E. 3.2). The requirement of impartiality (*Sachlichkeit*) demands that explanatory booklets be well-balanced, present a comprehensive picture of the proposal with its advantages and disadvantages, and enable voters to make a well-founded assessment.

**N. 10** **Official intervention in voting campaigns**: In relation to their own proposals, authorities have a limited advisory function; they may explain and recommend the decision of the voters, but remain bound by the requirement of objectivity. Whether authorities may intervene in votes concerning proposals not originating from them depends on the existence of an immediate and special interest in the outcome of the vote (**BGE 143 I 78** E. 4.3–4.6). Cantonal interventions in federal votes are permissible only where the canton is particularly affected compared to the other cantons; in such cases the canton may take a position, but is bound by the requirement of objectivity, without being required to set out all arguments for and against a proposal (**BGE 143 I 78** E. 6.2).

**N. 11** **Protection against private influence**: Freedom of voting also protects against impermissible influence by private parties. Such influence exists where intervention in the voting campaign takes place at a very late stage with manifestly false and misleading statements, making it factually impossible for voters to form a correct opinion (**BGE 135 I 292** E. 4.1). The threshold for a violation is high: in view of freedom of expression, the annulment of a vote comes into consideration only in the case of very serious infringements.

**N. 12** **Unfalsiified expression of vote in electoral law**: In the area of proportional representation elections, Art. 34 para. 2 FC protects equality of voting power (*Stimmkraftgleichheit*). Electoral districts must be designed in such a way that the will of the electorate is reflected in the election result as undistorted as possible (**BGE 129 I 185** E. 7.2). The free mandate of elected representatives, by contrast, does not fall within the scope of protection: a candidate who changes party after the election does not violate the freedom of voting of their voters, because the voters elected the person and not the party affiliation (**BGE 135 I 19** E. 3).

### 4. Legal Consequences

**N. 13** A violation of Art. 34 FC leads to the **annulment** of the affected election or vote if the irregularities established are material and could have influenced the outcome. It is not necessary to prove that the defect actually influenced the result; it is sufficient that such an effect lies within the realm of possibility (**BGE 135 I 292** E. 4.4; **BGE 143 I 78** E. 7.1). In assessing causality, the Federal Supreme Court takes into account in particular the gravity of the defect, its significance in the context of the vote, and the margin of the result.

**N. 14** Where violations are established but are not relevant to the outcome, the Federal Supreme Court may declare the violation in the reasoning without annulling the vote. A formal declaration in the operative part (*Dispositiv*) — with the character of an admonition — is particularly appropriate where it is combined with a call to establish a constitutionally compliant state of affairs for a future ballot, for example in the case of systematically unconstitutional electoral systems (**BGE 138 I 61** E. 8.7; **BGE 129 I 185** Dispositiv).

**N. 15** **Subsequent judicial review**: Where serious defects in the voting procedure only become known after expiry of the time limits for complaint under Art. 77 para. 2 APR, a right to review the regularity of the vote may arise directly from Art. 29 para. 1 in conjunction with Art. 29a FC and from Art. 34 para. 1 FC. The Federal Supreme Court has jurisdiction to grant subsequent judicial review in such cases (**BGE 138 I 61** E. 4.3–4.4). A precondition is the existence of material facts that only became known subsequently and that render the voting procedure irregular; mere subsequent deviations from forecasts are not in themselves sufficient (**BGE 138 I 61** E. 8.4).

### 5. Contested Issues

**N. 16** **Limits of direct democracy**: The most significant tension within Art. 34 FC lies between the guarantee of the broadest possible popular rights and the rule-of-law constraints that limit their content. In **BGE 129 I 232**, the Federal Supreme Court declared invalid an initiative calling for a popular vote on naturalisation applications, because a democratically legitimised popular vote is structurally incapable of complying with the constitutional duty to state reasons (→ Art. 29 para. 2 FC) and the protection of the privacy of applicants (→ Art. 13 FC). This decision triggered an intense academic debate about the limits of direct-democratic procedures: Auer/von Arx (AJP 2000, 923 ff.) and Kiener (recht 18/2000, 213 ff.) welcomed the outcome and saw in the combination of the principle of democracy and the rule-of-law principle a mandatory ground for excluding individual administrative referendums. Hangartner (AJP 2001, 949 ff.) argued, by contrast, that a subsequent official statement of reasons for the ballot decision could be sufficient, and thus rejected total inadmissibility. The Federal Supreme Court followed the stricter view (E. 3.5–3.7).

**N. 17** **Official information activities — scope and limits**: The extent to which authorities may intervene in voting campaigns in relation to proposals not originating from them is disputed. Widmer (Wahl- und Abstimmungsfreiheit, 1989, p. 191 f.) and Corti (RDAT 1992 II, 365 f.) denied any cantonal intervention in federal voting campaigns. A more recent academic view — represented by Müller/Schefer (Grundrechte in der Schweiz, 4th ed. 2008, p. 629 f.) and Tschannen (Staatsrecht, 4th ed. 2016, p. 689) — permits cantonal interventions where special affectedness is demonstrated. Hangartner (AJP 1996, 272) took the most far-reaching position: cantons are entitled to issue voting recommendations even without special affectedness, provided these reflect the opinion of a clear parliamentary majority. The Federal Supreme Court followed the criterion of special affectedness in **BGE 143 I 78** E. 4.6.

**N. 18** **Subsequent judicial review of federal votes**: Before the entry into force of the 2007 judicial reform, it was widely accepted that the Federal Council's explanatory booklets could not be challenged (Art. 189 para. 4 FC). The more recent academic literature — Haller (in: BSK BV, Art. 189 N. 60), Tschannen (Staatsrecht, § 48 N. 41), Steinmann (BSK BGG, Art. 82 N. 95), Seiler (ZSR 129/2010 II, 396) — criticised this gap in legal protection, but could rely on Art. 189 para. 4 FC, which excludes acts of the Federal Council from challenge before the Federal Supreme Court. The Federal Supreme Court opened an indirect avenue of review in **BGE 138 I 61** E. 7.4: it is not the explanatory booklets themselves that are challenged, but rather the overall information situation that is examined from the perspective of freedom of voting.

**N. 19** **Materiality of defects**: Whether official misinformation is sufficiently material to justify the annulment of a vote is assessed by the Federal Supreme Court with full cognition based on the overall circumstances. Rhinow/Schefer/Uebersax (Schweizerisches Verfassungsrecht, 3rd ed. 2016, N. 1862 ff.) and Häfelin/Haller/Keller/Thurnherr (Schweizerisches Bundesstaatsrecht, 10th ed. 2020, N. 1394) emphasise that the Federal Supreme Court should exercise restraint where the voting result is very clear, while in the case of narrow results even less serious defects may lead to annulment. Besson (Behördliche Information vor Volksabstimmungen, 2003, p. 182 ff.) advocates stricter requirements regarding the quality of official information, in particular for complex financial proposals.

### 6. Practical Notes

**N. 20** **Avenue of complaint**: Violations of Art. 34 FC in cantonal and communal matters may be asserted by means of a voting rights complaint (*Stimmrechtsbeschwerde*) under Art. 82 lit. c SCA directly before the Federal Supreme Court; the ordinary cantonal appellate process must be exhausted pursuant to Art. 88 para. 2 SCA. In federal votes, a voting complaint (*Abstimmungsbeschwerde*) must be filed with the cantonal government pursuant to Art. 77 para. 1 lit. b and para. 2 APR within three days of discovering the ground of complaint, and at the latest on the third day after publication of the cantonal result in the cantonal official gazette (→ Art. 77 APR).

**N. 21** **Duty to raise objections in advance**: Defects in the voting procedure must in principle be raised immediately and before the ballot is held, insofar as they are recognisable. A person who is aware of a defect before the vote and does not file a complaint cannot without further ado raise the objection after the ballot (**BGE 147 I 194** E. 2). Only defects that become known exclusively after the fact justify an application for subsequent review under the principles developed in **BGE 138 I 61** E. 4.3–4.5.

**N. 22** **Relevant perspective**: When assessing official explanatory booklets, the Federal Supreme Court applies the standard of the impression created in the averagely attentive and politically interested voter (**BGE 130 I 290** E. 4.11). Not every legal imprecision or political exaggeration violates freedom of voting; only serious instances of misinformation that could have materially influenced the voting result are relevant.

**N. 23** **Relationship to other fundamental rights**: When designing voting procedures that affect third parties, a balancing exercise with conflicting fundamental rights must be carried out. In particular, → Art. 13 FC (privacy) and → Art. 29 para. 2 FC (right to be heard) limit the scope of direct-democratic forms of decision-making where these govern individual and specific legal relationships (**BGE 129 I 232** E. 4.4). The principle of democracy does not justify such rule-of-law deficits (**BGE 129 I 232** E. 5).
