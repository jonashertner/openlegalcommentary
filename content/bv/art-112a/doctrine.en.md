# Art. 112a FC — Supplementary Benefits

## Doctrine

### 1. Legislative History

**N. 1** Art. 112a FC was adopted by popular vote on 28 November 2004 and entered into force on 1 January 2008. The provision transferred the former transitional provision of Art. 196 para. 10 FC — which itself derived from Art. 112 para. 6 of the former FC — into the ordinary constitutional law. This transition marks a conceptual systemic shift: what had been a provisional subsidy basis became a permanent constitutional mandate.

**N. 2** The driving force behind the constitutional amendment was the reform of fiscal equalisation and the division of tasks between the Confederation and the cantons (NFA). Under the regime in force until the end of 2007, the Confederation paid contributions to the cantons towards the financing of supplementary benefits (SB) to the extent that these were structured in accordance with federal law requirements; the cantons retained considerable discretion and bore their own political responsibility. The Federal Council's dispatch of 14 November 2001 on the NFA (BBl 2001 2291 ff.) established that the Confederation was henceforth to be responsible primarily for SB — i.e. for securing subsistence — while the cantons would assume full responsibility for those areas of SB connected to residential care or health costs (BBl 2001 2436 para. 6.1.5.3.3).

**N. 3** The dispatch of 7 September 2005 on the implementing legislation (NFA; BBl 2005 6029 ff.) specified the division of tasks: coverage of general subsistence needs was to be borne five-eighths by the Confederation and three-eighths by the cantons; reimbursement of the costs of illness and disability was to fall entirely on the cantons (BBl 2005 6223 para. 2.9.8.1.6). Furthermore, the NFA revision aimed at extensive harmonisation: the hitherto significant cantonal special arrangements were to be substantially curtailed (BBl 2005 6228). At the same time, the Federal Council expressly stated that the character of SB would «not be changed compared to the existing regime» and that no social assistance entitlements were to be created (BBl 2001 2437).

**N. 4** Prior to the entry into force of Art. 112a FC, Art. 112 para. 6 of the former version of the FC and Art. 196 para. 10 FC contained no express obligation on the part of the Confederation *and* the cantons to provide benefits jointly. The Federal Office of Justice clarified in 2003 that the then draft Art. 112a FC contained no federal competence to oblige the cantons to introduce supplementary benefits for families — the new provision was confined to SB in the OASI/DI sphere (JAAC 67.37 para. I.4). This finding confirms the provision's close substantive link to old-age, survivors' and invalidity insurance (→ Art. 111 FC; ↔ Art. 112 FC).

---

### 2. Systematic Classification

**N. 5** Art. 112a FC forms part of the 3rd Section of Chapter 3 of the Federal Constitution («Social insurance», Art. 111–117 FC). The provision stands in immediate functional connection with Art. 112 FC, which governs the foundations of OASI/DI: Art. 112 para. 2 lit. b FC requires that OASI/DI pensions «adequately cover» subsistence needs. Art. 112a FC comes into play precisely where Art. 112 para. 2 lit. b FC falls short of its objective — when insurance benefits do not fully cover subsistence needs (↔ Art. 112 FC). The two provisions together form a complementary regulatory system for securing subsistence in old age and in cases of invalidity.

**N. 6** In constitutional law terms, Art. 112a FC is a **competence provision with a duty to provide benefits**: para. 1 establishes a joint obligation on the Confederation and the cantons to provide benefits; para. 2 contains a legislative competence of the Confederation to regulate the scope as well as the tasks and responsibilities. The provision is not a fundamental rights norm and does not establish direct individual subjective rights for the persons concerned; individuals may only assert their claims on the basis of the implementing Federal Act — the Federal Act of 6 October 2006 on Supplementary Benefits to OASI/DI (SBA, SR 831.30) — (Judgment 9C_822/2009 of 7 May 2010, consid. 3.6; → Art. 36 FC does not apply directly).

**N. 7** The provision must also be read in the context of Art. 41 FC: the social objectives of Art. 41 FC (in particular Art. 41 para. 1 lit. c FC concerning health and old-age provision, and Art. 41 para. 1 lit. d FC concerning persons unable to earn their living) define the policy framework, without being justiciable in themselves. Art. 112a FC goes beyond this programmatic content: it directly obliges the Confederation and the cantons to pay SB and gives the legislature a mandate requiring implementation (→ Art. 41 FC). The connection to Art. 12 FC (right to emergency relief) is systematically subordinate: Art. 12 FC protects the absolute minimum standard of living as a fundamental right, while Art. 112a FC uses a «subsistence need» defined in social insurance law as its benchmark — one that goes beyond that absolute minimum.

**N. 8** At the level of implementing law, the SBA (SR 831.30) constitutes the central concretisation. It is supplemented by the Ordinance on Supplementary Benefits to OASI/DI (SBO, SR 831.301). The SB Reform of 22 March 2019 (BBl 2016 7465), in force since 1 January 2021, substantially revised the SBA (notably income limits for entitlement and new rules on the depletion of assets), without altering the constitutional framework (→ Art. 190 FC).

### 3. Elements of the Provision / Normative Content

**N. 9** **Para. 1: Joint duty to provide benefits.** The provision is addressed to «the Confederation and the cantons» as jointly obligated providers. The wording deliberately differs from purely federal competence provisions: the Confederation and the cantons are *jointly* obliged to pay SB, even though the detailed allocation of tasks is reserved to legislation. This shared provision is reflected in the financing rule of Art. 13 SBA (5/8 Confederation, 3/8 cantons for annual SB; costs of illness and disability borne entirely by the cantons).

**N. 10** **Beneficiaries.** Entitled persons are those «whose subsistence needs are not covered by the benefits provided by the old-age, survivors' and invalidity insurance». Three elements are cumulative:

- *Connection to OASI/DI*: The person must be receiving (or be entitled to) OASI or DI benefits. The constitutional provision is confined to the OASI/DI sphere; an extension to supplementary benefits for families is not covered by Art. 112a FC (JAAC 67.37 para. I.4).
- *Subsistence need*: The concept of «subsistence need» is an indeterminate legal concept whose concretisation is the task of the legislature. According to Art. 10 SBA it encompasses amounts for general living costs, rent, premiums of compulsory health insurance and other recognised expenditure. The Federal Supreme Court has clarified that the «recognised expenditure» simultaneously defines the subsistence minimum that SB are intended to secure (Social Insurance Court of Zurich ZL.2024.00023 of 27 March 2025, with reference to Art. 2 para. 1 SBA).
- *Non-coverage*: A complete or partial gap between the subsistence need and the OASI/DI benefits suffices. SB are accessory to the insurance benefits and presuppose their priority (**BGE 139 V 358**, consid. 2.1).

**N. 11** **Para. 2: Legislative competence and reservation of statutory regulation.** Para. 2 obliges the legislature to regulate «the scope of supplementary benefits and the tasks and responsibilities of the Confederation and the cantons». Two consequences follow: First, no direct claim to a specific amount of SB can be derived from para. 1 alone — the scope of benefits is governed by the SBA (Judgment 9C_822/2009, consid. 3.6). Second, the delegation provision allows the legislature to leave the cantons independent room for manoeuvre, provided the constitutional core mandate is preserved.

---

### 4. Legal Consequences

**N. 12** **No direct justiciability.** Art. 112a FC does not establish directly justiciable individual subjective rights. Individuals may only assert their claims on the basis of the SBA and the FSSIA (SR 830.1). The Federal Supreme Court has consistently held: «No direct entitlement can be derived from Art. 112a FC, since the constitutional provision expressly states that the law determines the scope of supplementary benefits» (Judgment 9C_822/2009, consid. 3.6).

**N. 13** **Interpretive directive for legislation and application of law.** As a constitutional provision, Art. 112a FC provides an interpretive directive for the SBA and the SBO. When interpreting provisions of ordinary statute law, the constitutional mandate to secure subsistence must be taken into account. Thus, in **BGE 139 V 358**, consid. 5.1, the Federal Supreme Court expressly relied on the «constitutional right to subsistence security» in order to limit the scope of cantonal discretion in defining residential care facilities.

**N. 14** **Constraint on cantonal law.** Cantonal implementing law under the SBA must be compatible with Art. 112a FC. The Cantonal Court of Lucerne, in a landmark decision, declared a cantonal daily rate regulation inapplicable through concrete judicial review, because it systematically drove nursing home residents into dependence on social assistance and thereby violated federal law and the constitutional mandate to secure subsistence (Cantonal Court of Lucerne 5V 18 163 of 15 January 2020). This decision defines the limits of cantonal discretion in setting residential care tariffs under Art. 10 para. 2 lit. a SBA.

**N. 15** **Interaction with Art. 190 FC.** Art. 190 FC prohibits the Federal Supreme Court from reviewing Federal Acts for constitutionality and declining to apply them. In **BGE 143 V 9**, consid. 6.2, the Federal Supreme Court held that a statutory provision that contains no safeguard against social assistance dependence for residents of non-nursing-care facilities «must be accepted under the Constitution», because Art. 190 FC requires the application of the SBA (→ Art. 190 FC). This case law marks the point at which the constitutional interpretive directive yields to the express intent of the legislature.

### 5. Contested Issues

**N. 16** **Contested issue 1: Direct justiciability of Art. 112a para. 1 FC.** A minority view in legal scholarship holds that Art. 112a para. 1 FC has a minimum content that is directly enforceable before the courts once the legislature evidently fails to fulfil the constitutional core mandate. Rhinow/Schefer/Uebersax, Schweizerisches Verfassungsrecht, 3rd ed. 2016, N 3012 ff., identify in the SB provisions an «institutional guarantee» whose essential content may not be legislatively diminished. The predominant view in scholarship — represented in particular by Häfelin/Haller/Keller/Thurnherr, Schweizerisches Bundesstaatsrecht, 10th ed. 2020, N 2820 — and the consistent case law of the Federal Supreme Court treat Art. 112a FC as a purely legislative mandate norm without its own subjective-rights dimension. The Federal Supreme Court expressly left open in **BGE 143 V 9**, consid. 6.2, whether the constitutional legislator understood the objective of Art. 112a para. 1 FC to mean «that persons who are in principle entitled should not have to claim social assistance», and instead relied on Art. 190 FC.

**N. 17** **Contested issue 2: Scope of the prohibition on social assistance dependence in residential care costs.** The most important operative contested issue concerns whether Art. 112a FC constitutionally obliges the cantons to prevent social assistance dependence for all SB recipients in residential care. The Federal Supreme Court held in **BGE 143 V 9**, consid. 6.1, that Art. 10 para. 2 lit. a SBA obliges cantons only with respect to *recognised nursing homes* within the meaning of Art. 39 para. 3 HIA, not other institutions. The Cantonal Court of Lucerne (5V 18 163 of 15 January 2020) shifted this boundary and conducted concrete judicial review in cases where cantonal tariffs systematically produce social assistance dependence in nursing homes. The question of whether the constitutional mandate to secure subsistence under Art. 112a FC requires, beyond Art. 10 para. 2 lit. a SBA, a broad prohibition on social assistance dependence in *all* forms of residential care has not been definitively resolved at the highest judicial level. Kessler, BSK BV, Art. 112a N. 18, takes the view that Art. 112a FC contains a minimum guarantee that fundamentally precludes social assistance dependence as a consequence of inadequate SB financing; the opposing view relies on the parliamentary rejection of the corresponding minority proposal in the NFA debate (AB 2006 N 1248 f.) as well as on Art. 190 FC.

**N. 18** **Contested issue 3: Scope of the class of beneficiaries (family SB).** The Federal Office of Justice established in 2003 as a binding finding that Art. 112a FC contains no federal competence to oblige the cantons to introduce supplementary benefits for families (JAAC 67.37 para. I.4). This finding is generally accepted in the literature. Vallender/Wiederkehr, St. Galler Kommentar zur BV (SGK BV), Art. 112a N. 11 ff., note that the OASI/DI accessory character of the provision is constitutive and that an extensive teleological interpretation seeking to subsume family SB under Art. 112a FC would exceed the wording. The Confederation may, however, regulate family SB under Art. 116 para. 2 FC (JAAC 67.37 para. II.3).

**N. 19** **Contested issue 4: Relationship between Art. 112a FC and the 2019/2021 SB Reform.** The SB Reform 2019 (BBl 2016 7465) introduced an income limit (Art. 9a SBA) and stricter rules on the depletion of assets. Scholarship is divided on whether these restrictions fall below the constitutional minimum content of the subsistence-securing mandate under Art. 112a para. 1 FC. Müller, Rechtsprechung des Bundesgerichts zum ELG, 3rd ed. 2015, p. 86 f. N 208, had already drawn attention before the reform to the tensions between restrictive statutory eligibility conditions and the constitutional mandate. The Federal Supreme Court has not yet made any fundamental pronouncement on the constitutionality of the 2021 SB Reform; Art. 190 FC limits reviews in this regard.

### 6. Practical Notes

**N. 20** **Assessment of entitlement in individual cases.** The assessment of a SB entitlement is carried out exclusively according to the provisions of the SBA and the SBO. Art. 112a FC must be invoked when interpreting indeterminate legal concepts in the SBA, for example when assessing whether a benefit gap concerns «subsistence needs». For persons living in a residential care facility, the definition of such a facility under Art. 25a para. 1 SBO is decisive: a facility qualifies as a residential care facility if it is recognised as such by a canton or holds a cantonal operating licence (**BGE 139 V 358**, consid. 4.5).

**N. 21** **Coordination with social assistance.** Art. 112a FC and the SBA aim, where possible, to prevent dependence on social assistance (BBl 2005 6226 on Art. 2 draft SBA). For stays in recognised nursing homes (Art. 39 para. 3 HIA), cantons are obliged under Art. 10 para. 2 lit. a SBA to set daily rates such that social assistance dependence does not arise «as a rule». For other institutions this statutory obligation does not exist, which may in individual cases lead to the need for social assistance (**BGE 143 V 9**, consid. 6.1–6.2). In such constellations the social assistance authority is competent as the case may be; the parallel receipt of SB and social assistance is not categorically excluded.

**N. 22** **Cantonal judicial review.** Cantonal implementing law that in practice pushes access to SB below the minimum content of Art. 112a FC may be declared inapplicable through concrete judicial review (Cantonal Court of Lucerne 5V 18 163 of 15 January 2020). Lawyers and authorities involved in calculating SB must examine cantonal daily rate regulations to determine whether — particularly in the area of nursing homes within the meaning of Art. 39 para. 3 HIA — they systematically produce social assistance dependence.

**N. 23** **SB Reform 2021: New eligibility limits.** Since 1 January 2021, an income limit has applied to SB entitlement (Art. 9a SBA): persons whose attributable income exceeds a certain threshold no longer have an entitlement to SB, even if their subsistence need is arithmetically not covered. In addition, new rules on the depletion of assets and on the asset threshold have been introduced. These restrictions are of considerable practical significance and reduce the circle of entitled persons compared with the previous law.

**N. 24** **Coordination with invalidity insurance.** Coordination between SB and DI must be observed at several levels: institutions recognised by a canton under the FDIFA (SR 831.26) are also regarded as residential care facilities in SB law (Art. 9 para. 5 lit. h SBA in conjunction with Art. 25a SBO; **BGE 139 V 358**, consid. 4.3). The safeguard against social assistance dependence guaranteed by Art. 7 FDIFA applies, however, only to persons with invalidity and cannot be applied by analogy to persons without invalidity (**BGE 143 V 9**, consid. 6.3).

**N. 25** **Domicile and habitual residence.** Every SB entitlement requires domicile and habitual residence in Switzerland (Art. 4 SBA). The Federal Administrative Court confirmed in C-1393/2016 of 16 September 2016 that this connecting factor also applies to persons receiving foreign OASI pensions. The requirement of habitual residence in Switzerland is not to be derived from Art. 112a FC itself, but follows from the SBA as the relevant implementing enactment.
