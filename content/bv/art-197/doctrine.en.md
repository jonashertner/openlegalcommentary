# Art. 197 FC — Transitional provisions

## Doctrine

### 1. Legislative history

**N. 1** The transitional provisions of Art. 197 FC do not have a uniform legislative history, as they were added successively with each constitutional amendment. The original version of 18 April 1999 contained the transitional provisions for the total revision of the Federal Constitution (BBl 1997 I 1 ff., esp. 627 ff.). The constitutional legislator recognised the need to establish clear rules for the transition from old to new constitutional law in order to avoid legal uncertainty and interpretational problems.

**N. 2** Since then, Art. 197 FC has been extended by new paragraphs with each accepted popular initiative or constitutional amendment. Each transitional provision reflects the specific implementation requirements of the respective substantive constitutional norm. This led to the creation of transitional provisions for the deportation initiative (para. 8, BBl 2009 5097), the second homes initiative (para. 9, BBl 2008 8757), the OECD minimum taxation (para. 15, BBl 2022 2675) and most recently the 13th AHV pension (para. 16, BBl 2023 835).

### 2. Systematic classification

**N. 3** Art. 197 FC occupies a special position in the constitutional structure. The provision belongs to the final provisions (Title 3) and stands outside the substantive constitutional law. As intertemporal law, it regulates the temporal validity of new constitutional norms and creates bridges between old and new legal situations (Häfelin/Haller/Keller/Thurnherr, Schweizerisches Bundesstaatsrecht, 10th ed. 2020, N 144).

**N. 4** The transitional provisions are closely related to → Art. 195 FC (Entry into force), which orders the immediate entry into force of constitutional amendments upon their acceptance by the people and cantons. Art. 197 FC modifies this rule for specific subject areas and creates differentiated transitional regimes. The provision is also connected to → Art. 190 FC, as it raises the question of the extent to which transitional provisions are directly applicable or require legislative concretisation.

### 3. Elements of the offence / Normative content

**N. 5** Art. 197 FC does not contain uniform elements of the offence, but rather a multitude of different transitional regulations. These can be classified into the following categories:

- **Legislative mandates with deadline**: Many paragraphs oblige the legislature to enact implementing provisions within a certain deadline (e.g. para. 8: five years for the deportation initiative; para. 12: two years for the burqa ban).

- **Subsidiary ordinance competence**: In case of default by the legislature, the Federal Council receives competence to enact implementing provisions (para. 9 para. 1 on the second homes initiative; para. 11 para. 2 on the mass immigration initiative).

- **Directly applicable transitional rules**: Individual provisions have immediate legal effects (para. 9 para. 2: nullity of second home permits; para. 16: entitlement to 13th AHV pension).

**N. 6** The interpretation of transitional provisions follows the general rules of constitutional interpretation, with particular importance given to the purpose of the respective main norm. The Federal Court emphasises that transitional provisions must be interpreted in the context of the entire constitutional order and in observance of practical concordance (**BGE 139 I 16** E. 4.2).

### 4. Legal consequences

**N. 7** The legal consequences of transitional provisions vary considerably:

- **Procedural consequences**: Expiry of deadlines triggers duties to act or subsidiary competences (e.g. ordinance competence of the Federal Council according to para. 9 para. 1).

- **Substantive legal consequences**: Direct legal claims (para. 16: 13th AHV pension) or prohibitions (para. 9 para. 2: building permit prohibition).

- **Nullity consequences**: Art. 197 para. 9 para. 2 FC is the only transitional provision that explicitly orders the nullity of administrative acts.

**N. 8** The question of direct applicability must be examined separately for each transitional provision. The Federal Court affirmed the direct applicability of the second homes provision in **BGE 139 II 243**, but denied the direct applicability of the deportation initiative in **BGE 139 I 16**. The determining factors are the definiteness of the norm and compatibility with higher-ranking law.

### 5. Controversies

**N. 9** The central controversy concerns the **direct applicability** of transitional provisions. Epiney (Ausschaffungsinitiative und Freizügigkeitsabkommen, ZGRG 1/2010, p. 6 f.) and Reich (Verletzt die "Ausschaffungsinitiative" zwingende Bestimmungen des Völkerrechts?, ZSR 127/2008 I, p. 514 f.) advocate a restrictive position: transitional provisions that conflict with international law are not directly applicable. Hangartner (Unklarheiten bei Volksinitiativen, AJP 2011, p. 473) advocates for a differentiated approach taking into account practical concordance.

**N. 10** Regarding the **temporal scope of application**, opinions diverge. Waldmann (Die Zweitwohnungsverordnung, Jusletter 10 December 2012, para. 21) took the position regarding the second homes initiative that the transitional provision only becomes effective from the explicitly mentioned date (1 January 2013). The Federal Court contradicted this in **BGE 139 II 243**: the substantive constitutional norm applies from entry into force, the transitional provision only intensifies the legal consequences.

**N. 11** The **hierarchy between transitional provision and main norm** is disputed. Mastronardi (Verfassungslehre, 2007, N 784) sees transitional provisions as lex specialis to the main norm. Tschannen (Staatsrecht der Schweizerischen Eidgenossenschaft, 3rd ed. 2011, § 4 para. 38) emphasises, in contrast, the equal value of all constitutional norms and the necessity of practical concordance.

### 6. Practice notes

**N. 12** When applying transitional provisions, it must first be examined whether the provision itself or the associated main norm is directly applicable. The criteria are: sufficient definiteness, compatibility with higher-ranking law and suitability for immediate application without implementing legislation.

**N. 13** For administrative practice, the following applies: if an implementation deadline expires without the legislature having acted, it must be examined whether subsidiary competences exist. The Federal Council has made use of such competences with the Second Homes Ordinance (SR 702) and the Ordinance on International Minimum Taxation (SR 641.71).

**N. 14** In case of dispute, the case law of the Federal Court regarding the respective transitional provision must be consulted. The courts apply a constitution-conforming approach in interpretation that ensures practical concordance between different constitutional norms and conformity with international law. Private parties can only invoke sufficiently definite and directly applicable transitional provisions.
