## Case Law

### Leading Decisions on Federal Legislative Competence

**BGE 130 I 134** of 5 April 2004  
Federal law violation by the cantonal popular initiative «12 car-free Sundays» in the Canton of Appenzell Ausserrhoden. The cantons, unlike the Confederation, are not empowered to generally restrict motorised traffic in their territory by means of a legal norm. A Sunday driving ban applying in principle to the entire cantonal territory can only be enacted by means of a legal norm, for which the cantons do not have the necessary legislative competence.

> «According to Art. 82 Federal Constitution, the Confederation issues regulations on road traffic (para. 1) and exercises supreme supervision over roads of national importance, whereby it determines which through roads must remain open to traffic. (...) The cantons, unlike the Confederation, are not empowered to generally restrict motorised traffic in their territory by means of a legal norm.»

**BGE 150 II 444** of 15 March 2024  
Requirements for an expert report for the introduction of 30 km/h speed limits on a road section in the city of Bern. The Federal Supreme Court clarified the application of Art. 82 Federal Constitution in relation to the legal provisions on traffic restrictions and speed limits. The distinction between traffic-oriented and non-traffic-oriented roads is decisive for the expert report requirement.

> «The Confederation issues regulations on road traffic (Art. 82 para. 1 Federal Constitution). It exercises supreme supervision over roads of national importance; it may determine which through roads must remain open to traffic (Art. 82 para. 3 Federal Constitution). The Confederation regulates road traffic in the Road Traffic Act of 19 December 1958 (RTA; SR 741.01) and in the ordinances issued based thereon.»

### Supreme Supervision over Roads of National Importance

**BGE 91 I 405** of 31 January 1958  
Admissibility of delegation of legislative authority for through roads. The Federal Supreme Court confirmed the legality of a Solothurn Government Council ordinance on road traffic protection that prohibited the construction of entrances and exits on Class I through roads. Access to a through road outside the designated locations may be prohibited to all road users equally.

> «Access to a through road outside the generally designated locations may be prohibited to the adjacent property owner as well as to all other road users, even if this makes access to their property more difficult.»

### Fee-free Use of Roads

**BGE 122 I 279** of 11 October 1996  
Admissibility of parking fees and scope of fee exemption according to Art. 37 para. 2 former Federal Constitution (now Art. 82 para. 3 Federal Constitution). The Constitution guarantees fee exemption only for traffic within the framework of common use and the intended purpose of public areas. Fees may be charged for parking that is no longer compatible with common use. In urban areas, parking for more than 30 minutes can be considered as fee-based enhanced common use.

> «Art. 37 para. 2 Federal Constitution guarantees fee exemption only for traffic within the framework of common use and the intended purpose of public areas (...). Fees may be charged for parking that is no longer compatible with common use, regardless of whether free parking spaces are available nearby.»

**BGE 127 I 60** of 1 January 2001  
Cost reimbursement for traffic police operations by a municipality; disturber and polluter-pays principle. The Federal Supreme Court examined the compatibility of a cantonal cost-bearing regulation for police traffic control operations with federal road traffic law. It is not arbitrary to oblige the owner of a property to partially reimburse costs for necessary traffic control operations according to the disturber or polluter-pays principle.

> «Derogatory force of federal law: Compatibility of a cantonal (or municipal) cost-bearing regulation for police traffic control operations with federal road traffic law (...). It is not arbitrary to oblige the owner who makes his property available through a rental contract for non-zone-compliant use to partially reimburse costs for the necessary traffic control operations according to the disturber or polluter-pays principle.»

### Heavy Vehicle Fee

**BGE 136 II 337** of 19 April 2010  
Collection of the performance-related heavy vehicle fee (LSVA) based on Art. 85 Federal Constitution (not Art. 82 Federal Constitution). Although this decision primarily concerns Art. 85 Federal Constitution, it shows the distinction between various traffic-related constitutional provisions. The LSVA does not constitute a fee for road use within the meaning of Art. 82 para. 3 Federal Constitution, but rather a steering levy for cost coverage.

> «Legal basis for collecting the LSVA in the Constitution (consideration 2.1), law (consideration 2.2) and ordinance (consideration 2.3). (...) The proceeds of the levy may not exceed the uncovered infrastructure costs and the costs borne by the general public.»

### Other Judgments

**Judgment 1C_615/2021** of 15 March 2024  
Traffic restrictions, 30 km/h zones in the city of Bern. The Federal Supreme Court specified the requirements for traffic restrictions according to Art. 82 Federal Constitution in connection with the legal provisions on speed limits and the required expert reports.

**Judgment 1P.292/2003** of 5 April 2004  
Declaration of invalidity of the popular initiative «12 car-free Sundays» in Appenzell Ausserrhoden. Fundamental decision on the delimitation of competences between the Confederation and the cantons in road traffic law (→ BGE 130 I 134).

**Judgment 1C_276/2008** of 22 December 2008  
Environmental works and violation of clearance profile; restoration obligations. The Federal Supreme Court clarified the obligations in connection with supreme supervision over roads of national importance.

### Practical Notes

The case law on Art. 82 Federal Constitution shows a clear division of competences: The Confederation has exclusive legislative competence in road traffic law and exercises supreme supervision over roads of national importance. The cantons may only act within the framework of federal law and are not empowered to generally restrict motorised traffic.

The fee exemption according to Art. 82 para. 3 Federal Constitution is not absolute: It applies only to the common use of public roads. Enhanced common use (such as longer-term parking) may be made subject to fees. The Federal Assembly may approve exceptions to fee exemption, which is particularly relevant for steering levies such as the LSVA.

Recent case law (BGE 150 II 444) shows a tendency towards more detailed requirements for traffic restrictions, whereby a distinction is made between traffic-oriented and non-traffic-oriented roads.
