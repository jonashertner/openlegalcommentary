# Doctrine

## 1. Legislative History

**N. 1** Art. 32 FC was incorporated into the Federal Constitution as a new provision with the total revision of 1999. The Federal Council's dispatch of 20 November 1996 (BBl 1997 I 1) emphasises that the presumption of innocence was already recognised as an unwritten fundamental right before the constitutional revision. The constitutional legislator wanted to explicitly anchor the central criminal procedural guarantees in order to underscore their fundamental importance for the rule of law.

**N. 2** The three paragraphs of Art. 32 FC codify various aspects of procedural fairness in criminal proceedings: the presumption of innocence (para. 1), information and defence rights (para. 2), and the right to review by a higher instance (para. 3). These guarantees were already anchored in Art. 6 ECHR and Art. 14 ICCPR, but gained additional weight through their inclusion in the Federal Constitution (BBl 1997 I 184).

## 2. Systematic Classification

**N. 3** Art. 32 FC stands in the section on procedural guarantees and forms, together with → Art. 29 FC (general procedural guarantees), → Art. 30 FC (judicial proceedings) and → Art. 31 FC (deprivation of liberty), the centrepiece of constitutional procedural rights. The provision concretises the general rule-of-law principles of → Art. 5 FC for criminal proceedings.

**N. 4** In relation to international guarantees, Art. 32 FC ensures protection equivalent to Art. 6 ECHR and Art. 14 ICCPR. The case law of the ECtHR on Art. 6 ECHR is decisive for the interpretation of Art. 32 FC (Kiener/Kälin/Wyttenbach, Grundrechte, 3rd ed. 2018, § 47 N. 1). The Federal Criminal Procedure Code (CPC) concretises the constitutional requirements at the statutory level.

## 3. Elements of the Offence / Normative Content

### a) Presumption of Innocence (para. 1)

**N. 5** The presumption of innocence provides that every person is presumed innocent until legally convicted. It comprises three components: the rule on burden of proof («in dubio pro reo»), the prohibition of anticipatory determinations of guilt, and the right to treatment as an innocent person (Tschentscher/Lienhard/Sprecher, BSK BV, Art. 32 N. 8).

**N. 6** As a rule on burden of proof, the presumption of innocence means that the prosecution must prove guilt. In cases of irremovable doubt, the decision must be in favour of the accused (**BGE 127 I 38** E. 2b). The presumption is only rebutted by a legally binding conviction (Schweizer, St. Galler Kommentar BV, Art. 32 N. 6).

### b) Information and Defence Rights (para. 2)

**N. 7** The right to information about the charges requires «prompt and detailed» notification. This includes both the factual allegation and the legal qualification. The information must be provided in a language comprehensible to the accused (→ Art. 18 FC). The right of confrontation and the right to defence are inseparably connected with the right to information (Bommer, BSK BV, Art. 32 N. 15).

**N. 8** The defence rights include the right to inspect files, the right to make applications for evidence, the right of confrontation, and the right to legal assistance. These rights must be able to be effectively exercised, which also includes official defence in cases of indigence (**BGE 141 I 124** E. 3.1).

### c) Right of Appeal (para. 3)

**N. 9** Every convicted person has the right to have the judgment reviewed by a higher court. This right goes beyond the general guarantee of access to the courts under → Art. 29a FC and requires comprehensive review in factual and legal terms (Steinmann, St. Galler Kommentar BV, Art. 32 N. 26).

**N. 10** The exception for the Federal Court as sole instance must be interpreted narrowly. It concerns only the rare cases under Art. 23 CPC (offences by members of federal authorities). In all other cases, at least one appellate instance must be available (Wohlers, BSK BV, Art. 32 N. 23).

## 4. Legal Consequences

**N. 11** Violation of the presumption of innocence can have various legal consequences: exclusion of evidence, quashing of the judgment, claims for satisfaction, or disciplinary measures against errant officials. In case of violation of the «in dubio pro reo» principle, an acquittal is mandatory (**BGE 144 IV 345** E. 2.3.3).

**N. 12** Violations of information and defence rights regularly lead to quashing of the judgment and remand of the case. Cure is only possible if the defect was completely remedied in the further proceedings and no disadvantage arose (Schindler, St. Galler Kommentar BV, Art. 32 N. 19).

**N. 13** The absence of an appellate instance renders a judgment void. This guarantee is absolute and knows no exceptions outside the constellation mentioned in para. 3 (Donatsch/Hansjakob/Lieber, Kommentar StPO, 3rd ed. 2020, Preliminary remarks Art. 393 N. 1).

## 5. Disputed Issues

**N. 14** The scope of application of the presumption of innocence to administrative sanctions is disputed. While Tschannen/Zimmerli/Müller (Allgemeines Verwaltungsrecht, 4th ed. 2014, § 57 N. 12) favour broad application, Häner (in: Auer/Müller/Schindler, VwVG-Kommentar, 2nd ed. 2019, Art. 12 N. 28) takes a more restrictive position. The Federal Court applies the presumption of innocence to proceedings with criminal-law-like character (**BGE 139 I 72**).

**N. 15** The scope of the privilege against self-incrimination («nemo tenetur») is doctrinally disputed. Niggli/Heer/Wiprächtiger (BSK StPO, 2nd ed. 2014, Art. 113 N. 5) advocate comprehensive application, while Schmid/Jositsch (Handbuch StPO, 4th ed. 2022, N. 156) accept restrictions on passive cooperation (e.g. blood sampling). Practice follows the differentiating solution (**BGE 148 IV 205**).

**N. 16** Regarding the right of appeal, there is disagreement about the required cognition of the appellate instance. Schweizer (St. Galler Kommentar BV, Art. 32 N. 28) demands full cognition, while Kiener/Kälin/Wyttenbach (Grundrechte, 3rd ed. 2018, § 47 N. 116) consider review limited to questions of law sufficient. The prevailing doctrine requires at least comprehensive legal control with limited factual cognition.

## 6. Practice Notes

**N. 17** Criminal prosecution authorities must fully inform accused persons of their rights no later than the first interrogation (Art. 158 CPC). The information must include the concrete factual allegation, the possible legal assessment, and the procedural rights. Standard-form instructions suffice only if they are comprehensible and questions are answered.

**N. 18** For the practice of assessment of evidence: doubts must be concretely substantiated. It does not suffice to postulate abstract-theoretical doubts. The court's conviction must be based on an objectively comprehensible assessment of all evidence. Special care is required in word-against-word constellations (**BGE 129 I 49**).

**N. 19** Defence counsel should document violations of the presumption of innocence by authorities or media. Pre-judging statements can give rise to claims for satisfaction and in extreme cases lead to termination of proceedings. The objection must be raised in timely fashion, at the latest in the main hearing (Art. 147 para. 4 CPC).

**N. 20** In international cases, it should be noted that the right of appeal also applies to judgments of foreign courts that are to be enforced in Switzerland. Enforcement can be refused if there was no adequate possibility of review in the state of judgment (→ Art. 64 ILJA).
