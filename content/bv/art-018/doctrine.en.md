# Art. 18 FC — Freedom of Language

## Doctrine

### 1. Legislative History

**N. 1** Freedom of language was not a written fundamental right prior to the entry into force of the Federal Constitution of 18 April 1999. The Federal Supreme Court recognised it from **BGE 91 I 480** (1965) onwards as an unwritten liberty of the Federal Constitution (E. II/1), flowing from the personal sphere of the individual. This case law was consolidated in **BGE 100 Ia 462** (1974), **BGE 121 I 196 E. 2a** (1995) and **BGE 122 I 236 E. 2a** (1996).

**N. 2** In the context of the total revision of the Federal Constitution, the Federal Council proposed, in its Dispatch of 20 November 1996, an express positive enactment of freedom of language (BBl 1997 I 164, on Art. 15 of the draft). The Federal Council explained that the provision codified the existing unwritten freedom and was intended to anchor its applicability in the constitutional text. It deliberately refrained from expressly mentioning the territoriality principle in the same article, as that principle had been the subject of controversial debates and was already governed by Art. 70 FC (official languages) (BBl 1997 I 164).

**N. 3** Parliamentary deliberations on the total revision were contentious in the area of the language articles. In the earlier partial revision of Art. 116 of the former Federal Constitution (version of 10 March 1996), the Council of States had deleted the express mention of freedom of language (AB StR 1992 p. 1044 ff.); the National Council, after extensive debate, declined to reinsert it and adopted a version that mentioned neither freedom of language nor the territoriality principle expressly (AB NR 1993 p. 1541 ff.; **BGE 122 I 236 E. 2f**). Art. 18 FC 1999 ended this restraint through a concise but unambiguous positive enactment.

**N. 4** The Federal Act on the National Languages and Understanding between the Linguistic Communities (Languages Act, SpG; SR 441.1) of 5 October 2007 gives legislative effect to the constitutional mandate under Art. 18 in conjunction with Art. 70 FC, but does not create directly enforceable rights to use a specific language in dealings with authorities.

---

### 2. Systematic Classification

**N. 5** Art. 18 FC is situated in the second chapter of the Federal Constitution (Art. 7–36 FC) on fundamental rights. The provision is a classical defensive fundamental right (liberty right) and protects the individual against state interference in the free choice of language. As a defensive right it is directly justiciable (→ Art. 35 para. 1 FC).

**N. 6** Art. 18 FC forms part of the constitutional language trilogy of the Federal Constitution: Art. 4 FC (national languages), Art. 18 FC (freedom of language as an individual right) and Art. 70 FC (official languages, territoriality principle, promotion of languages). These three provisions are closely interrelated (**BGE 139 I 229 E. 5.1**). The institutional guarantee of the national languages (→ Art. 4 FC) and the territoriality principle (→ Art. 70 para. 2 FC) limit the individual freedom of language under Art. 18 FC.

**N. 7** Within the system of fundamental rights, Art. 18 FC relates to → Art. 8 para. 2 FC (prohibition of discrimination on the basis of language) and to → Art. 10 para. 2 FC (personal liberty, from which freedom of language was historically derived). In procedural matters, → Art. 29 ff. FC and → Art. 31 para. 2 FC complement freedom of language through specific rights to information and to interpretation. At the level of the ECHR, Art. 18 FC does not correspond to any specific individual guarantee; the prohibition of discrimination on the basis of language is found in Art. 14 ECHR in conjunction with Protocol No. 12.

**N. 8** Art. 18 FC must be distinguished from Art. 70 FC: whereas Art. 18 FC guarantees the *individual right* to a free choice of language, Art. 70 FC governs the *institutional* questions of official languages, the territoriality principle and the promotion of languages. Müller/Schefer describe Art. 18 FC as the «primary guarantee» and Art. 70 FC as its «institutional counterpart» (Müller/Schefer, Grundrechte in der Schweiz, 4th ed. 2008, p. 290 ff.).

### 3. Elements of the Provision / Normative Content

#### 3.1 Scope of Protection

**N. 9** According to consistent case law, freedom of language protects the right «to use a language of one's own choice, in particular also one's mother tongue» (**BGE 139 I 229 E. 5.4**; **BGE 138 I 123 E. 5.1**; **BGE 136 I 149 E. 4.1**; **BGE 122 I 236 E. 2b**; **BGE 121 I 196 E. 2a**). The mother tongue enjoys special protection, as it is an expression of the personality and cultural identity of the individual.

**N. 10** The Federal Supreme Court distinguishes in **BGE 139 I 229 E. 5.4** between the **active** and the **passive** dimension of freedom of language:
- **Active dimension**: The right of the individual to use their own language of free choice and to communicate in it — in particular in private dealings. The State must not interfere in this sphere.
- **Passive dimension**: The question of the language in which state authorities address the public (including the determination of the language of instruction). In this area, the Confederation, cantons and communes may and must take action.

This distinction is decisive for legal consequences: restrictions on the active dimension require justification under Art. 36 FC; the passive dimension does not constitute an interference with Art. 18 FC but rather a state regulation of the provision of services.

**N. 11** The substantive scope of protection extends to all languages, not only to the four national languages. Freedom of language also protects the use of foreign languages and regional idioms. In the area of Romansh, Art. 18 FC protects both the idioms and Rumantsch Grischun (**BGE 139 I 229 E. 5.4**, with reference to Müller/Schefer, op. cit., p. 294).

**N. 12** As regards the **personal scope of protection**, in principle any natural person may invoke Art. 18 FC, irrespective of nationality or residence status. Legal persons do not fall within the scope of protection, as freedom of language is a strictly personal right (Kägi-Diener, in: Ehrenzeller/Schindler/Schweizer/Vallender [eds.], St. Galler Kommentar zur Schweizerischen Bundesverfassung, 4th ed. 2023, Art. 18 N. 16 [hereinafter: SGK-BV]).

#### 3.2 Relationship to the Territoriality Principle

**N. 13** The territoriality principle constitutes the most important structural limitation on freedom of language. It provides that each territory is in principle assigned to one language and that residents must use the official language of that territory in dealings with authorities (**BGE 136 I 149 E. 4.2–4.3**; **BGE 122 I 236 E. 2c**). The territoriality principle is not an independent individual right but a limitation on freedom of language constitutionally recognised by Art. 70 para. 2 FC.

**N. 14** According to case law, the territoriality principle pursues two objectives (**BGE 122 I 236 E. 2d–e**): first, the practicability and cost-efficiency of the discharge of public tasks (in particular public schooling); second, the protection of threatened languages and the preservation of linguistic peace. The more threatened a language is, the more far-reaching restrictions on individual freedom of language may be justified (**BGE 122 I 236 E. 2g**, with reference to AB NR 1993 p. 1544, committee rapporteur Bundi).

**N. 15** The territoriality principle prohibits the **deliberate displacement of established language boundaries** or the suppression of indigenous linguistic minority groups (**BGE 100 Ia 462 E. 2b**; **BGE 139 I 229 E. 5.5**; **BGE 122 I 236 E. 2h**). It thereby simultaneously protects minority languages — in particular Italian and Romansh (→ Art. 70 para. 5 FC).

#### 3.3 Freedom of Language in Dealings with Authorities and in Proceedings

**N. 16** In dealings with authorities, the official language generally applies. There is no right to communicate with authorities in a language of one's choice (**BGE 136 I 149 E. 4.3**; **BGE 139 I 229 E. 5.5**; **BGE 143 IV 117 E. 1**). This principle is subject to special rights (e.g. Art. 31 para. 2 FC; Art. 5 para. 2 and Art. 6 para. 3 lit. a ECHR in criminal proceedings).

**N. 17** In order to prevent excessive formalism, a criminal authority must, in the case of a submission filed within the time limit but not drawn up in the language of the proceedings, grant an additional time limit for translation, provided it does not accept the document as submitted or translate it itself (**BGE 143 IV 117 E. 1**).

#### 3.4 Freedom of Language in the School System

**N. 18** Freedom of language does not confer a right to receive school instruction in a language of one's choice (including one's mother tongue). Rather, instruction takes place in the language that the cantons — or, according to cantonal law, the communes — determine in accordance with the principles of Art. 70 para. 2 FC (**BGE 139 I 229 E. 5.6**; **BGE 122 I 236 E. 2d**; **BGE 100 Ia 462 E. 2**). The determination of the language of instruction does not affect the active dimension of freedom of language but is a matter of the provision of state services within the framework of → Art. 19 and Art. 62 para. 2 FC.

**N. 19** In traditionally bilingual or multilingual territories, however, a right to receive instruction in one of the several traditional languages may arise from freedom of language — provided this does not impose a disproportionate burden on the community (**BGE 139 I 229 E. 5.6**; **BGE 125 I 347 E. 5c**; **BGE 122 I 236 E. 2d**).

**N. 20** There is no obligation to provide newly immigrated linguistic minorities with instruction in their mother tongue. Those who move to an area with a different official language must bear the resulting linguistic consequences (**BGE 122 I 236 E. 2d**).

### 4. Legal Consequences

**N. 21** As a defensive fundamental right, Art. 18 FC primarily obliges the State to refrain from acting: state measures that prohibit or disproportionately restrict the use of a language in the private sphere are unconstitutional. A prohibition on speaking a particular language in private would constitute a fundamental interference with Art. 18 FC.

**N. 22** Restrictions on freedom of language are permissible pursuant to → Art. 36 FC only if they are based on a legal basis, justified by a public interest or by the need to protect the fundamental rights of others, and comply with the principle of proportionality (**BGE 122 I 236 E. 4**). The core of freedom of language — the right to use any language at all in the private sphere — is absolutely protected under Art. 36 para. 4 FC.

**N. 23** Art. 18 FC does not give rise to **positive entitlements** to state communication or school instruction in any desired language. To that extent, Art. 18 FC remains a purely defensive right. By contrast, → Art. 70 para. 5 FC contains an institutional mandate to promote threatened languages, which is however addressed to the legislature and does not create individually enforceable rights.

**N. 24** A violation of Art. 18 FC gives rise to a right to the annulment of the offending state measure and — within the framework of Art. 35 FC — indirect third-party effect in private legal relationships.

### 5. Contested Issues

#### 5.1 Scope of Protection: Mother Tongue or Any Language?

**N. 25** Academic literature disputes whether Art. 18 FC protects only the mother tongue or any language. Malinverni (Kommentar BV, N. 5 f. on freedom of language [former FC]) argued already under the previous constitution that the scope of protection encompasses every language that a person wishes to use. Müller (Die Grundrechte der schweizerischen Bundesverfassung, 1991, p. 82) and Viletta (Grundlagen des Sprachenrechts, 1978, p. 287) supported this broad interpretation. By contrast, Kägi-Diener (SGK-BV, Art. 18 N. 13) and Müller/Schefer (Grundrechte in der Schweiz, 4th ed. 2008, p. 290 f.) emphasise the special significance of the mother tongue as the core of the protection. The Federal Supreme Court leaves the question open but in any event expressly protects the mother tongue (**BGE 122 I 236 E. 2b**; **BGE 121 I 196 E. 2a**).

#### 5.2 Scope of the Territoriality Principle

**N. 26** The relationship between individual freedom of language and the collective territoriality principle has for decades been the principal contested issue in Swiss language-law doctrine. The parliamentary expert group Saladin criticised in 1989 that the territoriality principle had in practice operated predominantly to the detriment of linguistic minorities, and placed greater emphasis on the importance of freedom of language (Arbeitsgruppe EDI, Zustand und Zukunft der viersprachigen Schweiz, 1989, p. 200 ff.).

**N. 27** Malinverni (op. cit., N. 40 f.) and Müller (op. cit., p. 84 f.) argue, by contrast, that the territoriality principle is not necessarily contained in Art. 70 FC and that a deliberate policy of multilingualism may serve the promotion of linguistic peace equally as well as the homogenisation of linguistic territories. Rossinelli (La question linguistique en Suisse, ZSR 108/1989 I, p. 163 ff.) regards the territoriality principle not as a constitutional imperative but as a political option. By contrast, Marti-Rolli (La liberté de la langue en droit suisse, 1978, p. 41) and Viletta (op. cit., p. 342) take the view that newcomers are obliged to assimilate in public language use. The Federal Supreme Court adheres to the territoriality principle as a constitutional regulative, but emphasises its proportionate application and rejects it as a rigid imperative (**BGE 122 I 236 E. 2g–h**).

#### 5.3 Positive-Rights Dimension

**N. 28** Whether Art. 18 FC — in conjunction with → Art. 8 para. 2 FC (prohibition of discrimination) — gives rise to at least minimal positive duties of the State is disputed in academic writing. Fleiner (Sprachenfreiheit, in: Handbuch der Grundrechte, vol. VII/2, 2007, p. 406 f.) advocates a more positive-rights-oriented reading and argues for a right to instruction in the minority language in traditionally multilingual areas. The Federal Supreme Court affirms such an obligation in **BGE 139 I 229 E. 5.6** and **BGE 125 I 347 E. 5c** only under the dual condition that the area is traditionally multilingual and that the provision does not impose a disproportionate burden on the community. Kägi-Diener (SGK-BV, Art. 18 N. 13) and Kiener/Kälin (Grundrechte, 2007, p. 261) likewise affirm a constitutional right to instruction in the language spoken locally in such areas.

#### 5.4 Protection of Private Schools

**N. 29** Whether the State may require private schools to use an official language was answered affirmatively by the Federal Supreme Court in **BGE 91 I 480 E. II/3**. Parts of the academic literature criticise this decision as an excessively far-reaching restriction on private freedom of language (Haefliger, Die Sprachenfreiheit in der bundesgerichtlichen Rechtsprechung, Mélanges Zwahlen, 1977, p. 82; Malinverni, op. cit., N. 33; Marti-Rolli, op. cit., p. 58 ff.; Morand, Liberté de la langue et principe de territorialité, ZSR 112/1993 I, p. 24). This criticism points out that an extension of the territoriality principle to private educational establishments touches on the core substance of freedom of language.

### 6. Practical Notes

**N. 30** **Dealings with authorities:** When submitting documents to federal authorities, every person is free to choose among the official languages of the Confederation (Art. 6 SpG). In dealings with cantonal and communal authorities, by contrast, the respective official language applies. In bilingual cantons (Fribourg, Berne, Valais, Graubünden), cantonal law provisions may grant more extensive rights of choice (cf. **BGE 136 I 149** on the Fribourg cantonal constitution).

**N. 31** **Criminal proceedings:** In criminal proceedings, the accused has the right to be informed of the charges in a language they understand and to receive interpretation assistance (Art. 5 para. 2 and Art. 6 para. 3 lit. a ECHR; Art. 68 CrimPC). The choice of language of the proceedings is, however, governed by the official language of the competent court. Where submissions are filed within the time limit in a language other than the language of the proceedings, an additional time limit for translation must be granted (**BGE 143 IV 117**).

**N. 32** **School system:** Parents wishing to have their children taught in a particular language have no constitutional right to that provision, unless the area is traditionally multilingual and the provision does not impose a disproportionate burden on the community (**BGE 122 I 236 E. 2d**; **BGE 139 I 229 E. 5.6**). Where a commune voluntarily agrees to admit a child to a school of a different language and the parents bear the costs, a prohibition on attending that school is disproportionate (**BGE 122 I 236 E. 4**).

**N. 33** **Romansh:** In dealings with Romansh-speaking persons, Romansh is also an official language of the Confederation (Art. 70 para. 1 FC). The SpG (Art. 6 para. 3) provides that persons of Romansh language may address federal authorities in their idiom or in Rumantsch Grischun; the authorities respond in Rumantsch Grischun. The choice between an idiom and Rumantsch Grischun as the language of instruction is a question of language policy, not of fundamental rights (**BGE 139 I 229 E. 5.7.5**).

**N. 34** **Fundamental rights interference — proportionality review:** In the case of interferences with Art. 18 FC, a statutory basis, a public interest and proportionality must be examined pursuant to → Art. 36 FC. The public interest in maintaining homogeneous linguistic territories and the protection of threatened languages are recognised grounds for interference. The more threatened the language to be protected, the more intensive restrictions on individual freedom of language are constitutionally permissible (**BGE 122 I 236 E. 2g**; **BGE 116 Ia 345 E. 5b**).
