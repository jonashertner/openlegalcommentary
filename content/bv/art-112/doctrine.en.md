# Art. 112 FC — Old-age, Survivors' and Invalidity Insurance

## Doctrine

### 1. Legislative History

**N. 1** Art. 112 FC traces back to the popular vote of 3 December 1972, by which the so-called three-pillar principle was incorporated into the then Federal Constitution (Art. 34quater oFC). The constituent legislator thereby enshrined not only the OASI and II as the first pillar, but also their fundamental structural principles — compulsory insurance, parity of contributions, coverage of subsistence needs, and the pension band — at the constitutional level (Rhinow/Schefer/Uebersax, Schweizerisches Verfassungsrecht, 3rd ed. 2016, N. 3440 ff.).

**N. 2** The Federal Council's dispatch on the new Federal Constitution of 1999 carried over the content of Art. 34quater oFC largely unchanged into the present Art. 112 FC (BBl 1997 I 1, 359). The Federal Council described the principles contained therein as «binding constitutional requirements» assigning the legislature a substantively determined competence that cannot be exercised at discretion (BBl 1997 I 359). The provision was adopted in the popular vote of 18 April 1999 together with the new Federal Constitution.

**N. 3** Para. 2 let. a*bis* was inserted by the popular vote of 28 November 2004 (Federal Decree of 3 October 2003, in force since 1 January 2008) and supplemented the catalogue of principles by expressly anchoring cash and benefits in kind. This explicitly secured the rehabilitation function of the II at the constitutional level as well.

**N. 4** On 3 March 2024, the Swiss electorate adopted the popular initiative «For a better life in old age (Initiative for a 13th OASI pension)». The initiative did not result in an amendment to Art. 112 para. 2, but rather in the insertion of a transitional provision in Art. 197 FC (no. 16), which obliges the Confederation to adapt the legislation accordingly within two years of adoption. This transitional provision mandates an additional annual pension equivalent to one monthly pension, but contains no provisions on the modalities of financing. Its compatibility with Art. 112 para. 2 let. c FC (pension band 1:2) is the subject of ongoing discussion (cf. N. 27 below).

### 2. Systematic Classification

**N. 5** Art. 112 FC is located in Chapter 3 of Title 3 of the Federal Constitution («Social goals and social insurance», Arts. 111–117 FC). Together with Art. 111 FC (old-age provision), Art. 112a FC (supplementary benefits), Art. 112b FC (promotion of the integration of persons with disabilities) and Art. 113 FC (occupational benefits), the provision forms the constitutional foundation of the Swiss social insurance system.

**N. 6** Art. 112 FC is a competence provision with substantive directives. Para. 1 contains the legislative competence of the Confederation as an exclusive federal competence (→ Art. 3 FC, → Art. 49 FC). The cantons may not enact competing OASI/II regulations. The «principles» referred to in para. 2 are binding substantive requirements for the legislature, not mere programmatic norms (Biaggini, BSK BV, Art. 112 N. 13). However, they do not give rise to directly enforceable subjective rights (→ Art. 41 FC).

**N. 7** The relationship to Art. 12 FC (right to assistance in situations of need) is specifically shaped by para. 2 let. b: OASI/II pensions shall «adequately» cover subsistence needs, which goes beyond the mere minimum standard of Art. 12 FC. Where OASI/II pensions are unable to cover subsistence needs, supplementary benefits under → Art. 112a FC apply. Arts. 112 and 112a FC thereby form a functional system for securing subsistence in old age and in the event of invalidity (Judgment 9C_982/2009 of 12 February 2010, consid. 2.1).

**N. 8** The design directives contained in para. 2 bind the legislature at the statutory level: the OASIA (SR 831.10) and the IIA (SR 831.20) must comply with all principles of Art. 112 para. 2 FC. Ordinances of the Federal Council are additionally subject to review against the statutory law (→ Art. 190 FC); the Federal Supreme Court reviews ordinance provisions for constitutionality and conformity with statute (**BGE 150 V 410**, consid. 9.1).

### 3. Elements of the Provision / Normative Content

#### 3.1 Legislative Competence (Para. 1)

**N. 9** Para. 1 grants the Confederation exclusive competence to legislate on old-age insurance, survivors' insurance and invalidity insurance. The phrase «issues regulations» is to be understood as an obligation: the Confederation is required to legislate, not merely authorised to do so (Häfelin/Haller/Keller/Thurnherr, Schweizerisches Bundesstaatsrecht, 10th ed. 2020, N. 1922). The three insurance branches mentioned together constitute the «first pillar» within the meaning of Art. 111 para. 1 FC.

#### 3.2 Compulsory Insurance (Para. 2 Let. a)

**N. 10** The compulsory insurance obligation covers in principle all persons resident or gainfully employed in Switzerland (Art. 1a OASIA). It gives effect to the concept of universal insurance: the OASI is not a private insurance concluded on the basis of individual risk assessment, but a public-law solidarity system. The Federal Supreme Court has characterised the OASI on multiple occasions as a «universal insurance» and derived from this principle limits on the exercise of rights (**BGE 131 V 97**, consid. 4.3.3).

**N. 11** The compulsory insurance obligation also protects actuarial solidarity: since the contribution obligation is unlimited on the upper end (Arts. 4, 5 and 8 OASIA), while pension benefits are capped by the pension band (para. 2 let. c), redistribution from higher to lower incomes takes place (Locher, Grundriss des Sozialversicherungsrechts, 3rd ed. 2003, § 2 N. 13). Anyone who abuses the OASI as a pure financial investment vehicle for the purpose of maximising individual returns acts in an abusive manner under Art. 2 para. 2 CC and cannot invoke the right to be admitted to the OASI (**BGE 131 V 97**, consid. 4.3.3–4.3.6; → on the topic of abuse of rights in detail N. 24 below).

#### 3.3 Cash and Benefits in Kind (Para. 2 Let. a*bis*)

**N. 12** The OASI grants cash benefits (in particular old-age and survivors' pensions, helplessness allowances). In addition to cash benefits (invalidity pensions, daily allowances), the II also grants benefits in kind, in particular rehabilitation measures of a medical, vocational and educational nature (Arts. 8 ff. IIA). The express mention of both types of benefit at the constitutional level underscores that rehabilitation takes precedence over pensions as a matter of constitutional principle (Rhinow/Schefer/Uebersax, op. cit., N. 3446).

#### 3.4 Adequate Coverage of Subsistence Needs (Para. 2 Let. b)

**N. 13** Art. 112 para. 2 let. b FC obliges the legislature to structure pensions in such a way that they «adequately» cover subsistence needs. The adjective «adequately» is an indeterminate legal concept. It sets a standard that goes beyond the minimum subsistence of Art. 12 FC, but does not guarantee a comfortable standard of living (Biaggini, BSK BV, Art. 112 N. 17). The Federal Supreme Court understands this to mean the coverage of a modest but dignified livelihood, supplementable by supplementary benefits under Art. 112a FC.

**N. 14** The provision constitutes a legislative mandate, not a subjective right. The Federal Supreme Court has expressly held that Art. 112 para. 2 let. b FC «contains a mere legislative mandate within the scope of which the legislature may freely determine the content» (Judgment 9C_982/2009 of 12 February 2010, consid. 2.1). Individuals cannot derive direct entitlements to benefits from it.

**N. 15** Where OASI/II pensions do not cover subsistence needs, supplementary benefits under Art. 112a FC supplement them. The interplay between Art. 112 para. 2 let. b and Art. 112a FC forms a two-tier guarantee of subsistence security. The Federal Supreme Court held that Art. 112a FC directs persons «whose subsistence needs are not covered by the benefits of old-age, survivors' and invalidity insurance» to supplementary benefits (**BGE 139 V 358**, consid. 2.1; Judgment 9C_51/2013 of 26 June 2013, consid. 2.1).

#### 3.5 Pension Band (Para. 2 Let. c)

**N. 16** The maximum pension may not exceed twice the minimum pension (ratio 1:2). This bandwidth is understood as an expression of actuarial solidarity: while contributions are levied in proportion to income and are unlimited on the upper end, benefits are capped. A person who makes high contributions receives a higher pension than a person with low contributions, but not proportionally more (Locher, op. cit., § 2 N. 13; Häfelin/Haller/Keller/Thurnherr, op. cit., N. 1924).

**N. 17** The current statutory implementation is found in Art. 34 OASIA (full pensions) and Art. 38 OASIA (partial pensions). The constitutional pension band applies to full pensions; the partial pension regime under Art. 38 OASIA concerns the contribution period, not the pension amount as such (**BGE 131 V 97**, consid. 4.3.3).

#### 3.6 Adjustment for Price Increases (Para. 2 Let. d)

**N. 18** Pensions shall be adjusted «at least» in line with price developments. The word «at least» leaves the legislature room for more far-reaching adjustments (e.g. a mixed index of price and wage developments). The statutory rule in Art. 33ter OASIA provides for adjustment according to the so-called mixed index, which is calculated from the wage index and the price index. This solution goes beyond the constitutional minimum, but is permissible (Rhinow/Schefer/Uebersax, op. cit., N. 3448).

#### 3.7 Financing (Paras. 3–5)

**N. 19** Para. 3 establishes the financing sources: contributions from insured persons (with a mandatory equal split between employers and employees, Art. 3 let. a) and Confederation contributions (Art. 3 let. b). The parity sharing of contributions between employers and employees is one of the constitutive features of OASI financing (Häfelin/Haller/Keller/Thurnherr, op. cit., N. 1925).

**N. 20** Para. 4 sets an upper limit for Confederation contributions: at most half of the expenditure. This cap is intended to prevent excessive dependence on the general federal budget and to preserve contribution-based financing as the main pillar.

**N. 21** Para. 5 prescribes the priority sources of Confederation contributions: the net proceeds from the tax on tobacco, the tax on distilled spirits and the gaming house levy. This earmarking has constitutional status. The Federal Administrative Court held that the tobacco tax thereby constitutes a hypothecated tax «at the constitutional level» (FAC A-1211/2018 of 11 March 2019, consid. 4.1; the decision was overturned by the Federal Supreme Court with Judgment 2C_350/2019 of 29 January 2020, however without calling into question the hypothecated tax character).

### 4. Legal Effects

**N. 22** As a competence provision with substantive directives, Art. 112 FC produces the following effects:

- **Legislative duty**: The Confederation must enact and keep current the OASIA and IIA.
- **Substantive binding**: The legislature must comply with the principles of para. 2; derogating rules are unconstitutional.
- **Limits on ordinance law**: The Federal Council may enact ordinances pursuant to statutory delegation, but is bound by statute and constitution. The Federal Supreme Court sets aside ordinance provisions that are contrary to statute (**BGE 150 V 410**, consid. 10.6: Art. 26*bis* para. 3 OIIA in the version of 3 November 2021 held to be contrary to federal law insofar as it intended an exhaustive regulation of the deduction from statistical wages).
- **No direct entitlements to benefits**: The principles of para. 2 do not, in themselves, give rise to enforceable individual rights; they bind the legislature as design directives (Judgment 9C_982/2009 of 12 February 2010, consid. 2.1).

**N. 23** The financing provisions of paras. 3–5 bind both the legislature (in shaping the financing regime) and the ordinance-maker. The ceiling on Confederation contributions (para. 4) is a directly applicable constraint on any financing legislation.

### 5. Contested Issues

#### 5.1 Terminology of «Principles»

**N. 24** Academic doctrine is divided on whether the «principles» referred to in para. 2 constitute genuine principles or concrete substantive requirements. Biaggini (BSK BV, Art. 112 N. 13) emphasises that the term «principles» is terminologically imprecise: they are in fact precise, binding design directives, not principles capable of being weighed in the jurisprudential-theoretical sense. This assessment is shared by Rhinow/Schefer/Uebersax (op. cit., N. 3443): while the constitutional provision leaves the legislature a degree of discretion in implementation, it binds the legislature substantively far more strongly than a mere objective norm. Häfelin/Haller/Keller/Thurnherr (op. cit., N. 1922) speak of «binding constitutional requirements», thereby underscoring the normative density of the provision.

#### 5.2 Abuse of Rights in the OASI Compulsory Insurance

**N. 25** The relationship between the compulsory insurance obligation (para. 2 let. a) and the prohibition of abuse of rights (Art. 2 para. 2 CC) was fundamentally clarified by the Federal Supreme Court in **BGE 131 V 97**. The Federal Insurance Court held that foreign investors who participated in a limited partnership primarily with a view to a future OASI pension could not invoke the right to be admitted to the OASI:

> «The partners cannot invoke the right to be admitted to the OASI because the OASI is here assigned the function of a pure financial investment vehicle, intended to generate the largest possible individual return by exploiting actuarial solidarity.»

The court relied on the universal insurance character of the OASI under Art. 112 para. 2 let. a FC and the systemic importance of actuarial solidarity (**BGE 131 V 97**, consid. 4.3.3). This case law was confirmed and developed further in **BGE 136 V 258** (limited partner with German domicile) and **BGE 141 V 234** (amendment of contribution law by the CISA).

#### 5.3 Proportionality of Art. 26*bis* Para. 3 OIIA and Conformity of the Assessment of Invalidity with the Constitution

**N. 26** The design of the assessment of invalidity by the ordinance-maker has been the subject of a profound dispute since the further development of the II (in force as of 1 January 2022). Art. 26*bis* para. 3 OIIA (version of 3 November 2021) had restricted the deduction from statistical wages to a single factor («part-time work», 10%) and thereby effectively abolished the established case law on a multi-criteria deduction (up to 25%).

**N. 27** The Federal Supreme Court held in **BGE 150 V 410**, consid. 10.6, that Art. 26*bis* para. 3 OIIA in the version of 3 November 2021 «does not withstand scrutiny under federal law with regard to the exhaustive regulation of the deduction from statistical wages thereby intended». As grounds, it referred to the legislative history (Dispatch of 15 February 2017, BBl 2017 2668: regulatory intent to incorporate the existing Federal Supreme Court practice), the unity of the legal order (Art. 16 ATSG applies across branches), and the purpose of Art. 28a IIA. This decision indirectly concerns the constitutional requirement of adequate coverage of subsistence needs under Art. 112 para. 2 let. b FC, since the correction factors are intended to ensure that the calculated invalidity income reflects the actual economic situation of the insured person (BGE 150 V 410, consid. 9.5.1 and 10.4.2).

#### 5.4 13th OASI Pension and Compatibility with Art. 112 Para. 2 Let. c FC

**N. 28** The popular initiative «For a better life in old age (Initiative for a 13th OASI pension)», adopted on 3 March 2024, resulted in a transitional provision in Art. 197 no. 16 FC. This obliges the Confederation to adapt the OASI legislation such that every person receiving a full pension is entitled each year to an annual pension increased by one twelfth (Popular vote of 3 March 2024, Federal Council Decree of 15 April 2024, BBl 2024 856).

**N. 29** The question of whether the 13th OASI pension is compatible with the pension band of Art. 112 para. 2 let. c FC has not been resolved in academic doctrine. Art. 112 para. 2 let. c FC establishes a structural bandwidth of 1:2 between the minimum and maximum pension. The 13th monthly pension is conceived as a proportional supplement to the current pension and thus increases both the minimum and maximum pension proportionally by the same amount. Provided the 1:2 ratio is maintained, no violation of Art. 112 para. 2 let. c FC arises. The primary source for this question is the Federal Council's explanatory notes on the popular initiative (Explanations of the Federal Council, Popular vote of 3 March 2024, BBl 2024 117); no completed academic commentary was available at the time of writing. The financing question — which specific sources will be drawn upon for the 13th pension — was the subject of parliamentary deliberations at the time of writing (cf. Federal Council Dispatch of 20 November 2024 on the amendment of the OASIA, BBl 2024 2853).

### 6. Practical Notes

**N. 30** **Legislative mandate and revision cycles**: Art. 112 FC contains no mandate for regular pension revisions. The obligation to adjust for price increases under para. 2 let. d requires, however, periodic reviews. In practice, pension adjustments are made on the basis of Art. 33ter OASIA at the beginning of each year following the determination of the relevant price and wage developments.

**N. 31** **Pension band and implementation**: The statutory implementation of the 1:2 pension band (Art. 34 OASIA) is stable and uncontested in practice. Challenges arise when popular initiatives or legislative amendments alter the pension structure. The introduction of the 13th OASI pension (→ Art. 197 no. 16 FC) requires careful statutory implementation in order to respect the constitutional pension band (→ N. 28–29).

**N. 32** **Abuse of rights in the OASI compulsory insurance**: For practical purposes, **BGE 131 V 97** is authoritative. Compensation funds and social insurance courts must examine ex officio, in constellations where the OASI is systematically used as a financial investment, whether an abuse of rights exists (Art. 2 para. 2 CC in conjunction with Art. 112 para. 2 let. a FC). The prohibition of abuse of rights applies in every individual case; individual fault on the part of those involved is not required (**BGE 131 V 97**, consid. 4.3.3).

**N. 33** **Assessment of invalidity and conformity with the constitution**: **BGE 150 V 410** has clarified that the ordinance-maker, when designing the correction factors for the assessment of invalidity, is bound by the delegation norm (Art. 28a para. 1 IIA) and the legislative intent (BBl 2017 2668). Insofar as Art. 26*bis* para. 3 OIIA does not allow for adequate correction, the existing case-law principles apply supplementarily. The version of Art. 26*bis* para. 3 OIIA in force as of 1 January 2024 (AS 2023 635) must be assessed separately.

**N. 34** **Financing provisions**: The earmarking of the tobacco tax, the tax on distilled spirits and the gaming house levy (para. 5) removes these revenues from general budgetary appropriation. A reallocation by ordinary statute would be incompatible with Art. 112 para. 5 FC. To the extent that revenues from these sources do not fully cover the Confederation's contributions, other federal revenues must be drawn upon, provided the half-ceiling of para. 4 is observed.

**N. 35** **Demarcation from Art. 41 FC**: The social goals of Art. 41 FC are not justiciable and do not give rise to subjective rights. By contrast, the principles of Art. 112 para. 2 FC contain binding design directives that bind the legislature substantively. This distinction is central to the review of OASI and II legislation for conformity with higher-ranking law (Biaggini, BSK BV, Art. 112 N. 13; → Art. 41 FC).
