## Doctrine

### 1. Historical Development

**N. 1** The right of petition belongs to the oldest political rights in Swiss constitutional history. The Federal Constitution of 1848 already guaranteed in Art. 57 the right to address petitions to authorities. The total revision of 1874 adopted this guarantee unchanged in Art. 57 old FC.

**N. 2** In the total revision of 1999, the right of petition was anchored in Art. 33 new FC. The Federal Council Message on a New Federal Constitution of 20 November 1996 (BBl 1997 I 1, 183) emphasises the unchanged adoption of the existing constitutional guarantee: «The right of petition is a liberal fundamental right from the time of the Restoration. It guarantees the freedom to approach authorities unimpeded with written submissions (requests, suggestions, complaints).»

**N. 3** The constitutional legislator deliberately refrained from extending the right of petition to include a substantive duty to respond. The parliamentary constitutional commission discussed various proposals to strengthen the right of petition, but retained the traditional content as a pure liberty right without a corresponding claim to performance (AB 1998 p 392).

### 2. Systematic Classification

**N. 4** Art. 33 FC stands in the section on political rights (Art. 34-40 FC), immediately before the right to free formation of opinion and unvitiated voting (Art. 34 FC). This placement underscores the political character of the right of petition as an instrument of democratic participation.

**N. 5** The right of petition has close connections to other fundamental rights:
- → Art. 16 FC (freedom of expression): The petition is a qualified form of expression of opinion vis-à-vis authorities
- → Art. 22 FC (freedom of assembly): Collective petitions and signature collections combine both fundamental rights
- → Art. 29 FC (general procedural guarantees): Demarcation between informal petitions and formal procedural submissions

**N. 6** In relation to popular rights (Art. 136-142 FC), the right of petition represents a low-threshold participation instrument. Unlike initiative and referendum, it knows neither quorums nor formal requirements or deadlines. It is open to all persons, regardless of nationality or age.

### 3. Elements of the Offence / Normative Content

**N. 7** **Persons entitled to petition**: «Every person» within the meaning of Art. 33 para. 1 FC encompasses natural and legal persons, Swiss nationals and foreigners, persons of full age and minors. Even groups of persons without legal personality can submit petitions (Häfelin/Haller/Keller/Thurnherr, Schweizerisches Bundesstaatsrecht, 10th ed. 2020, N 854).

**N. 8** **Petition**: A petition is any submission directed to an authority with requests, suggestions, proposals, criticism or complaints of a general nature. The form is free; petitions can be submitted in writing, orally or electronically. However, submissions in formal proceedings or legal remedies are not petitions (**BGE 119 Ia 53** E. 4b).

**N. 9** **Concept of authority**: Addressees can be all authorities — executive, legislative and administrative at federal, cantonal and municipal level. The admissibility of petitions to courts is disputed. The prevailing doctrine and case law rejects this for specific court proceedings in order to preserve judicial independence (Müller/Schefer, Grundrechte in der Schweiz, 4th ed. 2008, p. 747; **BGE 119 Ia 53** E. 4c).

**N. 10** **Prohibition of disadvantage**: The petitioner may not suffer any disadvantages on account of submitting a petition. This encompasses legal, factual, direct and indirect disadvantages. The prohibition is absolute and knows no restrictions according to Art. 36 FC (Rhinow/Schefer/Uebersax, Schweizerisches Verfassungsrecht, 3rd ed. 2016, N 3856).

### 4. Legal Consequences

**N. 11** **Duty to take cognisance**: Art. 33 para. 2 FC obliges authorities to take cognisance of petitions. This means that the authority must receive the petition, take note of it and examine whether action is needed. Mere receipt without taking substantive cognisance is insufficient (St. Galler Kommentar BV, Ehrenzeller/Schindler/Schweizer/Vallender, 4th ed. 2023, Art. 33 N 18).

**N. 12** **No duty to respond**: The right of petition does not establish a claim to a formal response or a reasoned decision. The authority may take silent cognisance of the petition. However, a duty to respond may arise from cantonal law (BSK BV, Waldmann/Belser/Epiney, 2nd ed. 2024, Art. 33 N 14).

**N. 13** **No claim to specific action**: The right of petition does not give rise to a claim to specific official action or to the issuing of a ruling. The authority decides at its discretion according to its duties on further procedure (Federal Supreme Court judgment 1C_473/2010 of 31.1.2011 E. 2.2).

### 5. Disputed Issues

**N. 14** **Petitions to courts**: While Kiener (in: St. Galler Kommentar BV, Art. 33 N 23) categorically excludes petitions to courts, Schefer (Müller/Schefer, Grundrechte, p. 748) differentiates between general legal policy concerns (admissible) and influencing specific proceedings (inadmissible). The Federal Supreme Court follows the differentiating view (**BGE 119 Ia 53** E. 4c).

**N. 15** **Scope of duty to take cognisance**: Steinmann (BSK BV, Art. 33 N 19) represents a minimalist position: mere receipt suffices. In contrast, Häfelin/Haller/Keller/Thurnherr (Bundesstaatsrecht, N 856) demand substantive examination with a duty to forward to the competent body. Case law tends towards the more extensive interpretation.

**N. 16** **Electronic petitions**: The admissibility of e-petitions is disputed. Biaggini (BV Kommentar, 2nd ed. 2017, Art. 33 N 4) affirms them with reference to freedom of form. Glaser (in: Berner Kommentar, Art. 33 N 28) expresses scepticism because of authentication problems with mass petitions. The Federal Council recognises e-petitions in practice.

### 6. Practice Notes

**N. 17** **Demarcation from formal proceedings**: Petitions must be clearly distinguished from submissions in formal proceedings. If a submission designated as a petition is filed in pending proceedings, it is to be treated as a procedural submission with all procedural consequences (deadlines, legitimation, duty to give reasons).

**N. 18** **Mass petitions**: For collective or mass petitions, examination of a representative sample of signatures suffices. The invalidity of individual signatures does not render the petition inadmissible in its entirety (**BGE 104 Ia 434**). The authority should have a contact person designated.

**N. 19** **Duty to forward**: If a petition is received by an incompetent authority, it must be forwarded to the competent body. The petitioners are to be informed of the forwarding. In case of obvious incompetence, returning it to the petitioner may be indicated.

**N. 20** **Documentation**: Authorities should document the receipt and handling of petitions. This serves as proof of having taken cognisance and enables traceability in supervisory complaints. A standardised acknowledgment of receipt is recommended but not legally mandatory.
