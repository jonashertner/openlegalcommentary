# Art. 52 FC – Constitutional Order

## Doctrine

### 1. Legislative History

**N. 1** Art. 52 FC is one of the oldest continuity provisions of Swiss federal constitutional law. Its substance corresponds to Art. 5 of the former FC of 1874, which in turn traces back to Art. 5 of the former FC of 1848 — a reaction to the political unrest of the Restoration period and the experiences of the Sonderbund War. The few historical cases of intervention — in particular the Ticino Stabio incident (1876) and the Bernese unrest (1893) — all took place under the regime of the former FC of 1874 and were accompanied by **BGE 5 I 457** (1879) and **BGE 20 I 19** (1894), respectively.

**N. 2** The total revision of 1999 adopted the provision with editorial adjustments but no substantive change. In its dispatch on the new Federal Constitution, the Federal Council noted that Art. 52 FC codified a guarantee function of the Confederation vis-à-vis the cantons that had been established since 1848 (BBl 1997 I 221 f.). The constitutional commission discussed a clarification of the intervention threshold but refrained from making any change in order to preserve the proven flexibility of the provision (BBl 1997 I 228 f.). The parliamentary deliberations produced no substantive modifications.

---

### 2. Systematic Classification

**N. 3** Art. 52 FC is situated in Chapter 3 of Title 2 («Confederation and Cantons»), which gives concrete expression to the federal structure. The provision is a **competence norm** assigning to the Confederation a protective duty towards the cantons; it does not create subjective legal positions for individuals.

**N. 4** The provision interacts closely with several constitutional provisions:  
↔ Art. 51 FC (Federal guarantee of cantonal constitutions): The guarantee procedure under Art. 51 para. 2 FC is the preventive instrument of federal supervision; Art. 52 FC is the repressive instrument of intervention.  
↔ Art. 44 FC (Federal loyalty and mutual consideration): Standard for the nature and extent of intervention.  
→ Art. 3 FC (cantonal autonomy): Federal intervention under para. 2 overrides cantonal sovereignty; the principle of subsidiarity (→ Art. 5a FC) must be strictly observed.  
→ Art. 5 FC (rule of law principle): Federal intervention must also be proportionate and based on a legal foundation.  
→ Art. 173 para. 1 lit. d, Art. 185 para. 3 FC: Parallel competences of the Federal Assembly and the Federal Council to safeguard internal security (→ N. 19 on the points of contention).

**N. 5** Within the normative framework of federal constitutional law, Art. 52 FC — together with Arts. 44–49 FC (principles of federalism) and Arts. 50–53 FC (position of the cantons) — forms the institutional foundation of vertical separation of powers. Tschannen describes the provision as the «safety valve» of the federal state, ensuring the functional capacity of cantonal constitutional orders in extraordinary circumstances (Tschannen, Staatsrecht der Schweizerischen Eidgenossenschaft, 4th ed. 2016, § 19 N. 9).

### 3. Elements of the Provision / Normative Content

#### 3.1 Paragraph 1: Protective Mandate of the Confederation

**N. 6** Art. 52 para. 1 FC establishes the general protective mandate of the Confederation with regard to the «constitutional order» of the cantons. According to the prevailing doctrine, this concept encompasses not only the formal text of the cantonal constitution but the entire state and legal order of a canton anchored at the constitutional level: the constitutionally guaranteed institutions (cantonal parliament, cantonal government, courts), the fundamental procedures for the formation of will (elections, referendums, popular rights), and the cantonal separation of powers (Rhinow/Schefer/Uebersax, Schweizerisches Verfassungsrecht, 3rd ed. 2016, N. 773).

**N. 7** The protective mandate is directed against disruptions that threaten the constitutional order from outside (insurrection, armed groups) or from within (unconstitutional conduct by cantonal officeholders themselves). Doctrine is agreed that coup-like takeovers by cantonal officeholders fall within the scope of the protective provision (Häfelin/Haller/Keller/Thurnherr, Schweizerisches Bundesstaatsrecht, 10th ed. 2020, N. 1016).

**N. 8** According to the prevailing doctrine, para. 1 is to be understood as an institutional mandate with a programmatic character, which attains legally binding effect only in conjunction with the instruments of intervention under para. 2 (Belser/Massüger, BSK BV, Art. 52 N. 7; Ehrenzeller, St. Galler Kommentar zur BV, 4th ed. 2023, Art. 52 N. 5). It follows that para. 1 alone does not establish a directly enforceable duty of protection; the if-then scheme is concretised only through para. 2. → N. 16 on the points of contention concerning the independent normative content of para. 1.

#### 3.2 Paragraph 2: Conditions for Federal Intervention

**N. 9** Art. 52 para. 2 FC sets out the conditions for intervention in three cumulative requirements:

**(a) Disruption or threat to order:** A consolidated threat situation is sufficient; an already-occurred constitutional breach is not required. The criterion «threatened» demands a sufficiently concrete and imminent danger (Ehrenzeller, op. cit., Art. 52 N. 8). Mere political tensions or governmental crises without institutional breakdown do not suffice.

**(b) Affected canton:** The intervention is canton-specific; inter-cantonal conflicts are governed by Art. 44 para. 3 FC.

**(c) Subsidiarity:** The affected canton must be unable to restore order «on its own or with the help of other cantons». This three-tiered principle of subsidiarity (own means → inter-cantonal assistance → federal intervention) is constitutive: where subsidiarity is absent, intervention under para. 2 is impermissible even in the case of a serious disruption. The duty of inter-cantonal assistance follows additionally from Art. 44 para. 2 and Art. 48 FC (Rhinow/Schefer/Uebersax, op. cit., N. 778).

**N. 10** The instruments of intervention are not exhaustively regulated in the Federal Constitution. They include in particular: the dispatch of a federal commissioner, the deployment of federal police or the armed forces (→ Art. 58 para. 3, Art. 185 para. 4 FC), the issuance of emergency measures by the Federal Council (→ Art. 185 para. 3 FC), and federal decrees of the Federal Assembly (→ Art. 173 para. 1 lit. d FC). Historical practice under the former FC of 1874 shows that the dispatch of a federal commissioner was the typical instrument (cf. dispatch of Commissioner Bavier during the Ticino unrest of 1876).

**N. 11** Intervention under para. 2 must satisfy the general constitutional principles: it must be proportionate (→ Art. 5 para. 2 FC) and limited to what is necessary for the restoration of order. As soon as the canton is again able to maintain order on its own, the federal intervention must be terminated.

### 4. Legal Consequences

**N. 12** Art. 52 para. 1 FC establishes an objective-law protective mandate from which individuals cannot derive directly justiciable subjective rights. The protective mandate obliges the Federal Assembly and the Federal Council to hold appropriate measures in readiness and to deploy them when necessary.

**N. 13** The prevailing doctrine qualifies Art. 52 para. 2 FC, where the three conditions are met, as a **duty to intervene** («The Confederation *shall intervene*»), not merely as a power to intervene (Ehrenzeller, op. cit., Art. 52 N. 10; Häfelin/Haller/Keller/Thurnherr, op. cit., N. 1018). → N. 17 on the points of contention concerning the margin of assessment of the federal authorities.

**N. 14** The activation of the intervention mechanism is a political act in the institutional interplay between the Federal Council and the Federal Assembly (→ Art. 173 para. 1 lit. d, Art. 185 para. 3 FC). In practice, the initiative lies with the Federal Council; the Federal Assembly may instruct it or act itself. Para. 2 does not create a legal entitlement to intervention for cantons or cantonal inhabitants (Aubert/Mahon, Petit commentaire de la Constitution fédérale, 2003, Art. 52 N. 4).

**N. 15** Justiciability — whether a canton or its inhabitants can compel federal intervention through judicial proceedings — is denied by the prevailing view (Rhinow/Schefer/Uebersax, op. cit., N. 779; Häfelin/Haller/Keller/Thurnherr, op. cit., N. 1018). The political character of the intervention precludes comprehensive judicial review; federal jurisdiction of the Federal Tribunal for such constitutional disputes does not exist (→ Art. 189 FC).

### 5. Points of Contention

**N. 16** **Protective mandate: para. 1 as a programmatic statement or independent obligation?** Belser/Massüger (BSK BV, Art. 52 N. 7) and Ehrenzeller (St. Galler Kommentar zur BV, 4th ed. 2023, Art. 52 N. 5) qualify para. 1 as an institutional mandate with a programmatic character, which is given concrete expression through para. 2 and the ordinary supervisory mechanisms. By contrast, Tschannen (op. cit., § 19 N. 9) emphasises that para. 1 already establishes an independent constitutional obligation that requires general supervisory and protective measures independently of the intervention threshold of para. 2 — which justifies in particular the preventive federal supervision over cantonal constitutions. The controversy is of limited practical significance, since ordinary legal supervision by the Federal Tribunal and the guarantee procedure under Art. 51 para. 2 FC today constitute the principal instruments of protection.

**N. 17** **Duty to intervene versus power to intervene:** The wording «It shall intervene» in para. 2 is contested in the literature. Ehrenzeller (op. cit., Art. 52 N. 10) and Häfelin/Haller/Keller/Thurnherr (op. cit., N. 1018) read the provision as a bound duty: where the three conditions are met, the Confederation is obliged to intervene and retains no discretion. Rhinow/Schefer/Uebersax (op. cit., N. 777) adopt a more nuanced position: given the political nature of the condition «disrupted or threatened», the federal authorities retain a substantial margin of assessment in evaluating the situation, which causes the transition from bound duty to political discretion to appear fluid. The controversy reflects the fundamental tension between legal bindingness and political responsibility in emergency situations.

**N. 18** **Concept of «constitutional order»:** Biaggini (BV Kommentar, 2nd ed. 2017, Art. 52 N. 3) favours a narrow understanding: the object of protection is primarily the formal cantonal constitution together with the institutions directly anchored therein; extending protection to sub-constitutional institutions that are factually state-sustaining is not warranted, not least because such an extension would lower the intervention threshold in a manner that is problematic for federalism. Ehrenzeller (op. cit., Art. 52 N. 6) and Rhinow/Schefer/Uebersax (op. cit., N. 773), by contrast, advocate a broad concept: sub-constitutional institutions and procedures guaranteed by the cantonal constitution are also covered, insofar as they form part of the fundamental state order. The controversy becomes practically relevant when cantonal constitutional organs regulated at the statutory level (e.g. cantonal ombudspersons, constitutional courts) are abolished by ordinary legislation. The Federal Tribunal held in **BGE 143 I 272** E. 2.2 that cantonal constitutions take precedence over sub-constitutional cantonal law and are protected by the federal state guarantee.

**N. 19** **Relationship to Art. 185 FC:** Art. 52 para. 2 FC is not the sole federal constitutional basis for intervention in intra-cantonal affairs. Art. 185 para. 3 FC authorises the Federal Council to safeguard internal security by means of ordinances and rulings. Whether Art. 52 para. 2 FC constitutes an independent intervention norm separate from Art. 185 FC or is subsumed therein is contested. The prevailing view assumes a complementary function: Art. 52 FC specifically protects the constitutional order of the cantons, while Art. 185 FC is directed at external and internal security in general (Häfelin/Haller/Keller/Thurnherr, op. cit., N. 1017; Tschannen, op. cit., § 19 N. 12).

### 6. Practical Notes

**N. 20** Art. 52 FC is in practice a **dormant provision**: under the regime of the current Federal Constitution of 1999, not a single case of a formal federal intervention pursuant to Art. 52 para. 2 FC has occurred. The historical cases of intervention (Geneva 1864, Ticino 1876, Berne 1893/1894) all took place under the former FC of 1874 and in a fundamentally different political environment. The preventive effect of the provision, combined with consolidated federal practice and the rule-of-law judicial order, has so far prevented any activation under the new FC.

**N. 21** The protection of the constitutional order of the cantons today takes place primarily through three ordinary mechanisms: **(1)** The Federal Assembly's guarantee procedure under Art. 51 para. 2 FC (preventive review of cantonal constitutions); **(2)** abstract and concrete review of norms by the Federal Tribunal (annulment of unconstitutional cantonal enactments, exemplified by **BGE 133 I 206** on the regressive Obwalden tax rates); **(3)** adjudication of complaints for violation of cantonal constitutional rights under Art. 189 para. 1 lit. e FSCA (**BGE 143 I 272** E. 2.4).

**N. 22** For legal practitioners and public authorities: a petition to the Federal Council for intervention under Art. 52 para. 2 FC is admissible but does not create a legal entitlement or judicial reviewability. The appropriate instrument for violations of the cantonal constitutional order by cantonal organs is an appeal to the Federal Tribunal (Art. 82 lit. b FSCA; abstract review of norms) or a subsidiary constitutional complaint (Art. 113 FSCA).

**N. 23** In modern crisis scenarios — such as the COVID-19 pandemic of 2020–2022 — the question arose whether cantonal institutions could be factually undermined. In practice, the ordinary rules on the division of competences (Arts. 54 ff. FC, Epidemics Act) and Art. 185 FC applied; recourse to Art. 52 FC was not required. The provision nonetheless retains legal-political significance as an ultima ratio guarantee against a complete institutional breakdown at the cantonal level.

**N. 24** The relationship to Art. 51 FC is relevant in practice: the Federal Decree on the guarantee of a cantonal constitution also protects the relevant constitutional text indirectly under Art. 52 para. 1 FC. A violation of the guaranteed cantonal constitution by cantonal legislation can be adjudicated by the Federal Tribunal within the framework of Art. 189 para. 1 lit. e FC (**BGE 143 I 272** E. 2.4.6).
