# Art. 35 BGFA — Referendum

## Doctrine

### 1. Legislative History

**N. 1** Art. 35 BGFA contains the referendum clause of the Lawyers Act. In the Federal Council's dispatch of 28 April 1999, the provision appears as Art. 32 Draft-BGFA without substantive commentary; the Federal Council treated it as a standard legislative formula (BBl 1999 6013, 6060). The parliamentary deliberations in both the National Council and the Council of States proceeded without any discussion of this provision (AB 1999 N 1569; AB 1999 S 1173). The referendum clause was merely renumbered during the coordination phase. The BGFA was adopted on 23 June 2000 (AS 2002 863) and entered into force on 1 June 2002. The optional referendum under Art. 141 FC was not invoked; no popular vote took place.

**N. 2** Art. 35 BGFA is a provision that is technically required as a matter of legislative drafting, yet its content is entirely determined by superior constitutional law. Its wording corresponds to the standard formulation used since then by the Federal Council for the optional legislative referendum, as prescribed by the legislative drafting guidelines of the Federal Office of Justice (Federal Office of Justice, Legislative Drafting Guide, 3rd ed. 2007, para. 872 ff.): the referendum clause names the act and the official publication organ, refers to the referendum period under Art. 141 para. 1 FC, and thereby provides the relevant implementation framework for the legal practitioner.

---

### 2. Systematic Classification

**N. 3** Art. 35 BGFA is located in the final section of the Act and, together with Art. 36 BGFA (entry into force), forms the formal concluding part of the Act. The two articles serve different legislative-technical functions: Art. 35 BGFA governs democratic legitimacy review by the people (referendum clause), while Art. 36 BGFA contains the delegation norm for bringing the Act into force by the Federal Council. The entry into force itself is therefore not determined by Art. 35 BGFA but by Art. 36 BGFA; both provisions stand in a sequential relationship: only after the unused expiry of the referendum period under Art. 35 BGFA can Art. 36 BGFA be implemented by the Federal Council.

**N. 4** Systematically, Art. 35 BGFA stands outside the substantive regulatory framework of the Act. It contains no professional law requirements and creates no subjective rights or obligations for lawyers, authorities, or third parties. Its sole function is of a constitutional procedural nature: it draws attention to the right to a popular referendum existing under Art. 141 FC and thereby sets the framework for implementing the legislative resolution of the Federal Assembly.

**N. 5** Within the overall system of the BGFA, Art. 35 BGFA marks the transition from substantive professional law (Art. 1–34a BGFA) to the legislative-technical conclusion. The referendum clause makes visible that the federal legislature has subjected the Lawyers Act — unlike, for example, emergency legislation under Art. 165 FC — to the ordinary legislative procedure with referendum rights. This is significant from a professional law perspective: the BGFA thereby enjoys the legitimacy of a federal act subject to referendum and is subject to the supremacy of federal law over cantonal lawyers law under Art. 49 FC (cf. Fellmann/Zindel [eds.], Basler Kommentar zum Anwaltsgesetz, 2nd ed. 2019, Intro. N. 5).

→ Art. 36 BGFA (entry into force; sequential relationship with the referendum clause)
→ Art. 141 para. 1 lit. a FC (requirements of the optional referendum)
→ Art. 49 para. 1 FC (supremacy of federal law)
→ Art. 3 BGFA (reservation of cantonal law; based on the federal statutory foundation of the BGFA)

---

### 3. Content of the Provision

**N. 6** Art. 35 BGFA declares the BGFA subject to the optional referendum under Art. 141 FC. The optional referendum is the popular right by which a federal act adopted by the Federal Assembly may be put to a popular vote if, within 90 days of the official publication of the legislative resolution in the Federal Gazette, 50,000 eligible voters or 8 cantons request a popular referendum (Art. 141 para. 1 lit. a FC). The optional referendum must be strictly distinguished, both conceptually and functionally, from the popular initiative (Art. 138 f. FC), which constitutes a popular proposal for a constitutional amendment and requires 100,000 signatures. The referendum clause in Art. 35 BGFA concerns exclusively the optional popular referendum under Art. 141 FC.

**N. 7** The wording «This Act is subject to the optional referendum» corresponds to the standard formulation prescribed by the Federal Office of Justice for referendum clauses in federal acts (Legislative Drafting Guide, 3rd ed. 2007, para. 872). This standard formulation makes clear that the BGFA is not an act subject to the mandatory referendum under Art. 140 FC — which would be the case for a federal act with constitutional amendment content or for a federal act declared urgent that has no constitutional basis (Art. 140 para. 1 lit. b and c FC).

**N. 8** The referendum clause is limited to the statement that the Act is subject to the optional referendum. It regulates neither the period (which derives from Art. 141 para. 1 FC: 90 days), nor the signature quorum (50,000 eligible voters or 8 cantons pursuant to Art. 141 para. 1 lit. a FC), nor the modalities of the vote (which are governed by the Federal Act on Political Rights, SR 161.1). The provision therefore has a purely declaratory character: it merely repeats what constitutional law already provides for every act of the Federal Assembly that takes the form of a federal act and is not subject to the mandatory referendum under Art. 140 FC.

---

### 4. Legal Consequences

**N. 9** Art. 35 BGFA does not itself produce any legal consequences. Legal consequences arise only through its interaction with constitutional law (Art. 141 FC) and the Federal Act on Political Rights (FAPR, SR 161.1): if the referendum is not invoked, or if the popular vote is won, the Federal Council may bring the Act into force under Art. 36 BGFA. If the referendum is successfully invoked and the Act is rejected in the popular vote, it does not enter into force. In the present case, the optional referendum against the BGFA was not invoked; the Act entered into force on 1 June 2002 (AS 2002 863).

**N. 10** The professional law relevance of the absence of a referendum vote lies in the fact that the BGFA has since been regarded as an ordinary federal act that has remained democratically unchallenged. This circumstance lends support to the thesis that the federal statutory regulation of lawyers' freedom of movement — including the register requirements and professional rules — was accepted as such by the body of citizens. No consultation of the democratic sovereign took place; for revision of the BGFA, Art. 141 FC applies once again (cf. Nater/Zindel, Kommentar zum Anwaltsgesetz, 2020, N. 1 on Art. 35).

---

### 5. Contested Issues

**N. 11** Art. 35 BGFA is not controversial in legal scholarship. The only area of debate, in the field of general legislative law, concerns whether referendum clauses in federal acts have constitutive or merely declaratory significance. According to the prevailing view in constitutional law (Hangartner/Kley, Die demokratischen Rechte in Bund und Kantonen der Schweizerischen Eidgenossenschaft, 2000, N. 2180 f.), the clause is declaratory: every federal act is subject to the optional referendum by virtue of the Constitution, without an express clause being required. Its inclusion in the Act serves merely to provide legal clarity and an information function for legal practitioners and the public.

**N. 12** From a professional law perspective, the question has been raised whether the delegation of powers to the cantons in the BGFA (notably the maintenance of the register under Art. 6 BGFA and disciplinary supervision under Art. 14 ff. BGFA) is so extensive that a mandatory referendum under Art. 140 FC ought in fact to have taken place. Bohnet/Martenet (Droit de la profession d'avocat, 2009, para. 3) answer this in the negative: while the BGFA establishes a federally structured framework, it contains exhaustive federal subject-matter regulation in the field of professional law; constitutional amendment content within the meaning of Art. 140 para. 1 lit. a FC is not present. This conclusion corresponds to the undisputed finding in practice: the Act was brought into force without a popular vote.

---

### 6. Practical Notes

**N. 13** Art. 35 BGFA has no independent significance for ongoing legal practice. It is relevant to understanding the entry into force of the Act: the BGFA entered into force on 1 June 2002 because the optional referendum was not invoked (AS 2002 863). For revision matters — for example, the introduction of Art. 34a BGFA through the BGFA revision of 19 March 2021 (AS 2022 406) — the revision act is likewise subject to the optional referendum under Art. 141 FC; an express referendum clause in the revision act is again to be included pursuant to the Legislative Drafting Guide.

**N. 14** Of particular importance for legal practice is the relationship between Art. 35 BGFA and Art. 36 BGFA: the Federal Council may not set the date of entry into force under Art. 36 BGFA until after the expiry of the 90-day referendum period pursuant to Art. 141 para. 1 FC. If a quorum of 50,000 eligible voters or 8 cantons invokes the referendum, the Federal Council is bound by a suspensive condition: the Act may only be brought into force after an affirmative popular vote. This sequence — referendum period (Art. 35 BGFA in conjunction with Art. 141 FC) before the power to bring into force (Art. 36 BGFA) — has been codified by the Federal Office of Justice's Legislative Drafting Guide as mandatory under the rule of law (Legislative Drafting Guide, 3rd ed. 2007, para. 879).

**N. 15** In future revisions of the BGFA — notably in the event of a possible extension of the professional rules or a redesign of the EU/EFTA freedom of movement law — the optional referendum is the constitutionally prescribed instrument for popular participation. Bar organisations and professional associations wishing to challenge amendments to the BGFA must collect 50,000 signatures of eligible voters within 90 days of publication of the legislative resolution in the Federal Gazette in order to bring about a popular vote (Art. 141 para. 1 lit. a FC in conjunction with Art. 59 ff. FAPR).
