# Art. 19 BGFA — Limitation

## Doctrine

### 1. Legislative History

**N. 1** Art. 19 BGFA originates from the Federal Council's preliminary draft, which for the first time provided for a uniform federal-law limitation regime for the disciplinary prosecution of lawyers. Prior to the entry into force of the BGFA on 1 June 2002, disciplinary law was governed by cantonal law; several cantons did not recognise any statutory limitation periods at all and confined themselves to taking the passage of time into account as a matter of practice under the principle of expediency (e.g. the Canton of Lucerne, cf. judgment 2A.168/2005 of 6 September 2005, cons. 3.2).

**N. 2** In the Dispatch on the BGFA, the Federal Council justified the codification of limitation on the grounds of legal certainty and the protection of the lawyer concerned against an indefinitely threatening prosecution: «Der Anwalt muss wissen, dass ihm ein Fehlverhalten nicht ewig entgegengehalten werden kann» (BBl 1999 6013, 6058 item 233.5). The model of a one-year relative period and a ten-year absolute period was deliberately chosen following the example of other professional supervision statutes (cf. Art. 46 MedBG, Art. 39 RAG) and with regard to the systematic approach to limitation in criminal procedure law (Poledna, in: Fellmann/Zindel [eds.], BSK BGFA, 2nd ed. 2011, Art. 19 N. 1). Art. 19 para. 4 BGFA, which reserves the longer criminal-law limitation period where the conduct in question constitutes a criminal offence, was adopted during parliamentary deliberations without material amendment.

---

### 2. Systematic Classification

**N. 3** Art. 19 BGFA, together with Art. 17 (disciplinary measures), Art. 18 (supervisory authority) and Art. 20 (disciplinary register), forms the core of the disciplinary law of the BGFA. The limitation provision places a temporal constraint on the disciplinary discretion of the supervisory authority conferred by Art. 17. It is mandatory in nature and can neither be waived by agreement nor extended by the supervisory authority.

**N. 4** Art. 19 BGFA exhaustively governs disciplinary law (→ Art. 17 BGFA; **BGE 129 II 297** cons. 1.1). The cantons are precluded from extending the limitation periods through cantonal lawyers' law or from establishing their own limitation rules for the federal law applicable under the BGFA. However, they retain scope to provide their own time limits for supervisory measures that do not constitute disciplinary sanctions within the meaning of Art. 17 BGFA (e.g. remedial orders under cantonal law) (judgment 2C_665/2010 of 24 May 2011, cons. 6.1).

**N. 5** The provision is functionally linked to Art. 20 BGFA (disciplinary register): the deletion periods of the register (→ Art. 20 BGFA) and the limitation periods of Art. 19 BGFA together pursue the objective of placing a temporal limit on the legal consequences of past misconduct. In **BGE 150 II 308** (2024) the Federal Supreme Court clarified the interaction between Art. 19 para. 3 BGFA and Art. 20 BGFA in the context of the determination of sanctions: disciplinary measures that have already been deleted from the register but fall within the absolute limitation period may be taken into account when determining new sanctions.

**N. 6** Cross-references: → Art. 17 BGFA (disciplinary measures whose prosecution is time-barred); → Art. 18 BGFA (supervisory authority as holder of the power to manage time limits); → Art. 20 BGFA (disciplinary register and deletion periods); ↔ Art. 46 MedBG (identical parallel provision in the Federal Act on Health Professions); ↔ Art. 39 RAG (identical provision for auditors); → Art. 97 ff. StGB (criminal-law limitation periods under para. 4).

### 3. Elements of the Provision / Normative Content

#### 3.1 Relative Limitation Period (para. 1)

**N. 7** The relative limitation period is **one year** from the time at which the supervisory authority «had knowledge of the incident in question». The decisive moment is the authority's actual knowledge, not the point at which the authority could or should have obtained knowledge. The Federal Supreme Court expressly confirmed this in judgment 2C_665/2010, cons. 3.3, with reference to the «clear wording» of Art. 19 para. 1 BGFA: a mere reference to a potentially objectionable activity (in that case: a website address) does not set the period running as long as the authority does not yet have knowledge of the specific objectionable content.

**N. 8** «Knowledge of the incident in question» requires that the supervisory authority be aware of the facts to an extent sufficient to assess whether a breach of professional duties is conceivable at all (Poledna, BSK BGFA, Art. 19 N. 3; Fellmann, Anwaltsrecht, 2nd ed. 2017, para. 696). Merely latent or general knowledge of the lawyer's activity does not suffice.

**N. 9** In the case of **continuing offences** (ongoing breach of duty, e.g. unlawful conduct persistently maintained on a website), the one-year period does not begin to run until the day on which the authority obtains knowledge of the continuing violation. A fresh starting point for the period may arise for each newly identified act of misconduct (judgment 2C_665/2010, cons. 3.4, applying Art. 98 lit. c StGB on continuing offences by analogy).

#### 3.2 Interruption (para. 2)

**N. 10** The one-year period is interrupted by «any investigative act of the supervisory authority». This concept is to be construed broadly. Acts recognised as interrupting the period include in particular: the formal opening of disciplinary proceedings, the request for a statement from the lawyer concerned, further inquiries by the authority into the facts, additional taking of evidence, and procedural orders made by the appellate authority in the course of pending disciplinary proceedings (judgment 2A.168/2005, cons. 3.1; judgment 2A.496/2005, cons. 3.2; judgment 2C_1065/2016, cons. 2.3; Poledna, BSK BGFA, Art. 19 N. 8).

**N. 11** In judgment 2A.496/2005, cons. 3.2, the Federal Supreme Court expressly clarified that **procedural orders of the appellate authority** also interrupt limitation, in so far as they serve the conduct of the disciplinary proceedings. The same applies to referral-back decisions of the appellate authority to the supervisory authority (confirmed in judgment 2C_1065/2016, cons. 2.3). Purely internal administrative orders of the authority do not interrupt limitation, even if they are communicated to the parties (Poledna, BSK BGFA, Art. 19 N. 5; judgment 2C_114/2020, cons. 2.2.2).

**N. 12** Interruption of limitation takes effect at the moment the investigative act is **carried out** (e.g. the decision to open disciplinary proceedings) and not only upon notification to the person concerned. The Federal Supreme Court confirmed this in judgment 2C_114/2020, cons. 2.4.2, applying Art. 309 para. 3 StPO by analogy.

#### 3.3 Absolute Limitation Period (para. 3)

**N. 13** The **absolute** limitation period is **ten years** after the «incident in question». It runs independently of the supervisory authority's knowledge and cannot be interrupted by any investigative act. The relevant point in time is the commission of the act giving rise to the complaint (or, in the case of omissions, the time at which the required act ought to have been performed); in the case of continuing offences, the period begins to run from the end of the ongoing breach of duty. This period protects the lawyer against indefinite disciplinary liability for past misconduct (BBl 1999 6058; Poledna, BSK BGFA, Art. 19 N. 6).

**N. 14** In judgment 2C_834/2010 (revision law), the Federal Supreme Court applied Art. 19 para. 3 BGFA as a benchmark for analogous fields and emphasised that after ten years have elapsed, breaches of professional duties committed in the past «in principle can no longer be held against a person concerned» (cons. 6.2.4). This statement is applicable by analogy to lawyers' law (Poledna, BSK BGFA, Art. 19 N. 6).

#### 3.4 Longer Criminal-Law Period (para. 4)

**N. 15** Where the breach of professional rules also constitutes a criminal offence, the **longer** limitation period provided for under criminal law applies. Para. 4 is a lex specialis in relation to paras. 1 and 3 and displaces them where the criminal-law limitation period exceeds the lawyers' disciplinary periods. The provision requires that the breach of professional rules satisfies the elements of a criminal offence; a criminal conviction is not, however, required (Poledna, BSK BGFA, Art. 19 N. 9; Bohnet/Martenet, Droit de la profession d'avocat, 2009, N. 2636).

**N. 16** The applicable criminal-law limitation period is the one in force at the time of the disciplinary authority's decision, i.e. for criminal offences subject to the new limitation law, the periods under Art. 97 StGB (relative period by category of offence). The Federal Supreme Court addressed this question in **BGE 150 II 308**, cons. 4, holding that the reference in para. 4 to the «longer limitation period provided for under criminal law» as a matter of principle refers to the absolute criminal prosecution limitation period and not merely to the relative one.

---

### 4. Legal Consequences

**N. 17** A limitation period that has expired constitutes a **procedural bar** that must be raised ex officio. Where limitation has occurred, the supervisory authority may no longer impose a disciplinary sanction; any proceedings already opened must be discontinued. The limitation defence may be raised at every level of proceedings, including before the Federal Supreme Court.

**N. 18** Limitation affects only **disciplinary prosecution** (Art. 17 BGFA), not supervisory measures of other kinds (e.g. deletion from the register of lawyers under Art. 9 BGFA for failure to meet the conditions for registration). Within the framework of its supervisory duty, the supervisory authority may examine the conditions for registration even after the limitation period has expired (→ Art. 9 BGFA).

**N. 19** Interruption of limitation by an investigative act (para. 2) causes the one-year period to begin to run afresh, but always only within the absolute ten-year period of para. 3. A new relative period begins on the day of the interrupting act (by analogy with Art. 137 CO; cf. Poledna, BSK BGFA, Art. 19 N. 8).

### 5. Contested Issues

#### 5.1 Meaning of «Knowledge» (para. 1): Actual vs. Constructive Knowledge

**N. 20** Academic commentary is divided on whether the start of the relative limitation period is determined by the supervisory authority's **actual** («positive») knowledge or whether it is sufficient that the authority ought to have obtained knowledge through the exercise of due diligence («constructive knowledge»). Poledna (BSK BGFA, Art. 19 N. 3) and Fellmann (Anwaltsrecht, para. 696) favour reliance on actual knowledge, based on the wording («had knowledge»). The Federal Supreme Court endorsed this view and expressly rejected constructive knowledge in judgment 2C_665/2010, cons. 3.3: the protection afforded to the lawyer by the absolute period (para. 3) offsets the interest in an early start to the period.

**N. 21** Bohnet/Martenet (Droit de la profession d'avocat, 2009, N. 2630), by contrast, lean towards a teleological interpretation that requires the supervisory authority to make reasonable enquiries as soon as a person under a duty to report draws attention to a possible breach. The practical relevance of this position is, however, limited, given that the Federal Supreme Court has definitively resolved the question in favour of the textual interpretation.

#### 5.2 Scope of the Concept of «Investigative Act» (para. 2)

**N. 22** It is unclear whether a **precautionary stay** of the disciplinary proceedings ordered by the supervisory authority (e.g. pending the conclusion of parallel criminal proceedings) constitutes an investigative act and thus interrupts limitation. Poledna (BSK BGFA, Art. 19 N. 8) answers this in the affirmative, provided the order is procedurally related; purely internal administrative acts that are not communicated to the person concerned are, however, excluded. The Federal Supreme Court left this question open (judgment 2C_665/2010, cons. 3.5).

**N. 23** The question whether a **referral-back decision** of the appellate authority to the supervisory authority (without any new investigative act) interrupts limitation was also long unresolved. The Federal Supreme Court answered it in the affirmative in judgment 2A.496/2005, cons. 3.2, confirmed in judgment 2C_1065/2016, cons. 2.3: procedural orders made in appellate proceedings serve the conduct of the disciplinary proceedings and therefore constitute interrupting acts.

#### 5.3 Relationship Between Para. 3 and Para. 4 Where Periods Compete

**N. 24** It is disputed whether para. 4 extends only the **absolute** criminal-law limitation period (Art. 97 StGB) or also the **relative** criminal-law period. Poledna (BSK BGFA, Art. 19 N. 9) takes the view that para. 4 displaces all criminal-law limitation periods (both relative and absolute) in so far as they are longer than the disciplinary periods. Bohnet/Martenet (Droit de la profession d'avocat, N. 2636) share this view. The Federal Supreme Court confirmed this interpretation in principle in **BGE 150 II 308**, cons. 4, but clarified that the reference to the criminal-law period is to be understood dynamically and applies the most lenient law at the time of the offence.

#### 5.4 Distinction Between Disciplinary Limitation and Deletion from the Disciplinary Register

**N. 25** A systematic tension exists between the absolute limitation period (10 years, Art. 19 para. 3) and the deletion periods of the disciplinary register (Art. 20 BGFA). In **BGE 150 II 308** the Federal Supreme Court clarified that, in the determination of sanctions, disciplinary measures already deleted from the register may be taken into account if the absolute limitation period under Art. 19 para. 3 BGFA has not yet expired. This has attracted criticism in the literature: Hinsler (ZBJV 2025, p. 120 ff.) regards it as an erosion of the protective purpose of the deletion periods.

### 6. Practical Notes

**N. 26** **Calculation of time limits:** The one-year period (para. 1) begins on the day on which the supervisory authority actually obtains knowledge of the incident in question. In the case of written complaints, this is the date of receipt, provided the content of the complaint points with sufficient specificity to a breach of professional rules. For the lawyer who has a potential prosecution in view, the starting point of the period is regularly a decisive procedural issue.

**N. 27** **Active management of proceedings by the lawyer under investigation:** The complainant in judgment 2C_1065/2016 attempted, by accumulating recusal requests and appellate proceedings, to delay the disciplinary proceedings long enough for the one-year period to expire without interruption. The Federal Supreme Court characterised this procedural strategy as an unsuccessful attempt at circumvention, since the procedural orders issued in the appellate proceedings also interrupted limitation. Abusive delay of proceedings therefore affords no limitation protection.

**N. 28** **Parallel criminal investigation:** Where a criminal investigation is running in parallel to the disciplinary proceedings, the supervisory authority must examine whether para. 4 is applicable (breach of professional rules as a criminal offence). If so, the authority may stay the disciplinary proceedings until the conclusion of the criminal proceedings without having to fear the expiry of the short one-year period — provided that the stay order itself qualifies as an investigative act within the meaning of para. 2. To avoid legal uncertainty, it is advisable in such constellations to document every procedural act consistently.

**N. 29** **Continuing offences:** In the case of ongoing breaches of duty (e.g. a permanent conflict of interest, maintenance of an impermissible practice organisation), the relative period begins afresh for each newly identified act. For the lawyer under investigation, this means that the limitation period cannot be obtained by mere inaction as long as the non-compliant situation persists (judgment 2C_665/2010, cons. 3.4). Only the definitive termination of the breach of duty sets the point in time that is decisive for the calculation of the period.

**N. 30** **Analogical application to other professional supervision statutes:** Art. 19 BGFA serves in practice as an interpretive reference for identical limitation provisions in notarial law (§ 40 BeurG/AG, cantonal Notarial Act of the Canton of Berne Art. 48 NG/BE), in audit law (Art. 39 RAG) and in medical professions law (Art. 46 MedBG). The case law on Art. 19 BGFA is indirectly authoritative for these parallel provisions (judgment 2C_114/2020, cons. 2.2.1, referring to Art. 19 BGFA as the model provision for § 40 BeurG/AG).
