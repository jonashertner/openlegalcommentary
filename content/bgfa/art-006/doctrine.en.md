# Art. 6 BGFA — Entry in the Register

## Doctrine

### 1. Legislative History

**N. 1** The BGFA is the product of decades of efforts to harmonise intercantonal lawyers' law. Until its entry into force on 1 June 2002, admission to the legal profession and the intercantonal licence to practise were governed exclusively by cantonal law; no federal register of lawyers existed (BBl 1999 6013, para. 114; judgment 2C_897/2015 of 25 May 2016 consid. 8).

**N. 2** On 28 April 1999 the Federal Council submitted its dispatch on the Lawyers Act (BBl 1999 6013). The primary objectives of the Act were to facilitate the intercantonal mobility of lawyers and to implement the bilateral agreements with the EU (BBl 1999 6020, para. 13). To this end, the Federal Council's draft provided that the cantonal register of lawyers would form the basis for a licence to practise valid throughout Switzerland.

**N. 3** With regard to Art. 6(1), the Federal Council noted in its dispatch that even a lawyer with several offices need only register in the canton in which his or her principal office is located (BBl 1999 6046). Parliamentary deliberations confirmed this view without reservation (AB 1999 N 1553; AB 1999 S 1164), which the Federal Supreme Court in **BGE 131 II 639** consid. 3.3 drew upon as the interpretive basis for holding that simultaneous entry in several cantonal registers is excluded.

**N. 4** Whether employed lawyers could be entered in the register was the central controversial issue in the legislative process. The original draft (Art. 7 lit. e in conjunction with Art. 11 lit. b of the draft) would have left the supervisory authorities broad discretion to handle the matter on a case-by-case basis (BBl 1999 6038 f., para. 172.17). However, the parliamentary majority opted for a more restrictive, institutional-structural definition of independence, which was reflected in the definitive wording of Art. 8(1)(d) BGFA (AB 1999 N 1556–1566; thereon **BGE 130 II 87** consid. 5.1.1 p. 99 f.).

**N. 5** Art. 6(4) BGFA, which grants the cantonal bar association a right of appeal against register entries, stems from the concern to protect the collective interest in upholding the requirement of independence by procedural means; the right of appeal also extends to entries of third parties (**BGE 130 II 87** consid. 1 p. 90).

---

### 2. Systematic Classification

**N. 6** Art. 6 BGFA is situated in the first section of the Act («Freedom of movement and the register», Arts. 1–11) and constitutes the core mechanism of the federal law right of free movement. Read together with → Art. 4 BGFA (intercantonal freedom of movement) and → Art. 5 BGFA (cantons' obligation to maintain the register), Art. 6 BGFA establishes the admission procedure for the monopoly activity. The register entry replaces the former intercantonal licence to practise.

**N. 7** The register is the connecting element between the minimum requirements under federal law (→ Art. 7 BGFA: professional requirements; → Art. 8 BGFA: personal requirements) and the legal consequences (→ Art. 4 BGFA: right to practise throughout Switzerland without further authorisation; → Art. 12 BGFA: professional rules; ↔ Art. 9 BGFA: removal as the mirror image of entry). The BGFA exhaustively governs the conditions for entry in the register (judgment 2C_897/2015 consid. 5.3 with reference to **BGE 130 II 270** consid. 1.1 and consid. 3.1.1; Staehelin/Oetiker, BSK BGFA, Art. 6 N. 18).

**N. 8** Parallel provisions exist for lawyers from EU or EFTA member states: the permanent practice under the original professional title requires entry in a public list (→ Arts. 27–29 BGFA); entry in the cantonal register of lawyers is possible under → Arts. 30–34a BGFA and places such lawyers on an equal footing with domestically licensed lawyers (→ Art. 30(2) BGFA). The entry governed by Art. 6 BGFA, by contrast, is reserved for holders of a cantonal licence to practise law (Abs. 1).

**N. 9** Art. 6 BGFA applies exclusively to the so-called monopoly activity, i.e. professional representation of parties before judicial authorities within the meaning of → Art. 2(1) BGFA. Advisory activities without a judicial dimension are not subject to the registration requirement (**BGE 130 II 87** consid. 3 p. 92; judgment 2C_897/2015 consid. 5.2.1).

### 3. Constituent Elements / Normative Content

#### 3.1 Persons Entitled to Register (para. 1)

**N. 10** Persons entitled to be entered in the register are lawyers who (i) hold a cantonal licence to practise law and (ii) wish to represent parties before judicial authorities. The formulation «shall have themselves entered» indicates that registration does not occur automatically but requires an application (→ Art. 5(1) BGFA; Fellmann, Anwaltsrecht, 2nd ed. 2017, para. 684 f.). The cantonal licence to practise law is a formal condition for admission; it is primarily evidence of professional competence and, as such, does not directly confer the right to represent parties, but merely constitutes a precondition for entry in the register (judgment 2C_897/2015 consid. 7.2.2).

**N. 11** The register of the canton in which the lawyer has his or her business address has jurisdiction. According to the practice of the Federal Supreme Court, «business address» means the address of the office from which the legal activity is principally exercised (**BGE 131 II 639** consid. 3.5). The Federal Council expressly clarified in its dispatch that even a lawyer with multiple offices may only be entered once, in the canton of the principal office (BBl 1999 6046).

**N. 12** Simultaneous entry in several cantonal registers is impermissible (**BGE 131 II 639** consid. 3.3–3.4). If a lawyer moves his or her principal activity to another canton, he or she is obliged to have the entry in the previous register deleted (→ Art. 9 BGFA) and to register in the new canton (**BGE 131 II 639** consid. 3.5; Staehelin/Oetiker, BSK BGFA, Art. 6 N. 12). Multiple entries would provoke jurisdictional conflicts in disciplinary supervision (→ Art. 16 BGFA) and would contradict the teleological unity of the Act (**BGE 131 II 639** consid. 3.4.2 p. 643 f.).

#### 3.2 Examination and Decision by the Supervisory Authority (para. 2)

**N. 13** The cantonal supervisory authority (→ Art. 14 BGFA) decides on the register entry. Entry is made once the authority has established that the requirements of Art. 7 (professional) and Art. 8 (personal) BGFA are satisfied. The procedure is initiated upon application (**BGE 130 II 87** consid. 6.1 p. 104 f.). The lawyer must provide all information and supporting documents necessary to assess the requirements of Art. 8 BGFA (**BGE 130 II 87** consid. 6.1 p. 104 f.); this applies in particular to information regarding the employment relationship of employed lawyers. The duty to cooperate is strict: a person who refuses to provide the necessary information cannot claim the right to be entered in the register (**BGE 130 II 87** consid. 7 p. 107 f.; judgment 2A.110/2003 of 29 January 2004 consid. 7).

**N. 14** The supervisory authority has no genuine margin of discretion in its examination: once the statutory requirements have been established, there is a legal entitlement to registration (Bohnet/Martenet, Droit de la profession d'avocat, 2009, para. 576 ff.; Fellmann, Anwaltsrecht, 2nd ed. 2017, para. 684). Conversely, registration must be refused if any one of the requirements is not met. A refusal to register on grounds of lack of independence (→ Art. 8(1)(d) BGFA) touches upon the scope of protection of Art. 27 FC (freedom of economic activity), which must be taken into account when interpreting the concept of independence (**BGE 130 II 87** consid. 3 p. 92 f.).

#### 3.3 Publication (para. 3)

**N. 15** Every register entry must be published in an official cantonal publication organ (e.g. the cantonal official gazette). Publication serves transparency and the information of the public seeking legal assistance. It is of constitutive significance for third-party awareness, in particular in the context of the right of appeal (para. 4). The cantonal legal orders determine the relevant publication organ.

#### 3.4 Right of Appeal of the Bar Association (para. 4)

**N. 16** Against entries in the cantonal register, the right of appeal also extends to the cantonal bar association. This rule is an exception to the principle that only directly affected persons have standing to appeal, and is intended to safeguard the collective interest of the legal profession in upholding the requirement of independence (**BGE 130 II 87** consid. 1 p. 90). The association's right of appeal enables review by the Federal Supreme Court as an administrative court of appeal (today: appeal in public-law matters, → Arts. 82 ff. BGG). The association may also act against entries of third parties, i.e. persons who applied to be entered in the register (**BGE 130 II 87** consid. 1; judgment 2A.295/2003 of 3 June 2004 consid. 1.4). The right of appeal under Art. 6(4) BGFA exists independently of any general right of associations to bring proceedings under cantonal law.

### 4. Legal Consequences

**N. 17** The register entry under Art. 6 BGFA confers on the registered lawyer the right to represent parties before judicial authorities throughout Switzerland without any further authorisation (→ Art. 4 BGFA). It is therefore the decisive basis for intercantonal freedom of movement. In addition, the entry opens up the scope of application of the BGFA disciplinary law (→ Arts. 12 ff., Art. 17 BGFA) and supervision by the cantonal supervisory authority (→ Art. 14 BGFA).

**N. 18** The register entry entitles the lawyer to use the professional title customary in the canton of registration (→ Art. 11(1) BGFA) and to indicate the entry in business dealings (→ Art. 11(2) BGFA). It also establishes a duty of publication on the part of the supervisory authority (para. 3).

**N. 19** The disappearance of any one of the conditions for registration under Art. 7 or Art. 8 BGFA leads to deletion from the register (→ Art. 9 BGFA). The register entry is an ongoing, not merely a once-and-for-all, requirement. In particular, the existence of certificates of unpaid debts (→ Art. 8(1)(c) BGFA) or the loss of independence (→ Art. 8(1)(d) BGFA) may, upon occurrence of the relevant circumstances, lead to deletion (judgment 2C_897/2015 consid. 5.2.1).

**N. 20** Refusal of registration and deletion from the register are administrative (not disciplinary) measures; they serve to prevent unauthorised practice of the profession, but are not punitive in character (judgment 2C_897/2015 consid. 5.2; **BGE 137 II 425** consid. 7.2 p. 429). They do not preclude a subsequent re-entry once the impediment has been removed.

### 5. Disputed Questions

#### 5.1 Multiple Entries in Different Cantonal Registers

**N. 21** Staehelin/Oetiker, in BSK BGFA, Art. 6 N. 12, take the view that where a lawyer has several business addresses, registration must be effected in the canton of the principal activity; multiple entries are impermissible. The Federal Supreme Court expressly endorsed this position in **BGE 131 II 639** consid. 3.5. The appellant in those proceedings had argued that the advertising benefit of multiple entries and the interests of large law firms with offices in several cantons justified permitting simultaneous entries; the Federal Supreme Court rejected this argument on the basis of the risk of jurisdictional conflicts in disciplinary supervision (**BGE 131 II 639** consid. 3.4.2 p. 643 f.).

#### 5.2 Independence of Employed Lawyers as a Condition for Registration

**N. 22** The question of whether, and under what conditions, an employed lawyer may claim the right to be entered in the register was disputed in early literature and practice:

- **Nater** (SJZ 98/2002, p. 364) and, with some tendency, **Meier** (Plädoyer 2000/5, p. 40) argued that employed lawyers who had previously been admitted under cantonal law could claim registration on the basis of the transitional provision of Art. 36 BGFA even if they did not satisfy the more restrictive requirement of Art. 8(1)(d) BGFA.
- **Hess** (SJZ 98/2002, p. 493 f.) considered this to be excluded: the requirement of independence had already been a central precondition prior to the entry into force of the BGFA, and Art. 36 BGFA did not derogate from it, since that transitional provision covered only the professional, not the personal, requirements.

The Federal Supreme Court sided with Hess (**BGE 130 II 87** consid. 8.2 p. 109 f.): Art. 36 BGFA concerns exclusively the recognition of evidence of professional competence (professional requirements); a person who does not satisfy the personal requirements under Art. 8 BGFA cannot rely on the transitional provision.

**N. 23** The scope of the institutional concept of independence was also disputed. During the parliamentary deliberations, members such as Hochreutener (AB 1999 N 1557) and Nabholz (AB 1999 N 1557 f.) had advocated for a case-by-case examination tailored to the individual circumstances. However, the parliamentary majority opted for the institutional approach, which focuses on the structure of the employment relationship rather than the specific mandate (**BGE 130 II 87** consid. 5.1.1 p. 99 f.). The Federal Supreme Court nevertheless emphasised that this institutional presumption is rebuttable: a lawyer employed by an unregistered employer may claim registration if he or she creates a clear structural separation and his or her mandate activity clearly falls outside the scope of the employer's activities (**BGE 130 II 87** consid. 5.2 p. 102 f., consid. 6 p. 104 ff.; judgments 2A.110/2003 consid. 5.2 and 2A.295/2003 consid. 3).

#### 5.3 Right of Appeal of the Bar Association

**N. 24** Art. 6(4) BGFA expressly grants the bar association a right of appeal. Academic commentary recognises that this right is autonomous and independent of any general right of associations to bring proceedings (Fellmann/Zindel [eds.], BSK BGFA, Art. 6 N. 20 ff.; Bohnet/Martenet, Droit de la profession d'avocat, 2009, para. 576). The question of whether other associations or third parties, such as out-of-canton bar associations, could also have a right of appeal has not been finally resolved; however, the wording («the bar association of the canton concerned») makes clear that only the association of the canton whose register is affected has standing.

#### 5.4 Character of the Cantonal Licence to Practise Law

**N. 25** According to the case law, the cantonal licence to practise law as a precondition for registration (para. 1) has primarily the character of evidence of professional competence, not of a police licence to practise; the actual licence to exercise the monopoly activity is only established by the register entry (judgment 2C_897/2015 consid. 7.2.2). This classification is significant: it means that revocation of the licence by the canton (upon loss of personal requirements) — to the extent provided for under cantonal law — is not incompatible with the BGFA, since federal law does not exhaustively regulate the acquisition and revocation of the licence (judgment 2C_897/2015 consid. 6.2.3, 6.3). This is not uncontested in academic commentary: **Kettiger** (Jusletter 28 September 2009, p. 4 f.) argued that federal law leaves no room for cantonal personal requirements regarding the licence; the case law and prevailing academic opinion (Nater, BSK BGFA, Art. 3 N. 3; Bohnet/Othenin-Girard/Schweizer, CR LLCA, Art. 3 N. 16; Fellmann, Anwaltsrecht, 2nd ed. 2017, para. 684 f.) disagreed with that view.

### 6. Practical Notes

**N. 26** **Business address as the connecting factor:** Registration is effected in the canton of the business address. For lawyers with multiple offices, only the canton of the principal activity is determinative. A change of principal activity to another canton triggers the obligation to transfer the register entry; as long as the lawyer is predominantly active in the previous canton, there is neither an obligation nor a right to register in the new canton (**BGE 131 II 639** consid. 3.5).

**N. 27** **Duty to cooperate upon registration:** Lawyers in an employment relationship must provide the supervisory authority with complete information about their employment. In particular for full-time employees, the following documents and clarifications are required: (a) confirmation by the employer of consent to the secondary legal activity; (b) express exclusion of any right of instruction by the employer with regard to independently conducted mandates; (c) exclusion of the representation of the employer, affiliated companies, and the employer's clients; (d) proof of a separate business address and physical separation of activities; (e) proof of measures to safeguard professional secrecy (**BGE 130 II 87** consid. 6.3 p. 105 ff.; judgment 2A.110/2003 consid. 6.3).

**N. 28** **Full-time employment is not an absolute bar:** Full-time employment alone does not preclude registration. However, the lawyer must credibly demonstrate that, despite full-time employment with an unregistered employer, independence in respect of the secondary legal activity is guaranteed. For part-time employees, submission of a correspondingly structured employment contract may be dispensed with where the nature of the employment relationship already makes clear that there is no risk of impairment of independence (**BGE 130 II 87** consid. 6.2, 6.3.1 p. 104 ff.).

**N. 29** **Representation of the employer and the employer's clients:** The representation of one's own employer as well as the employer's clients and affiliated companies is in principle incompatible with the requirement of independence and cannot be waived by contractual agreement (judgment 2A.295/2003 consid. 3). The presumption of dependence cannot be rebutted in respect of such mandates.

**N. 30** **Change of circumstances after registration:** If, following a register entry, a circumstance arises that impairs independence (e.g. taking up employment with an unregistered employer), the lawyer must inform the supervisory authority (by analogy with Art. 12(a) BGFA; **BGE 130 II 87** consid. 7 p. 108). The supervisory authority may request the necessary information and, if appropriate, arrange for deletion under → Art. 9 BGFA.

**N. 31** **No registration required for advisory activities:** The registration requirement under Art. 6 BGFA applies exclusively to the representation of parties before judicial authorities within the meaning of Art. 2(1) BGFA. Lawyers engaged exclusively in advisory work require neither registration nor a cantonal licence to practise law (judgment 2C_897/2015 consid. 5.2.1, 7.2.1; Bohnet, Droit des professions judiciaires, 3rd ed. 2014, p. 27 para. 29).

**N. 32** **Right of appeal of the bar association:** Cantonal bar associations are entitled to lodge an appeal against register entries, even if they are not themselves a party to a specific set of proceedings. This right is confined exclusively to the association of the canton whose register is concerned. The right of appeal serves the collective safeguarding of the requirement of independence and is not intended as a professional-political instrument for restricting competition (**BGE 130 II 87** consid. 3 p. 92 f.).
