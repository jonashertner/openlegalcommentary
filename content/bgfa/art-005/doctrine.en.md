# Art. 5 BGFA — Cantonal Register of Lawyers

## Doctrine

### 1. Legislative History

**N. 1** The BGFA was adopted on 23 June 2000 and entered into force on 1 June 2002. It replaced the fragmented system of cantonal lawyers' acts in the area of intercantonal freedom of movement. The central regulatory objective, as the title of the Act already indicates, was to facilitate the mobility of lawyers across cantonal boundaries; the register serves as the technical instrument for achieving this goal (BBl 1999 6013, 6039 ff.).

**N. 2** An earlier draft of the Federal Council had envisaged that lawyers would be required to register in the registers of *all* cantons in which they had a business address. In its Message of 28 April 1999, the Federal Council deliberately departed from this approach and instead established the principle of a *single register canton*: a lawyer with several law offices must register in the canton «in which he maintains his principal office» (BBl 1999 6046). The Federal Assembly agreed to the draft on this point without reservation (AB 1999 N 1553; AB 1999 S 1164).

**N. 3** During the parliamentary deliberations, the scope of the register's contents was debated controversially. In particular, the question of the public availability of disciplinary measures was disputed; the legislature ultimately opted for a system that retains non-deleted measures in the register and makes them accessible to certain authorities (Art. 10 BGFA), without providing for general publication — a policy decision that the Federal Supreme Court recently confirmed (**BGE 150 II 308 E. 7.5**).

---

### 2. Systematic Classification

**N. 4** Art. 5 is situated in the first section of the BGFA («Freedom of Movement») and, together with Art. 4 (entitlement) and Art. 6 (registration procedure), forms the core of the register system. Art. 5 establishes the registration obligation on the cantonal side, Art. 6 governs the application obligation and the procedure on the part of lawyers, and Art. 9 regulates deletion as the mirror image of registration (→ Art. 9 BGFA).

**N. 5** The provision has a dual character: as an *organisational norm* it charges each canton with maintaining a register and establishing a supervisory authority for that purpose (Art. 5 para. 3 BGFA; → Art. 14 BGFA). As a *substantive norm* it exhaustively and uniformly establishes under federal law which data the register must contain (Art. 5 para. 2 BGFA). The Federal Supreme Court has repeatedly characterised the register law order of the BGFA as exhaustive under federal law and held cantonal additions to be inadmissible (**BGE 130 II 270 E. 3**; **BGE 150 II 308 E. 7.4**).

**N. 6** Art. 5 is closely connected to Art. 3 BGFA: Art. 3 para. 1 BGFA preserves the cantonal right to determine the requirements for obtaining the patent of lawyer, while Art. 5 regulates the register-law admission to intercantonal practice under federal law (→ Art. 3 BGFA). The register thus connects cantonal patent law with the federal law right to freedom of movement (↔ Art. 4 BGFA). According to case law, deletion from the register does not necessarily entail withdrawal of the patent; the two are separate legal institutions (judgment **2C_897/2015** of 25.5.2016 E. 5.2.1–5.2.2).

### 3. Elements of the Provision / Normative Content

#### 3.1 Cantonal Registration Obligation (Art. 5 para. 1)

**N. 7** Each canton is obliged under federal law to maintain a register. This obligation exists regardless of whether the canton has its own cantonal lawyers' act. The register covers all lawyers who (i) have a business address within the cantonal territory *and* (ii) fulfil the professional and personal requirements under Art. 7 and 8 BGFA.

**N. 8** *Single-register principle*: A lawyer may register in the register of only one canton. Simultaneous registration in several cantonal registers of lawyers is excluded under federal law (**BGE 131 II 639 E. 3.3–3.5**). Anyone with several business addresses must register in the register of the canton in which he or she is *predominantly active*. The Federal Supreme Court derives the single-register principle from the legislative history and the purpose of the provision: multiple registration would give rise to conflicts of jurisdiction in disciplinary supervision and would undermine the simplification sought by the BGFA (BBl 1999 6046; **BGE 131 II 639 E. 3.4.2**). Staehelin/Oetiker, BSK BGFA, 2nd ed. 2011, Art. 6 N. 12, confirm that the principle follows necessarily from the systematic structure of the Act.

**N. 9** *Business address within the cantonal territory*: The business address is the spatial connecting factor for the register canton. It must comprise a physical location that enables the lawyer to practise the profession independently and in compliance with the duty of confidentiality, and to be reachable by clients and authorities. A mere post office box address or a «care of» address does not suffice according to unanimous scholarly opinion (Bohnet/Martenet, Droit de la profession d'avocat, 2009, § 649; Staehelin/Oetiker, BSK BGFA, 2nd ed. 2011, Art. 5 N. 14). The use of new technologies does not change this in any fundamental respect: the Federal Supreme Court held in **BGE 145 II 229 E. 8.3** that even with enhanced digital mobility a lawyer always requires a physical location at which to work and to physically receive clients («même si les nouvelles technologies lui offrent une mobilité accrue, l'avocat a toujours besoin d'un emplacement physique pour travailler et y rencontrer physiquement ses clients»). Federal law deliberately contains no detailed requirements regarding the nature of the office; the requirements are to be developed from the interaction of Art. 5 para. 2 lit. d BGFA, Art. 8 para. 1 lit. d BGFA (independence) and Art. 13 BGFA (professional confidentiality).

#### 3.2 Contents of the Register (Art. 5 para. 2)

**N. 10** The register contains the following data exhaustively:
- **lit. a**: surname, first name, date of birth, place of origin or nationality (identification data);
- **lit. b**: copy of the patent of lawyer (professional qualification);
- **lit. c**: certificates confirming fulfilment of the personal requirements under Art. 8 BGFA (in particular proof of legal capacity, extract from the criminal record, certificate regarding loss certificates);
- **lit. d**: business addresses and, where applicable, the name of the law firm;
- **lit. e**: *non-deleted* disciplinary measures.

**N. 11** The catalogue in Art. 5 para. 2 is exhaustive under federal law. Cantons may not maintain additional data in the «cantonal register of lawyers»; broader cantonal registers (e.g. for lawyers not subject to the BGFA who practise solely in their home canton) are possible, but must be kept separate from this register. This distinction has concrete consequences for data protection and the rights of access under Art. 10 BGFA. It must also be borne in mind that international efforts towards transparency of beneficial ownership — in particular recommendations of the FATF and the OECD within the framework of the BEPS project — could in the medium term raise the question of whether the exhaustive data catalogue of Art. 5 para. 2 BGFA requires adaptation in order to reconcile professional supervisory transparency obligations with the protection of professional confidentiality (Art. 13 BGFA).

**N. 12** *Disciplinary measures (lit. e)*: Only *non-deleted* measures appear in the register. Art. 20 BGFA governs the deletion periods: warnings, reprimands and fines are deleted after five years; a temporary ban on practice ten years after its lifting. The Federal Supreme Court clarified in **BGE 150 II 308 E. 5.7** that Art. 5 para. 2 lit. e BGFA exhausts the register-law order and that no prohibition on the use of already deleted measures can be derived therefrom: supervisory authorities may take deleted measures into account when determining sanctions, but their significance diminishes with increasing lapse of time. On the practical question of which measures appear in the register and for how long, → N. 27 (practical notes).

**N. 13** *Business address and firm name (lit. d)*: The Federal Supreme Court emphasised in **BGE 130 II 87 E. 6.3.2** that the business address in the register is not a mere formality from the perspective of institutional independence. Physical separation of employed activity and independent legal practice is «indispensable»; the lawyer's business address must «in principle» be located in premises separate from those of the employer (**BGE 130 II 87 E. 6.3.2**). Registration of a business address at a company limited by shares functioning as a platform for independent lawyers is permissible under strict conditions, but requires structural independence and preservation of professional confidentiality (**BGE 145 II 229 E. 6.5, 7.5, 8.3–8.4**).

#### 3.3 Maintenance by the Cantonal Supervisory Authority (Art. 5 para. 3)

**N. 14** The register is maintained by the cantonal supervisory authority for lawyers (→ Art. 14 BGFA). For the coordination between cantonal supervisory authorities in disciplinary proceedings, the register of the «register canton» is authoritative (→ Art. 16 BGFA). Jurisdiction lies with the canton in which the lawyer is registered; that canton bears primary responsibility for supervision (BBl 1999 6059; **BGE 131 II 639 E. 3.4.2**).

**N. 15** *Public nature of the register*: Although Art. 5 itself does not explicitly regulate public access, it follows from Art. 6 para. 3 BGFA that registration in the register is *published*. This is the only form of publication of register contents provided for in the BGFA; in particular, the publication of disciplinary measures in cantonal official gazettes is foreign to the BGFA and has been characterised by the Federal Supreme Court as contrary to federal law (**BGE 150 II 308 E. 7.4–7.9**). The right of access is exhaustively regulated in Art. 10 BGFA (→ Art. 10 BGFA).

### 4. Legal Consequences

**N. 16** *Entitlement to intercantonal practice*: Registration in a cantonal register entitles the lawyer to represent parties before judicial authorities throughout Switzerland without any further authorisation (Art. 4 BGFA). The register entry concerns solely the so-called monopoly activity; it is not required for pure legal advice or other activities outside the lawyers' monopoly (**BGE 130 II 87 E. 3**).

**N. 17** *Refusal and deletion*: If a person does not fulfil the conditions for registration, the supervisory authority must refuse registration. If conditions subsequently cease to be met, deletion is mandatory; the authority has no discretion (→ Art. 9 BGFA). These measures are *administrative* in nature and are not disciplinary measures within the meaning of Art. 17 BGFA (judgment **2C_897/2015** E. 5.2.1; Poledna, BSK BGFA, 2nd ed. 2011, Art. 17 N. 16).

**N. 18** *Fundamental rights limitations*: Refusal of registration interferes with the economic freedom (Art. 27 Cst.) of the person concerned. Any restriction therefore requires a legal basis, must serve a public interest and must be proportionate (Art. 36 Cst.). The requirements relating to independence must «not be interpreted too narrowly» and must not go beyond what is necessary to achieve the regulatory objective (**BGE 130 II 87 E. 3**; **BGE 145 II 229 E. 6.2**).

**N. 19** *Duty to notify changes*: Lawyers are obliged to notify the competent supervisory authority of all changes in circumstances that are material to their registration (Art. 12 lit. j BGFA). This applies in particular to changes of business address, commencement or termination of an employment relationship, and changes affecting the personal requirements (judgment **BGE 130 II 87 E. 7**).

### 5. Contested Issues

#### 5.1 Requirements for the Business Address in the Digital Age

**N. 20** It is uncontested that a mere post office box address is insufficient as a business address (Bohnet/Martenet, Droit de la profession d'avocat, 2009, § 649; Staehelin/Oetiker, BSK BGFA, 2nd ed. 2011, Art. 5 N. 14). The contested question is the extent to which a physical office remains necessary in the age of digital working methods. While Gurtner (Les études d'avocats virtuelles aux Etats-Unis et en Suisse, RDS 2014 I p. 341) and Chappuis (La profession d'avocat, vol. II, 2016, p. 17 ff.) press for a functional approach and argue against overly strict requirements, Bohnet/Martenet (op. cit., § 1169) and Schiller (Schweizerisches Anwaltsrecht, 2009, § 1074 ff.) maintain that a physical location remains necessary in order to be accessible to clients and authorities and to preserve professional confidentiality. The Federal Supreme Court resolved this debate pragmatically in **BGE 145 II 229 E. 8.3**: the admissibility of new forms of working is to be assessed on a case-by-case basis without «excessive schematism»; however, a physical location remains required, and the requirements must not be set excessively high.

#### 5.2 Dual Nature of the Register and Data Protection

**N. 21** It was disputed whether the obligation to delete disciplinary measures under Art. 20 BGFA in conjunction with Art. 5 para. 2 lit. e BGFA gives rise to a *prohibition on the use* of deleted sanctions. Brunner/Henn/Kriesi (Anwaltsrecht, 2015, p. 251) argued that deleted measures must not be taken into account when determining sanctions. The Federal Supreme Court expressly rejected this view in **BGE 150 II 308 E. 5.7–5.10**: neither the text of the Act nor the purpose of the register-law order gives rise to a prohibition on use; supervisory authorities may take deleted measures into account when determining sanctions, but must give them less weight as more time has passed.

#### 5.3 Demarcation of the Register from the Cantonal List of Lawyers

**N. 22** Various cantons maintain, alongside the BGFA register, a cantonal *list of lawyers* for lawyers who practise solely locally (Art. 3 para. 2 BGFA). The question of how to demarcate these two instruments — in particular the admissibility and regulatory content of cantonal lists — has been discussed in legal scholarship. Nater (in: BSK BGFA, 2nd ed. 2011, Art. 3 N. 8b) considers the extension of disciplinary law to lawyers not registered in the BGFA register to be doubtful under federal law; the prevailing scholarly opinion and the Federal Supreme Court, however, acknowledge that cantons retain a margin of discretion within the framework of Art. 3 BGFA (judgment **2C_897/2015** E. 6.2.1; Bohnet/Othenin-Girard/Schweizer, CR LLCA, 2010, Art. 3 N. 16; Fellmann, Anwaltsrecht, 2nd ed. 2017, para. 684 f.).

#### 5.4 Single-Register Principle and Competitive Neutrality

**N. 23** Critics of the single-register principle — in particular large law firms with offices in several cantons — have argued that restriction to a single register entry creates competitive disadvantages relative to locally operating firms. The Federal Supreme Court rejected this argument in **BGE 131 II 639 E. 3.4.3**: large law firms enjoyed a variety of other advantages; moreover, freedom of movement (Art. 4 BGFA) was precisely the instrument that offsets the disadvantage.

### 6. Practical Notes

**N. 24** *Change of register upon relocation*: If a lawyer relocates the predominantly operated law office to another canton, re-registration is *mandatory*: the register entry in the previous canton must be deleted, and registration must be applied for afresh in the new canton. For as long as no new registration has been effected, the right to freedom of movement under Art. 4 BGFA does not extend to the new canton of location (judgment **VB.2017.00853** of the Administrative Court of Zurich of 5.7.2018; **BGE 131 II 639 E. 3.5**).

**N. 25** *Full-time employment with part-time legal practice*: For employed lawyers whose employer is not itself registered, heightened requirements apply to proof of independence under Art. 8 para. 1 lit. d BGFA. The contract of employment must clearly safeguard the independence of the independent legal practice; the employer may neither exercise a right of instruction nor have access to mandates. The lawyer's business address must be physically separate from the employer's premises (**BGE 130 II 87 E. 6.3.1–6.3.2**). The obligation to establish clear circumstances rests with the applicant; inadequate information is to the applicant's detriment (**BGE 130 II 87 E. 7**).

**N. 26** *Registration with service platforms*: Anyone wishing to maintain a business address at a company limited by shares functioning as a platform for lawyers (shared office, «legal co-working») must ensure that (i) no economic dependency arises that impairs structural independence within the meaning of Art. 8 para. 1 lit. d BGFA; (ii) no misleading impression of a joint law firm structure is created; and (iii) professional confidentiality is maintained also vis-à-vis employees of the platform. Contractual clauses that create a significant imbalance to the detriment of the lawyer (unilateral exclusion of liability, right to terminate at any time) are incompatible with the independence requirement (**BGE 145 II 229 E. 6.5**).

**N. 27** *Disciplinary measures in the register*: Of practical relevance is the question of which measures under Art. 17 BGFA appear in the register and for how long (cf. N. 12 above). Administrative authorities may not impose sanctions going beyond the catalogue of Art. 17 BGFA and may not order additional measures such as publication in official gazettes. Art. 5 para. 2 lit. e BGFA constitutes the register-law limit on the flow of data: only *non-deleted* measures appear in the register; the internal administrative use of deleted measures must be distinguished from this (**BGE 150 II 308 E. 7.4–7.9**; → Art. 17 BGFA; → Art. 20 BGFA).

**N. 28** *Relationship to cantonal lists and registers of lawyers*: Art. 3 para. 2 BGFA allows cantons to admit holders of their cantonal patent of lawyer to appear before their own judicial authorities without those lawyers being registered in the BGFA register. In practice, various cantons therefore maintain separate lists or registers. For lawyers who wish to practise exclusively in their home canton and waive the right to intercantonal freedom of movement, registration in the BGFA register is optional; Art. 5 BGFA does not apply in that respect. Cantonal admission then follows exclusively cantonal law.
