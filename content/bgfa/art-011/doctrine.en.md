# Art. 11 BGFA — Professional Title

## Doctrine

### 1. Legislative History

**N. 1** Art. 11 BGFA was introduced as part of the Federal Act on the Free Movement of Lawyers of 23 June 2000 and has been in force since 1 June 2002. The Federal Council's Message of 28 April 1999 (BBl 1999 6013, in particular 6042 f.) stated that the regulation of the professional title is a direct consequence of the freedom-of-movement system established by the BGFA: because the Act enables inter-cantonal practice by virtue of registration, it was also necessary to regulate which title lawyers may use in business dealings and which indication is mandatory vis-à-vis the general public seeking legal advice. The need for a regulation arose from the variety of title designations used in the cantons («Rechtsanwalt», «Advokat», «Fürsprech», «Avocat», «Avvocato», etc.), which would have hampered inter-cantonal freedom of movement without a common denominator (BBl 1999 6042).

**N. 2** The Message expressly referred to a public information need: by means of the mandatory reference to the register entry, the public should be placed in a position «to distinguish between lawyers practising before the courts who are subject to the professional rules of the federal Lawyers Act and other lawyers who are equally permitted to use the lawyer's title» (judgment **2P.159/2005** of 30 June 2006, recital 2.7, with reference to BBl 1999 6022; Staehelin/Oetiker, in: Fellmann/Zindel [eds.], Kommentar zum Anwaltsgesetz, 1st ed. 2005, N. 5 on Art. 11 BGFA). The reference to the register entry pursuant to Art. 11 para. 2 BGFA therefore aims to ensure transparency vis-à-vis the public regarding subjection to the professional law of the BGFA, and not to create a title monopoly in favour of registered lawyers.

**N. 3** During the 2006 summer session, the Federal Assembly expressly rejected a proposal to prohibit holders of cantonal licences who are not entered in the register from using the lawyer's title in general (AB 2006 N 901 f.; cf. judgment **2P.159/2005** of 30 June 2006, recital 2.7). This makes clear that Art. 11 para. 1 BGFA does not establish comprehensive title protection in favour of registered lawyers, but merely governs the modalities of title use by lawyers subject to the BGFA.

---

### 2. Systematic Classification

**N. 4** Art. 11 BGFA is located in the First Chapter of the BGFA («Freedom of Movement and Register», Arts. 1–11) and constitutes its final article. The provision links the registration system (→ Arts. 5–9 BGFA) with the professional rules (→ Art. 12 BGFA): on the one hand, Art. 11 para. 1 presupposes the existence of a cantonal law licence and registration in a cantonal register; on the other hand, the obligation to use the correct professional title protects the public, which systematically leads into the professional rules.

**N. 5** Art. 11 para. 2 BGFA is closely connected in substance to Art. 12 lit. a and lit. d BGFA (duty of care and restrictions on advertising). A breach of the obligation to disclose the register entry, or the use of a misleading professional title, is sanctioned as a violation of the professional rules (↔ Art. 12 lit. a and lit. d BGFA; → Art. 17 BGFA: disciplinary measures). The Federal Supreme Court and the cantonal courts have consistently assessed violations of Art. 11 under Art. 12 lit. a and lit. d BGFA (judgment **2C_985/2021** of 16 November 2022, recital 4.1 ff.; judgment **2P.159/2005**, recital 2.7; Administrative Court ZH VB.2020.00534 of 30 September 2021).

**N. 6** Art. 11 BGFA applies exclusively to lawyers who are subject to the BGFA, i.e. who hold a cantonal law licence and are entered in the cantonal register of lawyers (→ Art. 2 para. 1 BGFA). Those who no longer hold a licence or have renounced it do not fall within the scope of Art. 11 BGFA; their right to use the title is governed by cantonal law and by the unfair competition law protection afforded by Art. 3 para. 1 lit. c UCA (judgment **2C_897/2015** of 25 May 2016, recital 7.3; judgment **2P.159/2005**, recitals 2.6 f.).

### 3. Elements of the Provision and Normative Content

#### 3.1 Art. 11 para. 1 — Professional Title

**N. 7** **Obligation to use the title corresponding to the licence.** Lawyers who are subject to the BGFA shall use the professional title conferred by the law licence or an equivalent title of the canton in whose register they are entered. The provision requires the use of the title corresponding to the cantonal licensing law — i.e. «Rechtsanwältin», «Advokat», «Fürsprech», «Avocate», «Avvocato», etc. — or an equivalent title of the canton of registration. An «equivalent professional title» within the meaning of Art. 11 para. 1 exists where such a title is customary in the canton of registration and expresses the same professional qualification as the title used in the licence (Staehelin/Oetiker, in: Fellmann/Zindel [eds.], Kommentar zum Anwaltsgesetz, 1st ed. 2005, N. 3 on Art. 11 BGFA).

**N. 8** **No title monopoly for registered lawyers.** Art. 11 para. 1 BGFA does not create a comprehensive monopoly on professional titles in favour of registered lawyers. Lawyers who hold a cantonal law licence but are not entered in the register (e.g. in-house counsel or judges who do not practise before the courts) may equally use the lawyer's title under cantonal law and Art. 27 FC, provided they hold a valid licence. The Federal Supreme Court held as early as **BGE 112 Ia 318** (1986) that a general prohibition on the use of the lawyer's title by all licence holders not entered in the register would violate the principle of proportionality. The BGFA has not changed this position (judgment **2P.159/2005**, recitals 2.6 f.).

**N. 9** **Prohibition on use of the title following loss of the licence.** A person who has lost their law licence — whether by withdrawal or renunciation — is no longer permitted to use the lawyer's title. Use of the title following loss of the licence constitutes, depending on cantonal law, a violation of the cantonal lawyers act and may be characterised as unfair competition under Art. 3 para. 1 lit. c UCA (judgment **2C_897/2015**, recital 7.3; judgment **2P.159/2005**, recital 2.6). Under cantonal law, unlawful use of the title may additionally be punishable as a petty offence (cf. judgment **2C_897/2015**, recital 7.3 for the Canton of Lucerne: § 25 of the Petty Offences Act).

**N. 10** **Misleading professional title.** Art. 11 para. 1 BGFA prohibits not only the unauthorised use of the lawyer's title, but also the misleading use of professional titles. The Federal Supreme Court and the Zurich Administrative Court have held in cases involving the designation «Rechtsanwalt und öffentlicher Notar» (lawyer and public notary) that a lawyer practising in several cantons may not use the designation «Public Notary» without qualification if they are authorised to practise as a notary in only one of those cantons; such use is misleading to members of the public who are not legally trained (judgment **2C_985/2021**, recitals 4.3–4.5; Administrative Court ZH VB.2020.00534, recital 4.2). This obligation to use a clear and accurate title derives from both Art. 11 para. 1 BGFA and — in qualified form — from Art. 12 lit. d BGFA (prohibition on advertising containing misleading information).

#### 3.2 Art. 11 para. 2 — Reference to Register Entry in Business Dealings

**N. 11** **Obligation to indicate the register entry.** In the course of business dealings, lawyers must indicate their entry in a cantonal register. The term «business dealings» is to be interpreted broadly and covers letterheads, email signatures, websites, business cards and all other means of communication used in a professional context (Staehelin/Oetiker, Kommentar zum Anwaltsgesetz, 1st ed. 2005, N. 4 f. on Art. 11 BGFA; confirmed by judgment **2C_985/2021**, recital 4.3; Zurich Court of Appeal KG080012 of 6 November 2008). Purely private communications and general advisory activities outside of forensic practice are not covered.

**N. 12** **Content of the indication.** The indication must identify the specific canton of registration. A general reference to «a register entry» without naming the canton does not suffice; equally impermissible is the use of a register indication where no entry in fact exists. The Zurich Court of Appeal held in KG080012 that the use of the notation «Registered in the Lawyers' Register» on letterhead without an actual register entry constitutes a violation of Art. 12 lit. a and lit. d BGFA.

**N. 13** **Purpose of the indication.** The reference to the register entry is intended to place the public in a position to distinguish between lawyers practising before the courts who are subject to the professional rules of the BGFA and other holders of the title (BBl 1999 6022; judgment **2P.159/2005**, recital 2.7; Staehelin/Oetiker, Kommentar zum Anwaltsgesetz, 1st ed. 2005, N. 5 on Art. 11 BGFA). It thus serves the purpose of consumer protection and market transparency, not the creation of an exclusive title monopoly.

---

### 4. Legal Consequences

**N. 14** **Disciplinary sanctions.** A violation of Art. 11 BGFA — in particular the use of a misleading professional title or the failure to include a reference to the register entry in business dealings — is characterised as a breach of the professional rules under Art. 12 lit. a (duty of care) and lit. d (advertising) BGFA and may be sanctioned by disciplinary measures pursuant to Art. 17 BGFA (→ Art. 17 BGFA: reprimand to permanent prohibition on practising). The Federal Supreme Court confirmed that the sanction of a reprimand was proportionate in the case of a lawyer practising in two cantons who had used a misleading notarial designation (judgment **2C_985/2021**, recitals 4.4–4.6).

**N. 15** **No intent required.** A disciplinary sanction for violation of the professional title provisions does not require intent. The misconduct may be committed negligently, unconsciously or through mere ignorance of the rule (judgment **2C_985/2021**, recital 4.6; cf. also **BGE 148 I 1**, recital 12.2 concerning physicians, applied by analogy).

**N. 16** **Complementary protection under unfair competition law.** Under federal law, the lawyer's title is not directly protected by the BGFA; Art. 11 BGFA contains no direct penal provision. Protection against the unlawful use of the title is provided indirectly via Art. 3 para. 1 lit. c UCA (inaccurate titles or professional designations), which, in cases of intentional commission, carries criminal sanctions under Art. 23 para. 1 UCA (judgment **2C_897/2015**, recital 7.3; Chappuis, La profession d'avocat, Tome I, 2nd ed. 2016, p. 22). In addition, cantonal law (cantonal petty offences acts) may penalise the unlawful assumption of the title.

**N. 17** **Entry and deletion from the register.** Art. 11 para. 1 BGFA presupposes registration pursuant to Arts. 5 ff. BGFA. Deletion from the register — e.g. as a result of the loss of personal requirements under Art. 9 BGFA — means that the deleted person is no longer entitled to refer to a register entry in business dealings (→ Art. 9 BGFA; judgment **2C_897/2015**, recital 5.2.1). Whether the loss of the licence, over and above the deletion from the register, also extinguishes the right to use the lawyer's title is determined by the law of the canton that issued the licence; a residual right based on a potentially still-valid licence issued by another canton must be examined separately (cf. judgment **2P.159/2005**, recital 4.3).

### 5. Disputed Questions

#### 5.1 Title Monopoly or Mere Transparency Requirement?

**N. 18** It is disputed whether Art. 11 para. 1 BGFA establishes a monopoly on the lawyer's title in favour of registered lawyers, or merely governs the modalities of title use. **Staehelin/Oetiker** (Kommentar zum Anwaltsgesetz, 1st ed. 2005, N. 5 on Art. 11 BGFA) and the Message (BBl 1999 6022) advocate the transparency approach: Art. 11 para. 2 BGFA is intended solely to inform the public, not to confer title protection. The Federal Supreme Court expressly confirmed this view in judgment **2P.159/2005**, recital 2.7: the reference to the register entry «is aimed at ensuring unrestricted freedom of movement for lawyers practising before the courts throughout Switzerland. It is not, however, intended to secure a monopoly on the title for lawyers entered in the register.» This is consistent with the earlier case law in **BGE 112 Ia 318**, which held that a general prohibition on the use of the title by licence holders not entered in the register is disproportionate.

**N. 19** The opposing position — a de facto monopoly for registered lawyers — cannot be substantiated in legal scholarship. While it might be argued from the interplay of Art. 11 para. 1 and cantonal title protection law that only registered lawyers may use the lawyer's title, the Federal Assembly rejected an amendment to that effect in 2006 (AB 2006 N 901 f.). **Bohnet/Martenet** (Droit de la profession d'avocat, 2009, N. 578) note that the public interest in consumer protection in respect of title use takes precedence in principle over the private interest in using the lawyer's title — without, however, deriving a general monopoly from this.

#### 5.2 Scope of «Business Dealings» (Art. 11 para. 2 BGFA)

**N. 20** The scope of the term «business dealings» in Art. 11 para. 2 BGFA is unclear. The Message (BBl 1999 6042) provides no conclusive definition. **Staehelin/Oetiker** (Kommentar zum Anwaltsgesetz, 1st ed. 2005, N. 4 on Art. 11 BGFA) take a broad view, encompassing any professional appearance. The Federal Supreme Court, in judgment **2C_985/2021**, recital 4.3, attributed letterheads, email signatures and websites to «business dealings» without drawing an express distinction, where these serve advertising purposes. **Fellmann** (Anwaltsrecht, 2nd ed. 2017, para. 563) advocates an interpretation focused on the advertising function: what is decisive is not the physical form but the function of the communication in the professional environment. A minority view would limit «business dealings» to forensic practice; this position finds no support in practice.

#### 5.3 Relationship Between Art. 11 BGFA and Art. 12 lit. a and d BGFA

**N. 21** Case law and legal scholarship debate whether Art. 11 BGFA constitutes an autonomous professional rule or whether violations are always to be subsumed under Art. 12 BGFA. The Federal Supreme Court has consistently assessed violations of Art. 11 under Art. 12 lit. a and lit. d BGFA, without invoking Art. 11 as an independent disciplinary basis (judgment **2C_985/2021**, recitals 4.1 ff.; judgment **2P.159/2005**, recital 2.7). **Fellmann** (Anwaltsrecht, 2nd ed. 2017) and **Bohnet/Martenet** (Droit de la profession d'avocat, 2009, N. 1514) treat Art. 11 systematically as an annex to Art. 12 lit. d BGFA (prohibition on advertising), which supports the Federal Supreme Court's approach. Art. 11 BGFA therefore has primarily an informative and declaratory character; the disciplinary effect is produced principally through Art. 12 BGFA.

#### 5.4 Law Licence versus Practice Authorisation — Effect on the Right to Use the Title

**N. 22** The relationship between the law licence as a certificate of competence on the one hand and the register entry as a practice authorisation on the other is of central importance for Art. 11 BGFA. The Federal Supreme Court held in judgment **2C_897/2015**, recitals 7.2 ff., in respect of the Canton of Lucerne, that the law licence there does not constitute a practice authorisation but rather a declaratory ruling confirming the existence of the professional and personal requirements; actual admission to practice is effected by registration. By contrast, the Federal Supreme Court had traditionally characterised the law licence in judgment **2P.159/2005**, recital 3.2, as a police permit. The consequence for Art. 11 BGFA is as follows: the right to use the title depends — at least for forensic practice — on the register entry and not on the licence alone; for lawyers engaged in advisory work who hold a valid licence, cantonal law may independently regulate the use of the title. **Kettiger** (Jusletter 28 September 2009, p. 4) had advocated a consistent separation between certificate of competence and practice authorisation, which would have allowed for clearer regulation of the right to use the title; the current law remains incomplete in this respect.

### 6. Practical Notes

**N. 23** **Letterhead and digital communications.** Lawyers must indicate both their professional title corresponding to their licence and the canton of their register entry on their letterhead, email signature and website. A mere statement «registered in the lawyers' register» without naming the canton is insufficient. For law firms with offices in several cantons, a clear presentation differentiated by location is recommended, showing which activities (in particular secondary activities such as notarial practice) are carried out where. Failure to provide this clarification risks a disciplinary sanction for violation of Art. 12 lit. d BGFA even in the absence of intent (judgment **2C_985/2021**, recital 4.6; Administrative Court ZH VB.2020.00534, recital 4.2).

**N. 24** **Multiple professional titles and secondary activities.** A person who practises a further regulated profession in addition to the legal profession (e.g. notary, tax adviser) must make clear in their professional communications the geographic or substantive scope within which they are authorised to practise that secondary profession. A blanket combination «Rechtsanwalt und Öffentlicher Notar» does not suffice where the notarial authorisation exists in only one of several cantons of activity. This applies regardless of whether a clarifying notice is posted under a separate section of the website: each page and each means of communication must be comprehensible and non-misleading on its own (judgment **2C_985/2021**, recital 4.5).

**N. 25** **Deletion from the register and use of the title.** Upon deletion from the register — e.g. under Art. 9 BGFA following the issuance of certificates of unpaid debts — the entitlement to refer to a register entry in business dealings lapses. Whether the right to use the lawyer's title itself also lapses depends on cantonal law: if the law licence remains in force despite deletion from the register, cantonal law may continue to permit use of the title (cf. judgment **2C_897/2015**, recitals 5.2.1, 7.2 f.). Where the licence is withdrawn (as opposed to mere deletion from the register), the right to use the title lapses under the law of the canton that issued the licence; a residual right based on a potentially still-valid licence issued by another canton must be examined separately (cf. judgment **2P.159/2005**, recital 4.3).

**N. 26** **Advertising and online presence.** Websites serve (among other things) advertising purposes within the meaning of Art. 12 lit. d BGFA, even where the lawyer uses them primarily for information. Statements concerning the professional title on the website are therefore subject to the prohibition on misleading information. Lawyers are well advised, when relaunching their website, to clarify in advance with the bar supervisory authority whether the website complies with Art. 11 BGFA and Art. 12 lit. d BGFA, or to ensure that the presentation is transparent and clearly differentiated.

**N. 27** **Relationship to cantonal law and the UCA.** Protection of the lawyer's title operates in multiple layers: Art. 11 BGFA sets out the obligations for lawyers subject to the BGFA; Art. 3 para. 1 lit. c UCA provides — as a complementary federal law mechanism — protection against misleading use of the title by third parties (including non-licensed persons); cantonal criminal law may penalise the unlawful assumption of the title as a petty offence. These layers are cumulatively applicable (judgment **2C_897/2015**, recital 7.3).
