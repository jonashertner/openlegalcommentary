# Art. 3 BGFA — Relationship to Cantonal Law

## Doctrine

### 1. Legislative History

**N. 1** The BGFA was adopted as the Federal Act on Freedom of Movement for Lawyers on 23 June 2000 and entered into force on 1 June 2002. Its primary objective is to realise intercantonal and European freedom of movement for lawyers by establishing uniform minimum requirements for the practice of the profession (BBl 1999 6042, para. 22; judgment 2C_505/2019 of 13.9.2019, cons. 5.1).

**N. 2** The Federal Council's preliminary draft contained no independent provision on the relationship to cantonal law. Art. 3 BGFA is the result of a «federalist» initiative by the Council of States committee, which was adopted in the parliamentary deliberations without significant opposition (BO 1999 CE 1163–1164; BO 2000 CN 37). The aim was to expressly clarify and preserve cantonal competence to regulate the bar examination and local party representation, without calling into question the principle of freedom of movement under federal law (BBl 1999 6013, 6042 f., para. 22).

**N. 3** The Federal Council's dispatch expressly emphasised that the BGFA does not aim at the full harmonisation of the law governing the legal profession, but rather, as a special act, guarantees intercantonal freedom of movement and establishes a federal minimum standard (BBl 1999 6037 ff., para. 172.17). Those sub-areas of professional law governing lawyers not covered by federal law were intended to remain within cantonal jurisdiction (BBl 1999 6042 f., para. 22; judgment 2C_505/2019 of 13.9.2019, cons. 5.1).

---

### 2. Systematic Classification

**N. 4** Art. 3 BGFA, together with Art. 1 (subject matter) and Art. 2 (scope of application), forms the systematic framework of the Act. It defines the federalist reservation that limits the scope of federal regulation. The BGFA exhaustively governs the intercantonal monopoly area — that is, the conditions for registration and the resulting freedom of movement throughout Switzerland (→ Art. 4 BGFA, → Art. 6 ff. BGFA). Art. 3 BGFA makes clear that cantonal law retains autonomy in two areas: (1) the requirements for obtaining a cantonal law licence, and (2) party representation before cantonal courts by holders of the cantonal law licence.

**N. 5** Art. 3 BGFA stands in tension with several provisions:
- → Art. 7 BGFA: Federal law lays down minimum requirements relating to the law licence for the purpose of registration (university degree, traineeship, examination), without determining the content of cantonal examinations.
- ↔ Art. 2 para. 4 BGBM (Internal Market Act): The cantonal powers under Art. 3 para. 1 BGFA must be compatible with the principle of free market access (**BGE 134 II 329** cons. 5).
- → Art. 127 para. 5 CrimPC: Art. 3 para. 2 BGFA is superseded in criminal procedural law by the federal monopoly provision (**BGE 147 IV 379** cons. 1.2.2).
- → Art. 27 FC: Restrictions on access to the legal profession engage the freedom of commerce and must be measured against the principle of proportionality (**BGE 130 II 87** cons. 3).

---

### 3. Normative Content

#### 3.1 Paragraph 1: Cantonal Requirements for the Law Licence

**N. 6** Art. 3 para. 1 BGFA preserves cantonal competence to regulate «within the framework of this Act» the requirements for obtaining a cantonal law licence. The reservation is a qualified one: the cantons act *within the framework* of the BGFA, meaning they must comply with the federal minimum requirements under Art. 7 BGFA. It is disputed whether cantonal regulatory authority extends to the conditions for admission to the examination, the content of the examination, and the examination modalities (cf. N. 12 ff. below).

**N. 7** The Federal Supreme Court held explicitly in **BGE 131 I 467** cons. 3.3: «In comparing with the examination regulations of the neighbouring canton, the appellant fails to recognise that each canton has the right to determine for itself the requirements for obtaining the cantonal law licence (Art. 3 para. 1 BGFA).» Accordingly, each canton may enact its own examination regulations, determine its own examination subjects, and set its own admission requirements, without any intercantonal equal treatment being required.

**N. 8** In **BGE 134 II 329**, the Federal Supreme Court specified the limits of cantonal regulatory authority under Art. 3 para. 1 BGFA: cantonal competence is confined to regulating training requirements. To the extent that cantonal regulations impinge upon the area of free exercise of the profession — for instance, by restricting the authority to employ trainees — the Internal Market Act applies alongside the BGFA, and the cantonal regulation must satisfy the requirements of Art. 3 BGBM (**BGE 134 II 329** cons. 5.3–5.4). In particular, a cantonal requirement demanding *five years of practice in the canton*, without crediting equivalent professional experience acquired in another canton, violates the principle of proportionality (**BGE 134 II 329** cons. 6.2.3).

**N. 9** The content of the examination falls in principle within cantonal competence. Federal law (Art. 7 para. 1 lit. b BGFA) merely requires an examination in theoretical and practical legal knowledge; the specific content and modalities of the bar examination remain a matter of cantonal law. This applies equally to subjects that fall outside the cantonal lawyers' monopoly (e.g. constitutional and administrative law in Zurich, even though no lawyers' monopoly exists in those proceedings): an examination in constitutional and administrative law does not violate the freedom of commerce if it is based on a sufficient legal basis and serves a public interest — namely, quality assurance for persons seeking legal assistance (judgment 2C_505/2019 of 13.9.2019, cons. 5.2.2–5.2.3).

**N. 10** Art. 3 para. 1 BGFA also applies to the assessment of examination performance. According to **BGE 131 I 467** cons. 2.9, the substantive review of examination results (assessment of professional suitability) does not involve a justiciable «dispute» within the meaning of Art. 6 para. 1 ECHR; the Convention does not apply in this regard. Formal questions of procedural lawfulness, however, remain reviewable.

#### 3.2 Paragraph 2: Party Representation by Holders of the Cantonal Law Licence

**N. 11** Art. 3 para. 2 BGFA permits cantons to allow holders of their cantonal law licence to represent parties before their *own* courts, even if those persons are not entered in the cantonal register of lawyers under Art. 6 ff. BGFA. This provision constitutes an exception to the principle in Art. 4 BGFA, according to which only lawyers entered in the register may represent parties before judicial authorities.

**N. 12** The practical scope of Art. 3 para. 2 BGFA has been significantly curtailed by the unification of procedural law. With the entry into force of the Swiss Code of Criminal Procedure (1 January 2011), a lawyers' monopoly exists by virtue of federal law in criminal matters (Art. 127 para. 5 CrimPC). The Federal Supreme Court held explicitly in **BGE 147 IV 379** cons. 1.2.2 that, while Art. 3 para. 2 BGFA formally remains in force, its scope of application has been substantially restricted by federal procedural law: in criminal matters, cantons retain competence for derogating rules only in minor offence proceedings (Art. 127 para. 5, 2nd clause CrimPC), and such a derogating rule requires a sufficiently clear cantonal provision (**BGE 147 IV 379** cons. 1.6.3). In civil procedural law, Art. 68 para. 2 CPC limits the monopoly area to professional party representation; Art. 3 para. 2 BGFA has similarly lost some of its independent significance there.

**N. 13** For non-professional representations outside the codified procedural codes (e.g. in cantonal administrative proceedings), Art. 3 para. 2 BGFA retains independent significance. Nater observes that the cantons' room for derogating rules in criminal matters is narrowly circumscribed by the requirements of federal law (Nater, BSK BGFA, Art. 3 N. 6b, 2nd ed. 2011), and concludes from this that cantons may only permit non-lawyers to engage in professional party representation in minor offence proceedings — a position which the Federal Supreme Court did not fully endorse in **BGE 147 IV 379** cons. 1.6.2, in that it construed the scope more broadly and referenced the requirements of Art. 127 para. 4 CrimPC.

### 4. Legal Consequences

**N. 14** Cantonal rules grounded in Art. 3 para. 1 BGFA are consistent with federal law provided that:
1. They do not fall below the minimum requirements of Art. 7 BGFA;
2. They respect the principle of proportionality (Art. 36 para. 3 FC);
3. Where they exceed the purely educational domain, they satisfy the requirements of the BGBM — in particular Art. 2 para. 4 and Art. 3 BGBM (**BGE 134 II 329** cons. 5.3–5.4).

**N. 15** A cantonal rule that is formally grounded in Art. 3 para. 1 BGFA but in fact restricts free exercise of the profession (market freedom) is assessed against the standard of the BGBM. A clause that infringes the principle of freedom of market access is contrary to federal law notwithstanding the BGFA reservation (**BGE 134 II 329** cons. 5.3–6.3).

**N. 16** Cantonal examination decisions based on an examination regulated pursuant to Art. 3 para. 1 BGFA are subject only to limited review before the Federal Supreme Court: in substantive questions of examination assessment (professional suitability), an appeal in public law matters is excluded (Art. 83 lit. t BGG); only the subsidiary constitutional complaint is admissible (Art. 113 ff. BGG), limited to allegations of violation of constitutional rights (**BGE 131 I 467** cons. 2.9; judgment 2C_505/2019 of 13.9.2019, cons. 1.1–1.2). Formal procedural questions (the lawfulness of the examination procedure), however, remain fully reviewable.

**N. 17** Cantonal rules under Art. 3 para. 2 BGFA that conflict with federal procedural codes are inapplicable by reason of the supremacy of federal law (Art. 49 para. 1 FC). Cantons wishing to permit non-lawyers to act as defence counsel in minor offence proceedings require an explicit, sufficiently clear cantonal provision (**BGE 147 IV 379** cons. 1.6.3).

### 5. Disputed Questions

**N. 18** **Relationship between Art. 3 para. 1 BGFA and the BGBM — lex specialis or concurrent application?** The relationship between the BGFA and the BGBM was, following the BGFA's entry into force, initially governed by the principle of *lex specialis derogat generali*: the BGFA, as the later and more specific act, took precedence over the BGBM (cf. judgment 2A.443/2003 of 29.3.2004, cons. 5.2, cited in **BGE 134 II 329** cons. 5.2). Following the revision of the BGBM on 1 July 2006 — which expanded the principle of freedom of market access for persons domiciled in another canton (Art. 2 para. 4 BGBM as amended) — this relationship changed: the Federal Supreme Court held in **BGE 134 II 329** cons. 5.2–5.4 that the ordinary rules on conflicts of norms (lex specialis, lex posterior) are no longer sufficient, and that concurrent application exists depending on the subject matter of the regulation.

**N. 19** Bohnet/Martenet take the view that cantonal room for derogating rules under Art. 3 para. 2 BGFA in minor offence proceedings is limited by the requirements of Art. 127 para. 4 CrimPC, without any further restriction to professional party representation being assumed (Bohnet/Martenet, Droit de la profession d'avocat, 2009, para. 973, cited in **BGE 147 IV 379** cons. 1.6.2). Nater, by contrast, confines cantonal room for manoeuvre more narrowly to professional party representation by non-lawyers in minor offence proceedings (Nater, BSK BGFA, Art. 3 N. 6b). The Federal Supreme Court aligned itself in **BGE 147 IV 379** cons. 1.6.2 with the broader view of Bohnet/Martenet.

**N. 20** **Scope of cantonal examination competence.** It is disputed how far the reservation «within the framework of this Act» in Art. 3 para. 1 BGFA restricts cantonal autonomy in examination matters. The minimum requirements of Art. 7 BGFA relate to the conditions for registration, not to examination content. Meier/Reiser take the view that the BGFA contains no rules on the content and modalities of the bar examination and that this area remains entirely a matter for the cantons (Meier/Reiser, in: Valticos/Reiser/Chappuis [eds.], Loi sur les avocats, 2010, Art. 7 N. 43). This corresponds to the Federal Supreme Court's case law, which leaves cantons scope to require examination subjects that fall outside the cantonal lawyers' monopoly (judgment 2C_505/2019 of 13.9.2019, cons. 5.1).

**N. 21** **Reverse discrimination.** During the parliamentary deliberations, concern was expressed that a restrictive approach to registration — in particular with regard to the independence requirement (Art. 8 para. 1 lit. d BGFA) — would result in reverse discrimination, since EU/EFTA lawyers would be subject to less stringent requirements (statement Nabholz, AB 1999 N 1558, cited in **BGE 130 II 87** cons. 5.1.2). The Federal Supreme Court rejected this concern in **BGE 130 II 87** cons. 5.1.2: the requirement of independence applies equally to EU/EFTA lawyers (Art. 25 BGFA in conjunction with Art. 12 BGFA; Art. 6 Directive 98/5/EC), so that no discrimination exists.

### 6. Practical Notes

**N. 22** Cantonal legislators who, on the basis of Art. 3 para. 1 BGFA, enact requirements relating to the bar examination or legal training must observe the following:
- The federal minimum requirements (Art. 7 para. 1 BGFA) constitute the lower limit; cantons may impose more stringent, but not less stringent, requirements.
- Examination rules must be based on a sufficient legal foundation; delegation to subordinate authorities (e.g. the Superior Court) is permissible provided the essential principles are enshrined in formal legislation (judgment 2C_505/2019 of 13.9.2019, cons. 5.2.2).
- Rules that go beyond purely educational matters and impinge upon the market (e.g. requirements relating to supervising lawyers) must comply with the limitations imposed by the BGBM (**BGE 134 II 329** cons. 5.3–5.4).

**N. 23** For the practice of party representation under Art. 3 para. 2 BGFA, the following applies: cantons wishing to confer a right of audience outside the register on holders of their cantonal law licence must anchor this in a clear cantonal provision. In minor offence proceedings, where a lawyers' monopoly applies under federal law (Art. 127 para. 5 CrimPC), a mere inference by way of contraposition from a generally worded cantonal monopoly provision is insufficient; an express opening clause is required (**BGE 147 IV 379** cons. 1.6.3; example: § 11 para. 3 AnwG/ZH, which explicitly excludes non-professional defence in minor offence proceedings from the monopoly).

**N. 24** Examination candidates wishing to challenge cantonal examination decisions before the Federal Supreme Court may lodge only a subsidiary constitutional complaint (Art. 83 lit. t, Art. 113 ff. BGG). The Federal Supreme Court reviews only violations of constitutional rights (in particular, arbitrariness in assessment, violation of the right to be heard) and exercises considerable restraint in the substantive assessment of examination performance (**BGE 131 I 467** cons. 3.1–3.2; judgment 2C_505/2019 of 13.9.2019, cons. 2.1).

**N. 25** The relationship between Art. 3 BGFA and the cantonal legislation on the legal profession (e.g. AnwG/ZH, LLCA/GE, AnwG/SG) is central to everyday practice: the BGFA provisions on registration, professional rules, and disciplinary supervision are exhaustive for the monopoly area. Cantonal legislation on the legal profession may enact supplementary rules only for areas that the BGFA does not exhaustively cover. Art. 49 para. 1 FC (supremacy of federal law) must be observed: cantonal provisions that conflict with Art. 4 or Art. 12 BGFA are inapplicable (→ Art. 12 BGFA; → Art. 17 BGFA).
