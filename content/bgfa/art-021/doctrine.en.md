# Art. 21 BGFA — Principles (Freedom to Provide Services)

## Doctrine

### 1. Legislative History

**N. 1** Art. 21 BGFA forms the opening provision of Section 4 (Arts. 21–26 BGFA), which governs the activities of EU/EFTA lawyers operating under the freedom to provide services. The Federal Council submitted the draft on 28 April 1999 (BBl 1999 6013). The provision transposes Art. 5 of the Agreement on the Free Movement of Persons of 21 June 1999 (AFMP; SR 0.142.112.681) and Council Directive 77/249/EEC of 22 March 1977 to facilitate the effective exercise by lawyers of freedom to provide services (the so-called Services Directive) into Swiss law. The BGFA is also based on the Directive on the recognition of higher-education diplomas (89/48/EEC) and the Establishment Directive (98/5/EC) (BBl 1999 6022 ff.; **BGE 151 II 640** E. 4.3).

**N. 2** In the explanatory report, the Federal Council explained that EU/EFTA lawyers wishing to operate in Switzerland only on an occasional basis may represent parties before judicial authorities for up to 90 working days per calendar year without registering in a cantonal lawyers' register or the EU/EFTA lawyers' list (BBl 1999 6063 Ziff. 234.21). This approach follows the European law distinction between freedom to provide services (temporary and occasional) and freedom of establishment (permanent and stable). The Federal Council's draft of Art. 21 was adopted without discussion in the Federal Assembly (AB 1999 N 1551 ff.; **BGE 151 II 640** E. 5.3).

**N. 3** The material scope of the BGFA was shaped by Annex III of the AFMP, which expressly refers to the Services Directive and the Establishment Directive. The objective is to achieve international free movement for nationals of EU/EFTA Member States on the basis of the provisions in force in the EU (Preamble AFMP; Art. 16 para. 1 AFMP; BBl 1999 6061; judgment **2A.536/2003** of 9 August 2004 E. 3.1).

---

### 2. Systematic Classification

**N. 4** Art. 21 BGFA stands at the head of Section 4 and sets out the fundamental conditions for the freedom to provide services for EU/EFTA lawyers. The statute governs three levels of integration of foreign lawyers into the Swiss legal system: (1) provision of services without an obligation to register (Arts. 21–26 BGFA), (2) permanent practice under the original professional title with registration in the EU/EFTA lawyers' list (Arts. 27–29 BGFA), (3) registration in the cantonal lawyers' register with full equivalence of status (Arts. 30–34a BGFA) (**BGE 151 II 640** E. 4.2; judgment **2A.536/2003** E. 3.2; Fellmann, Anwaltsrecht, 2nd ed. 2017, N. 176–179).

**N. 5** Within the overall system of the BGFA, Art. 21 is the counterpart to Art. 5 BGFA, which requires domestic lawyers to be registered in the lawyers' register as a precondition for representing parties in proceedings subject to the representation monopoly. Art. 21 BGFA creates — as an exception to the domestic system — access for EU/EFTA lawyers without prior registration and without a residence permit (Art. 20 Annex I AFMP; judgment **2A.536/2003** E. 3.2.1).

**N. 6** The reservation in Art. 3 para. 2 BGFA in favour of cantonal law applies only to a limited extent to Section 4: in the area of freedom to provide services, federal law is largely exhaustive by virtue of the requirements of European law (Dreyer, CR LLCA, Preliminary remarks on Arts. 21–26 BGFA N. 6; Bohnet/Martenet, Droit de la profession d'avocat, 2009, N. 306). → Art. 3 BGFA; → Art. 5 BGFA; ↔ Art. 27 BGFA.

### 3. Elements of the Provision / Normative Content

#### 3.1 Personal Scope (para. 1, sentence 1)

**N. 7** Art. 21 para. 1 BGFA requires three cumulative conditions:

**(a) Nationality of an EU or EFTA Member State.** The provision applies to nationals of the Member States of the European Union and of the EFTA states (Iceland, Liechtenstein, Norway). Nationals of third states who are admitted to the bar in an EU/EFTA state do not fall within the personal scope of application (Fellmann, Anwaltsrecht, N. 176; Kellerhals/Baumgartner, in: Fellmann/Zindel [eds.], BSK BGFA, 2nd ed. 2011, Preliminary remarks on Arts. 21–26 N. 4).

**(b) Entitlement to practise in the home state.** The person concerned must be entitled to practise as a lawyer in their home state. The decisive factor is the professional title of the respective home state listed in the annex to the BGFA (e.g., «Rechtsanwalt»/«Rechtsanwältin» for Germany and Austria, «Avocat(e)» for France and Belgium). The Federal Supreme Court has expressly confirmed the requirement of a professional title listed in the annex to the BGFA (**BGE 151 II 640** E. 4.2.1). The list in the annex follows the framework of European directive law; cf. Art. 1 lit. a of Directive 77/249/EEC (Kellerhals/Baumgartner, BSK BGFA, Preliminary remarks on Arts. 21–26 N. 5).

**(c) Acting under the freedom to provide services.** The activity must be temporary and occasional; the distinction from permanent practice (Art. 27 BGFA) is drawn by reference to frequency, duration, and intention (judgment **2A.536/2003** E. 3.2.2; **BGE 151 II 640** E. 4.2.1). The establishment of an infrastructure in Switzerland does not automatically exclude the character of a service (ECJ, Case C-55/94 *Gebhard*, [1995] ECR I-4165, paras. 25–28; judgment **2A.536/2003** E. 3.2.2). As a reference point, the 90-working-day threshold under Art. 5 AFMP applies, which according to case law does not constitute a rigid threshold (Chappuis/Châtelain, CR LLCA, Art. 27 BGFA N. 7; Nater/Wipf, in: Bilaterale Verträge Schweiz-EG, 2002, pp. 256 f.; **BGE 151 II 640** E. 5.7.2).

#### 3.2 Material Scope: Representation before Judicial Authorities

**N. 8** Art. 21 para. 1 BGFA limits the authorisation to provide services to the representation of parties before judicial authorities. This covers ordinary courts (cantonal courts, Federal Supreme Court), administrative courts, and criminal justice authorities. The interplay with Art. 127 para. 5 CrimPC means that EU/EFTA lawyers who satisfy the conditions of Arts. 21 ff. BGFA are also entitled to act as defence counsel (**BGE 147 IV 379** E. 1.2.2). Out-of-court advisory activities are not covered by the material scope of application; the BGFA imposes no restriction on such activities (Fellmann, Anwaltsrecht, N. 179).

**N. 9** In proceedings subject to mandatory representation by a lawyer, lawyers providing services are required to act in concert with a lawyer registered in a cantonal lawyers' register (→ Art. 23 BGFA; BBl 1999 6064). Since mandatory representation is rarely provided for in Swiss procedural law today, this restriction is of limited practical significance (judgment **2A.536/2003** E. 3.2.1).

#### 3.3 No Obligation to Register and No Right to Register (para. 2)

**N. 10** Art. 21 para. 2 BGFA makes clear that lawyers providing services are not registered in the cantonal lawyers' registers. They are likewise not to be included in the EU/EFTA lawyers' list pursuant to Art. 28 BGFA (**BGE 151 II 640** E. 4.2.1; judgment **2A.536/2003** E. 4.2). Lawyers operating solely under the freedom to provide services have no entitlement to voluntary registration in the EU/EFTA lawyers' list. The dispensation from an obligation to register is consistent with the principle of proportionality: as the Federal Supreme Court notes with reference to the legislative materials, it would entail a significant and disproportionate burden if service providers were required to register for each temporary activity (**BGE 151 II 640** E. 5.4.1).

**N. 11** Upon request by the court or supervisory authorities, lawyers providing services must furnish proof of their legal qualifications (→ Art. 22 BGFA). Prior proof of qualification is not required (BBl 1999 6063; judgment **2A.536/2003** E. 4.3).

#### 3.4 Professional Title and Discipline

**N. 12** Lawyers providing services in Switzerland use their original professional title in the official language of their home state, together with the name of the professional organisation to which they are accountable or the court before which they are admitted under the rules of their home state (→ Art. 24 BGFA). The disciplinary supervision by Swiss supervisory authorities and the available sanctions are governed by → Arts. 25 and 26 BGFA.

---

### 4. Legal Consequences

**N. 13** EU/EFTA lawyers who satisfy the conditions of Art. 21 para. 1 BGFA are directly and without any further act of admission entitled to represent parties before judicial authorities in Switzerland under the freedom to provide services. The entitlement arises by operation of law, not by administrative decision (Fellmann, Anwaltsrecht, N. 179; judgment **6B_68/2018** of 7 November 2018 E. 1).

**N. 14** The entitlement covers representation before all judicial authorities in Switzerland, including the Federal Supreme Court (Art. 40 para. 1 BGG in conjunction with Arts. 21 ff. BGFA; judgment **6B_68/2018** of 7 November 2018 E. 1). No residence permit is required as long as the 90-working-day threshold per calendar year (Art. 5 AFMP) is not exceeded (Art. 20 Annex I AFMP; judgment **2A.536/2003** E. 3.2.1). Where an activity is intended to be of indefinite duration or exceeds 90 working days, the regime of → Art. 27 BGFA replaces the freedom-to-provide-services regime (**BGE 151 II 640** E. 5.7.2).

**N. 15** Lawyers providing services are subject to the professional rules of the host state pursuant to Art. 25 BGFA and to disciplinary sanctions pursuant to Art. 26 BGFA. This ensures a minimum level of supervision without requiring full registration (Arts. 4 and 7 para. 2 of Directive 77/249/EEC; judgment **2A.536/2003** E. 4.2).

### 5. Contested Issues

#### 5.1 Distinction between Provision of Services and Permanent Practice

**N. 16** The distinction between activity under the freedom to provide services (Art. 21 BGFA) and permanent professional practice (Art. 27 BGFA) has been the subject of considerable development in case law and doctrine. In judgment **2A.536/2003** of 9 August 2004 (E. 3.2.2), the Federal Supreme Court adopted the ECJ's *Gebhard* jurisprudence (Case C-55/94) and drew the distinction by reference to frequency and duration, without treating the mere existence of an infrastructure in Switzerland as a decisive criterion. In judgment **2C_694/2011** of 19 December 2011 (E. 4.4), the Federal Supreme Court clarified that permanent activity requires integration into the economy of the host state, and that the legal practice in Switzerland must constitute the principal focus of the overall professional activity. An activity exceeding 90 working days was used as an indicator of permanence.

**N. 17** Dreyer (CR LLCA, Preliminary remarks on Arts. 21–26 BGFA N. 11) and Fellmann (Anwaltsrecht, N. 179) take the view that the 90-day threshold is not a rigid criterion of distinction but serves as an indicator. Chappuis/Châtelain (CR LLCA, Art. 27 BGFA N. 7) and Nater/Wipf (Bilaterale Verträge Schweiz-EG, pp. 256 f.) emphasise the temporal dimension: activity intended to exceed 90 days or of indefinite duration indicates establishment, so that registration in the EU/EFTA lawyers' list becomes necessary.

**N. 18** **BGE 151 II 640** (E. 5.7) has refined prior practice in light of more recent ECJ jurisprudence — notably *Torresi* (C-58/13) and *Monachos Eirinaios* (C-431/17) — and Establishment Directive 98/5/EC: registration in the EU/EFTA lawyers' list does not require a prior economic focus in Switzerland. Judgment **2C_694/2011** is departed from to the extent that it required a predominant activity in Switzerland prior to registration. For the purposes of the distinction under Art. 21 BGFA, this means: activity that is essentially temporary and occasional in nature (up to 90 working days, without intention to establish) remains subject to the services regime; the intention to engage in permanent professional practice — evidenced by the opening of an office with corresponding arrangements — triggers the regime of Art. 27 BGFA.

#### 5.2 Scope of the Concept of «Judicial Authorities»

**N. 19** It is disputed whether the term «judicial authorities» in Art. 21 para. 1 BGFA also covers authorities with a quasi-judicial function (e.g., conciliation authorities, administrative authorities with decision-making functions in disputes). Bohnet/Martenet (Droit de la profession d'avocat, N. 841) favour a broad interpretation in the interest of free movement; Kellerhals/Baumgartner (BSK BGFA, Preliminary remarks on Arts. 21–26 N. 4) tend towards a narrower interpretation oriented around the bodies of the ordinary and administrative court systems. The Federal Supreme Court's case law has not yet conclusively resolved this question in relation to Art. 21 BGFA.

#### 5.3 Relationship to the Representation Monopoly under Art. 127 para. 5 CrimPC

**N. 20** **BGE 147 IV 379** (E. 1.2.2) has clarified that the representation monopoly under criminal procedure law for the defence of the accused person pursuant to Art. 127 para. 5, first clause, CrimPC applies to EU/EFTA lawyers admitted under the freedom-to-provide-services regime of Arts. 21 ff. BGFA; they are accordingly entitled to act as defence counsel without registration. The Federal Supreme Court regards this lower threshold of admission compared to the domestic system as justified by the regulatory purpose of the AFMP. Fellmann (Anwaltsrecht, N. 824) shares this assessment; Nater (BSK BGFA, Art. 3 N. 6a) points to the systemic tension arising from the free movement concept, but considers the outcome required by EU law.

### 6. Practical Notes

**N. 21** **No prior notification requirement.** EU/EFTA lawyers operating in Switzerland under the freedom to provide services require neither a residence permit nor prior registration (**BGE 151 II 640** E. 4.2.1). They must, however, comply with reporting obligations under foreign nationals law (Art. 2 para. 4 Annex I AFMP; Art. 9 para. 1bis OFPA, SR 142.203). For short-term appearances before Swiss courts — for example in a single civil proceeding or as defence counsel in an individual mandate — no prior interaction with Swiss authorities is required.

**N. 22** **Proof of qualification.** Upon request by courts or supervisory authorities, lawyers must furnish proof of their legal qualifications (→ Art. 22 BGFA). It is advisable to keep the practising certificate or a current certificate from the professional association to hand. For registration in the EU/EFTA lawyers' list under Art. 28 para. 2 BGFA, the statute expressly requires that the certificate of registration with the competent authority of the home state must not be more than three months old; this statutory standard may serve as a reference point for practice under Art. 22 BGFA (**BGE 151 II 640** E. 4.2.2; Art. 28 para. 2 BGFA). The court is not obliged to request proof of its own motion; however, failure to furnish proof may result in procedural disadvantages for the party concerned.

**N. 23** **Exceeding the 90-day threshold.** Anyone who anticipates exceeding the threshold of 90 working days per calendar year (Art. 5 AFMP) or who wishes to operate permanently in Switzerland must register in the EU/EFTA lawyers' list in good time (→ Art. 27 BGFA). Continuing to practise after exceeding the threshold without registration is impermissible and subject to disciplinary measures under Art. 26 BGFA (Weber-Stecher, in: Professional Legal Services: Vom Monopol zum Wettbewerb, 2000, p. 59; **BGE 151 II 640** E. 4.2.1). The question of whether only physical presence in Switzerland, or also the management of mandates from abroad for Swiss clients, counts towards the 90-day calculation has not yet been conclusively determined by the highest court (Einhaus, in: Das künftige Berufsbild des Anwalts in Europa, 2000, p. 54; **BGE 151 II 640** E. 5.7.2).

**N. 24** **Professional title.** The use of Swiss professional titles such as «Rechtsanwalt/-anwältin» or «Advokat/-in» is not permitted for service providers; the professional title of the home state must be used (→ Art. 24 BGFA). Violations may be subject to disciplinary proceedings.

**N. 25** **Relationship to cantonal implementing legislation.** Various cantons have enacted implementing legislation for the BGFA. Such legislation may specify the procedure for furnishing proof of qualification, but may not tighten the federal-law conditions for admission, since federal law is largely exhaustive in the area of freedom to provide services (Art. 3 BGFA; Dreyer, CR LLCA, Preliminary remarks on Arts. 21–26 BGFA N. 6; Bohnet/Martenet, Droit de la profession d'avocat, N. 306). → Art. 3 BGFA.
