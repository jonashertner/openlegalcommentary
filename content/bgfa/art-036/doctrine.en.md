# Art. 36 BGFA — Transitional Provision: Registration of Existing Patent Holders

## Doctrine

### 1. Legislative History

**N. 1** Art. 36 BGFA corresponds to Art. 33 of the preliminary draft and was incorporated into the Act as the sole transitional provision of the BGFA. The Federal Council's Message of 28 April 1999 justifies the necessity of the provision on the ground that the BGFA introduced, for the first time at federal law level, minimum professional requirements for inter-cantonal practice (in particular the minimum one-year traineeship under Art. 7 lit. b BGFA). Persons who had obtained their cantonal licence (*Anwaltspatent*) under the previous cantonal law without satisfying these new professional requirements were not to be excluded retroactively from inter-cantonal practice (BBl 1999 6070 f.).

**N. 2** The Message identifies as the paradigmatic case the lawyer who had obtained a cantonal licence without having previously completed a minimum one-year traineeship, as well as the Bernese barristers (*Fürsprecher*) who, under the old regime, could obtain their licence without a formal law degree (*Lizenziat*) (BBl 1999 6070 f.; likewise Valloni/Steinegger, Bundesgesetz über die Freizügigkeit der Anwältinnen und Anwälte, 2002, p. 64 fn. 126). In both cases only professional requirements are at issue.

**N. 3** The constitutional basis of the transitional provision is Art. 196 No. 5 Cst. (transitional provision to Art. 95 Cst.), which obliged the cantons to mutually recognise educational qualifications until federal legislation was enacted. Pursuant to Art. 95 para. 2 Cst., the Confederation ensures that persons holding a cantonal or cantonally recognised educational qualification may practise their profession throughout Switzerland. Art. 36 BGFA implements this constitutional obligation: cantonal licences already held, which under previous law entitled their holders to practise inter-cantonally, are to remain valid under the new federal law.

**N. 4** In the National Council the question of transitional law was hardly contentious. The intensive parliamentary debates concerned the independence requirement in Art. 8 para. 1 lit. d BGFA (AB 1999 N 1556–1566), but not the design of the transitional law. The fact that the transitional provision was to cover only professional — and not personal — requirements was treated as an implicit premise.

### 2. Systematic Classification

**N. 5** Art. 36 BGFA, as the sole transitional provision, appears at the end of the BGFA, after the final provisions on EU/EFTA lawyers (Arts. 21–34a) and the repealing provision (Art. 35). The provision is a genuine intertemporal conflict-of-laws rule: it determines which law governs cantonal licences acquired before the BGFA entered into force on 1 June 2002.

**N. 6** Systematically, Art. 36 BGFA must be read in conjunction with the professional registration requirements (→ Art. 7 BGFA). The provision is the transitional-law counterpart of Art. 7 BGFA: whereas Art. 7 BGFA sets out the ordinary professional requirements for registration in the cantonal register, Art. 36 BGFA allows registration even where the new requirements are not formally satisfied, provided that the previous cantonal licence would have entitled the holder to practise inter-cantonally. The personal requirements of → Art. 8 BGFA are unaffected by Art. 36 BGFA (→ N. 10–12).

**N. 7** The provision is closely related to freedom of movement, which is the central subject-matter of the BGFA. The Act primarily aims to promote the inter-cantonal mobility of lawyers (BBl 1999 6020; **BGE 130 II 270** E. 3.1). Art. 36 BGFA ensures that the benefit of freedom of movement also extends to those who were already entitled to practise (inter-)cantonally under the previous law. ↔ Art. 6 BGFA (registration procedure); ↔ Art. 7 BGFA (professional requirements); → Art. 8 BGFA (personal requirements).

**N. 8** The BGFA contains no general transitional provision for disciplinary law. The Federal Supreme Court filled this gap by applying the principle of *lex mitior* by analogy: cantonal disciplinary decisions issued after the BGFA entered into force but concerning facts arising under the previous cantonal law must be assessed under both the old cantonal law and the new federal law, with the more lenient law applied (**BGE 130 II 270** E. 1.2). Art. 36 BGFA, by contrast, governs exclusively the requirements for registration, not disciplinary law.

---

### 3. Substance of the Provision

**N. 9** Art. 36 BGFA establishes an **entitlement to registration** for persons who cumulatively satisfy two requirements:

**(1) Prior cantonal licence:** The person concerned must hold a cantonal licence (*Anwaltspatent*) obtained under previous cantonal law. The decisive point in time is the legal position at the date the BGFA entered into force (1 June 2002). Licences obtained thereafter are not governed by Art. 36 BGFA but by the ordinary admission rules.

**(2) Eligibility for inter-cantonal authorisation under Art. 196 No. 5 Cst.:** The person concerned must have been eligible to obtain a professional-practice authorisation in other cantons pursuant to Art. 196 No. 5 Cst. (hypothetical assessment). This constitutional transitional provision obliged the cantons to mutually recognise educational qualifications. The reference to Art. 196 No. 5 Cst. and thereby to Art. 95 Cst. makes clear that the provision is exclusively concerned with **the validity and recognition of certificates of competence**, not with personal characteristics (**BGE 130 II 87** E. 8.1; judgment 2A.285/2003 of 7 April 2004 E. 3.1–3.2).

**N. 10** Art. 36 BGFA is a **transitional provision for professional requirements**. It exempts from compliance with the professional requirements of Art. 7 BGFA, but not from the personal requirements of Art. 8 BGFA. The Federal Supreme Court has explicitly and comprehensively reasoned this in **BGE 130 II 87** E. 8.2: the wording of the provision — in particular the reference to Art. 196 No. 5 Cst. and Art. 95 Cst. — relates exclusively to certificates of competence. There are no clear indications in the legislative materials for an interpretation that would also cover personal requirements.

**N. 11** If a person satisfies the ordinary requirements of the BGFA for registration (Arts. 7 and 8 BGFA), reliance on Art. 36 BGFA is unnecessary. The transitional provision operates only subsidiarily, i.e. only where ordinary registration under the new federal law is not possible, but where an inter-cantonal practice authorisation could have been obtained under the previous law (judgment 2A.101/2003 of 13 December 2003 E. 2.1; judgment 2A.285/2003 of 7 April 2004 E. 1.1).

**N. 12** It is undisputed in the literature that a lawyer who does not satisfy the personal requirements under Art. 8 para. 1 lits. a–c BGFA (legal capacity, absence of relevant criminal record entries, absence of certificates of unpaid debts) cannot rely on Art. 36 BGFA (**BGE 130 II 87** E. 8.2). The transitional provision cannot substitute for personal integrity.

---

### 4. Legal Consequences

**N. 13** The legal consequence of Art. 36 BGFA is the **entitlement to entry in the cantonal lawyers' register** (registration within the meaning of → Art. 6 BGFA). Where the requirements are met, the competent cantonal supervisory authority is obliged to register the applicant («sind einzutragen» — «shall be registered»). The wording is imperative; the supervisory authority has no discretion.

**N. 14** Registration pursuant to Art. 36 BGFA produces the same legal effects as ordinary registration under Art. 6 BGFA: inter-cantonal freedom of movement, i.e. the right to represent parties before the courts of all cantons without any additional authorisation. A lawyer registered under Art. 36 BGFA is fully subject to the professional rules (→ Art. 12 BGFA) and disciplinary supervision (→ Arts. 14–19 BGFA).

**N. 15** A registration made on the basis of Art. 36 BGFA may be cancelled pursuant to Art. 9 BGFA if the requirements subsequently cease to be met (e.g. upon loss of a personal requirement under Art. 8 BGFA). The transitional provision does not confer a permanent right to registration that is independent of the ongoing requirements.

**N. 16** The procedure is governed by cantonal law (→ Art. 34 para. 1 BGFA); the cantons must provide for a simple and expeditious procedure (Art. 34 para. 2 BGFA). An ordinary appeal lies against a negative decision of the cantonal supervisory authority; the cantonal bar association has standing to appeal (Art. 6 para. 4 BGFA; **BGE 130 II 87** E. 1).

### 5. Contested Issues

**N. 17** The most important contested issue concerned the **scope of Art. 36 BGFA with regard to employed lawyers**: may lawyers who had been admitted to practise under the more liberal previous cantonal law rely on Art. 36 BGFA to obtain registration, even if they do not satisfy the more restrictive registration requirement of independence under Art. 8 para. 1 lit. d BGFA?

**N. 18** **Position of Nater/Meier (liberal):** Hans Nater emphatically took the view that lawyers who had been admitted under a liberal cantonal practice with respect to employed lawyers must be registered on the basis of Art. 36 BGFA, even if they did not satisfy the more restrictive requirement under Art. 8 para. 1 lit. d BGFA (Nater, Steiniger Weg zur Harmonisierung des Anwaltsrechts in der Schweiz, SJZ 2002, pp. 362 ff., p. 364). Meier tended in the same direction: Bundesanwaltsgesetz: Probleme in der Praxis, plädoyer 2000/5, pp. 30 ff., p. 40, relying on previous Federal Supreme Court practice which the Federal Supreme Court itself later characterised as overly liberal.

**N. 19** **Position of Hess (restrictive):** Beat Hess, by contrast, denied that employed lawyers who, by reason of the presumption of lack of independence expressed in Art. 8 para. 1 lit. d BGFA, are not regarded as independent, could obtain registration on transitional grounds (Hess, Umsetzung des BGFA durch die Kantone, SJZ 2002, pp. 485 ff., pp. 493 f.).

**N. 20** **Federal Supreme Court (restrictive, prevailing view):** In **BGE 130 II 87** E. 8.2 the Federal Supreme Court aligned itself with Hess's restrictive view and rejected Nater's position. Its reasoning was as follows: by its wording — through the reference to Art. 196 No. 5 Cst. and thereby to Art. 95 Cst. — Art. 36 BGFA governs exclusively the recognition of certificates of competence. The independence requirement under Art. 8 para. 1 lit. d BGFA, by contrast, is a personal requirement that is not covered by Art. 36 BGFA. The Federal Supreme Court emphasised that the independence requirement was not newly introduced but had already applied as a central requirement for the practice of law in the reserved domain (*Monopolbereich*) before the BGFA entered into force. The transitional provision must therefore be read as exhaustively and exclusively addressing the issue of protection of legitimate expectations for existing licence holders with regard to professional requirements (judgment 2A.285/2003 of 7 April 2004 E. 3.3).

> «Art. 36 BGFA regelt als Übergangsbestimmung die Anerkennung von Anwaltspatenten, die möglicherweise den Voraussetzungen nicht genügen, welche nunmehr nach dem Anwaltsgesetz gelten. Nach seinem Wortlaut, insbesondere durch die Bezugnahme auf Art. 196 Ziff. 5 BV und damit auf Art. 95 BV, geht es ausschliesslich um die Massgeblichkeit und Anerkennung von Fähigkeitsausweisen. Angesprochen sind damit die fachlichen Voraussetzungen für die Berufsausübung bzw. den Registereintrag im Sinne von Art. 7 BGFA, nicht hingegen die persönlichen Voraussetzungen gemäss Art. 8 BGFA.»
> — **BGE 130 II 87** E. 8.2

**N. 21** The related question of **protection of legitimate expectations** (reliance on the previous liberal cantonal practice) was likewise rejected by the Federal Supreme Court: by enacting Art. 36 BGFA as a transitional provision while limiting concessions to professional requirements, the legislature exhaustively regulated the issue of protection of legitimate expectations (judgment 2A.285/2003 E. 3.3). The Internal Market Act (SR 943.02, BGBM) did not confer any more extensive protection of legitimate expectations, since it permitted cantons to impose stricter requirements regarding independence on lawyers resident in their territory (Art. 3 BGBM; BGE 125 II 56 E. 4 p. 61 f.).

**N. 22** A further — less contentious — consequential question concerns the application of Art. 36 BGFA where several applications are lodged simultaneously: Art. 36 BGFA contains no provision on simultaneous registration. The Federal Supreme Court held in **BGE 131 II 639** E. 3 in connection with ordinary applications that registration in only one single cantonal register is permissible; the same applies correspondingly to transitional applications under Art. 36 BGFA (→ Art. 6 para. 1 BGFA).

### 6. Practical Notes

**N. 23** Since the BGFA entered into force on 1 June 2002 and the brief transitional period of the early years, Art. 36 BGFA has largely lost its practical significance. Given that all cantonal licences issued under the previous cantonal law were either processed during the period of validity of the BGFA or have since had to be renewed on an ordinary basis under Art. 7 BGFA, the scope of application of the provision is confined to historical cases. Current applications are regularly based on Art. 6 in conjunction with Arts. 7 and 8 BGFA.

**N. 24** In the years 2002–2004 an extensive body of Federal Supreme Court case law on Art. 36 BGFA developed, manifesting itself primarily in the Canton of Zurich, where a particularly liberal practice had previously existed with regard to the admission of employed lawyers (the so-called «Zurich practice»). Numerous employed lawyers who had been admitted under this practice lodged applications under Art. 36 BGFA. These were rejected by the Federal Supreme Court — on the basis of the leading judgment **BGE 130 II 87** — to the extent that the applicants were unable to demonstrate satisfaction of the independence requirement under Art. 8 para. 1 lit. d BGFA (cf. judgments 2A.110/2003, 2A.126/2003, 2A.255/2003, 2A.285/2003, 2A.295/2003, 2A.333/2003, 2A.353/2003, 2A.357/2003, all from 2004).

**N. 25** For lawyers who obtained their cantonal licence under the previous cantonal law without a one-year traineeship, Art. 36 BGFA remains relevant where the validity of their original admission arises in disciplinary or registration proceedings. In such constellations, the historical facts must be examined under the previous cantonal law; Art. 36 BGFA ensures that the registration cannot be called into question on the ground of the missing traineeship.

**N. 26** For transitional registration applications based on Art. 36 BGFA, the supervisory authority must nonetheless examine whether the personal requirements of Art. 8 BGFA are fully satisfied — in particular independence, legal capacity, and the absence of certificates of unpaid debts and relevant criminal record entries. A dispensation application relies on Art. 36 BGFA only with respect to professional requirements (no traineeship, no law degree); personal integrity must be demonstrated without restriction.

**N. 27** The provision illustrates the general intertemporal principle: transitional law that exhaustively regulates the protection of legitimate expectations does not permit analogical extension in favour of further exceptional categories. A person who does not satisfy the personal requirements under the new law cannot rely on the previous more liberal cantonal practice — not even where that person practised without challenge for many years under that practice (judgment 2A.285/2003 E. 3.3).
