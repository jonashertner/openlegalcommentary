# Art. 9 BGFA — Removal from the Register

## Doctrine

### 1. Legislative History

**N. 1** Art. 9 BGFA is based on the Federal Council's draft of 28 April 1999 (BBl 1999 6013). In its dispatch, the Federal Council presented removal as the automatic consequence of the lapse of a condition for registration: anyone who no longer fulfils the personal or professional conditions under Art. 7 and 8 BGFA immediately loses the basis for the entry (BBl 1999 6050 para. 232.52). The provision was uncontested during the legislative process; the parliamentary debates focused on the substantive conditions for registration, in particular independence (Art. 8 para. 1 lit. d BGFA) and the question of criminal conviction for acts incompatible with the legal profession (Art. 8 para. 1 lit. b BGFA), and not on the removal mechanism as such (cf. AB 1999 NR 1567 f.).

**N. 2** As an example of acts that are incompatible with the legal profession and may lead to removal, the Federal Council expressly cited in its dispatch convictions for offences against property (BBl 1999 6050 para. 232.52). This illustrative mention has been developed in case law into a non-exhaustive catalogue of incompatible acts (→ N. 14 ff.).

---

### 2. Systematic Classification

**N. 3** Art. 9 BGFA falls within the first section of the Act («Freedom of movement and register», Art. 1–11 BGFA) and is the mirror image of the obligation to register under Art. 6 para. 2 BGFA. While Art. 6 para. 2 BGFA obliges the supervisory authority to register a lawyer when the conditions under Art. 7 and 8 BGFA are met, Art. 9 BGFA obliges the same authority to remove the entry as soon as one of those conditions lapses. ↔ Art. 6 BGFA (registration and removal as mirror images).

**N. 4** Removal under Art. 9 BGFA is an **administrative measure**, not a disciplinary measure within the meaning of Art. 17 BGFA. This distinction is fundamental both dogmatically and in practice: administrative measures serve a protective purpose — they are intended to prevent the harmful effects of an activity in the future; they are imposed when the statutory conditions for practising the profession are no longer fulfilled. Disciplinary measures under Art. 17 BGFA, by contrast, operate repressively and sanction breaches of professional rules (cf. judgment 2C_90/2019 of 22.8.2019, consid. 4.3; **BGE 137 II 425** consid. 7.2; judgment 2C_897/2015 of 25.5.2016, consid. 5.2). The two proceedings are independent of each other: removal from the register neither precludes a parallel or subsequent disciplinary procedure nor requires it (**BGE 137 II 425** consid. 7.2).

**N. 5** Since Art. 9 BGFA refers exhaustively to Art. 7 and 8 BGFA for the conditions of registration, federal law conclusively governs the removal mechanism in the field of inter-cantonal freedom of movement. The BGFA exhaustively regulates professional duties, disciplinary law and administrative measures (registration and removal) (cf. judgment 2C_897/2015 of 25.5.2016, consid. 5.3, with reference to **BGE 130 II 270** consid. 1.1 and 3.1.1). → Art. 3 BGFA (reservation of cantonal law limited to the cantonal licence to practise, not to registration in the register).

---

### 3. Content of the Provision

#### 3.1 Conditions: Lapse of a Condition for Registration

**N. 6** Art. 9 BGFA is satisfied when a lawyer **no longer fulfils one of the conditions for registration**. The provision thereby refers to the entire catalogue of professional conditions under Art. 7 BGFA (cantonal licence to practise, law degree, traineeship with examination) and the personal conditions under Art. 8 para. 1 BGFA:

- **lit. a**: Legal capacity (Art. 13 CC in conjunction with Art. 16 CC): loss of legal capacity, in particular in the case of permanent incapacity of judgement with respect to legal activity, leads to removal (judgment 2C_430/2013 of 22.7.2013, consid. 4.3 f.); a merely temporary incapacity of judgement does not, however, trigger removal.
- **lit. b**: No criminal conviction for acts incompatible with the legal profession, for as long as the conviction appears in the extract from the criminal register for private individuals.
- **lit. c**: No unsatisfied claims certificates: the existence of unsatisfied claims certificates mandatorily leads to removal (judgment VB.2012.00460 of the Administrative Court of Zurich; judgment VB.2018.00666 of the Administrative Court of Zurich).
- **lit. d**: Ability to practise independently and employment only with persons registered in the register: the subsequent lapse of institutional independence — for example, through a change in the shareholder structure of a law firm organised as a corporation — triggers the obligation to remove under Art. 9 BGFA (**BGE 147 II 61** consid. 4.2).

**N. 7** The conditions are satisfied upon the occurrence of the lapse; removal is tied to an objective state of affairs and not to fault or subjective culpability. The supervisory authority has no discretion as to whether to remove: once the lapse of a condition has been established, removal is mandatory (→ N. 10).

**N. 8** The existence of the lapse must be examined by the competent supervisory authority (→ Art. 14 BGFA). In the case of criminal convictions under Art. 8 para. 1 lit. b BGFA, the examination is linked to the final criminal judgment; the bar authority does not review the substantive correctness of the judgment (judgment 2C_1039/2021 of 26.8.2022, consid. 4.2, 6.2 f.; judgment VB.2021.00459 of the Administrative Court of Zurich, consid. 3.4.1). An administrative authority may depart from the factual findings of a criminal judgment only if new facts come to light or the criminal court has not investigated certain legal questions (judgment 2C_90/2019 of 22.8.2019, consid. 3.1).

#### 3.2 The Assessment of «Incompatibility» under Art. 8 para. 1 lit. b BGFA

**N. 9** In the case of criminal convictions, a two-stage examination applies: **Stage 1** — The supervisory authority assesses, with considerable discretion and in observance of the principle of proportionality, whether the acts for which the lawyer was convicted are compatible with the legal profession or not. The decisive criterion is an objective assessment of whether the offence committed is capable of damaging the reputation of the legal profession and undermining the necessary relationship of trust between lawyer and client (**BGE 137 II 425** consid. 6.1; judgment 2C_119/2010 of 1.7.2010, consid. 2.2). The acts need not necessarily have been committed in the context of professional activities; acts in the private sphere may also be professionally incompatible (**BGE 137 II 425** consid. 6.1, 6.3). A certain degree of gravity of the act is required, which must stand in a reasonable proportion to the removal. **Stage 2** — Once the supervisory authority has established the incompatibility and thereby affirmed the lapse of the condition under Art. 8 para. 1 lit. b BGFA, there is no further margin of discretion: removal must be ordered mandatorily (**BGE 137 II 425** consid. 6.1 in fine, consid. 7.1; judgment 2C_119/2010 of 1.7.2010, consid. 3; judgment 2C_1039/2021 of 26.8.2022, consid. 5.3, 6.7).

---

### 4. Legal Consequences

**N. 10** The legal consequence of removal is **mandatory** and **bound**: the supervisory authority has no discretionary power with respect to whether to effect removal at all once the lapse of one of the conditions has been established. The proportionality assessment takes place at Stage 1 (whether the act is incompatible), and not at Stage 2 (whether to refrain from removal despite established incompatibility) (**BGE 137 II 425** consid. 7.1; judgment 2C_430/2013 of 22.7.2013, consid. 4.4; judgment 2C_1039/2021 of 26.8.2022, consid. 6.7). Individual circumstances such as a first-time conviction, a remote date of the offence, remorse, or the absence of a financial motive do not alter the obligation to remove once incompatibility has been established (judgment 2C_1039/2021 of 26.8.2022, consid. 6.7; judgment 2C_90/2019 of 22.8.2019, consid. 6.1).

**N. 11** Removal takes effect **ex nunc** from the time the condition lapses (judgment VB.2018.00666 of the Administrative Court of Zurich). It has the effect that the lawyer loses the inter-cantonal freedom of movement associated with registration under Art. 4 BGFA and is no longer entitled to represent parties before judicial authorities within the scope of the lawyers' monopoly.

**N. 12** Removal under Art. 9 BGFA does not affect the cantonal licence to practise as such. The licence is an independent certificate of competence, the revocation of which is governed by cantonal law (judgment 2C_897/2015 of 25.5.2016, consid. 5.1, 7.2). However, removal from the lawyers' register may indirectly result in the loss of cantonal entitlements linked to registration — for example, the lapse of notarial powers in cantons that make such powers contingent on registration (judgment 2C_119/2010 of 1.7.2010, consid. 4.2; judgment 2C_430/2013 of 22.7.2013, consid. 5).

**N. 13** A lawyer who has been removed may apply for **re-registration** once the grounds for removal have ceased to exist. Since these are ongoing situations (e.g. recovery of legal capacity, deletion of the criminal conviction from the extract from the criminal register for private individuals pursuant to Art. 371 para. 3bis SCC), there is a right to a fresh assessment upon a material change in circumstances (judgment 2C_430/2013 of 22.7.2013, consid. 4.5). A lawyer who was removed on account of a conviction may apply for re-registration after the expiry of the probationary period and hence after deletion of the conviction from the criminal register extract (**BGE 137 II 425** consid. 7.1).

**N. 14** Removal under Art. 9 BGFA applies to lawyers subject to the BGFA, i.e. persons who represent parties before judicial authorities within the scope of the lawyers' monopoly (Art. 2 para. 1 BGFA). Lawyers who act exclusively in an advisory capacity without being registered are not covered by Art. 9 BGFA (judgment 2C_897/2015 of 25.5.2016, consid. 5.2.1).

**N. 15** Where a lawyer is employed by a law firm organised as a corporation, the lawyer **personally** bears responsibility for ensuring that the conditions of Art. 8 BGFA continue to be fulfilled. The supervisory authority may call upon the lawyer — but not the company directly — to regularise the situation; failure to do so renders removal under Art. 9 BGFA the mandatory consequence (**BGE 147 II 61** consid. 4.1 f.).

### 5. Contested Issues

**N. 16** **Temporal effect of removal.** Staehelin/Oetiker, in Fellmann/Zindel (eds.), Kommentar zum Anwaltsgesetz, 2nd ed. 2011, N. 8 on Art. 9 BGFA, take the view that removal has retroactive effect to the point in time when the grounds for removal arose. The Administrative Court of Zurich has in principle agreed, setting the date of removal at the time the conditions for removal were fulfilled (judgment VB.2018.00666). In practice, however, removal regularly takes effect ex nunc from the date of the supervisory authority's decision, since retroactive consequences for mandates carried out in the intervening period would be impractical. The question has not been conclusively resolved in the doctrine.

**N. 17** **Scope of the proportionality assessment.** Bohnet/Martenet, Droit de la profession d'avocat, 2009, N. 699 p. 309 and N. 610 f., regard proportionality as a constraint to be observed when assessing incompatibility under Art. 8 para. 1 lit. b BGFA, but one that leaves no further margin once incompatibility has been established. This position has been expressly confirmed by the Federal Supreme Court in **BGE 137 II 425** consid. 6.1 and judgment 2C_119/2010 of 1.7.2010, consid. 3. Meier/Reiser, in: Valticos/Reiser/Chappuis (eds.), Commentaire romand, Loi sur les avocats, 2010, N. 6 on Art. 9 LLCA, adopt a similar structure: broad discretion at the stage of the compatibility assessment, followed by mandatory removal. A dissenting view favouring a more extensive proportionality assessment at Stage 2 is not found in the case law.

**N. 18** **Relationship to a ban on practising a profession under Art. 67 SCC.** Bohnet/Martenet, op. cit., N. 613 p. 273, take the view that a ban on practising a profession imposed under Art. 67 para. 1 SCC is sufficient in itself to justify removal under Art. 8 para. 1 lit. b in conjunction with Art. 9 BGFA. The Federal Supreme Court clarified in **BGE 137 II 425** consid. 6.3 that such a ban on practising obviously justifies removal, but is **not** a prerequisite for it: even without a ban on practising, a lawyer's acts may be classified as professionally incompatible and thus trigger removal.

**N. 19** **Removal in the case of law firms organised as corporations.** Fellmann, Rechtsformen der Zusammenarbeit von Rechtsanwälten, Anwaltsrevue 2003, pp. 339 ff., 350, identified at an early stage the problem of structural independence in the case of companies limited by shares. The Federal Supreme Court clarified in **BGE 147 II 61** consid. 4.3–4.5 that the supervisory authority cannot order amendments to the articles of association beyond those available under Art. 685b ff. CO; the company and any non-lawyer shareholders remain outside the supervisory scope of the BGFA. Only the lawyer registered in the register may be called upon to remove under Art. 9 BGFA. Châtelain, L'indépendance de l'avocat et les modes d'exercice de la profession, 2017, N. 1437, and Fischer/Du Pasquier, RSDA 2008, pp. 550 f., have drawn attention to the difficulty of safeguarding structural independence in the case of single-member corporations.

**N. 20** **Cantonal revocation of licence to practise vs. removal from register.** Kettiger, Entzug des Anwaltspatents, Jusletter 28.9.2009, pp. 4 ff., takes the view that Art. 3 para. 1 BGFA does not confer upon the cantons the power to revoke the licence to practise upon the lapse of personal conditions, since Art. 3 para. 1 BGFA aims only at professional conditions. In contrast, Bohnet/Othenin-Girard/Schweizer, Commentaire romand, 2010, N. 16 on Art. 3 BGFA, as well as Bohnet/Martenet, op. cit., N. 576 ff., and the Federal Supreme Court's case law (judgment 2C_897/2015 of 25.5.2016, consid. 6.2.1) affirm the cantonal competence to revoke the licence to practise. The decisive point is: the BGFA governs only removal from the register (Art. 9 BGFA), while the revocation of the licence remains a matter of cantonal law and is to be distinguished in substance from removal.

### 6. Practical Notes

**N. 21** **Duty to notify.** The Federal Supreme Court derives from Art. 12 lit. a BGFA (duty of care) an obligation on the part of the lawyer to inform the supervisory authority of changes in circumstances that are relevant to the question of whether the conditions for registration are still fulfilled (**BGE 130 II 87** consid. 7 in fine). The same applies by analogy to Art. 12 lit. j BGFA (duty to report changes relevant to registration in the register). A breach of this duty by the lawyer does not alter the authority's obligation to remove, but may be relevant under disciplinary law.

**N. 22** **Significance of the criminal register extract.** A criminal conviction under Art. 8 para. 1 lit. b BGFA ceases to be a ground for removal as soon as it no longer appears in the extract from the criminal register for private individuals (Art. 371 para. 3bis SCC). The time of deletion depends on the sanction imposed and the expiry of the probationary period. Already when assessing incompatibility at Stage 1, the authority may take into account how long the conviction will remain in the extract; a first-time offender subject to a suspended sentence may expect a rapid expiry of the probationary period (**BGE 137 II 425** consid. 7.1).

**N. 23** **Distinction between disciplinary and administrative proceedings.** Since removal proceedings under Art. 9 BGFA are administrative rather than disciplinary proceedings, procedurally reduced requirements apply. In particular, under cantonal law there is generally no right to a public oral hearing where the matter concerns legal questions only and no personal impression of the person concerned is decisive (judgment 2C_90/2019 of 22.8.2019, consid. 4.1–4.3; no violation of Art. 6 para. 1 ECHR). The cantons govern procedure (Art. 34 para. 1 BGFA).

**N. 24** **Concurrent grounds.** Where several grounds for removal exist concurrently (e.g. unsatisfied claims certificates and a criminal conviction), proof of a single ground suffices for removal. The supervisory authority is not obliged to examine and give reasons for all grounds for removal (cf. judgment 2C_430/2013 of 22.7.2013, consid. 4.6, where the Federal Supreme Court confirmed removal on grounds of lack of legal capacity and left open whether the criminal conviction would also have led to removal).

**N. 25** **Re-registration.** A removed lawyer remains entitled to subsequent re-registration once the grounds for removal have ceased to exist. The authority is obliged, upon a corresponding application, to carry out a fresh assessment. In the case of unsatisfied claims certificates, re-registration is possible as soon as no such certificates remain outstanding; in the case of criminal convictions, as soon as these have been deleted from the extract from the criminal register for private individuals. The requirements for re-registration are the same as for initial registration (Art. 6 para. 2 in conjunction with Art. 7 and 8 BGFA).
