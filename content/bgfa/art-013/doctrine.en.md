# Art. 13 BGFA — Professional Secrecy

## Doctrine

### 1. Legislative History

**N. 1** The attorney's professional secrecy already existed in Switzerland prior to the BGFA, but was regulated differently across cantons. The legislature's aim was to achieve federal-law harmonisation of professional duties, including in particular professional secrecy. The Dispatch of 28 April 1999 noted that professional secrecy is «one of the most important elements of legal practice» and an indispensable prerequisite for clients to be able to place complete trust in their lawyer (BBl 1999 6039 para. 172.2).

**N. 2** Art. 13 BGFA follows the structure of Art. 321 SCC, which governs the criminally sanctioned violation of professional secrecy. The Federal Council sought to harmonise the professional-law duty of confidentiality with criminal-law protection, while limiting its scope to the lawyer-specific mandate. The Dispatch expressly distinguishes between lawyer-specific activity and other services that could equally be provided by asset managers or trustees (BBl 1999 6039 para. 172.2). The Federal Council's original proposal on criminal procedure law would have obliged lawyers released from professional secrecy to testify as witnesses. Parliament rejected this approach and upheld the absolute character of professional secrecy (**BGE 145 II 229** E. 7.1; **BGE 147 IV 385** E. 2.2).

**N. 3** Since the BGFA entered into force on 1 June 2002, attorneys' professional duties are exhaustively governed by federal law (**BGE 136 III 296** E. 2.1; **BGE 130 II 270** E. 3.1.1). Cantonal law cannot establish additional or deviating duties. The older cantonal case law, which for instance permitted fee claims without prior release, is no longer authoritative (**BGE 150 II 300** E. 5.1, 5.4).

---

### 2. Systematic Classification

**N. 4** Art. 13 BGFA is situated in the second chapter of the Act (Art. 12–20), which governs the rules of professional conduct and supervision. Professional secrecy, together with diligent professional practice (Art. 12 lit. a BGFA) and independence (Art. 12 lit. b BGFA), forms the core of substantive professional law. Systematically, Art. 13 BGFA is simultaneously a precondition for and a concretisation of registration eligibility: a person who is unable or unwilling to maintain the confidentiality of mandate information does not satisfy the personal requirements under Art. 8 para. 1 lit. d BGFA.

**N. 5** Art. 13 BGFA is closely related to the following provisions:

- ↔ Art. 321 SCC: parallel provision with criminal sanction; professional law imposes stricter standards than criminal law, which constitutes only the minimum threshold (**BGE 142 II 307** E. 4.3.2; **BGE 150 II 300** E. 5.6).
- → Art. 171 para. 1 CrimPC: procedural right of lawyers to refuse to testify in criminal proceedings; Art. 171 para. 4 CrimPC expressly reserves the stricter professional-law rule.
- → Art. 264 para. 1 lit. d CrimPC: prohibition on seizure of correspondence between clients and their lawyers entitled to practise under the BGFA.
- → Art. 160 para. 1 lit. b CivPC: right to refuse production in civil proceedings.
- → Art. 17 BGFA: violation of Art. 13 BGFA entails disciplinary measures.
- → Art. 12 lit. a BGFA: confidentiality forms part of diligent professional practice.
- ↔ Art. 13 FC (protection of private life): professional secrecy gives concrete expression to the constitutional protection of clients' personal data.

**N. 6** Professional secrecy under Art. 13 BGFA serves two protective functions: the individual protection of confidence of each client, and the public interest in a functioning administration of justice. Only when clients can place complete trust in their lawyers — in the knowledge that shared information will remain confidential — can the legal profession fulfil its systemic function in a rule-of-law state (**BGE 150 II 300** E. 5.5; **BGE 135 III 597** E. 3.4; Fellmann, Anwaltsrecht, 2nd ed. 2017, para. 520).

### 3. Elements of the Provision / Normative Content

#### 3.1 Material Scope of Protection (Art. 13 para. 1 sentence 1 BGFA)

**N. 7** Protected is «everything that has been entrusted to them by their clients in the course of their professional activity». The material scope of protection is broad: it covers not only the content of communications, but already the mere existence of a mandate relationship (**BGE 150 II 300** E. 5.2; **BGE 143 IV 462** E. 2.2). Protected are facts and documents that bear a connection — even a loose one — to the conduct of the mandate, in particular the existence of the mandate, fee claims, and the entire correspondence within the scope of the engagement (**BGE 143 IV 462** E. 2.2).

**N. 8** Activities performed outside the lawyer-specific mandate are not protected. Professional secrecy extends only to typical legal activity: advice and representation in legal matters, drafting of legal instruments, and conduct of proceedings. Acts that the lawyer could equally perform as a board member, asset manager, or trustee are not protected (**BGE 135 III 597** E. 3.3; **BGE 143 IV 462** E. 2.2; Nater/Zindel, Kommentar zum Anwaltsgesetz, Fellmann/Zindel [eds.], 2nd ed. 2011, N. 29 on Art. 13 BGFA). This distinction is particularly demanding in practice for lawyers who also perform advisory or managerial functions.

**N. 9** In-house counsel are in principle not covered by the protection of Art. 13 BGFA, because they are not entered in the attorneys' register and lack the requisite structural independence. This distinction from the Anglo-American concept of «legal privilege» is well established in case law (cf. Bohnet/Martenet, Droit de la profession d'avocat, 2009, N. 1811 p. 747; Nater/Zindel, op. cit., N. 29 on Art. 13 BGFA). By contrast, the criminal-law professional secrecy under Art. 321 SCC is broader in scope than that under Art. 13 BGFA: under certain conditions, it also covers foreign lawyers and patent attorneys not entered in the register (**BGE 147 IV 385** E. 2.6.2).

#### 3.2 Temporal and Personal Scope (Art. 13 para. 1 sentence 1 BGFA)

**N. 10** The duty of confidentiality applies «without temporal limitation and towards everyone». Professional secrecy does not expire upon termination of the mandate or upon the death of the lawyer. Professional secrecy may as a rule also be asserted against the heirs of the client (**BGE 135 III 597** E. 3.3–3.4; Fellmann, Anwaltsrecht, op. cit., para. 573). The prevailing doctrine holds that the right to release the secrecy holder (the client) is a strictly personal right that does not pass to the heirs (Bohnet/Martenet, op. cit., N. 1919–1921; Fellmann, Kommentar zum Anwaltsgesetz, N. 73 on Art. 13 BGFA).

#### 3.3 Discretion Following Release (Art. 13 para. 1 sentence 2 BGFA)

**N. 11** «Release does not oblige them [the lawyer] to disclose what has been entrusted to them.» Even after release by the client or by the supervisory authority, the lawyer retains the discretion whether to provide information (**BGE 136 III 296** E. 3.3; Bohnet/Martenet, op. cit., N. 1853 and 1869). Neither the client nor the supervisory authority can compel the lawyer to testify. In this sense professional secrecy is absolute: after release, the lawyer is authorised — but not obliged — to disclose.

**N. 12** Release from professional secrecy is governed exclusively by federal law (**BGE 142 II 307** E. 4.3.1; **BGE 150 II 300** E. 5.1). For a release granted by the supervisory authority, the requirements of a criminal-law ground of justification under Art. 321 ch. 2 SCC must at minimum be met, i.e. a clearly overriding public or private interest must be present (**BGE 142 II 307** E. 4.3.2–4.3.3). An advance and blanket release from professional secrecy — for example in respect of future fee disputes — is generally inadmissible, because at the time of signing the client cannot foresee the substantive and quantitative implications of the disclosure (**BGE 150 II 300** E. 5.8).

#### 3.4 Auxiliary Persons (Art. 13 para. 2 BGFA)

**N. 13** Art. 13 para. 2 BGFA obliges the lawyer to «ensure that professional secrecy is maintained by their auxiliary persons». The concept of auxiliary person corresponds to that in Art. 101 CO and is to be interpreted broadly: it covers employees, secretarial staff, trainee lawyers, and also external third parties to whom tasks are delegated, in particular cleaning companies, translation services, banks, and IT service providers (cloud providers) (**BGE 145 II 229** E. 7.3; Bohnet/Martenet, op. cit., N. 1861 p. 764–765).

**N. 14** The lawyer cannot discharge the obligation under Art. 13 para. 2 BGFA by means of contractual arrangements with auxiliary persons. In particular, it does not suffice to require an auxiliary person to ensure in turn that third parties engaged by that person comply with the secrecy obligation (sub-delegation) — such an arrangement transfers the primary responsibility to the auxiliary person, whereas that responsibility rests with the lawyer alone (**BGE 145 II 229** E. 7.4; Fellmann, Anwaltsrecht, op. cit., para. 649). The lawyer must carefully select auxiliary persons and instruct them on professional secrecy, where appropriate by means of a written confidentiality agreement (**BGE 145 II 229** E. 7.2; Bohnet/Martenet, op. cit., N. 1861 p. 765).

---

### 4. Legal Consequences

**N. 15** If a lawyer violates professional secrecy or fails to ensure its maintenance by auxiliary persons, this entails professional-law and criminal-law consequences:

- **Under professional law**: The supervisory authority may impose disciplinary measures under Art. 17 BGFA, ranging from a warning to a permanent prohibition on practising. → Art. 17 BGFA.
- **Under criminal law**: Art. 321 ch. 1 SCC makes violation of professional secrecy punishable by a custodial sentence of up to three years or a monetary penalty; prosecution requires a complaint by the aggrieved party.
- **Under civil law**: Violation of professional secrecy constitutes an infringement of the client's personality rights under Art. 28 CC and may give rise to claims for damages and satisfaction (Bohnet/Martenet, op. cit., N. 1799).

**N. 16** Professional secrecy also produces procedural protective effects. Lawyers may refuse to testify in criminal proceedings (Art. 171 CrimPC), in civil proceedings (Art. 163 para. 1 lit. b, Art. 166 para. 1 lit. b CivPC), and in administrative proceedings (Art. 13 para. 1bis APA). Correspondence between clients and their lawyer is exempt from seizure (Art. 264 para. 1 lit. d CrimPC). These procedural rights belong jointly to the client and the lawyer, but — by reason of the absolute character of the secrecy under sentence 2 — cannot be compelled by either party (**BGE 136 III 296** E. 3.3).

### 5. Disputed Questions

#### 5.1 Scope of Material Protection in Cases of Mixed Activities

**N. 17** It is disputed how the attorney's professional secrecy is to be delimited where the lawyer simultaneously performs non-legal functions (e.g. as a board member or asset manager). The Federal Supreme Court holds that professional secrecy protects only «genuinely legal activity» (**BGE 135 III 597** E. 3.3; **BGE 143 IV 462** E. 2.2). Fellmann (Anwaltsrecht, op. cit., para. 536 ff.) and Nater/Zindel (op. cit., N. 29 on Art. 13 BGFA) affirm protection for all activities bearing a connection — even a loose one — to a legal relationship. Bohnet/Martenet (op. cit., N. 1811), by contrast, advocate a view more narrowly oriented around the reserved domain. Case law on internal investigations (corporate investigations) has not yet been definitively settled: Chappuis (Enquête interne et secret professionnel, Revue de l'Avocat 2017 p. 44) and Groth/Ferrari-Visca (GesKR 2016 p. 500 ff.) hold diverging positions on the question of whether and to what extent professional secrecy protects compliance-related activities.

#### 5.2 Release from Professional Secrecy by the Client: Scope and Limits

**N. 18** Whether and under what conditions a release from professional secrecy may be validly granted was long disputed. The Federal Supreme Court clarified in **BGE 142 II 307** (E. 4.3) and **BGE 150 II 300** (E. 5.6–5.8) that criminal law (Art. 321 ch. 2 SCC) constitutes only a minimum threshold for professional law: a valid release requires a specific, foreseeable interest situation. Fellmann (Anwaltsrecht, op. cit., para. 547 ff.) had previously advocated a closer alignment of professional law and criminal law and favoured an autonomous professional-law balancing of interests, while Nater/Zindel (op. cit., N. 153 on Art. 13 BGFA) and Schiller (Schweizerisches Anwaltsrecht, 2009, N. 547) had already earlier affirmed the Federal Supreme Court's minimum requirement. Regarding advance release — for example in respect of future fee disputes — Fellmann (op. cit., para. 573) and Chappuis/Maurer (Commentaire romand, Loi sur les avocats, 2nd ed. 2022, N. 294 on Art. 13 BGFA) take a negative position, which the Federal Supreme Court confirmed in **BGE 150 II 300** (E. 5.8).

#### 5.3 Concept of Auxiliary Person and Cloud Computing

**N. 19** A current area of dispute concerns whether and under what conditions external IT service providers and cloud providers qualify as auxiliary persons within the meaning of Art. 13 para. 2 BGFA. The Federal Supreme Court held in **BGE 145 II 229** (E. 7.3) that external service providers qualify as auxiliary persons insofar as they process mandate-related information. Chappuis/Alberini (Secret professionnel de l'avocat et solutions cloud, Revue de l'avocat 8/2017, p. 340), Maurer/Gross (Commentaire romand, Loi sur les avocats, 2010, N. 100 on Art. 13 BGFA), and Schwarzenegger/Thouvenin et al. (Revue de l'avocat 1/2019, p. 37) affirm the classification of external IT service providers as auxiliary persons, but require proportionate protective measures. Nater/Zindel (op. cit., N. 51 on Art. 13 BGFA) emphasise the breadth of the concept of auxiliary person but identify the problem as lying in the lawyer's practical ability to exercise oversight. The prohibition on sub-delegation without the lawyer's knowledge and oversight is undisputed (**BGE 145 II 229** E. 7.4).

#### 5.4 Scope of Application for Foreign Lawyers

**N. 20** The Federal Supreme Court held in **BGE 147 IV 385** (E. 2.8) that the procedural protection under Art. 264 para. 1 lit. d CrimPC is limited to lawyers entitled to practise under the BGFA. Non-European lawyers do not enjoy this specific procedural-law protection, even if they may be subject to professional secrecy under Art. 321 SCC (which has a broader personal scope). Part of the doctrine (Chappuis/Steiner, Revue de l'avocat 2017, p. 88 ff.; Burckhardt/Ryser, AJP 2013, p. 159 ff.) criticises this distinction as systematically incoherent and difficult to apply in practice given the international nature of many mandates.

### 6. Practical Notes

**N. 21** **Clarify the material scope of protection in each individual case**: Lawyers who provide services beyond their lawyer-specific activity (board mandates, trust services, compliance) must document precisely for each mandate which activities are lawyer-specific. Only those activities are covered by professional secrecy under Art. 13 BGFA.

**N. 22** **No advance release in mandate agreements**: Clauses in mandate agreements or powers of attorney releasing the client in advance from professional secrecy are generally inadmissible insofar as they relate to future, unspecified fee disputes (**BGE 150 II 300** E. 5.8). Such clauses do not afford adequate protection to the client, because the duration, scope, and content of any potential disclosure cannot be determined with sufficient certainty at the time of signing.

**N. 23** **Carefully select and instruct auxiliary persons**: Lawyers must carefully select all persons and organisations that may have access to confidential client information and instruct them in writing on professional secrecy. Confidentiality agreements with auxiliary persons are recommended, but do not release the lawyer from their own duty of oversight (**BGE 145 II 229** E. 7.2).

**N. 24** **Cloud services**: The use of cloud services is compatible with Art. 13 para. 2 BGFA provided that the cloud provider is contractually bound to secrecy as an auxiliary person within the meaning of Art. 13 para. 2 BGFA, no inadmissible sub-delegation to third parties takes place, the lawyer retains control over the data, and the data is adequately secured. The Swiss bar associations have published recommendations in this regard.

**N. 25** **Search and sealing**: In the event of searches of law offices, lawyers have the right to request the sealing of all documents. The unsealing procedure is handled by the compulsory measures court, which carries out a «triage» between documents subject to professional secrecy and other documents. Unsealing is permissible only if the law office itself may be implicated in the matters under investigation (**BGE 130 II 193** E. 4.3–5.1).

**N. 26** **International tax administrative assistance**: In the area of international tax administrative assistance, professional secrecy may be invoked against access to legal documents (cf. judgment 2C_257/2023 [BGE 151 II 873]). The distinction between lawyer-specific activity and purely financial advice is decisive in this context.

**N. 27** **Relationship to Art. 321 SCC and the release procedure**: A lawyer who considers disclosing confidential information (e.g. to enforce a fee claim) must, prior to commencing legal proceedings, obtain release from the client or, if refused, from the competent supervisory authority. Release by the supervisory authority requires that the interest in disclosure clearly outweighs the interest in confidentiality (**BGE 142 II 307** E. 4.3.3). Release by the client itself requires that the client acts with knowledge of the implications of their declaration (**BGE 150 II 300** E. 5.7).
