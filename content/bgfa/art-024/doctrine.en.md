# Art. 24 BGFA — Professional Title

## Doctrine

### 1. Legislative History

**N. 1** Art. 24 BGFA originates from the Federal Council's draft bill of 28 April 1999 (BBl 1999 6013). In its dispatch, the Federal Council explained that the provision was intended to «prevent confusion with the professional titles of lawyers entered in a cantonal register» (BBl 1999 6064 para. 234.24). It implements Art. 4(1) of Directive 98/5/EC of the European Parliament and of the Council of 16 February 1998 (the so-called Establishment Directive; OJ L 77 of 14.3.1998, p. 36) and Art. 5(3) of Council Directive 77/249/EEC of 22 March 1977 (the so-called Services Directive; OJ L 78 of 26.3.1977, p. 17) into Swiss law. Both Directives are listed in Annex III to the Agreement on the Free Movement of Persons of 21 June 1999 (AFMP; SR 0.142.112.681) and are binding on Switzerland by virtue of Art. 16 AFMP (judgment **2A.536/2003** of 9 August 2004, consid. 3.1).

**N. 2** The Federal Council's draft of Art. 24 was adopted by the Federal Assembly without debate (AB 1999 N 1551 ff.; AB 1999 S 1173). The statutory text is therefore identical to the Federal Council's original draft. The European model, Art. 4(1) of Establishment Directive 98/5/EC, provides that a migrating lawyer shall practise under their home-State professional title and that such title «must be expressed in the official language or one of the official languages of the home Member State, in such a manner as to avoid confusion with the professional title of the host Member State». The Swiss legislature gave concrete form to this requirement by imposing a duty to indicate the professional organisation or the authorising court.

**N. 3** Historical context: Prior to the entry into force of the BGFA on 1 June 2002, there was no uniform federal-law framework governing the activities of foreign lawyers in Switzerland. EU lawyers could appear under cantonal law subject to varying requirements. The BGFA replaced the previous fragmentation of the law with a uniform duty to use a specified professional title (Fellmann, Anwaltsrecht, 2nd ed. 2017, para. 184; Bohnet/Martenet, Droit de la profession d'avocat, 2009, para. 820).

---

### 2. Systematic Classification

**N. 4** Art. 24 BGFA is situated in the 4th Section of the BGFA (Arts. 21–26), which governs the activities of EU/EFTA lawyers providing services under the freedom to provide services. Together with Art. 22 (proof of professional qualification), Art. 23 (obligation to act through a local lawyer), and Art. 25 (professional conduct rules), Art. 24 forms the framework of obligations applicable to lawyers providing services. The provision contains no cross-reference to the regime governing permanent practice; however, Art. 27(2) BGFA expressly provides that Art. 24 BGFA also applies to lawyers registered on the EU/EFTA lawyers' list under Art. 28 BGFA (**BGE 151 II 640**, consid. 4.2.2; judgment **2A.536/2003**, consid. 3.2.2).

**N. 5** Art. 24 BGFA therefore applies to two categories of EU/EFTA lawyers: (1) lawyers providing services within the meaning of Art. 21(1) BGFA who are active in Switzerland on a temporary basis and without registration; (2) lawyers registered on the EU/EFTA lawyers' list within the meaning of Art. 27(1) BGFA who practise on a permanent basis under their home-State professional title (Art. 27(2) in conjunction with Art. 24 BGFA). The provision does not apply to EU/EFTA lawyers who have been entered in the cantonal lawyers' register pursuant to Art. 30 BGFA: such lawyers use the professional title customary in the canton of their establishment (Art. 33 BGFA; judgment **2A.536/2003**, consid. 3.2.3).

**N. 6** In relation to Art. 12(d) BGFA (prohibition of false or misleading information), Art. 24 BGFA is lex specialis: infringements of the duty to use the correct professional title are primarily subsumed under Art. 24 BGFA and may be pursued by way of disciplinary proceedings under Art. 26 BGFA; at the same time, a violation of the general prohibition of misleading conduct under Art. 12(d) BGFA may also be present (Supervisory Commission Zurich, KG10008 of 26 August 2010, consid. 4.3.2.5; Fellmann, BSK BGFA, Art. 12 N. 115).

→ Art. 21 BGFA (freedom to provide services); → Art. 22 BGFA (proof of qualification); → Art. 23 BGFA (obligation to act through a local lawyer); → Art. 25 BGFA (professional conduct rules); → Art. 26 BGFA (disciplinary sanctions); ↔ Art. 27(2) BGFA (permanent practice); → Art. 33 BGFA (cantonal professional title following registration); → Art. 12(d) BGFA (prohibition of misleading conduct).

### 3. Elements of the Provision / Normative Content

#### 3.1 Persons Subject to the Obligation

**N. 7** Art. 24 BGFA is addressed to «lawyers providing services» within the meaning of Art. 21(1) BGFA. These are nationals of EU or EFTA member states who are entitled to practise as lawyers in their home state under one of the professional titles listed in the Annex to the BGFA, and who temporarily represent parties before judicial authorities in Switzerland under the freedom to provide services (→ Art. 21 BGFA). By virtue of the reference in Art. 27(2) BGFA, Art. 24 BGFA applies equally to permanently practising lawyers registered on the EU/EFTA lawyers' list (**BGE 151 II 640**, consid. 4.2.2; Dreyer, CR LLCA, Art. 24 N. 1).

#### 3.2 Duty to Use the Home-State Professional Title

**N. 8** The provision mandatorily requires the use of the «home-State professional title». «Home-State» refers to the professional title under which the lawyer is admitted to the bar in their home state and which is listed in the Annex to the BGFA for that state. For Germany and Austria this is «Rechtsanwalt»/«Rechtsanwältin», for France «Avocat(e)», for Italy «Avvocato»/«Avvocatessa», and for the United Kingdom (EEA via EFTA agreement) «Barrister» or «Solicitor» (Kellerhals/Baumgartner, BSK BGFA, Art. 27 N. 4).

**N. 9** The use of Swiss professional titles such as «Rechtsanwalt/-anwältin» or «Advokat/-in» without registration in the cantonal lawyers' register is impermissible. Such titles create the false impression that the person in question has obtained their qualification in Switzerland and is entered in the cantonal register. The protective purpose of the provision requires a clear demarcation (Supervisory Commission Zurich, KG10008 of 26 August 2010, consid. 4.3.3.7, with reference to the dispatch, BBl 1999 6066; Nater/Tuchschmid, in: Bilaterale Verträge I/II Schweiz-EU, 2007, p. 320).

#### 3.3 Official Language of the Home State

**N. 10** The professional title must be used in the official language of the home state. Where a home state has several official languages (e.g. Belgium with French, Dutch, and German), the language applicable to the relevant bar association or licensing authority must be used. The original designation is determinative; a translation into a Swiss national language is impermissible because it increases rather than reduces the risk of confusion (Dreyer, CR LLCA, Art. 24 N. 3; Bohnet/Martenet, Droit de la profession d'avocat, para. 820).

#### 3.4 Indication of the Professional Organisation or the Authorising Court

**N. 11** In addition to the home-State professional title, either the professional organisation to which the lawyer belongs (e.g. «Rechtsanwaltskammer München») or the court before which the lawyer is authorised to practise under the rules of the home state (e.g. «zugelassen beim Oberlandesgericht Frankfurt») must mandatorily be indicated. In Germany and Austria, the law provides for membership of a bar association (Kammer); in countries with court-based admission (e.g. England and Wales), the authorising court must be specified. The person concerned has a free choice between the professional organisation and the authorising court; both alternatives equally fulfil the objective of transparency (BBl 1999 6064 para. 234.24; Dreyer, CR LLCA, Art. 24 N. 4).

**N. 12** This additional indication is not confined to pleadings or written submissions before a court. It must appear wherever the professional title is used: on letterhead, business cards, the law firm's website, in brochures, and in all other means of communication (Supervisory Commission Zurich, KG10008 of 26 August 2010, consid. 4.3.3.5; Nater/Tuchschmid, loc. cit., p. 320). The indication may take the form of a footnote or a parenthetical addition, provided that the connection between the professional title and the indication of home-state origin remains clearly identifiable to the public seeking legal assistance.

#### 3.5 Extension to Specialist Lawyer Titles and Specialisation Designations

**N. 13** According to the practice of the Supervisory Commission Zurich, Art. 24 BGFA applies not only to the general professional title but also to specialist lawyer titles and comparable specialisation designations obtained in another state. A German «Fachanwältin für Familienrecht» (specialist lawyer in family law) must therefore also indicate the home-state origin for that title, since specialisations «in terms of their content must likewise be characterised as a professional title within the meaning of Art. 24 BGFA» (Supervisory Commission Zurich, KG10008 of 26 August 2010, consid. 4.3.3.8 f., 4.3.4.3). An indication accompanying the general professional title does not suffice for the specialist lawyer title, because no automatic connection to the home-State title is established.

**N. 14** The rationale underlying this extended application is the same as for the basic title: persons seeking legal assistance should be able to recognise that the title is based on a foreign specialist lawyer scheme and does not certify knowledge of Swiss law. A particular risk of confusion exists in Switzerland, where the Swiss Bar Association (SAV) awards its own protected «Fachanwalt SAV» (SAV specialist lawyer) title (Supervisory Commission Zurich, KG10008 of 26 August 2010, consid. 4.3.4.4).

#### 3.6 Protective Purpose of the Provision

**N. 15** The purpose of Art. 24 BGFA is threefold: (1) **Transparency for the public seeking legal assistance**: clients should be able to recognise without difficulty that the lawyer has not obtained their qualification in Switzerland and is not registered in a cantonal register (BBl 1999 6064; Supervisory Commission Zurich, KG10008, consid. 4.3.3.7). (2) **Prevention of the risk of confusion**: the original title in the language of the home state ensures that no equation with the local professional title arises, as the ECJ also emphasised in C-168/98 (*Luxembourg v European Parliament*) of 7 November 2000, paras. 28 and 34. (3) **Assistance in deciding on the mandate**: the home-State professional title indicates which legal system the lawyer primarily masters, and enables an informed decision on the grant of a mandate (Kellerhals/Baumgartner, BSK BGFA, Art. 27 N. 4).

### 4. Legal Consequences

**N. 16** Art. 24 BGFA establishes a direct, statutory obligation. Breach of the duty regarding professional titles constitutes a violation of professional conduct rules and may be sanctioned by the cantonal supervisory authorities by means of the disciplinary sanctions provided for in Art. 26 BGFA. The possible sanctions range from a warning and a reprimand to a fine of up to CHF 20,000 and, in the case of serious violations, a practice prohibition (Art. 26(1) BGFA; → Art. 17 BGFA for the corresponding catalogue of sanctions in the domestic register).

**N. 17** In addition, a misleading professional title may constitute a violation of the general professional prohibition of misleading conduct under Art. 12(d) BGFA, which gives rise to independent disciplinary consequences (Supervisory Commission Zurich, KG10008, consid. 4.3.2.5; Fellmann, BSK BGFA, Art. 12 N. 115). In cantons that provide for their own protection of professional titles, deliberate deception as to qualification may additionally give rise to criminal liability (Art. 148a SCC, obtaining a service without paying; Art. 251 SCC, forgery of documents) where false professional titles are used in written documents — although such cases are rare in practice.

**N. 18** A breach of the duty regarding professional titles does not affect the validity of procedural acts. Submissions filed under an incorrect professional title remain procedurally valid insofar as the material standing to act as representative (Arts. 21 or 27(1) BGFA) was present. The duty regarding professional titles is a professional-law requirement, not a procedural-law admissibility requirement (Dreyer, CR LLCA, Art. 24 N. 5; Bohnet/Martenet, Droit de la profession d'avocat, para. 820).

### 5. Contested Issues

#### 5.1 Legal Character of the Obligation — Professional Law or Procedural Law?

**N. 19** Dreyer (CR LLCA, Art. 24 N. 5) and Bohnet/Martenet (Droit de la profession d'avocat, para. 820) characterise Art. 24 BGFA as a purely professional-law obligation without procedural-law effects on the validity of submissions. Nater/Tuchschmid (Bilaterale Verträge I/II Schweiz-EU, 2007, p. 320) share this view. By contrast, it remains unsettled whether courts might, in particular circumstances — for instance in cases of systematic concealment of foreign origin — consider refusing a request for party costs or imposing similar procedural consequences. Case law has not yet decided this question.

#### 5.2 Permissibility of Indications in Several Languages

**N. 20** It is disputed whether the professional title may only be indicated in the official language of the home state or whether a supplementary indication in a Swiss national language is permissible. Dreyer (CR LLCA, Art. 24 N. 3) rejects any translation addition because it may increase the risk of confusion. Kellerhals/Baumgartner (BSK BGFA, Art. 27 N. 4) accept an explanatory translation in parentheses provided that the home-State character of the title remains unambiguously recognisable to the public. The Supervisory Commission Zurich did not give a definitive ruling on this point in KG10008 but, in practice, accepted footnotes and parenthetical additions as sufficient where the link to the home-State title was clear (KG10008, consid. 4.3.4.2).

#### 5.3 Relationship to Art. 27(2) BGFA: Separate Provision or Reference?

**N. 21** Art. 27(2) BGFA refers to Art. 24 BGFA without repeating its wording. Chappuis/Châtelain (CR LLCA, Art. 27 N. 8) and Kellerhals/Baumgartner (BSK BGFA, Art. 27 N. 4) understand Art. 24 BGFA as a uniform provision which, by virtue of the reference, applies both to lawyers providing services and to permanently practising EU/EFTA lawyers, without any separate requirements. By contrast, Dreyer (CR LLCA, Art. 24 N. 1) raises the question whether the use of the professional title by lawyers registered on the EU/EFTA lawyers' list should be subject to stricter requirements — for instance because such lawyers practise with a permanent office in Switzerland and therefore create a greater risk of confusion. The prevailing doctrine rejects such a distinction because the statute provides no basis for differentiation.

#### 5.4 Applicability in Hybrid Mandate Arrangements

**N. 22** It is unsettled whether Art. 24 BGFA also applies when an EU/EFTA lawyer appears jointly with a lawyer registered in Switzerland (cf. → Art. 23 BGFA) and is referred to merely as «co-counsel». The dispatch is silent on this point (BBl 1999 6064). Bohnet/Martenet (Droit de la profession d'avocat, para. 820) take the view that Art. 24 BGFA applies to any form of appearance by an EU/EFTA lawyer as soon as the foreign qualification is communicated externally in any form. This broad interpretation accords with the protective purpose of the provision and is to be preferred.

### 6. Practical Notes

**N. 23** **Design of letterhead and website.** EU/EFTA lawyers should use their home-State professional title in the official language of their home state and immediately thereafter — in a footnote or parenthesis — indicate the competent professional organisation or the authorising court. A formally correct example for a German female lawyer: «Rechtsanwältin (zugelassen bei der Rechtsanwaltskammer Hamburg, Deutschland)» [lawyer admitted with the Hamburg Bar Association, Germany]. General designations such as «Anwältin» (lawyer) or «Juristin» (legal professional) without an indication of home-State origin do not satisfy Art. 24 BGFA (Supervisory Commission Zurich, KG10008, consid. 4.3.3.5; Dreyer, CR LLCA, Art. 24 N. 4).

**N. 24** **Specialist lawyer titles.** A lawyer who uses a foreign specialist lawyer title in addition to their general professional title must also indicate the home-State origin for that title. The indication accompanying the general professional title does not suffice. As a compliant formulation, the Supervisory Commission Zurich recognised in KG10008, for example, «Deutsche Fachanwältin für Familienrecht» [German specialist lawyer in family law] or «Fachanwältin für Familienrecht gemäss deutscher Fachanwaltsordnung» [specialist lawyer in family law under the German specialist lawyer scheme]. The use of the title «Fachanwalt SAV» by a person not certified by the SAV is impermissible without further examination (KG10008, consid. 4.3.4.4).

**N. 25** **Transition from the provision of services to permanent practice.** EU/EFTA lawyers who register on the EU/EFTA lawyers' list (→ Arts. 27(1) and 28 BGFA) remain bound by Art. 27(2) in conjunction with Art. 24 BGFA to use the home-State professional title. Only registration in the cantonal lawyers' register under Art. 30 BGFA permits the use of the local Swiss professional title (Art. 33 BGFA). Lawyers seeking to make this transition should carefully maintain the professional title throughout the three-year period of registration on the list (Art. 30(1)(b) BGFA) in order to avoid disciplinary proceedings (**BGE 151 II 640**, consid. 4.2.2–4.2.3).

**N. 26** **Appearance before the Federal Supreme Court.** Under Art. 40(1) BGG in conjunction with Arts. 21 ff. BGFA, EU/EFTA lawyers may represent parties before the Federal Supreme Court. In the written appeal, the professional title must be indicated in accordance with Art. 24 BGFA. The Federal Supreme Court does not examine compliance with this obligation ex officio but may draw attention to an incorrect designation and notify the competent cantonal supervisory authority (judgment **6B_68/2018** of 7 November 2018, consid. 1; Fellmann, Anwaltsrecht, para. 184).

**N. 27** **Supervisory practice.** Cantonal supervisory authorities have increasingly refined the practice relating to professional titles. The Supervisory Commission of the Canton of Zurich has, in several decisions, objected to designations that concealed the home-State connection or came too close to Swiss title protection. Lawyers newly registering on the EU/EFTA lawyers' list are well advised to submit their communication materials to the competent supervisory authority for review in advance, in order to avoid disciplinary proceedings.
