# Art. 10 BGFA — Inspection of the Register

## Doctrine

### 1. Legislative History

**N. 1** Art. 10 BGFA originates from the Federal Council's draft of 28 April 1999. In the Dispatch (BBl 1999 6013, 6045), the right of inspection of the cantonal lawyers' register is conceived as a necessary complement to the keeping of the register under Art. 5 BGFA: the register can fulfil its protective and transparency function only if the relevant authorities actually have access to the information entered therein. From the outset, the Federal Council distinguished between an official right of inspection (para. 1) and a limited right of public access to information (para. 2).

**N. 2** According to the Dispatch, the provisions of the BGFA governing the register pursue «mere harmonisation between the cantons» (BBl 1999 6013, 6061; confirmed in **BGE 150 II 308** E. 5.7). The right of inspection under Art. 10 BGFA is therefore primarily an instrument of intercantonal coordination; it does not create a substantive supervisory power but presupposes one that already exists.

**N. 3** Art. 10 BGFA was not materially altered during the parliamentary deliberations. The differentiated structure — with letter c as a particularly significant provision for intercantonal supervisory authority coordination — remained unchanged from the Federal Council's draft. The BGFA entered into force on 1 June 2002 (AS 2002 863).

---

### 2. Systematic Classification

**N. 4** Art. 10 BGFA falls within the first chapter of the BGFA (Art. 1–11) on freedom of movement and the register system. The provision connects directly to Art. 5 BGFA (content of the register): under Art. 5 para. 2 let. e BGFA, the register contains, among other things, disciplinary measures that have not yet been deleted; Art. 10 BGFA governs access to this information. → Art. 5 BGFA (register content); ↔ Art. 20 BGFA (deletion of disciplinary measures).

**N. 5** The provision has a dual function: on the one hand it serves official supervision of the practice of the profession (para. 1), and on the other it serves transparency vis-à-vis the public (para. 2). Both functions exist in tension with data protection and the right to informational self-determination of the registered lawyers (→ Art. 13 FC; Data Protection Act). Bohnet/Martenet, Droit de la profession d'avocat, 2009, pp. 316 f., characterise the public right to information under para. 2 as a right that was deliberately not made contingent on any specific interest of the person making the request («inconditionnel»), but is narrowly circumscribed in terms of content.

**N. 6** Systematically, Art. 10 BGFA must be read in conjunction with Art. 18 para. 2 BGFA: that provision requires that prohibitions on practising the profession be notified to all cantonal supervisory authorities. Art. 10 para. 1 let. c BGFA supplements this duty of notification with a comprehensive right of inspection on request. Both mechanisms together ensure nationwide information on prohibitions on practising the profession (**BGE 150 II 308** E. 7.8). ↔ Art. 18 para. 2 BGFA (duty of notification to cantonal supervisory authorities).

---

### 3. Content of the Provision

#### 3.1 Official Right of Inspection (para. 1)

**N. 7** Art. 10 para. 1 BGFA sets out an exhaustive list of authorities entitled to inspect the register:

**(a) Federal and cantonal judicial and administrative authorities (let. a):** All authorities «before which lawyers appear» are entitled. The term «appear» encompasses, according to the purpose of the provision, any form of professional representative activity before the authority, including not only oral hearings but also written representation. Fellmann, Anwaltsrecht, 2nd ed. 2017, para. 360, notes that the concept of authority is to be construed broadly and includes both judicial and administrative authorities.

**(b) EU/EFTA authorities (let. b):** This provision reflects Switzerland's integration into the European legal market through the Agreement on the Free Movement of Persons (AFMP, SR 0.142.112.681) and the second chapter of the BGFA (Art. 21 ff.). Foreign authorities before which lawyers registered in Switzerland appear are entitled. → Art. 21 ff. BGFA (cross-border activities).

**(c) Cantonal supervisory authorities (let. c):** This provision is of central practical importance. The cantonal supervisory authorities receive, upon request, access to the register of another canton. The purpose is adequate intercantonal information on disciplinary measures (**BGE 150 II 308** E. 5.7; **BGE 148 I 226** E. 5.3.4). Staehelin/Oetiker, in: Fellmann/Zindel (eds.), BSK Anwaltsgesetz, 2nd ed. 2019, Art. 10 N. 1 and 3, emphasise that this right of inspection is granted upon request («only upon request»), so as to avoid automatism and to preserve the principle of proportionality.

**(d) Lawyers in relation to their own entry (let. d):** Every registered person has the right to inspect their own register entry. This individual right of inspection derives from the principle of informational self-determination (Art. 13 para. 2 FC) and the right of access under data protection law; Staehelin/Oetiker, BSK Anwaltsgesetz, 2nd ed. 2019, Art. 10 N. 4, characterise it as a concretisation of the right of access under data protection law in the context of professional law.

#### 3.2 Public Right to Information (para. 2)

**N. 8** Art. 10 para. 2 BGFA grants every person the right to information as to whether a lawyer is registered and whether a prohibition on practising the profession has been imposed against them. The right is unconditional in the sense that no legitimate interest on the part of the person making the request is required — it does not presuppose any legal interest — but is strictly limited in terms of content: it is confined to the question of registration and the existence of a prohibition on practising the profession.

**N. 9** The scope of the public right to information is deliberately restricted. Disciplinary measures falling short of a prohibition on practising the profession (caution, reprimand, fine) are not covered by para. 2. The public therefore has no general right of access to a lawyer's disciplinary history. This restriction corresponds to the legislative intent to strike a balanced relationship between transparency and protection of the personality of registered lawyers (BBl 1999 6013, 6045; Bohnet/Martenet, Droit de la profession d'avocat, 2009, pp. 316 f.).

**N. 10** The protective function of the register in favour of the public manifests itself primarily through Art. 10 para. 2 BGFA: the public can verify at any time whether a lawyer is authorised to practise. This transparency serves to maintain trust in the profession (Judgment 2C_430/2013 of 22 July 2013 E. 4.4: «The public […] can rightly expect a registered lawyer to be in principle capable of representing clients before a court»).

#### 3.3 Relationship to Art. 6 para. 3 BGFA (Publication in the Register)

**N. 11** Art. 6 para. 3 BGFA obliges the cantons to publish registration in the register in the cantonal official gazette. This duty of publication differs fundamentally from the unsolicited publication of disciplinary measures: the former is provided for by statute; the latter is not. The Federal Court has clarified that the publication of a disciplinary measure in the cantonal official gazette on the basis of cantonal law is contrary to federal law, because the BGFA exhaustively governs disciplinary measures (**BGE 150 II 308** E. 7.4–7.9). Art. 10 BGFA constitutes, in this context, an essential normative component of the exhaustive federal regulatory system: authorities throughout Switzerland have the necessary information available through Art. 10 para. 1 and Art. 18 para. 2 BGFA; further publication in the official gazette is therefore neither necessary nor compatible with federal law (**BGE 150 II 308** E. 7.8). → Art. 6 para. 3 BGFA (publication in the official gazette); → Art. 17 BGFA (exhaustive disciplinary measures).

### 4. Legal Consequences

**N. 12** The right of inspection under para. 1 is a subjective public right of the listed authorities vis-à-vis the canton maintaining the register; Staehelin/Oetiker, BSK Anwaltsgesetz, 2nd ed. 2019, Art. 10 N. 1, characterise it as an official right of inspection that obliges the register-keeping authority to provide information. The cantons regulate the procedure pursuant to Art. 34 para. 1 BGFA.

**N. 13** The right to information under para. 2 is structured as an individual right available to any person. The cantonal register-keeping authorities are obliged to provide information; charging fees for simple inquiries is, according to the purpose of the provision, hardly compatible with the transparency objective of para. 2 to the extent that it factually impedes the exercise of that right. The information is limited to registration and, where applicable, an existing prohibition on practising the profession; further information on the disciplinary history must not be provided (BBl 1999 6013, 6045).

**N. 14** If a cantonal authority unlawfully refuses inspection under para. 1 or information under para. 2, the entitled authority or person has access to the cantonal legal remedies (Art. 34 para. 1 BGFA). The procedure is governed by cantonal law; final cantonal decisions may be appealed to the Federal Court by way of a public law appeal (Art. 82 let. a BGG).

### 5. Contested Issues

#### 5.1 Scope of the Public Right to Information under para. 2

**N. 15** It is undisputed in the legal literature that the public right to information under Art. 10 para. 2 BGFA is limited to the question of registration and the existence of a prohibition on practising the profession. Bohnet/Martenet, Droit de la profession d'avocat, 2009, pp. 316 f., and Staehelin/Oetiker, BSK Anwaltsgesetz, 2nd ed. 2019, Art. 10 N. 3, agree that the right is unconditional but narrowly limited in terms of content. The question debated is whether the cantons could de lege ferenda create greater transparency. According to Federal Court practice, this is excluded, as the BGFA exhaustively governs disciplinary law including questions of publicity (**BGE 150 II 308** E. 7.4).

#### 5.2 Use of Deleted Disciplinary Measures in Intercantonal Proceedings

**N. 16** A significant contested issue concerned the treatment of disciplinary measures that have been deleted from the register pursuant to Art. 20 BGFA: may a supervisory authority, in the context of new disciplinary proceedings, take into account sanctions imposed by another canton that have come to its attention but have already been deleted from the register? Brunner/Henn/Kriesi, Anwaltsrecht, 2015, p. 251, held the minority view that measures no longer apparent from the register should be disregarded. The Federal Court expressly rejected this view in **BGE 150 II 308** E. 5.5–5.10 and confirmed the contrary position: supervisory authorities may take prior misconduct into account — including sanctions deleted from the register — when determining disciplinary measures. However, deleted measures lose weight as the time elapsed increases (clarification of case law, **BGE 150 II 308** E. 5.10; → Art. 20 BGFA).

**N. 17** The Federal Court's reasoning is based on teleological considerations: Art. 10 BGFA governs only inspection of the registers, but does not create a bar on the use of information that has come to the knowledge of an authority by other means (**BGE 150 II 308** E. 5.7). The former criminal law bar on use under the former Art. 369 para. 7 SCC, on which the minority view had relied, did not apply comprehensively even before its repeal by the Criminal Records Act (CRA, in force since 23 January 2023); moreover, the legislature, by removing the bar on use, expressed that sector-specific consideration of prior conduct despite deletion from the register is permissible (**BGE 150 II 308** E. 5.6.4).

#### 5.3 Publication of Disciplinary Measures in the Official Gazette

**N. 18** Isolated cantonal legal orders provided for the publication of disciplinary measures in the cantonal official gazette (e.g. § 23 para. 1 let. d EG BGFA/ZG). The legal question of whether this was compatible with the exhaustive regulation of the BGFA was contested before **BGE 150 II 308**. Poledna, in: Kommentar zum Anwaltsgesetz, 2nd ed. 2011, Art. 17 N. 1, and Bauer/Bauer, in: Commentaire romand, Loi sur les avocats, 2nd ed. 2022, Art. 17 N. 1, proceeded on the basis of an exhaustive catalogue of disciplinary measures without specifically addressing the question of publication. The Federal Court in **BGE 150 II 308** E. 7.4–7.9 characterised the publication in the official gazette as an autonomous repressive sanction and declared it contrary to federal law, as it is not provided for in the exhaustive catalogue of Art. 17 BGFA.

### 6. Practical Notes

**N. 19** Lawyers appearing before an authority may assume that that authority can verify their registration and any prohibitions on practising the profession at any time. They have their own right to inspect their register entry (Art. 10 para. 1 let. d BGFA) and, where appropriate, to request corrections pursuant to Art. 6 para. 2 BGFA.

**N. 20** Cantonal supervisory authorities should, when initiating disciplinary proceedings against a lawyer registered in another canton, formally request inspection of the register of the canton of registration pursuant to Art. 10 para. 1 let. c BGFA. This enables the disciplinary history to be taken into account in accordance with Art. 16 para. 2 BGFA and **BGE 150 II 308** E. 5.9. In parallel, any disciplinary measures imposed must be notified to the canton of registration pursuant to Art. 16 para. 1 BGFA. ↔ Art. 16 BGFA (intercantonal coordination in disciplinary proceedings).

**N. 21** Cantonal supervisory authorities may, pursuant to **BGE 150 II 308** E. 5.10, take deleted disciplinary measures into account when determining sanctions. However, as the time elapsed increases, past measures carry less weight; the authority must take this into account in the exercise of its discretion. This principle must be expressly set out in the reasons given for the disciplinary decision.

**N. 22** Cantonal provisions providing for the publication of disciplinary measures in the official gazette are, pursuant to **BGE 150 II 308** E. 7.9, contrary to federal law and must not be applied. The information necessary for the protection of the public is conveyed exclusively through the mechanisms of Art. 10 BGFA and Art. 18 para. 2 BGFA.

**N. 23** Third parties (persons seeking legal advice, potential clients) may, pursuant to Art. 10 para. 2 BGFA, enquire with the competent cantonal authority — generally the cantonal supervisory authority — whether a particular person is registered and whether a prohibition on practising the profession has been imposed against them. No information on a lawyer's disciplinary history may be provided; such information would exceed the scope of Art. 10 para. 2 BGFA and would infringe data protection provisions (Bohnet/Martenet, Droit de la profession d'avocat, 2009, p. 317).
