# Art. 7 BGFA — Professional Requirements

## Doctrine

### 1. Legislative History

**N. 1** The BGFA was adopted on 23 June 2000 (SR 935.61) and entered into force on 1 June 2002. Art. 7 traces back to decades-long efforts to harmonise Swiss lawyer law at the federal level. As early as the beginning of the 20th century, attempts to establish a federal lawyers' patent failed; the federal legislature therefore confined itself to minimum standards and left the design of cantonal examination regulations to the cantons (Message of 28 April 1999 on the BGFA, BBl 1999 6013, 6019 para. 114; confirmed by judgment 2C_897/2015 of 25.5.2016 para. 8).

**N. 2** In its original version, Art. 7 para. 1 lit. a BGFA referred to the «licentiate» as the relevant qualification, which corresponded to the study structure of the time. In the course of the Bologna Reform — the introduction of the two-tier Bachelor/Master system at Swiss universities — Art. 7 BGFA was revised by the Federal Act of 23 June 2006 (in force since 1 January 2007; RO 2006 4399, 4401): lit. a was supplemented to include the Master's degree; para. 3, which designates the Bachelor's degree as a sufficient condition for admission to traineeship, was newly inserted (Message of 26 October 2005 on the amendment of the BGFA, BBl 2005 6207, 6211 ff.). The aim was to open direct access to traineeship for students upon completion of their Bachelor's degree, thereby enabling greater flexibility in legal training (BBl 2005 6213 f.).

**N. 3** Art. 7 para. 1 lit. b remained unchanged in its basic structure: the traineeship of at least one year with a concluding examination was from the outset the second core element. The Message emphasised that this established a minimum standard that made the inter-cantonal recognition of the lawyers' patent possible in the first place (BBl 1999 6041 f. para. 211).

---

### 2. Systematic Classification

**N. 4** Art. 7 BGFA falls within the first section of the Act («Freedom of Movement and Register», Arts. 1–11) and governs the *professional* requirements for registration. It must be strictly distinguished from Art. 8 BGFA, which governs the *personal* requirements (legal capacity, no relevant criminal record, no certificates of unpaid debts, independence). The requirements of both articles must be cumulatively satisfied before the supervisory authority may register an applicant (Art. 6 para. 2 BGFA; **BGE 130 II 87** para. 2.2).

**N. 5** Art. 7 is the link between cantonal patent law and federal freedom of movement: the lawyers' patent is granted by the cantons (Art. 3 para. 1 BGFA); however, the federal regulation conclusively sets the *minimum requirements* that a canton must demand when granting a patent entitling the holder to inter-cantonal registration. Beyond this, the cantons may impose stricter professional requirements (Staehelin/Oetiker, BSK BGFA, Art. 7 N. 5; Nater, BSK BGFA, Art. 3 N. 12c). They may not, however, fall below the minimum level without violating federal law (Art. 49 para. 1 FC).

**N. 6** ↔ Art. 3 BGFA (cantonal regulatory reservation): The cantons retain the right to determine the requirements for obtaining the lawyers' patent, «within the framework of this Act» (Art. 3 para. 1 BGFA). Art. 7 BGFA sets this framework for professional requirements. → Art. 8 BGFA: Personal requirements. → Art. 36 BGFA: Transitional provision for patents obtained under former law; the transitional provision releases holders only from the *professional* requirements of Art. 7, not from the personal requirements of Art. 8 (**BGE 130 II 87** para. 8.2). → Arts. 21 ff. BGFA: Special rules for EU/EFTA lawyers, who may take an aptitude test (Art. 31 BGFA) or a professional interview (Art. 32 BGFA) in lieu of Art. 7 BGFA.

### 3. Elements of the Provision / Normative Content

#### 3.1 Overview of Requirements

**N. 7** Art. 7 para. 1 BGFA sets out two cumulatively required professional prerequisites for the granting of the cantonal lawyers' patent — and thus indirectly for registration:

- **lit. a**: Legal studies completed with a licentiate or Master's degree from a Swiss university, or an equivalent diploma from a state with a mutual recognition agreement;
- **lit. b**: A traineeship in Switzerland of at least one year, concluded with an examination on theoretical and practical legal knowledge.

Art. 7 para. 3 BGFA clarifies that a Bachelor's degree in law is sufficient for admission to traineeship.

#### 3.2 Legal Studies (Art. 7 para. 1 lit. a)

**N. 8** What is required is *legal studies* («études de droit»; «studi di diritto»). The Message emphasises that this means a course of study in *law* that concludes with the award of an academic qualification in that discipline (BBl 1999 6042). A doctorate in law built upon a non-legal licentiate (e.g. from a Faculty of Arts) does not suffice — the candidate lacks an underlying legal degree. The Administrative Court of Aargau held that a licentiate from a Faculty of Arts combined with a doctorate in law does not satisfy the admission requirement (Decision WBE.2016.385 of 18.1.2017; published in AGVE 2017 42).

**N. 9** *Swiss university*: Recognised qualifications are the licentiate and the Master's degree. Since the Bologna Reform (Art. 7 para. 1 lit. a in the version of 2007), the Master's degree is explicitly mentioned; the licentiate remains relevant for qualifications obtained under former law. The designation of the institution as «Swiss» refers to university-level institutions offering degree programmes in law; universities of applied sciences are not covered, as they lack the authority to award a Master's degree in law (Meier/Reiser, CR LLCA, Art. 7 N. 9).

**N. 10** *Equivalent university diploma from a recognised state*: The reference standard is the Swiss licentiate or Master's degree in law. The foreign diploma must be equivalent in content, i.e. it must provide a sufficient grounding in the core areas of law. The Federal Supreme Court has held that this principle is to be interpreted teleologically: the decisive question is whether the person concerned possesses sufficient foundational knowledge (**BGE 146 II 309** paras. 4.4.4–4.4.6). Recognition agreements exist in particular with EU/EFTA states under the Agreement on the Free Movement of Persons (AFMP, SR 0.142.112.681) and the EFTA Convention; outside these treaties, individual recognition assessments are required.

#### 3.3 The Traineeship (Art. 7 para. 1 lit. b)

**N. 11** The traineeship must last *at least one year* and must be completed *in Switzerland*. Activities abroad are not creditable. The minimum duration of one year is a federal minimum standard; the cantons may prescribe a longer traineeship (Staehelin/Oetiker, BSK BGFA, Art. 7 N. 4a; Bohnet/Martenet, Droit de la profession d'avocat, 2009, N. 528). The traineeship must be concluded with an *examination* on theoretical and practical legal knowledge; the content and form of the examination fall within cantonal competence (judgment 2C_505/2019 of 13.9.2019 para. 5.1).

**N. 12** The Federal Supreme Court has clarified that the BGFA contains no provisions regarding the content and modalities of the bar examination; this area falls within the competence of the cantons (judgment 2C_505/2019 para. 5.1 with reference to Meier/Reiser, CR LLCA, Art. 7 N. 43). A canton may therefore include in the bar examination subjects extending beyond the narrow scope of the lawyers' monopoly, provided there is a public interest in doing so (e.g. competent representation of clients' interests across all areas of law) (judgment 2C_505/2019 paras. 5.2.2 f.).

**N. 13** The creditability of traineeship periods is governed by cantonal law, which must however respect the federal framework. Cantonal decisions confirm that activities at federal authorities (e.g. the Directorate of International Law), at private companies, or outside the actual administration of justice are only partially or not at all creditable as traineeship if they do not meet the objective — the practice of core lawyering activities (Decision AG Lawyers' Commission AVV.2012.15 of 24.5.2012, AGVE 2012 3).

#### 3.4 The Bachelor's Degree as a Sufficient Condition for Admission to Traineeship (Art. 7 para. 3)

**N. 14** Art. 7 para. 3 BGFA provides: «A Bachelor's degree in law is a sufficient condition for admission to traineeship.» («Le bachelor en droit est une condition suffisante pour l'admission au stage.») The Federal Supreme Court analysed this wording in detail in **BGE 146 II 309**.

**N. 15** The wording — «sufficient» (not: «necessary») condition — raises the question of whether a Master's degree without a prior legal Bachelor's degree equally suffices for admission to traineeship. The Federal Supreme Court answered this in the negative in **BGE 146 II 309** para. 4.4.5, on teleological grounds: the Bachelor's degree systematically imparts the foundations of Swiss law; the Master's degree deepens specialist areas without necessarily covering all core fields. A Master's degree without a prior legal Bachelor's degree therefore does not guarantee sufficient foundational knowledge for admission to traineeship. A foreign Bachelor's degree from a recognised state is creditable on an equivalent basis, but must correspond in content to the Swiss Bachelor's degree (**BGE 146 II 309** para. 4.4.6).

### 4. Legal Consequences

**N. 16** If the requirements of Art. 7 BGFA are satisfied, the canton *may* grant the lawyers' patent — the decision as to whether and how the patent is structured (e.g. the granting of the title) is governed by cantonal law. Without a patent, registration under Art. 6 para. 2 in conjunction with Art. 7 BGFA is precluded.

**N. 17** The lawyers' patent is primarily a *certificate of competence* (declaratory order). It does not directly entitle its holder to represent parties before judicial authorities in other cantons; for this purpose, registration under Arts. 4–6 BGFA is always required (judgment 2C_897/2015 para. 7.2.2; Fellmann, Anwaltsrecht, 2017, para. 672). At the cantonal level, Art. 3 para. 2 BGFA may empower cantons to exempt holders of their own patent from the registration requirement — whether a canton makes use of this option is determined by the cantonal lawyers' act.

**N. 18** If the professional requirements are subsequently found to be absent (e.g. upon discovery of a fraudulently obtained qualification), the registration must be deleted under Art. 9 BGFA. Cantonal revocation of the patent is to be distinguished from this: it is governed by cantonal law, but is only permissible where the cantons have also linked the patent to the satisfaction of personal requirements (judgment 2C_897/2015 paras. 6.2 f.).

**N. 19** The transitional provision of Art. 36 BGFA releases holders of patents obtained under former law from the professional requirements of Art. 7 BGFA, but not from the personal requirements of Art. 8 BGFA (**BGE 130 II 87** para. 8.2; Valloni/Steinegger, Bundesgesetz über die Freizügigkeit der Anwältinnen und Anwälte, 2002, p. 64 fn. 126).

### 5. Disputed Questions

#### 5.1 Relationship between Bachelor and Master under Art. 7 para. 3 BGFA

**N. 20** The most significant disputed question concerns the interpretation of Art. 7 para. 3 BGFA: does a Master's degree in law without a prior legal Bachelor's degree suffice for admission to traineeship?

**N. 21** The Message of 2005 assumed that a Master's degree guarantees «at least the same knowledge and competences» as a Bachelor's degree (BBl 2005 6217); Meier/Reiser (CR LLCA, Art. 7 N. 32) and Bohnet/Martenet (Droit de la profession d'avocat, 2009, N. 528) followed this premise and affirmed admission to traineeship on the basis of a Master's degree without a legal Bachelor's degree.

**N. 22** Staehelin/Oetiker (BSK BGFA, Art. 7 N. 4a), by contrast, took the view that it was not for the admission authorities to call into question university decisions on the award of a Master's degree.

**N. 23** The Federal Supreme Court rejected both positions. In **BGE 146 II 309** paras. 4.4.3–4.4.5, it held that the Message had proceeded from a false premise (that the Master's degree guarantees sufficient foundational knowledge of Swiss law) and that the teleological interpretation — protection of those dependent on the justice system and ensuring competent legal advice — requires the Bachelor's degree as a cumulative prerequisite. In so doing, the Federal Supreme Court deliberately departs from the prevailing doctrine, justifying this on the basis that the purpose of the norm takes precedence over the historical legislative intent where the latter rests on a false factual assumption.

#### 5.2 Cantonal Examination Autonomy and Federal Law

**N. 24** The extent of cantonal discretion regarding the content and form of the bar examination is disputed. The Federal Supreme Court has clarified that cantons may examine subjects not forming part of the core area of the lawyers' monopoly (e.g. constitutional and administrative law), provided there is a public interest in doing so and the examination requirements are proportionate (judgment 2C_505/2019 paras. 5.2.2 f.). This constitutes a broad interpretation of cantonal autonomy under Art. 3 para. 1 BGFA.

**N. 25** However, cantons may not impose additional requirements that effectively undermine the condition designated as sufficient in Art. 7 para. 3 BGFA (Bachelor's degree) by requiring a Master's degree for admission to traineeship (**BGE 146 II 309** para. 4.3). Such cantonal law would violate the supremacy of federal law (Art. 49 para. 1 FC).

#### 5.3 Legal Nature of the Lawyers' Patent: Authorisation or Declaratory Order?

**N. 26** Traditional scholarship characterised the lawyers' patent as a police authorisation (Fellmann, Anwaltsrecht, 2017, para. 672 with further references). The Federal Supreme Court clarified in judgment 2C_897/2015 para. 7.2.2 that the lawyers' patent — at least in cantons without exemption from the registration requirement (Art. 3 para. 2 BGFA) — is not an authorisation to practise a profession but rather a *declaratory order*, since it does not directly entitle the holder to represent parties. Kettiger (Entzug des Anwaltspatents, Jusletter 28.9.2009, p. 4) had already taken this position earlier. The qualification has practical significance for the permissibility of patent revocation: as a declaratory order, the general conditions for revocation apply (judgment 2C_897/2015 para. 6.3).

#### 5.4 Cantonal Personal Requirements for the Patent

**N. 27** Whether cantons may, in addition to the federal minimum requirements of Art. 7 para. 1 BGFA (professional requirements), also impose *personal* requirements for the granting of the cantonal lawyers' patent was disputed. Federal Supreme Court case law and the prevailing doctrine answer this in the affirmative: **BGE 134 II 329** para. 5.1; Nater, BSK BGFA, Art. 3 N. 3; Bohnet/Othenin-Girard/Schweizer, CR LLCA, Art. 3 N. 16; Fellmann, Anwaltsrecht, 2017, paras. 684 f. Kettiger (op. cit., pp. 4 f.) had argued the contrary position, pointing to the systematic structure of the BGFA, under which the term «lawyers' patent» appears only in connection with *professional* requirements (Art. 7), not personal ones (Art. 8). The Federal Supreme Court rejected this systematic interpretation in judgment 2C_897/2015 para. 6.2.3.

### 6. Practical Notes

**N. 28** *University qualification*: Candidates with a foreign university degree must actively demonstrate the substantive equivalence of their diploma. It is not sufficient that the diploma formally originates from a recognised state; it must substantively cover the foundations of Swiss law (**BGE 146 II 309** para. 4.4.6). Candidates holding a foreign Master's degree without a legal Bachelor's degree should at an early stage examine whether their qualification will be recognised as «equivalent to a Bachelor's degree».

**N. 29** *Choice and duration of traineeship*: The creditability of traineeship positions varies considerably between cantons. Activities outside traditional legal practice (legal departments of public administrations, international organisations, NGOs) are sometimes only partially credited by cantonal authorities (cf. Decision AG Lawyers' Commission AVV.2012.15; AGVE 2006 11). Trainees should clarify creditability with the cantonal supervisory authority before taking up a position.

**N. 30** *Bar examination*: The Federal Supreme Court exercises considerable restraint in the substantive review of examination results and intervenes only in cases of arbitrariness (**BGE 131 I 467** para. 3.1). Candidates wishing to challenge a negative examination result must raise specific procedural errors (formal defects); mere criticism of the assessment (appellate complaints) does not suffice. Complaints under Art. 6 para. 1 ECHR are not admissible with respect to purely substantive assessment questions (**BGE 131 I 467** para. 2.9).

**N. 31** *Examination content*: Cantons are free to extend the examination content beyond the monopoly area, provided there is a statutory basis and a public interest (judgment 2C_505/2019 para. 5.2.2). Candidates should therefore prepare for the full, cantonally defined examination syllabus, even where individual subjects are not directly attributable to the lawyers' monopoly.

**N. 32** *Bachelor pathway*: Students wishing to commence traineeship directly upon completion of their Bachelor's degree have a federally guaranteed right to admission — the canton may not impose a Master's degree as an additional condition (**BGE 146 II 309** para. 4.3). Traineeship may thus be completed concurrently with the Master's degree, which reduces the overall duration of legal training.

**N. 33** *Transitional law*: For lawyers who obtained their patent under former law and do not hold a licentiate, Art. 36 BGFA applies. This transitional provision protects only the professional qualification, not any deficiency in the personal requirements. In practice, this remains relevant where deletion from the register is threatened and the person concerned invokes Art. 36 BGFA (**BGE 130 II 87** paras. 8.2 f.).
