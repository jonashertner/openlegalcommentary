# Art. 2 BGFA — Personal Scope of Application

## Doctrine

### 1. Legislative History

**N. 1** Art. 2 BGFA originates from the Federal Council's draft Message of 28 April 1999 (BBl 1999 6013). The Lawyers Act was enacted in response to the unsatisfactory state of inter-cantonal freedom of movement for lawyers under the former regime of the transitional provisions of the Federal Constitution (Art. 5 Trans. Prov. aCst.) and the Internal Market Act (BGBM, SR 943.02). The Federal Supreme Court had held in **BGE 125 II 56 E. 3** that the existing system of cantonal authorisation procedures was «perceived in practice as bureaucratic and unsatisfactory».

**N. 2** The Federal Council proposed a register system whereby a single cantonal entry confers the right to practise throughout Switzerland in the monopoly domain (BBl 1999 6045 f., Nos. 232.1 and 232.2). The personal scope of application was conceived from the outset as a two-part structure: para. 1 covers lawyers admitted to the bar in Switzerland; para. 2 establishes a framework for EU/EFTA lawyers and — following the revision upon conclusion of the agreement with the United Kingdom (SR 0.142.113.672) — for British nationals.

**N. 3** During the parliamentary deliberations, para. 1 was uncontested. Discussions focused on the EU/EFTA provisions (Art. 21 et seq. BGFA) and on the question of professional independence of employed lawyers (Art. 8 para. 1 let. d BGFA; cf. AB 1999 N 1553 et seq.; AB 1999 S 1164 et seq.). Art. 2 itself was adopted without material amendments to the draft. The addition in para. 2 let. b concerning British nationals was made by the amendment of 19 March 2021 (AS 2021 908), in force since 1 September 2021.

---

### 2. Systematic Classification

**N. 4** Art. 2 BGFA is the pivotal provision for the scope of application of the entire Act. The Federal Supreme Court has repeatedly held that Art. 2 para. 1 BGFA «defines the personal scope of application of the Act and does not constitute a provision conferring competence» (judgment 2C_505/2019 of 13 September 2019 E. 5.1). It follows that no positive rights of admission can be derived from Art. 2; the provision merely delimits the class of persons subject to the subsequent obligations and rights.

**N. 5** Within the systematic structure of the BGFA, Art. 2 constitutes the gateway to two layers of regulation:

- *First layer* (para. 1): lawyers admitted to the bar throughout Switzerland — governed by Art. 3–20 BGFA (freedom of movement, register, professional rules, supervision);
- *Second layer* (para. 2): EU/EFTA lawyers and British nationals — governed by Art. 21–34a BGFA.

**N. 6** Cross-references: Art. 2 para. 1 is directly linked to → Art. 3 BGFA (reservation of cantonal provisions), → Art. 4 BGFA (freedom of movement upon registration), → Art. 6 BGFA (registration) and ↔ Art. 12 BGFA (professional rules, which for their part apply without restriction only to lawyers active in the monopoly domain). For EU/EFTA lawyers, para. 2 refers to → Art. 21 et seq. BGFA (provision of services) and → Art. 27 et seq. BGFA (permanent professional practice).

**N. 7** The provision coordinates the BGFA with procedural law: the lawyers' monopoly under Art. 2 para. 1 BGFA is reflected in procedural law in Art. 68 para. 2 CPC (professional representation of parties in civil proceedings) and Art. 127 para. 5 CrimPC (criminal defence). ↔ Art. 171 para. 1 CrimPC (lawyers' right to refuse to testify) and Art. 160 para. 1 let. b CPC in turn attach to the class of persons defined by Art. 2 BGFA.

### 3. Content of the Provision

#### 3.1 Paragraph 1: Lawyers Admitted to the Bar Throughout Switzerland

**N. 8** Art. 2 para. 1 BGFA sets out two cumulative requirements for the personal scope of application:

1. **Bar admission (Anwaltspatent)**: The person must hold a cantonal bar admission. The bar admission is granted by the cantons in accordance with Art. 7 BGFA (at least a one-year traineeship with a final examination; university degree in law). The cantons regulate the content and conduct of the bar examination independently — the BGFA merely sets minimum requirements (judgment 2C_505/2019 E. 5.1; BBl 1999 6042, No. 22).

2. **Representation of parties within the monopoly domain**: The person must represent parties before judicial authorities. The lawyers' monopoly covers the professional representation of parties in court proceedings (civil proceedings, criminal proceedings, administrative justice), but not pure legal advice activities.

**N. 9** *Concept of the lawyers' monopoly*: The Federal Supreme Court has clarified that «the lawyers' monopoly» does not constitute a genuine monopoly in the legal sense, but rather a classic regulatory admission requirement (**BGE 130 II 87 E. 3 p. 92**). The monopoly domain exclusively concerns the professional representation of parties before judicial authorities; it does not extend to extrajudicial legal advice (Fellmann, Anwaltsrecht, 2nd ed. 2017, para. 16 et seq.; Bohnet/Martenet, Droit de la profession d'avocat, 2009, para. 8 et seq.).

**N. 10** *Professional secrecy and the monopoly domain*: The lawyer's professional secrecy (→ Art. 13 BGFA) extends beyond the monopoly domain. In **BGE 150 IV 470 E. 3.1** the Federal Supreme Court held: «The protection of professional secrecy is not limited to the monopoly domain of legal activities (cf. Art. 2 para. 1 BGFA, Art. 68 para. 2 CPC and Art. 127 para. 5 CrimPC), but covers all professionally typical legal activities.» Accordingly, Art. 2 para. 1 BGFA as a provision on the scope of application of the Act is not identical to the scope of protection afforded by professional secrecy.

**N. 11** *Dimension in criminal proceedings*: Art. 127 para. 5 CrimPC codifies the lawyers' monopoly in criminal proceedings. In **BGE 147 IV 379 E. 1.2.2–1.2.3** the Federal Supreme Court clarified that the monopoly domain in criminal proceedings applies to both professional *and* non-professional defence counsel, whereas Art. 68 para. 2 CPC limits the monopoly domain in civil proceedings to the professional representation of parties.

**N. 12** *Registration in the cantonal lawyers' register as an expression of the monopoly domain*: Registration (→ Art. 6 BGFA) is the formal prerequisite for the right to practise throughout Switzerland in the monopoly domain. In **BGE 131 II 639 E. 3.1** the Federal Supreme Court held that lawyers «may register in the cantonal lawyers' register of only one canton» — simultaneous registration in multiple cantons is excluded (confirmed by BBl 1999 6046). Where a lawyer maintains several offices, the canton in which the lawyer predominantly practises is determinative.

**N. 13** *Economic freedom*: Legal practice in the monopoly domain falls under the protection of Art. 27 Cst. (economic freedom). Restrictions on the right to practise require a legal basis, must be proportionate and must be justified by a public interest (**BGE 130 II 87 E. 3 p. 92**). This is of particular significance for the interpretation of the independence requirement (→ Art. 8 para. 1 let. d BGFA).

#### 3.2 Paragraph 2: EU/EFTA Lawyers and British Nationals

**N. 14** Art. 2 para. 2 BGFA provides that the Act «governs the modalities for the representation of parties before judicial authorities» by EU/EFTA lawyers and by British nationals within the meaning of the agreement of 25 February 2019 (SR 0.142.113.672, «CH-UK Agreement»). This provision contains no substantive rule, but refers to the special provisions in Art. 21–34a BGFA.

**N. 15** For EU/EFTA lawyers, the BGFA distinguishes between:
- *Temporary provision of services* (Art. 21–26 BGFA): time-limited activity under the home country professional title, without registration;
- *Permanent professional practice* (Art. 27–29 BGFA): establishment in Switzerland under the home country professional title, with entry on the cantonal EU/EFTA lawyers' list;
- *Registration following an aptitude test or three years of practice* (Art. 30–34 BGFA): admission under the Swiss professional title.

**N. 16** In **BGE 151 II 640 E. 5.1–5.6** (judgment 2C_271/2024 of 26 February 2025) the Federal Supreme Court clarified the requirements for entry on the EU/EFTA lawyers' list: proof of legal qualification *and* demonstrated intention to establish oneself in Switzerland are required. Operating a permanent office in principle suffices as proof of the intention to establish oneself.

### 4. Legal Consequences

**N. 17** Art. 2 BGFA itself contains no provision as to legal consequences. The provision constitutes a condition for the applicability of all subsequent provisions of the BGFA. The consequences flowing from the personal scope of application include in particular:

- **Obligation to register** (→ Art. 6 BGFA): any person wishing to practise in the monopoly domain must be registered.
- **Subjection to professional rules** (→ Art. 12 BGFA): the professional rules apply to registered lawyers in the exercise of all professional activities, not only in the monopoly domain (**BGE 130 II 270 E. 4**).
- **Disciplinary supervision** (→ Art. 17 BGFA): registered lawyers are subject to the disciplinary supervision of the canton of registration.
- **Professional secrecy** (→ Art. 13 BGFA): protection beyond the monopoly domain for all professionally typical activities.

**N. 18** *Non-application of the BGFA outside the monopoly domain*: Any person who does not hold a bar admission and/or is not active in the monopoly domain does not fall within the personal scope of application of the BGFA. In-house lawyers without registration are excluded from the scope of application, but may in certain circumstances obtain registration (→ Art. 8 BGFA; **BGE 130 II 87** on the question of employed lawyers).

### 5. Contentious Issues

#### 5.1 Extent of the Monopoly Domain in Relation to Legal Advice

**N. 19** It is uncontested that pure legal advice does not form part of the monopoly domain. However, it is disputed where the boundary lies between the representation of parties subject to the monopoly and legal advice not subject to the monopoly, in particular in the case of activities combining both elements (drafting of legal submissions without actual court representation; negotiation before authorities in administrative pre-proceedings). Bohnet/Martenet (Droit de la profession d'avocat, 2009, para. 9–12) advocate a narrow interpretation of the monopoly domain in line with the limits imposed by economic freedom. Fellmann (Anwaltsrecht, 2nd ed. 2017, para. 20), by contrast, emphasises a functional approach: the decisive factor is whether the activity formally or factually presupposes party status before a court.

#### 5.2 Relationship between Art. 2 para. 1 BGFA and Cantonal Monopoly Law

**N. 20** Art. 3 para. 2 BGFA reserves more extensive cantonal monopoly regulations. Commentators agree that the cantons retain the power to independently shape their own monopoly law for proceedings before cantonal authorities — to the extent that federal procedural law (CPC, CrimPC) does not provide exhaustive regulation — (Nater, BSK BGFA, Art. 2 N. 6 et seq.; Bohnet/Martenet, op. cit., para. 973). It is, however, contested to what extent cantons may extend the monopoly to types of proceedings that do not fall within the monopoly domain under Art. 2 para. 1 BGFA at all. The Federal Supreme Court in **BGE 151 I 285 E. 5** (cantonal lawyers' monopoly in the administrative justice domain of the Canton of Basel-Stadt) affirmed in principle the admissibility of cantonal extensions of the monopoly subject to the requirements of Art. 29 Cst. and Art. 36 Cst., while emphasising the principle of proportionality as a limiting factor.

#### 5.3 Scope of Application for EU/EFTA Lawyers: «Temporary» vs. «Permanent»

**N. 21** The distinction between the temporary provision of services (Art. 21 et seq. BGFA) and permanent professional practice (Art. 27 et seq. BGFA) is disputed in legal writing and case law. Fellmann/Zindel (BSK BGFA, before Art. 21 N. 15 et seq.) emphasise the criterion of objective regularity and duration of the activity. The Administrative Court of Aargau held in WBE.2017.393 (judgment of 24 January 2018) that the requirement of permanent professional practice does not preclude EU/EFTA lawyers from opening a secondary office or business establishment in Switzerland. In **BGE 151 II 640** the Federal Supreme Court further clarified the requirements for entry on the EU/EFTA lawyers' list (intention to establish oneself).

#### 5.4 Reverse Discrimination Regarding Employed Lawyers

**N. 22** During the parliamentary deliberations, concern was expressed that the restrictive independence requirement for employed lawyers could give rise to reverse discrimination, since EU law prohibits a similar restriction for foreign lawyers (speech Nabholz, AB 1999 N 1558). The Federal Supreme Court rejected this concern in **BGE 130 II 87 E. 5.1.2**: EU law (in particular Art. 6 of Directive 98/5/EC and Art. 6 of Directive 77/249/EEC) is based on the principle of national treatment; foreign lawyers are placed on an equal footing with their domestic counterparts as regards professional obligations.

### 6. Practical Notes

**N. 23** *Verification of the scope of application*: Before addressing any question of substantive lawyers' law, it must be examined whether the person concerned falls within the personal scope of application of the BGFA at all. Holders of a cantonal bar admission who are *not* active in the monopoly domain are subject to the BGFA only in a limited manner (in particular to professional secrecy under Art. 13 BGFA, which applies beyond the monopoly domain).

**N. 24** *Bar admission holders without registration*: A person who holds a bar admission but is not registered in a cantonal register falls within Art. 2 para. 1 BGFA only if they are actually active in the monopoly domain. The Federal Supreme Court clarified in **BGE 130 II 87 E. 3** that registration pertains exclusively to the monopoly domain; outside this domain, legal advice may be provided without registration.

**N. 25** *Registration in only one canton*: Registration must be limited to one canton (**BGE 131 II 639 E. 3.5**). For lawyers with offices in several cantons, the canton in which the activity is predominantly exercised is determinative. A deliberate circumvention — for example by formally maintaining a registration in a «more permissive» canton — may entail disciplinary consequences (→ Art. 17 BGFA).

**N. 26** *Professional secrecy and the monopoly domain*: On the practically significant question of whether professional secrecy also applies outside the monopoly domain, Art. 2 para. 1 BGFA is not conclusive. According to **BGE 150 IV 470 E. 3.1**, professional secrecy applies to all professionally typical legal activities — including legal advice without court representation — to the extent that commercial-operational elements do not objectively predominate.

**N. 27** *EU/EFTA lawyers in practice*: EU/EFTA lawyers who are only temporarily active in Switzerland do not require registration, but are nonetheless subject to the professional rules of Art. 12 BGFA (→ Art. 25 BGFA). For lawyers from third countries — with the exception of British nationals under Art. 2 para. 2 let. b BGFA — the BGFA contains no regulation; in principle they require a cantonal authorisation if they wish to practise in the monopoly domain.

**N. 28** *Historical transitional law*: Art. 36 BGFA (transitional provision) facilitates registration for holders of cantonal bar admissions obtained before the entry into force of the BGFA (1 June 2002). However, the Federal Supreme Court has clarified that Art. 36 BGFA only relaxes the professional qualification requirements (Art. 7 BGFA) — the personal requirements (Art. 8 BGFA), in particular independence, must also be satisfied under transitional law (**BGE 130 II 87 E. 8.2**).
