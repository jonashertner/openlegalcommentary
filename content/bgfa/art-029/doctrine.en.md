# Art. 29 BGFA — Cooperation with the Competent Authority of the Home State

## Doctrine

### 1. Legislative History

**N. 1** Art. 29 BGFA implements Art. 7(2) of Directive 98/5/EC of the European Parliament and of the Council of 16 February 1998 to facilitate practice of the profession of lawyer on a permanent basis in a Member State other than that in which the qualification was obtained (Establishment Directive, OJ L 77 of 14 March 1998, p. 36) into Swiss law. The BGFA is itself based on the Agreement on the Free Movement of Persons between Switzerland and the EC of 21 June 1999 (AFMP, SR 0.142.112.681), whose Annex III refers to the Services Directive (77/249/EEC) and the Establishment Directive (BBl 1999 6013, 6022 ff.).

**N. 2** The Federal Council's Dispatch of 28 April 1999 expressly characterises the notification obligation provided for in Art. 29 BGFA as *formal* in nature: it is not intended to delay the opening of disciplinary proceedings in Switzerland (BBl 1999 6013, 6045, para. 234.33). The Establishment Directive leaves the form of communication open; it requires only that notification take place «within the shortest possible time» (Art. 7(2) sentence 1 of Directive 98/5/EC). The Federal Council's draft was adopted without debate in both chambers (AB 1999 N 1551 ff.; AB 1999 S 1173), so the parliamentary deliberations provide no additional interpretive elements.

**N. 3** The background to the cooperation obligation is the dual subjection of the permanently established EU/EFTA lawyer: he remains registered with the competent authority of his home state (→ Art. 28(2) BGFA) and is simultaneously subject to the professional rules of the host state (→ Art. 12 BGFA). This dual subjection makes coordinated supervision necessary in order to prevent the disciplinary rules of one or the other state from being circumvented (Kellerhals/Baumgartner, BSK BGFA, Art. 29 N. 1; Chappuis/Châtelain, CR LLCA, Art. 29 N. 1).

---

### 2. Systematic Classification

**N. 4** Art. 29 BGFA falls within the 5th section of the BGFA («Permanent practice under the original professional title», Arts. 27–29) and forms its conclusion. It governs cooperation in disciplinary proceedings as a consequence of the registration regime under Art. 27(1) and Art. 28 BGFA.

**N. 5** The systematic counterpart to Art. 29 BGFA for EU/EFTA lawyers who wish to integrate fully into the cantonal lawyers' register is → Art. 30 BGFA. Where full registration has been effected pursuant to Art. 30 BGFA, the special cooperation obligation under Art. 29 BGFA no longer applies, since the lawyer is then subject to the same rights and duties as a Swiss lawyer (Art. 30(2) BGFA).

**N. 6** Art. 29 BGFA does not apply to lawyers who are active solely in the free provision of services (→ Arts. 21–26 BGFA). These lawyers are not entered in the EU/EFTA lawyers' list; a comparable cooperation obligation is not provided for under the services regime, since → Art. 25(2) BGFA restricts the notification duty to the cantonal supervisory authority to a minimum.

**N. 7** The provision is to be read as a mirror image: where a Swiss lawyer is permanently active in an EU/EFTA state, the corresponding cooperation obligations under Art. 7(2) of Directive 98/5/EC apply in favour of the Swiss home authority. Swiss law does not provide an express mirror provision for the passive side; the willingness of Swiss supervisory authorities to cooperate with foreign requests nevertheless follows from the general principle of openness to international law.

---

### 3. Content of the Provision

**N. 8** Art. 29 BGFA consists of two paragraphs with different obligations:

- **Para. 1** establishes a *prior notification obligation*: before the cantonal supervisory authority opens disciplinary proceedings against a permanently established EU/EFTA lawyer, it must inform the competent authority of the home state.
- **Para. 2** governs *ongoing cooperation during the proceedings*: the supervisory authority grants the home authority the opportunity to submit observations.

**N. 9** *Personal scope of application*: Art. 29 BGFA applies to lawyers who are permanently active in Switzerland under their original professional title pursuant to Art. 27(1) BGFA and who are entered in the EU/EFTA lawyers' list under Art. 28 BGFA. Whether the lawyer concerned is a national of the EU/EFTA member state in which they are admitted, or of a third state, is irrelevant, provided the registration requirements of Arts. 27 f. BGFA are met (**BGE 151 II 271** E. 4.5: registration in the Geneva EU/EFTA list as a Portuguese lawyer suffices, irrespective of Brazilian place of birth).

**N. 10** *Material scope of application*: The cooperation obligation is triggered in *disciplinary proceedings* — i.e. proceedings opened on account of an alleged breach of professional duties under → Art. 12 BGFA, with the aim of imposing a disciplinary measure under → Art. 17 BGFA. Supervisory measures that are not disciplinary in nature (e.g. administrative removal from the list for failure to fulfil registration requirements) are not covered by the scope of application.

**N. 11** *Notification obligation (para. 1)*: Notification must be given before the disciplinary proceedings are formally opened. It must be addressed to the «competent authority of the home state» — i.e. the professional court, the bar association, or the professional supervisory authority of the relevant EU/EFTA state, depending on its organisation. The BGFA does not prescribe the form of notification; according to the Dispatch, it is not of a constitutive character (BBl 1999 6013, 6045). The Federal Supreme Court has nonetheless characterised it as a mandatory procedural requirement (**BGE 151 II 271** E. 4.6).

**N. 12** *Cooperation during the proceedings (para. 2)*: The cantonal supervisory authority must afford the foreign authority the opportunity «to submit observations» («de déposer des observations»). This participation is *consultative* in nature: the home authority has no right of objection or veto; the Swiss authority remains free in its decision (Chappuis/Châtelain, CR LLCA, Art. 29 N. 3, 5 and 6; Kellerhals/Baumgartner, BSK BGFA, Art. 29 N. 3, 5 and 6). Cooperation takes place «in particular» by affording the opportunity to submit observations — the word «in particular» indicates that further forms of cooperation (e.g. exchange of information on the measure imposed) may also be encompassed.

**N. 13** *Purpose of the cooperation obligation*: Cooperation serves primarily to prevent the disciplinary rules of the home or host state from being circumvented through a skilful choice of place of activity. It allows the home authority to initiate parallel proceedings in its state or to take coordinated measures (Bohnet/Martenet, Droit de la profession d'avocat, 2009, para. 844; Kellerhals/Baumgartner, BSK BGFA, Art. 29 N. 1).

---

### 4. Legal Consequences

**N. 14** *Breach of the notification obligation (para. 1)*: If the cantonal supervisory authority fails to notify the home authority, the disciplinary decision issued in breach of Art. 29(1) BGFA is *contestable* (not void). The Federal Supreme Court clarified in **BGE 151 II 271** E. 4.7.2 that the breach does not constitute a particularly serious defect that would give rise to nullity; the applicable principle is that nullity is to be presumed only where there are grave and manifest defects (**BGE 148 II 564** E. 7.2). The decision is to be set aside and the matter referred back to the supervisory authority so that it may make the required notification and afford the home authority the opportunity to submit observations (Chappuis/Châtelain, CR LLCA, Art. 29 N. 11; Bohnet/Martenet, Droit de la profession d'avocat, 2009, para. 844).

**N. 15** *Breach of the cooperation obligation (para. 2)*: The same legal consequence applies where the supervisory authority fails to afford the home authority the opportunity to submit observations. Both violations together lead to the annulment of the contested decision (**BGE 151 II 271** E. 4.6, 4.7.2). The Federal Supreme Court applies Art. 29 BGFA ex officio (Art. 106(1) BGG), so that the affected lawyer may raise the objection for the first time before the Federal Supreme Court (**BGE 151 II 271** E. 4.1, with reference to **BGE 142 I 155** E. 4.4.3).

**N. 16** *Procedural consequence*: Following a referral back, the cantonal supervisory authority must recommence the proceedings after having given notification pursuant to Art. 29(1) BGFA and having afforded the home authority the opportunity to submit observations pursuant to Art. 29(2) BGFA. The substantive reassessment of the disciplinary matter remains entirely within the authority's discretion; the cooperation requirement is purely procedural in nature.

**N. 17** *No legal effects in favour of the affected lawyer*: Art. 29 BGFA does not confer a subjective right on the lawyer to a particular statement from the home authority; the lawyer may, however, invoke the breach of the cooperation obligation as a procedural objection.

---

### 5. Disputed Issues

**N. 18** *Legal nature of the notification obligation — formal or substantive?* The Dispatch characterises the notification obligation as «formal» in nature, without the possibility of delaying the opening of disciplinary proceedings (BBl 1999 6013, 6045). Kellerhals/Baumgartner (BSK BGFA, Art. 29 N. 1 and 3) and Chappuis/Châtelain (CR LLCA, Art. 29 N. 1 and 3) share this classification as a consultative, non-constitutive obligation. The Federal Supreme Court confirmed the matter in **BGE 151 II 271** E. 4.3 and 4.7.2: the formal character prevents the obligation from giving rise to *nullity* of the decision, but does not preclude its characterisation as a mandatory procedural requirement whose breach leads to annulment. In this respect, the Federal Supreme Court has «refined» the Dispatch insofar as formal nature must not be equated with procedural irrelevance.

**N. 19** *Timing of notification — before or upon opening?* Art. 29(1) BGFA requires the home authority to be informed «before» disciplinary proceedings are opened («avant d'ouvrir»). It is unclear whether a subsequent notification given immediately after the opening can cure the defect. Chappuis/Châtelain (CR LLCA, Art. 29 N. 3) deny the possibility of cure. This stricter view corresponds to the wording of the provision and the aim of enabling the home authority to exercise effective influence before the proceedings begin. In **BGE 151 II 271** E. 4.6, the Federal Supreme Court left the question of cure open, since the Geneva Bar Commission had given no notification whatsoever.

**N. 20** *Relationship to the procedural guarantee under Art. 29 Cst.*: It has been suggested in some quarters that the cooperation obligation under Art. 29 BGFA may also be understood as a concretisation of the constitutional right to fair proceedings (Art. 29 Cst.), since it effectively provides the lawyer with an additional procedural participant. This construction finds no support in case law; the Federal Supreme Court in **BGE 151 II 271** relies exclusively on the statutory text and the European law context. Bohnet/Martenet (Droit de la profession d'avocat, 2009, para. 844) regard the cooperation primarily as a protective function directed towards the home state, rather than as an individual guarantee in favour of the lawyer.

**N. 21** *Applicability to interim measures*: Whether Art. 29 BGFA also applies prior to the issuance of an *ex parte* disciplinary measure (e.g. provisional suspension from practice under cantonal law) has not been resolved by the highest court. Kellerhals/Baumgartner (BSK BGFA, Art. 29 N. 2) advocate for its application; Chappuis/Châtelain (CR LLCA, Art. 29 N. 2) acknowledge that the urgency of an immediate measure may justify subsequent notification, without definitively curing the defect.

### 6. Practical Notes

**N. 22** *Identification of the lawyer as an EU/EFTA-registered professional*: The cantonal supervisory authority must, at the outset of every disciplinary proceeding, verify whether the lawyer concerned is entered in the EU/EFTA lawyers' list (Art. 28 BGFA). Entry in the list is the decisive criterion for the applicability of Art. 29 BGFA — not the nationality of the lawyer (**BGE 151 II 271** E. 4.5).

**N. 23** *Form of notification*: Neither the BGFA nor Directive 98/5/EC prescribes a specific form. In practice, a written, documented notification to the named authority of the home state (bar association, professional court, etc.) with an appropriate time limit for the submission of observations is recommended. Failure to document the notification carries the risk that the Federal Supreme Court will find a breach of Art. 29 BGFA and annul the decision, even if the defect had no substantive effect on the outcome.

**N. 24** *Setting a time limit for the home authority*: The law does not specify a time limit within which the home authority must submit its observations. A period of four to six weeks appears practical; account should be taken of the varying procedural rules of the home states. If the home authority does not respond within the set time limit, the Swiss supervisory authority may continue the proceedings.

**N. 25** *Multilingual communication*: Since the competent home authorities communicate in the respective official languages of the EU/EFTA member states, the use of an internationally common language (English, French, or German) is recommended. There is no obligation to provide a translation; the communication serves the purpose of information, not the creation of pending legal proceedings.

**N. 26** *Proceedings following annulment for breach of Art. 29 BGFA*: Following a referral back by the Federal Supreme Court, the cantonal supervisory authority makes the requisite notification and affords the home authority an appropriate time limit to submit observations. Only thereafter may the authority make a fresh decision on the disciplinary measure. A decision identical in substance is legally permissible, provided the procedural requirements are complied with on this occasion.

**N. 27** *Relationship to other exchange obligations*: Art. 29 BGFA governs exclusively cooperation in disciplinary proceedings. Independently of this, cantonal supervisory authorities may exchange information with foreign authorities on a voluntary basis, to the extent that professional secrecy (→ Art. 13 BGFA) and the applicable data protection framework are observed.
