# Art. 26 BGFA — Information on Disciplinary Measures

## Doctrine

### 1. Legislative History

**N. 1** Art. 26 BGFA forms the capstone of Section 4 (Art. 21–26 BGFA), which governs the temporary provision of services by lawyers from EU and EFTA member states in Switzerland. The Federal Council submitted the draft on 28 April 1999 (BBl 1999 6013). The provision implements Art. 7(2) of Council Directive 77/249/EEC of 22 March 1977 (Services Directive; OJ EC No. L 78/17 of 26.3.1977), which provides for an obligation on the host state to notify the home state in the event of disciplinary measures. Since Switzerland is not a member of the EU, this obligation applies by virtue of the reference in Annex III.B.3 of the Agreement on the Free Movement of Persons of 21 June 1999 (AFMP; SR 0.142.112.681).

**N. 2** In the dispatch, the Federal Council explained that the supervisory authority of the canton in whose territory the activity was carried out is responsible for disciplinary supervision of EU/EFTA lawyers providing services. In the event of disciplinary measures, the competent authority in the home state must be informed promptly; such communication is of a purely formal nature and cannot delay the opening of disciplinary proceedings (BBl 1999 6065, para. 234.26). Parliament adopted the draft without any substantive amendment; Art. 26 BGFA was not controversially debated in the Federal Assembly (AB 1999 N 1569; AB 1999 S 1173).

**N. 3** Art. 26 BGFA entered into force on 1 June 2002 together with the BGFA as a whole (AS 2002 862). No substantive revisions have taken place. The provision has a counterpart in Art. 29 BGFA, which applies to EU/EFTA lawyers practising permanently under their home-country title and, based on Art. 7(2) of Directive 98/5/EC of 16 February 1998 (Establishment Directive; OJ EC No. L 77/36 of 14.3.1998), establishes a more extensive duty of cooperation (→ Art. 29 BGFA).

---

### 2. Systematic Classification

**N. 4** Art. 26 BGFA concludes Section 4 and constitutes the disciplinary-law counterpart to the substantive rules of Art. 21–25 BGFA. The provision governs two aspects of disciplinary supervision over EU/EFTA lawyers providing services: (1) the jurisdiction of the cantonal supervisory authority (para. 1) and (2) the duty to inform the competent authority in the home state in the event of disciplinary measures (para. 2). The provision thus establishes the interface between cantonal disciplinary authority and international cooperation in professional law.

**N. 5** Within the overall framework of the BGFA, Art. 26 BGFA serves as the complementary provision to Art. 14–16 BGFA (inter-cantonal disciplinary cooperation) and to Art. 29 BGFA (cooperation in the case of permanent practice). The disciplinary measures under Art. 17 BGFA applicable to lawyers entered in the cantonal register also apply, by virtue of Art. 26(1) BGFA, to EU/EFTA lawyers in the cross-border provision of services. The provision thereby ensures the enforceability of the professional rules referred to in Art. 25 BGFA (→ Art. 12 BGFA).

**N. 6** The relationship to the BGFA's register structure is of structural significance: since EU/EFTA lawyers providing services are not entered in a cantonal lawyers' register (Art. 21(2) BGFA), the typical link between supervision and the register canton (Art. 14(1) BGFA) is absent. Art. 26(1) BGFA compensates for this structural deficit by tying supervisory jurisdiction to the canton of activity (principle of the place of commission). ↔ Art. 14 BGFA; → Art. 21(2) BGFA; → Art. 29 BGFA.

### 3. Content of the Provision

#### 3.1 Paragraph 1: Disciplinary Supervision and Applicable Measures

**N. 7** Art. 26(1) BGFA subjects EU/EFTA lawyers temporarily providing services in Switzerland to the disciplinary supervision of the supervisory authority of the canton in whose territory they represent parties before judicial authorities. The determining factor is the canton of activity, not the lawyer's country of domicile or establishment. Where activities are carried out in several cantons, competing jurisdictions arise; in accordance with the general principle under Art. 14(1) BGFA, the priority rule of the first investigation initiated applies (Fellmann/Zindel, BSK BGFA, 2nd ed. 2011, Art. 26 N. 2; Dreyer, in: Fellmann/Zindel [eds.], Kommentar zum Anwaltsgesetz, 2nd ed. 2011, Art. 26 N. 3).

**N. 8** Art. 26(1) BGFA refers to the disciplinary measures under Art. 17 BGFA. The range extends from a caution through a reprimand and a fine of up to CHF 20,000 to a ban on practising the profession for a maximum of two years (Art. 17(1)(a)–(d) BGFA). The Federal Supreme Court confirmed in **BGE 150 II 308** E. 7.4 (2024) that the BGFA exhaustively regulates disciplinary measures; cantonal additions — in particular the publication of disciplinary measures in the cantonal official gazette — are contrary to federal law. This doctrine of exhaustiveness applies equally to measures under Art. 26 BGFA. → Art. 17 BGFA; → Art. 18 BGFA.

**N. 9** A ban on practising the profession under Art. 17(1)(d) BGFA operates against an EU/EFTA lawyer providing services as a de facto prohibition on activity in Swiss territory. Pursuant to Art. 18(2) BGFA, the ban must be communicated to the supervisory authorities of all cantons; this notification mechanism ensures that the ban can be enforced throughout Switzerland. Unlike lawyers entered in the register, no deletion from a register takes place; the measure must be recorded in the disciplinary file of the competent cantonal supervisory authority (Dreyer, Art. 26 N. 3). → Art. 18(2) BGFA.

#### 3.2 Paragraph 2: Duty to Inform the Home State

**N. 10** Art. 26(2) BGFA obliges the cantonal supervisory authority to inform the competent authority in the home state of the disciplinary measures taken. This duty arises after the disciplinary decision has been issued; unlike Art. 29(1) BGFA, which requires prior notification before the opening of proceedings, it is limited to communication of the outcome (Dreyer, Art. 26 N. 4 f.; Bohnet/Martenet, Droit de la profession d'avocat, 2009, N. 844). The dispatch expressly clarifies that the communication is «of a purely formal nature» and does not delay the opening of disciplinary proceedings (BBl 1999 6065, para. 234.26).

**N. 11** The purpose of the duty to inform is twofold: on the one hand, the competent authority in the home state should have the opportunity to take disciplinary action on its own part, if the lawyer concerned has violated analogous professional rules in the home state. On the other hand, the information serves to ensure the completeness of the lawyer's professional credentials record in the home state (Fellmann/Zindel, BSK BGFA, 2nd ed. 2011, Art. 26 N. 4; Kellerhals/Baumgartner, in: Fellmann/Zindel [eds.], BSK BGFA, 2nd ed. 2011, Art. 29 N. 1). The Federal Administrative Court highlighted in BVGE 2016/37 E. 3.3.3.2 that the duty to inform under Art. 26(2) BGFA is facilitated for the Swiss supervisory authority by the obligation under Art. 24 BGFA in conjunction with Art. 25 BGFA for the lawyer to indicate their home-country professional title and membership of the home-country professional organisation: «Compliance with this provision makes it easier for the supervisory authority of the competent canton to fulfil its duty to inform the home state of the disciplinary measures» (BVGE 2016/37 E. 3.3.3.2, with reference to Dreyer, Art. 26 N. 5).

**N. 12** The duty to inform under Art. 26(2) BGFA must be distinguished from the more extensive duty of cooperation under Art. 29 BGFA (permanent practice). Whereas Art. 29(1) and (2) BGFA requires active involvement of the home authority *before and during* the disciplinary proceedings — with a right to submit observations — Art. 26(2) BGFA is limited to subsequent notification of the measure taken. The Federal Supreme Court clarified in **BGE 151 II 271** E. 4.2 f. (2025) that Art. 29 BGFA requires genuine prior notification, the violation of which leads to the annulment of the decision (not to nullity); these principles do not apply without qualification to Art. 26(2) BGFA, since its duty to inform is more limited in time and in substantive weight. → Art. 29 BGFA.

### 4. Legal Consequences

**N. 13** If an EU/EFTA lawyer temporarily providing services violates the professional rules under Art. 12 BGFA (in conjunction with Art. 25 BGFA), the cantonal supervisory authority may order the measures under Art. 17(1) BGFA. The proceedings are governed by cantonal procedural law, subject to the minimum federal requirements (Art. 15 f. BGFA by analogy; Dreyer, Art. 26 N. 2; Bohnet/Martenet, Droit de la profession d'avocat, 2009, N. 843). The supervisory authority of the canton of activity has jurisdiction; it does not require a prior decision from a register canton, since no such entity exists in the case of temporarily active EU/EFTA lawyers.

**N. 14** After issuing the disciplinary measure, the cantonal supervisory authority is subject to the duty to inform under Art. 26(2) BGFA. This duty must be fulfilled ex officio; no application by the party concerned is required. The «competent authority in the home state» is determined in accordance with the law of the home state — as a rule the national or regional bar association or supervisory authority that is implicitly designated in the list of professional titles in the Annex to the BGFA.

**N. 15** A violation of the duty to inform under Art. 26(2) BGFA does not affect the validity of the disciplinary measure. Since the notification takes place exclusively after the conclusion of proceedings and — unlike Art. 29 BGFA — does not confer any rights of participation on the home authority, the information constitutes a mere procedural obligation of the Swiss supervisory authority without effect on the lawfulness of the measure (BBl 1999 6065; Dreyer, Art. 26 N. 5; correspondingly regarding Art. 29 BGFA: **BGE 151 II 271** E. 4.7.2).

**N. 16** The provisions of Art. 20 BGFA on the registration and deletion of disciplinary measures do not apply directly to temporarily active EU/EFTA lawyers — who are not entered in a cantonal lawyers' register. The measures must be documented in the internal file of the competent cantonal supervisory authority. Whether lapsed or time-barred measures may be taken into account in subsequent proceedings is governed by analogy with the principles developed in **BGE 150 II 308** E. 5.10 for registered lawyers: the supervisory authority may take earlier misconduct into account, though earlier measures lose significance as the time elapsed increases. → Art. 20 BGFA.

### 5. Disputed Issues

#### 5.1 Scope of the Duty to Inform: Prior or Subsequent Notification?

**N. 17** Academic commentary is divided on whether Art. 26(2) BGFA — in accordance with its wording — requires exclusively *subsequent* notification of the measure taken, or whether, by analogy with Art. 29(1) BGFA (permanent practice), *prior* notification before the opening of proceedings is required. Dreyer (Kommentar zum Anwaltsgesetz, 2nd ed. 2011, Art. 26 N. 5) and Kellerhals/Baumgartner (BSK BGFA, 2nd ed. 2011, Art. 26 N. 3 f.) advocate the restrictive reading: the wording of Art. 26(2) BGFA («informs») relates to the measure that has been taken, not to proceedings that are merely pending; prior notification would be inconsistent with the system because lawyers providing services have no dual registration that would justify close ongoing cooperation. Bohnet/Martenet (Droit de la profession d'avocat, 2009, N. 843 f.), by contrast, favour an extensive interpretation in line with Art. 7(2) of Directive 77/249/EEC and propose that the home authority be notified already at the opening of proceedings in order to prevent double prosecution. The Federal Supreme Court has not decided this question with respect to Art. 26 BGFA; its statements in **BGE 151 II 271** E. 4.2 f. on the prior notification requirement under Art. 29 BGFA cannot be directly transposed to Art. 26 BGFA given the incomparability of the regulatory structures.

#### 5.2 Relationship between Disciplinary Jurisdiction and Art. 14 BGFA

**N. 18** The disciplinary jurisdiction under Art. 26(1) BGFA (canton of activity) departs from the basic rule of Art. 14(1) BGFA, under which the supervisory authority of the register canton has primary jurisdiction. Dreyer (Art. 26 N. 2 f.) and Fellmann (Anwaltsrecht, 2nd ed. 2017, para. 1185) consider the jurisdiction under Art. 26(1) BGFA to be exclusive and exhaustive: in the absence of registration there is no «register canton», so that only the canton of activity can be considered. Nater/Zindel (Kommentar zum Anwaltsgesetz, 2020, Art. 26 N. 2) modify this position: if an EU/EFTA lawyer provides services essentially in a single canton, that canton becomes the de facto «register canton» with correspondingly concentrated supervisory competence. Where activities are carried out in several cantons, parallel jurisdiction creates coordination deficits which, de lege ferenda, should be resolved by centralised registration along the lines of Section 5 (Art. 27 ff. BGFA). The Federal Administrative Court highlighted in BVGE 2016/37 E. 3.3.3.2 the special character of supervision under Art. 26 BGFA as compared with inter-cantonal supervision under Art. 14–16 BGFA: in the international context, the institutional framework of Art. 16 BGFA (right of the register canton to submit observations before a measure is imposed) is absent, which structurally complicates the supervision of EU/EFTA lawyers providing services.

#### 5.3 Applicability of Art. 26 BGFA to Court-Appointed Representation

**N. 19** In BVGE 2016/37 (2016), the Federal Administrative Court examined whether EU/EFTA lawyers operating under the free movement of services regime may be appointed as court-appointed legal representatives. In doing so, the court established — invoking Art. 26 BGFA — that while disciplinary supervision of these lawyers exists, a structural deficit remains compared with lawyers entered in the register: in the international context the institutional notification of the «register canton» before a measure is imposed (Art. 16 BGFA) is absent; the subsequent notification requirement under Art. 26(2) BGFA does not constitute an «equivalent correlate» and negatively affects the authority's ability to assess the suitability of a court-appointed representative (BVGE 2016/37 E. 3.3.3.2, with reference to Dreyer, Art. 26 N. 4). This reasoning supported the conclusion that the requirement of entry in a cantonal lawyers' register for court-appointed representation is compatible with the AFMP.

Bohnet/Martenet (Droit de la profession d'avocat, 2009, N. 1703) have criticised this conclusion: Art. 26 BGFA ensures sufficient disciplinary integration; the unequal treatment compared with inter-cantonal Swiss lawyers cannot be justified. Dreyer (Kommentar zum Anwaltsgesetz, 2nd ed. 2011, Art. 25 N. 7) endorsed this criticism. The Federal Administrative Court did not follow this minority view in BVGE 2016/37 E. 3.5.

### 6. Practical Notes

**N. 20** **Determining jurisdiction.** A cantonal supervisory authority wishing to open disciplinary proceedings against an EU/EFTA lawyer providing services must first verify whether the lawyer in question is in fact operating under the services regime (Art. 21 BGFA) or already under the regime for permanent practice (Art. 27 BGFA). In the latter case, Art. 29 BGFA applies, requiring active cooperation with the home authority *before* the opening of proceedings; a violation of this obligation leads, pursuant to **BGE 151 II 271** E. 4.6 f., to the annulment of the disciplinary decision.

**N. 21** **Identifying the home authority.** To fulfil the duty to inform under Art. 26(2) BGFA, the supervisory authority must identify the competent authority in the home state. The obligation under Art. 24 BGFA for the lawyer to use their professional title *indicating the professional organisation to whose jurisdiction they are subject* substantially facilitates this identification; the supervisory authority may obtain the relevant information from the letterhead or court submissions (BVGE 2016/37 E. 3.3.3.2; Dreyer, Art. 26 N. 5). → Art. 24 BGFA.

**N. 22** **Enforcement of bans on practising the profession.** A ban on practising the profession imposed pursuant to Art. 26(1) in conjunction with Art. 17(1)(d) BGFA must be notified to all cantonal supervisory authorities pursuant to Art. 18(2) BGFA. In practice, however, enforcement against a lawyer domiciled abroad remains problematic since no deletion from a register is available as a mechanism. In particularly serious cases, the supervisory authority should — beyond the formal notification requirement under Art. 26(2) BGFA — proactively contact the home authority and encourage it to take further measures in the home state (Fellmann, Anwaltsrecht, 2nd ed. 2017, para. 1185; Bohnet/Martenet, Droit de la profession d'avocat, 2009, N. 844).

**N. 23** **Distinction from Art. 29 BGFA and risk of error.** In practice there is a risk that supervisory authorities will incorrectly assess the status of an EU/EFTA lawyer as «providing services» or «practising permanently». The Federal Supreme Court clarified in **BGE 151 II 271** E. 4.6 f. (2025) that entry in the EU/EFTA lawyers' list (Art. 28 BGFA) alone triggers the applicability of Art. 29 BGFA (with its stricter prior notification requirement). The supervisory authority must therefore verify, before opening proceedings, whether the lawyer concerned is entered in a cantonal register or in the EU/EFTA lawyers' list; if so, Art. 29 BGFA applies, not Art. 26 BGFA. ↔ Art. 29 BGFA.

**N. 24** **Relationship to cantonal implementing legislation.** Disciplinary proceedings against EU/EFTA lawyers providing services are governed by cantonal procedural law; the BGFA contains no independent procedural rules for Art. 26 BGFA. Cantonal implementing legislation (e.g. AnwG/ZH, EG BGFA/ZG) may provide further procedural detail but may not prescribe measures deviating from Art. 17 BGFA (**BGE 150 II 308** E. 7.4). → Art. 3 BGFA; → Art. 17 BGFA.
