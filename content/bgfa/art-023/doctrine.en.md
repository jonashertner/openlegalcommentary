# Art. 23 BGFA — Obligation to Act in Conjunction

## Doctrine

### 1. Legislative History

**N. 1** Art. 23 BGFA implements Art. 5 of Council Directive 77/249/EEC of 22 March 1977 to facilitate the effective exercise by lawyers of freedom to provide services (Services Directive) into Swiss law. That Directive is applicable to Switzerland pursuant to Annex III of the Agreement on the Free Movement of Persons (AFMP; SR 0.142.112.681). In the dispatch on the BGFA (BBl 1999 6064, para. 234.23), the Federal Council explained that the obligation to act in conjunction would be virtually without effect in practice, because Swiss procedural law in the proceedings to which the BGFA applies — in particular before the Federal Supreme Court and cantonal courts of ordinary jurisdiction — hardly knows (formal) mandatory representation by a lawyer. The Federal Council expressly noted in the dispatch that Art. 23 BGFA nevertheless serves as a catch-all provision in those cantonal proceedings that exceptionally provide for mandatory representation, while acknowledging the obligation under Union law to transpose Art. 5 of Directive 77/249/EEC (BBl 1999 6064).

**N. 2** The Federal Council's draft of Art. 23 BGFA was adopted by both chambers without amendment and without substantive debate (cf. AB 1999 N 1551 ff., 1569; AB 1999 S 1173). This reflects the limited practical significance that the legislature attributed to the provision. Art. 23 was conceived as an annex to Art. 21 para. 1 BGFA and forms part of the «freedom to provide services regime» of Arts. 21–26 BGFA; by virtue of the reference in Art. 27 para. 2 BGFA, it also applies by analogy to permanently practising EU/EFTA lawyers (→ Art. 27 para. 2 BGFA).

---

### 2. Systematic Classification

**N. 3** Art. 23 BGFA is situated in the 4th Section of the BGFA (Arts. 21–26 BGFA) on the «Freedom to Provide Services» of EU/EFTA lawyers. This section governs the temporary and occasional practice of the profession in Switzerland (so-called service providers), which may take place without registration in a cantonal lawyers' register and without a residence permit, and is limited to a maximum of 90 working days per calendar year (→ Art. 21 paras. 1 and 2 BGFA; Art. 5 AFMP).

**N. 4** The provision stands in a functional nexus with:
- → Art. 21 para. 1 BGFA (entitlement to represent parties before judicial authorities in the context of freedom to provide services)
- → Art. 22 BGFA (proof of lawyer's qualifications upon request)
- → Art. 24 BGFA (obligation to use the original professional title)
- → Art. 27 para. 2 BGFA (reference to Art. 23 BGFA for permanently practising EU/EFTA lawyers)

**N. 5** By virtue of the reference in Art. 27 para. 2 BGFA, the obligation to act in conjunction under Art. 23 BGFA applies not only to lawyers providing services (Arts. 21 ff. BGFA) but also to those who are permanently practising in Switzerland and have registered in the EU/EFTA lawyers' list under Art. 28 BGFA (cf. judgment **2A.536/2003** of 9 August 2004, consid. 3.2.2). This dual scope distinguishes Art. 23 from the other provisions in the freedom to provide services section. Lawyers who have been entered in the cantonal lawyers' register pursuant to Art. 30 BGFA are no longer subject to Art. 23 BGFA; they are fully governed by the general professional regime of Arts. 5 ff. BGFA (↔ Art. 30 para. 2 BGFA).

**N. 6** There is no comparable provision in relation to unregistered Swiss lawyers — such as those without a cantonal licence to practise law. Art. 23 BGFA is therefore a specifically treaty-based rule without equivalent in domestic law, a consideration that must be taken into account when interpreting it (Nater/Wipf, Internationale Freizügigkeit nach dem BGFA, in: Thürer/Weber/Zäch [eds.], Bilaterale Verträge Schweiz-EG, 2002, p. 258 f.; Bohnet/Martenet, Droit de la profession d'avocat, 2009, para. 597).

---

### 3. Elements of the Offence / Normative Content

**N. 7** **Mandatory representation** («proceedings with mandatory representation») is the central constituent element. Mandatory representation (also: lawyers' monopoly in the sense of an obligation to be represented) exists where the procedural law governing a specific proceeding expressly prescribes that party representation may only be carried out by an admitted lawyer. Whether such a requirement exists is determined solely by the applicable (cantonal or federal) procedural law — not by the BGFA itself. Art. 23 BGFA governs only the legal consequence of mandatory representation for service-providing EU/EFTA lawyers, not its prerequisites.

**N. 8** The Federal Council pointed out in the dispatch that formal mandatory representation is «hardly» encountered in Switzerland (BBl 1999 6064). The Federal Supreme Court confirmed this in **judgment 2A.536/2003** of 9 August 2004, consid. 3.2.1, noting that proceedings with mandatory representation are rare under the current legal framework. Art. 23 BGFA becomes practically relevant above all where cantonal procedural law still provided for mandatory representation, for example in certain appellate proceedings before cantonal superior courts under former cantonal law. Since the entry into force of the Swiss Civil Procedure Code (CPC; SR 272) on 1 January 2011 and the Swiss Code of Criminal Procedure (CrimPC; SR 312.0) on the same date, the practical significance of the provision has diminished further, as those codifications did not introduce a general obligation of mandatory representation. The CPC provides in Art. 68 para. 2 CPC merely that courts may require parties to be represented by a lawyer, without establishing a binding obligation to do so.

**N. 9** **Service-providing lawyers** within the meaning of Art. 23 BGFA are nationals of EU or EFTA member states who are entitled to practise the legal profession in their home state under one of the professional titles listed in the Annex to the BGFA and who are temporarily active in Switzerland (→ Art. 21 para. 1 BGFA). They are not registered in a cantonal lawyers' register (→ Art. 21 para. 2 BGFA) and do not require a residence permit. The Federal Supreme Court qualified service providers in **judgment 2A.536/2003**, consid. 3.2.1, as lawyers who may work «up to 90 working days in Switzerland». By virtue of the reference in → Art. 27 para. 2 BGFA, Art. 23 BGFA also applies to permanently practising lawyers entered in the EU/EFTA lawyers' list (Art. 28 BGFA).

**N. 10** **«Acting in conjunction»** designates the actual conduct obligation. The conjunction (Fr. *en accord*; It. *d'accordo*) is to be understood, according to the German wording, as substantive coordination: the service-providing lawyer must coordinate with a lawyer registered in a cantonal lawyers' register. According to Nater/Wipf (op. cit., p. 258 f.), this corresponds to the concept of «acting in conjunction» from Art. 5 of Directive 77/249/EEC: the visiting lawyer may not appear in proceedings with mandatory representation without the involvement of a locally registered lawyer. «Acting in conjunction» is narrower than a mere consultation but broader than a formal assumption of the principal mandate by the local lawyer: both must be in a position to actively direct the representation. Wipf, Das Anwaltsmonopol und dessen Umschreibung, SJZ 97/2001, p. 89, describes the function as a so-called «guest system», in which the local lawyer shares substantive responsibility for the mandate.

**N. 11** **Lawyer registered in a cantonal lawyers' register**: What is decisive is registration in one of the cantonal registers under Arts. 5 ff. BGFA (or Art. 30 BGFA). Registration in the EU/EFTA lawyers' list under Art. 28 BGFA is not sufficient; this in turn gives rise to the obligation to act in conjunction under Art. 27 para. 2 in conjunction with Art. 23 BGFA (cf. **judgment 2A.536/2003**, consid. 3.2.2, and Zurich Obergericht, KF100057, 5 October 2006, consid. 49 f.). The local lawyer must be registered in the register of the canton in which the proceedings are conducted — inter-cantonal register validity for registered lawyers is already ensured by Arts. 21 ff. BGFA (→ Art. 3 BGFA).

---

### 4. Legal Consequences

**N. 12** Where Art. 23 BGFA applies, the service-providing EU/EFTA lawyer is obliged to act in conjunction with a locally registered lawyer. The BGFA does not expressly regulate the consequences of a breach of this obligation. The literature (Bohnet/Martenet, op. cit., para. 597; Fellmann, Anwaltsrecht, 2nd ed. 2017, para. 176) takes the view that a breach may lead to the nullity of procedural acts, in so far as the applicable procedural law generally provides for this consequence in the event of a breach of mandatory representation rules. Art. 23 BGFA does not contain an independent sanction provision.

**N. 13** At the level of professional law, a breach of the obligation to act in conjunction may be qualified as a violation of the applicable professional rules. For service-providing lawyers, Art. 25 BGFA refers to the applicability of the professional rules of the host state, i.e. the Swiss professional rules (→ Art. 12 BGFA). Disciplinary measures are governed by → Art. 17 BGFA. Weber-Stecher, Internationale Freizügigkeit von RechtsanwältInnen im Verhältnis Schweiz-EU, in: Nater, Professional Legal Services, 2000, p. 57, notes that in practice disciplinary sanctions against foreign visiting lawyers are difficult to enforce for practical reasons.

**N. 14** For the local lawyer who acts in conjunction, co-responsibilities arise: that lawyer is liable under the general rules of the law of mandate (Art. 398 CO) for the quality of the representation to the extent that they participate in the mandate. The question of who bears primary responsibility vis-à-vis the client is to be assessed under private law and is determined by the mandating relationship.

---

### 5. Disputed Questions

**N. 15** **Scope of «acting in conjunction»**: The literature is divided on how intensive the involvement of the local lawyer must be. Wipf, SJZ 97/2001, p. 89, and Nater/Wipf, op. cit., p. 259, relying on Art. 5 of Directive 77/249/EEC, advocate a narrow interpretation: the conjunction requires only joint direction of the proceedings («acting in conjunction»), not a full assumption of responsibility by the local lawyer. Bohnet/Martenet, op. cit., para. 597, agree with this position and emphasise that the visiting lawyer retains the leading role, while the local lawyer must nonetheless be substantially involved. Fellmann, Anwaltsrecht, para. 176, by contrast, regards the requirement of acting in conjunction as a form of shared procedural responsibility that in practice may de facto lead to dual appearance by both lawyers, which he criticises as a disproportionate restriction on the freedom to provide services. A definitive ruling on this question by the highest court is not available — given the absence of relevant precedents — which underscores the limited practical significance of the provision.

**N. 16** **Compatibility with Union law**: Art. 5 para. 2 of Directive 77/249/EEC permits member states to maintain the conjunction requirement for proceedings with mandatory representation. Whether this rule is compatible with the primary-law requirement of Art. 56 TFEU (freedom to provide services) has not been specifically assessed by the CJEU in relation to the Swiss rule. Weber-Stecher, op. cit., p. 57, expresses concerns: the conjunction requirement could in individual cases constitute a disproportionate restriction on the freedom to provide services where no local lawyer willing to act in conjunction can be found in a given canton. Bohnet/Martenet, op. cit., para. 597, affirm compatibility with Union law, because Art. 5 of Directive 77/249/EEC contains the rule itself and Art. 23 BGFA is derived from it without additional burden. Since the AFMP pursuant to Art. 16 paras. 1 and 2 AFMP aims to achieve the closest possible parallel with the EU legal position, and the CJEU has never qualified the Directive as contrary to primary law on this point, the provision is likely to remain uncontested in the bilateral relationship.

**N. 17** **Determination of «mandatory representation»** in the absence of a federal law definition: Since the entry into force of the CPC and the CrimPC, the question of which proceedings still provide for mandatory representation is largely in need of clarification. The Zurich Obergericht ruled in UH150139 of 26 August 2015 that Art. 23 BGFA applies where «mandatory representation» within the meaning of the applicable procedural law exists, and referred to cantonal or federal procedural law for the definition of the concept. Art. 23 BGFA itself provides no autonomous federal law definition. Fellmann, Anwaltsrecht, para. 176, criticises this indeterminacy and proposes interpreting the concept in an autonomous European law sense in line with Directive 77/249/EEC, i.e. as any proceeding in which the national legal order makes representation by a qualified lawyer a condition for the admissibility of a procedural act.

---

### 6. Practical Notes

**N. 18** Art. 23 BGFA has extraordinarily little effect in current practice. The Federal Supreme Court itself stated in **judgment 2A.536/2003**, consid. 3.2.1, that proceedings with mandatory representation are «hardly» to be encountered «under the current framework». EU/EFTA lawyers providing services in Switzerland must first examine whether the specifically applicable procedural law provides for mandatory representation at all. In proceedings before the Federal Supreme Court, Art. 40 para. 1 BGG requires representation by registered lawyers (i.e. lawyers within the meaning of the BGFA); however, this does not constitute a conjunction requirement as such for service-providing EU/EFTA lawyers — a service provider under Arts. 21 ff. BGFA would not be admitted before the Federal Supreme Court by virtue of Art. 40 BGG alone (cf. judgment **6B_68/2018** of 7 November 2018, consid. 1, in which the Federal Supreme Court clarified that a lawyer from Liechtenstein acting in the context of freedom to provide services — absent a cantonal register entry — is not admitted before the Federal Supreme Court unless registered in the EU/EFTA lawyers' list under Arts. 27 ff. BGFA).

**N. 19** Service-providing EU/EFTA lawyers who wish to appear in proceedings with mandatory representation must, prior to their first appearance, ensure that the conjunction arrangement with a locally registered lawyer is documented. The precise form is not regulated by law. In practice, a written cooperation agreement that can be disclosed both to the court and to the client is recommended.

**N. 20** For permanently practising EU/EFTA lawyers (Arts. 27 ff. BGFA), the obligation to act in conjunction under Art. 27 para. 2 in conjunction with Art. 23 BGFA continues to apply unchanged as long as they have not been entered in the cantonal lawyers' register pursuant to Art. 30 BGFA. Registration in the EU/EFTA lawyers' list under Art. 28 BGFA does not lift the obligation to act in conjunction in proceedings with mandatory representation. Only full integration into the cantonal lawyers' register (→ Art. 30 para. 2 BGFA) establishes equal standing with Swiss lawyers and renders Art. 23 BGFA inapplicable.

**N. 21** The obligation to act in conjunction must be distinguished from the obligation to use the original professional title (→ Art. 24 BGFA) and the obligation to provide proof of qualifications (→ Art. 22 BGFA). The latter apply regardless of whether mandatory representation exists; Art. 23 BGFA, by contrast, is accessory to the mandatory representation requirement of the applicable procedural law. The Thurgau Obergericht (RBOG 2005 No. 38, 30 November 2005, consid. 2b) further clarified that the AFMP and the BGFA require non-discriminatory application of procedural rules vis-à-vis EU lawyers providing services; administrative obstacles that in effect amount to discrimination are impermissible — a consideration that must also be taken into account when interpreting the scope of the obligation to act in conjunction under Art. 23 BGFA.
