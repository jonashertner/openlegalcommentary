# Art. 31 BGFA — Aptitude Test

## Doctrine

### 1. Legislative History

**N. 1** Art. 31 BGFA has its basis in EU law in Council Directive 89/48/EEC of 21 December 1988 on a general system for the recognition of higher-education diplomas (the so-called Higher Education Diplomas Recognition Directive), which is expressly mentioned in Annex III of the Agreement on the Free Movement of Persons (AFMP, SR 0.142.112.681). The Directive provided in principle for the recognition of foreign diplomas for regulated professions — which include the legal profession — but permitted Member States to introduce an aptitude test for legal professions. The Federal Council adopted this concept directly into the bill and explained in the Dispatch of 28 April 1999 (BBl 1999 6013, 6024 f.) that the aptitude test was intended to address the substantial differences between national training systems. Parliament adopted Art. 31 BGFA without discussion (AB 1999 N 1551 ff.; AB 1999 S 1173).

**N. 2** After the enactment of the BGFA, the Higher Education Diplomas Recognition Directive 89/48/EEC was replaced by Directive 2005/36/EC on the recognition of professional qualifications. This replacement does not directly affect Art. 31 BGFA, since Annex III AFMP refers to the date of signature of the AFMP (21 June 1999) and the Federal Supreme Court has consistently held that it will depart from the interpretation of the CJEU only for «compelling reasons» (**BGE 151 II 640 E. 5.6.1**). The substantive design of the Swiss aptitude test regime thus remains oriented towards the regulatory model of the original Higher Education Diplomas Recognition Directive.

### 2. Systematic Classification

**N. 3** Art. 31 BGFA is situated in the sixth section of the Act («Entry in a cantonal register», Art. 30–34) and is the central implementing provision for → Art. 30 para. 1 lit. a BGFA, which makes the entry of EU/EFTA foreign lawyers in the cantonal register of lawyers conditional upon passing an aptitude test *or* demonstrating three years of practice in Swiss law (→ Art. 32 BGFA). The provision thus stands in an alternative relationship to → Art. 32 BGFA (interview on professional skills): the candidate chooses between the examination and the integration route.

**N. 4** The sixth section (Art. 30–34 BGFA) constitutes the third modality of the practice of law by EU/EFTA nationals in Switzerland. The three modalities are: (1) free provision of services for up to 90 working days per year without registration (→ Art. 21–26 BGFA); (2) permanent practice under the original professional title with entry in the EU/EFTA list of lawyers (→ Art. 27–29 BGFA); (3) full integration by entry in the cantonal register of lawyers pursuant to Art. 30–34 BGFA. The aptitude test under Art. 31 BGFA is the direct route to the third modality (judgment 2A.536/2003 of 9 August 2004 E. 3.2.3; **BGE 151 II 640 E. 4.2.3**).

**N. 5** Upon entry pursuant to Art. 30 para. 2 BGFA, EU/EFTA lawyers who have passed the aptitude test acquire *the same* rights and duties as lawyers holding a cantonal law licence. In particular, they are subject to the professional rules (→ Art. 12 BGFA), professional secrecy (→ Art. 13 BGFA) and disciplinary supervision (→ Art. 14–20 BGFA). The traineeship and bar examination pursuant to → Art. 7 para. 1 lit. b BGFA do not apply to this group; the aptitude test under Art. 31 BGFA takes their place.

### 3. Elements of the Provision / Normative Content

#### 3.1 Conditions for Admission (para. 1)

**N. 6** The aptitude test under Art. 31 para. 1 BGFA is open only to nationals of EU or EFTA Member States. Third-country nationals who hold a lawyer's title recognised in an EU/EFTA state but do not possess EU/EFTA nationality are not covered; for them, only the general provisions of Art. 6–8 BGFA apply (Kellerhals/Baumgartner, in: Fellmann/Zindel [eds.], Kommentar zum Anwaltsgesetz, 2nd ed. 2011, Art. 27 N. 1; cf. Administrative Court ZH, VB.2016.00490 E. 4.4 f., where full integration into the bar of the home state is highlighted as a prerequisite).

**N. 7** Art. 31 para. 1 lit. a BGFA requires a *university course of study of at least three years* as well as, where applicable, any professional training required beyond the degree programme. This formulation mirrors the structure of the Higher Education Diplomas Recognition Directive: the Act focuses primarily on the duration of study, not on the academic degree. A two-year Bachelor's degree is insufficient (cf. Cantonal Court UR, decision 04/05 55 of 21 May 2004, where a Bachelor of Laws from an Anglo-Saxon jurisdiction was equated only with the status of cand. iur.).

**N. 8** Art. 31 para. 1 lit. b BGFA requires a *diploma* that *entitles the holder to practise as a lawyer in a Member State of the EU or EFTA*. The diploma must therefore confer an actual right to practise in the home state. It is irrelevant whether the qualification was *originally* obtained in an EU/EFTA state, provided it is fully recognised in the home state and entitles the holder to practise under one of the professional titles listed in the Annex to the BGFA (Administrative Court ZH, VB.2016.00490 E. 4.4). What is decisive is full integration into the bar of the home state, not the place of training.

#### 3.2 Jurisdiction and Procedure (para. 2)

**N. 9** Art. 31 para. 2 BGFA assigns responsibility for conducting the aptitude test to the *bar examination board of the canton* in whose register the candidate wishes to be entered. Jurisdiction is therefore determined by the target canton of registration, not by place of domicile or the address of the law firm. This corresponds to the principle in → Art. 6 para. 1 BGFA, under which registration takes place in the canton of the business address. The cantons administer the aptitude test under their own authority; federal law provides only the framework. Cantonal administrative procedural law applies to appeals against examination decisions (Bohnet/Martenet, Droit de la profession d'avocat, 2009, para. 897 ff.).

#### 3.3 Content of the Examination (para. 3)

**N. 10** Art. 31 para. 3 BGFA governs the substantive scope of the aptitude test: it extends to *knowledge of Swiss law* required for the practice of the profession and takes into account the knowledge already acquired by the candidate abroad. The Act thus prescribes an *individualised* examination that takes into account the candidate's pre-existing qualifications. In concrete terms, this means that EU/EFTA lawyers do not have to repeat the full bar examination under → Art. 7 para. 1 lit. b BGFA, but are examined only on those areas in which a relevant gap in knowledge exists (BBl 1999 6024; Fellmann, Anwaltsrecht, 2nd ed. 2017, para. 221).

**N. 11** The detailed design of the examination — subjects, format, grading system, possibility of resitting — falls within cantonal competence. Federal law sets only the minimum substantive requirements. Constitutional law (Art. 29 para. 2 FC) provides a right to be heard; however, the scope of review by cantonal courts when examining examination decisions is limited (**BGE 121 I 225 E. 2**; Administrative Court ZH, VB.2018.00648 E. 1.3).

### 4. Legal Consequences

**N. 12** Passing the aptitude test under Art. 31 BGFA is one of the requirements for entry in the cantonal register of lawyers under → Art. 30 para. 1 lit. a BGFA. Upon registration, EU/EFTA lawyers are *fully equated* with lawyers holding a cantonal law licence pursuant to → Art. 30 para. 2 BGFA: they may practise across the entire scope of the Swiss legal monopoly, use the professional title customary in the relevant canton (→ Art. 33 BGFA) and enjoy intercantonal freedom of movement (→ Art. 4 BGFA).

**N. 13** Failure to pass the aptitude test precludes entry in the cantonal register of lawyers. The person concerned may still resort to the route of three years of practice on the EU/EFTA list of lawyers pursuant to → Art. 30 para. 1 lit. b and → Art. 32 BGFA, or — within 90 working days per year — continue to operate under the free provision of services pursuant to → Art. 21 BGFA. The cantons may — in accordance with the principle of proportionality (Art. 5 para. 2 FC) — provide for rules on resitting the examination, but are not obliged to allow unlimited resits (Bohnet/Martenet, op. cit., para. 899).

### 5. Contested Issues

**N. 14** *Obligation to administer the aptitude test vs. discretion.* Legal scholarship is divided on whether the cantons are obliged to administer the aptitude test once the conditions for admission under Art. 31 para. 1 BGFA are met, or whether they retain some discretion. Kellerhals/Baumgartner (Kommentar zum Anwaltsgesetz, 2nd ed. 2011, Art. 30 N. 5) argue that the obligation to administer the examination follows from the free movement purpose of the Act; a refusal without objective grounds would be contrary to the AFMP. By contrast, Fellmann (Anwaltsrecht, 2nd ed. 2017, para. 221) emphasises that the substantive design of the examination lies within cantonal discretion, which also includes a degree of flexibility in granting admission. The wording of the Act («shall be admitted») supports the duty thesis; a refusal based solely on resource constraints would likely be incompatible with the free movement objective.

**N. 15** *Design of the individualisation of the examination content.* Art. 31 para. 3 BGFA provides that knowledge already acquired must be taken into account. How thorough this consideration must be is unclear. Bohnet/Martenet (Droit de la profession d'avocat, 2009, para. 899) call for a genuine exemption from parts of the examination in which the candidate already demonstrably possesses sufficient knowledge; mere consideration as part of the overall assessment is insufficient. Nater/Tuchschmid (Die internationale Freizügigkeit nach dem BGFA, in: Thürer/Weber/Portmann/Kellerhals [eds.], Bilaterale Verträge I & II Schweiz–EU, 2007, p. 311 f., N. 22), by contrast, afford the cantons wide latitude in design and consider a formal consideration sufficient, provided the examination is not disproportionately burdensome. The Dispatch suggests that at least those subject areas should be excluded in which Swiss law largely corresponds to the law of the home state (BBl 1999 6025).

**N. 16** *Relationship to Directive 98/5/EC and the prohibition of abuse.* Although Art. 31 BGFA is primarily based on the Higher Education Diplomas Recognition Directive, interactions arise with the Establishment Directive 98/5/EC: EU/EFTA lawyers who choose the route via three years on the list (Art. 30 para. 1 lit. b BGFA) are protected under CJEU case law against excessive registration hurdles (**BGE 151 II 640 E. 5.6.3**). However, an abusive reliance on this alternative route solely for the purpose of circumventing the aptitude test is impermissible (BGE 151 II 640 E. 5.7.3 with reference to CJEU C-58/13 *Torresi*, para. 42). Scholarship is divided on how a suspicion of abuse is to be handled in practice: Kellerhals/Baumgartner (op. cit., Art. 30 N. 6 f.) favour an examination of actual activity in Swiss law; Bohnet/Martenet (op. cit., para. 897) caution against excessively strict requirements that would undermine the free movement objective.

**N. 17** *Origin-neutrality of qualifications.* The Administrative Court of the Canton of Zurich held, in relation to Art. 28 BGFA, that it is irrelevant whether the professional qualification was originally obtained in an EU/EFTA state, as long as the applicant is authorised and registered to practise in the home state under one of the professional titles listed in the Annex to the BGFA (VB.2016.00490 E. 4.4). This approach should equally apply to Art. 31 BGFA: what is decisive is not the place of training, but full integration into the bar of the EU/EFTA home state. A more restrictive interpretation requiring that training was originally obtained within the EU/EFTA area cannot be derived from either the wording or the Dispatch.

### 6. Practical Notes

**N. 18** EU/EFTA lawyers wishing to be entered in the cantonal register of lawyers must decide at an early stage on the *correct canton of registration*, since jurisdiction for the examination is determined by the target canton of registration (Art. 31 para. 2 BGFA). The cantonal bar examination boards differ considerably in how they administer the aptitude test in practice; advance enquiry about examination subjects, procedure and possibilities for resitting is indispensable.

**N. 19** For admission to the aptitude test, two documents are central under Art. 31 para. 1 BGFA: (1) proof of a university course of study of at least three years (university or higher education diploma, with translation and certification where required); (2) the diploma or certificate demonstrating the right to practise as a lawyer in the home state. Candidates whose training was partially completed outside the EU/EFTA must demonstrate that their qualification has been fully integrated into the bar of the EU/EFTA home state (VB.2016.00490 E. 4.5).

**N. 20** Passing the aptitude test leads to equal status under → Art. 30 para. 2 BGFA; the registered lawyers thereby enjoy the same intercantonal freedom of movement as holders of a cantonal law licence (→ Art. 4 BGFA). They may then — unlike EU/EFTA lawyers who are only entered on the list under Art. 28 BGFA — appear in proceedings requiring mandatory legal representation without the consent of another registered lawyer; the mandatory representation requirement of → Art. 23 BGFA no longer applies.

**N. 21** For candidates who wish to avoid the aptitude test or who fail it, the alternative route is available via three years on the EU/EFTA list of lawyers (→ Art. 27–28 BGFA) followed by proof of effective and regular practice in Swiss law (→ Art. 30 para. 1 lit. b and Art. 32 BGFA). Since **BGE 151 II 640**, the only requirements for entry on the list under Art. 28 BGFA are the intention to practise permanently in Switzerland (intention to establish oneself) and proof of the lawyer's qualification by means of a certificate of registration in the home state that is no more than three months old; a prior establishment of an economic focus in Switzerland may not be required.
