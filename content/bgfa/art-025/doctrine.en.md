# Art. 25 BGFA — Professional Rules

## Doctrine

### 1. Legislative History

**N. 1** Art. 25 BGFA forms part of Section 4 of the Act (Arts. 21–26), which governs the *temporary* provision of services in Switzerland by lawyers from EU and EFTA member states. The Federal Council's Dispatch of 28 April 1999 (BBl 1999 6013) proposed this Section as the direct transposition into Swiss law of Council Directive 77/249/EEC of 22 March 1977 to facilitate the effective exercise by lawyers of freedom to provide services (OJ EC No. L 78/17). The Directive was not directly applicable to Switzerland as a non-EU member state, but was incorporated into the bilateral legal order by reference in Annex III, B.3 of the Agreement on the Free Movement of Persons of 21 June 1999 (AFMP; SR 0.142.112.681).

**N. 2** The Federal Council's draft provided that EU/EFTA lawyers providing services on a temporary basis should in principle be subject to the same professional rules as Swiss lawyers, while retaining their home-state professional title (BBl 1999 6052 f., para. 231). The Federal Council based this on the principle of equal treatment with nationals, which is enshrined both in Art. 19 in conjunction with Annex I of the AFMP and in Art. 4(1) and (2) of Directive 77/249/EEC: «The person providing the services must respect the rules of the profession applicable in the host state» (BBl 1999 6052). Parliament adopted the draft without substantive amendment; Art. 25 BGFA was not the subject of any contentious debate in either chamber.

**N. 3** Art. 25 BGFA entered into force on 1 June 2002 together with the BGFA as a whole (AS 2002 862). No substantive revisions have taken place.

---

### 2. Systematic Classification

**N. 4** Art. 25 BGFA is the *professional-rules reference provision* for the temporary provision of services. Section 4 has a three-tier structure:

| Provision | Subject matter |
|-----------|----------------|
| Arts. 21–24 BGFA | Entitlement to act temporarily, professional title, involvement of a correspondent lawyer |
| **Art. 25 BGFA** | **Applicable professional rules** |
| Art. 26 BGFA | Disciplinary measures |

Art. 25 thus forms the bridge between the right of access (Arts. 21–24 BGFA) and disciplinary accountability (Art. 26 BGFA). Without Art. 25 BGFA it would be unclear what standard of conduct is binding on EU/EFTA lawyers providing services. ↔ Art. 12 BGFA; → Art. 17 BGFA (disciplinary measures, applicable via Art. 26 BGFA).

**N. 5** The relationship to the professional rules in Section 2 (Arts. 12–20 BGFA) is central: Art. 25 BGFA directs the *full application* of Art. 12 BGFA — and thereby the same catalogue of duties to which Swiss lawyers entered in the cantonal register are subject. This equal-treatment approach distinguishes Art. 25 BGFA from a mere minimum-standards reference.

**N. 6** Arts. 27–34 BGFA govern the *permanent* practice of the legal profession by EU/EFTA lawyers. Art. 27(2) BGFA refers expressly to Art. 25 BGFA: the professional rules under Art. 25 BGFA also apply to permanently practising EU/EFTA lawyers — the provision therefore has a radiating effect across the entire Section 5. This has been confirmed by the Federal Supreme Court in judgment 2A.110/2003 of 29 January 2004 at consid. 5.1.2 and judgment 2A.111/2003 of 29 January 2004 at consid. 5.1.2. → Art. 27(2) BGFA; → Art. 30(2) BGFA.

### 3. Content of the Provision

**N. 7** Art. 25 BGFA reads, in the authoritative text on Fedlex (SR 935.61):

> «Lawyers who represent parties before Swiss judicial authorities are subject to the professional rules under Article 12 and act in agreement with a lawyer who is entered in a cantonal lawyers' register (Art. 23). In Switzerland they use their original professional title in the official language of their home state, together with an indication of the professional organisation to whose jurisdiction they are subject or of the court before which they are authorised to practise under the rules of that state (Art. 24).»

The provision thus contains two substantive elements: (a) subjection to the professional rules under Art. 12 BGFA (together with the requirement to involve a correspondent lawyer under Art. 23 BGFA); and (b) the obligation to use the home-state professional title in accordance with Art. 24 BGFA.

#### 3.1 Obligation to Use the Home-State Professional Title

**N. 8** EU/EFTA lawyers temporarily practising in Switzerland must use their *home-state professional title* in the official language of the home state — for instance «Rechtsanwalt» (Germany), «Avvocato» (Italy), «Solicitor» (England and Wales), «Avocat» (France). They may *not* describe themselves as «Advokat», «Rechtsanwalt» or «Fürsprech» in the Swiss sense, since these titles are reserved by Art. 2 BGFA for persons holding a cantonal licence to practise law. The purpose of this obligation is to ensure transparency vis-à-vis the public seeking legal assistance. According to the prevailing scholarly view, a mere translation of the home-state professional title is impermissible, since Directive 77/249/EEC (Art. 3) and Art. 24 BGFA require the title to be given in the official language of the *home state* (Fellmann/Zindel, BSK BGFA, 2nd ed. 2011, Art. 24 N. 20 ff.; Bohnet/Martenet, Droit de la profession d'avocat, 2009, N. 2211 ff.). On letterheads, e-mail signatures and court submissions, the lawyer must additionally indicate to which professional register in the home state they belong; this follows directly from the wording of Art. 24 BGFA in conjunction with Art. 25 BGFA. Unauthorised use of a Swiss professional title gives rise not only to disciplinary proceedings under Art. 26 BGFA but also to potential liability under cantonal law.

#### 3.2 Applicability of the Professional Rules under Art. 12 BGFA

**N. 9** Art. 25 BGFA subjects EU/EFTA lawyers providing services temporarily to the professional rules under Art. 12 BGFA *in their entirety*. This includes in particular:

- Art. 12(a) BGFA: diligence and conscientiousness
- Art. 12(b) BGFA: **independence** — applicable without restriction to EU/EFTA lawyers providing services, according to Federal Supreme Court case law (judgment 2A.110/2003 of 29 January 2004 at consid. 5.1.2; judgment 2A.111/2003 of 29 January 2004 at consid. 5.1.2)
- Art. 12(c) BGFA: avoidance of conflicts of interest
- Art. 12(d) BGFA: limits on permissible advertising
- Art. 12(h) BGFA: segregation of client funds
- Art. 13 BGFA: professional secrecy (→ Art. 13 BGFA; for the controversy over the extension of Art. 13 BGFA to service providers see N. 16)

**N. 10** Subjection to Art. 12 BGFA occurs *in addition* to the binding professional rules of the home state. In the event of a conflict, the stricter rule applies (dual binding). This corresponds to the EU-law principle of host-state rules, codified in Art. 4(1) of Directive 77/249/EEC; compliance with the rules of the host state is mandatory (Fellmann, Anwaltsrecht, 2nd ed. 2017, para. 1180).

**N. 11** The involvement of a correspondent lawyer under Art. 23 BGFA constitutes a *specific* professional duty for the representation of parties before Swiss courts; the cross-reference in Art. 25 BGFA to Art. 23 BGFA makes clear that this duty continues to apply autonomously within the scope of Art. 25 BGFA. The correspondent lawyer must be entered in the cantonal lawyers' register and must share active responsibility for the mandate; purely formal involvement without substantive participation may constitute a breach of professional duties for both lawyers concerned. → Art. 23 BGFA.

#### 3.3 Material Scope of Application and Demarcation from Permanent Practice

**N. 12** Art. 25 BGFA applies to the *temporary* provision of services within the meaning of Art. 21 BGFA. The demarcation from *permanent* practice under Arts. 27 ff. BGFA must be drawn according to the criteria in Art. 5(1) AFMP: the determining factors are the duration, frequency, regularity and continuity of the provision of services (judgment 2A.536/2003 of 9 August 2004 at consid. 3.2.2). The 90-working-day threshold in Art. 5(1) AFMP serves as a temporal reference point; the overall picture of the activity is, however, decisive. In **BGE 151 II 640** at consid. 5.7.1 f., the Federal Supreme Court clarified that prior establishment of an economic centre of gravity in Switzerland may not be required for entry in the EU/EFTA lawyers' list (Art. 28 BGFA); it is sufficient that the lawyer intends to operate an office on a permanent basis in Switzerland and takes corresponding steps towards that end. Lawyers who act repeatedly in several proceedings in Switzerland should therefore examine at an early stage whether the conditions for permanent practice (Art. 27 BGFA) are met. → Art. 21 BGFA; → Art. 27 BGFA; → Art. 28 BGFA.

**N. 13** In the area of *extrajudicial legal advice* by EU/EFTA lawyers providing services, Art. 25 BGFA does not apply, since the BGFA under Art. 2(1) only covers persons who represent parties before courts within the framework of the lawyers' monopoly. Where there is no forensic activity but only legal advice, the monopoly activity is absent; EU/EFTA lawyers engaged exclusively in advisory work are subject neither to Art. 12 BGFA nor to Art. 25 BGFA (Bohnet/Martenet, Droit de la profession d'avocat, 2009, N. 2183 ff.; BBl 1999 6043 f.).

---

### 4. Legal Consequences

**N. 14** If an EU/EFTA lawyer providing services temporarily breaches the professional rules under Art. 12 BGFA, the *disciplinary measures* under Art. 26 BGFA in conjunction with Art. 17 BGFA are applicable. The disciplinary measures available, in ascending order of severity, are: caution, reprimand, fine of up to CHF 20,000, and a ban on professional practice of up to two years (Art. 17(1) BGFA). In the case of temporary service provision, a *ban on professional practice* operates as a de facto prohibition on activity in Switzerland. → Art. 26 BGFA; → Art. 17 BGFA.

**N. 15** Pursuant to Art. 14 BGFA, competence to impose disciplinary measures against EU/EFTA lawyers providing services rests in principle with the supervisory authority of the canton in which the legal activity is carried out (principle of the place of commission). Notification of the supervisory authority in the home state is required in serious cases (Art. 26(2) BGFA); however, the practical enforcement of disciplinary sanctions abroad remains difficult.

### 5. Disputed Questions

**N. 16** **Scope of subjection to professional rules: full equal treatment or differentiated application?** The prevailing scholarly view (Fellmann/Zindel, BSK BGFA, 2nd ed. 2011, Art. 25 N. 5 ff.; Bohnet/Martenet, Droit de la profession d'avocat, 2009, N. 2218) reads Art. 25 BGFA as a *complete* equality provision: EU/EFTA lawyers providing services are subject to the same duties as Swiss lawyers entered in the register. By contrast, Rothenbühler (Dienstleistungsfreiheit und Berufsanerkennung, in: Die sektoriellen Abkommen Schweiz-EG, Berner Tage für die juristische Praxis, Bern 2002, pp. 114 ff.) has pointed out that certain obligations under Art. 12 BGFA — in particular those structurally linked to the cantonal register — do not fit the situation of temporary service providers and should be applied with a teleological reduction. The Federal Supreme Court has not ruled explicitly on this question, but in judgment 2A.110/2003 of 29 January 2004 at consid. 5.1.2 and judgment 2A.111/2003 of 29 January 2004 at consid. 5.1.2 confirmed the full applicability of the duty of independence (Art. 12(b) BGFA) to EU/EFTA lawyers without differentiation.

**N. 17** **No reverse discrimination through the duty of independence.** During the parliamentary debates (AB 1999 N 1558, statement of Nabholz), concern was expressed that the restrictive Swiss concept of independence would disadvantage domestic lawyers relative to foreign lawyers. The Federal Supreme Court expressly rejected this concern in judgment 2A.110/2003 of 29 January 2004 at consid. 5.1.2 and judgment 2A.111/2003 of 29 January 2004 at consid. 5.1.2: since Art. 25 BGFA extends the Swiss duty of independence without exception to EU/EFTA lawyers — and since Art. 6 of Directive 77/249/EEC and Art. 8 of Directive 98/5/EC each enshrine the principle of national treatment — there is no discrimination against domestic lawyers:

> «Foreign lawyers who wish to practise in a contracting state are placed on an equal footing with their domestic professional colleagues, in particular as regards professional duties (such as the duty of independence)» (judgment 2A.111/2003 of 29 January 2004 at consid. 5.1.2).

**N. 18** **Extension of professional secrecy (Art. 13 BGFA) to service providers.** Art. 25 BGFA refers to Art. 12, not expressly to Art. 13 BGFA. Fellmann/Zindel (BSK BGFA, 2nd ed. 2011, Art. 25 N. 8) take the affirmative view that Art. 13 BGFA applies: that provision concretises Art. 12(a) BGFA (duty of diligence) and is therefore encompassed by the reference in Art. 25 BGFA; the protective purpose of professional secrecy cannot depend on the registration status of the lawyer. The contrary view (Nater/Zindel, Kommentar zum Anwaltsgesetz, 2020, Art. 25 N. 4) draws a distinction: Art. 13 BGFA produces, vis-à-vis EU/EFTA lawyers providing services, only the effect of defining the framework under criminal and civil law (Art. 321 SCC, Art. 171 CPC), but not the disciplinary dimension. In practical terms, this distinction gives rise to conflict-of-laws problems in cross-border proceedings: Swiss law protects professional secrecy even against foreign disclosure obligations; which law prevails in the event of a conflict must be determined on a case-by-case basis. ↔ Art. 13 BGFA; → Art. 321 SCC; → Art. 171 CPC.

**N. 19** **Demarcation between temporary and permanent practice and its significance for Art. 25 BGFA.** The demarcation between temporary service provision (Arts. 21–26 BGFA) and permanent practice (Arts. 27–34 BGFA) determines whether Art. 25 BGFA or Art. 27(2) in conjunction with Art. 25 BGFA applies. In **BGE 151 II 640** at consid. 5.7 (2025), the Federal Supreme Court refined its earlier case law and clarified that prior establishment of an economic centre of gravity in Switzerland may not be required for entry in the EU/EFTA lawyers' list. This clarification also influences the demarcation question within the scope of application of Art. 25 BGFA: a lawyer who wishes to practise in Switzerland having set up a permanent office infrastructure is, according to **BGE 151 II 640** at consid. 5.7.2, regularly no longer to be classified as a merely temporary service provider, even if they have not yet spent 90 working days in Switzerland.

### 6. Practical Notes

**N. 20** **Conflicts of interest in cross-border mandates.** Cross-border mandates carry a heightened risk of conflicts of interest — for instance where an EU lawyer advises companies of a group that are active both in the home state and in Switzerland. Art. 12(c) BGFA applies without restriction by virtue of Art. 25 BGFA; the conflict-of-interest rules applicable in the home state do not relieve the lawyer of the Swiss duty.

**N. 21** **Disciplinary jurisdiction.** In disciplinary proceedings against EU/EFTA lawyers providing services, the scholarly literature recommends (Fellmann, Anwaltsrecht, 2nd ed. 2017, para. 1185) contacting the supervisory authority of the canton in which the relevant activity was carried out. Notification of the supervisory authority in the home state is required in serious cases (Art. 26(2) BGFA).
