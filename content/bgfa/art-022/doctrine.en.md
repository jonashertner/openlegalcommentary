# Art. 22 BGFA — Proof of Qualification as a Lawyer

## Doctrine

### 1. Legislative History

**N. 1** Art. 22 BGFA originates from the Federal Council's draft of 28 April 1999 and was adopted without amendment by the Federal Assembly (AB 1999 N 1551 ff., 1569; AB 1999 S 1173). The provision implements Art. 7(1) of Council Directive 77/249/EEC of 22 March 1977 to facilitate the effective exercise by lawyers of freedom to provide services (Services Directive, OJ L 78 of 26 March 1977, p. 17) into Swiss law. The European Services Directive provides that the host Member State may require proof of admission to the legal profession; the verification is intended to ensure proper professional conduct in the context of the freedom to provide services, without creating a prior registration obligation.

**N. 2** In the Dispatch, the Federal Council explained that lawyers practising under the freedom to provide services would be required to prove their professional qualification upon request — citing expressly as examples of acceptable evidence «das Anwaltspatent, eine Bescheinigung über die Zulassung zum Anwaltsberuf im Herkunftsstaat usw.» [the licence to practise as a lawyer, a certificate of admission to the legal profession in the home State, etc.] (BBl 1999 6063 para. 234.22). The Federal Council emphasised that occasion-specific proof constituted a proportionate supervisory measure that preserved the effective functioning of the freedom to provide services without imposing unreasonable administrative burdens on lawyers from EU/EFTA States. The Federal Supreme Court confirmed this assessment: the proof provided for in Art. 22 BGFA was «weder unzumutbar noch geschäftsschädigend» [neither unreasonable nor detrimental to business] (judgment **2A.536/2003** of 9 August 2004, consid. 4.3).

**N. 3** The regulatory concept of Art. 22 BGFA stands in deliberate contrast to the regime applicable to EU/EFTA lawyers practising on a permanent basis (→ Art. 28(2) BGFA): for the latter, proof of qualification takes the form of a current certificate of registration from the home State (not more than three months old) as a constitutive requirement for registration. In the context of the freedom to provide services, by contrast, it is conceived as a declaratory duty to produce proof upon request, thereby allowing immediate activity without prior notification (BBl 1999 6063; judgment **2A.536/2003** consid. 4.3; **BGE 151 II 640** consid. 4.2.1).

---

### 2. Systematic Classification

**N. 4** Art. 22 BGFA is located in Section 4 of the Act (Art. 21–26 BGFA), which governs the activities of EU/EFTA lawyers under the freedom to provide services. It complements Art. 21 BGFA, which establishes the right to represent parties without prior registration, by providing authorities with an occasion-specific power of verification. Within Section 4, Art. 22 BGFA is the counterpart to Art. 21(2) BGFA (no registration obligation): the exemption from registration is offset by the authorities' right to require proof.

**N. 5** The overall framework of the BGFA reflects the three-tier integration of foreign lawyers: at the first tier — the freedom to provide services (Art. 21–26 BGFA) — Art. 22 BGFA is the central supervisory instrument available to the authorities. At the second tier — permanent practice under the original professional title (Art. 27–29 BGFA) — the occasion-specific proof is replaced by the constitutive certificate of registration under Art. 28(2) BGFA. At the third tier — registration in the register (Art. 30–34a BGFA) — full equivalence with domestic lawyers is achieved, for whom a registration obligation likewise applies (judgment **2A.536/2003** consid. 3.2; **BGE 151 II 640** consid. 4.2; Fellmann, Anwaltsrecht, 2nd ed. 2017, para. 176–179).

**N. 6** The reservation of cantonal law under → Art. 3 BGFA applies to Art. 22 BGFA only to a limited extent: federal law establishes a conclusive framework with the duty to produce proof upon request; the cantons may, within their implementing legislation, specify the procedure (e.g. time limits for production, competent authorities), but may not introduce any more far-reaching prior registration obligations that conflict with the federal law concept of Art. 22 BGFA (Dreyer, CR LLCA, Introductory remarks to Art. 21–26 BGFA, N. 6; Bohnet/Martenet, Droit de la profession d'avocat, 2009, para. 306). → Art. 3 BGFA; ↔ Art. 21 BGFA; → Art. 25 BGFA; ↔ Art. 28(2) BGFA.

### 3. Elements of the Provision / Normative Content

#### 3.1 Persons Subject to the Duty to Produce Proof

**N. 7** The duty to produce proof applies exclusively to «lawyers providing services» within the meaning of Art. 21(1) BGFA — i.e. nationals of EU or EFTA Member States who are entitled to practise as lawyers in their home State under one of the professional titles listed in the Annex to the BGFA, and who represent parties before judicial authorities in Switzerland on a temporary and occasional basis (BBl 1999 6063; judgment **2A.536/2003** consid. 3.2.1; **BGE 151 II 640** consid. 4.2.1). Lawyers who are registered in the EU/EFTA lawyers' register (→ Art. 27 f. BGFA) or who are entered in the cantonal lawyers' register (→ Art. 30 BGFA) are not subject to Art. 22 BGFA; their qualification is evidenced by the registration (Fellmann, Anwaltsrecht, para. 179; Kellerhals/Baumgartner, in: Fellmann/Zindel [eds.], BSK BGFA, 2nd ed. 2011, Introductory remarks to Art. 21–26, N. 4).

**N. 8** Nationals of third States who are admitted to the Bar in an EU/EFTA Member State do not fall within the personal scope of Art. 21 and therefore not within that of Art. 22 BGFA either. They require a special authorisation for any activity within the Swiss reserved domain (Kellerhals/Baumgartner, BSK BGFA, Introductory remarks to Art. 21–26, N. 4). Art. 22 BGFA likewise does not apply to domestic lawyers; they prove their qualification through their entry in the register (→ Art. 6 ff. BGFA).

#### 3.2 Authorities Entitled to Request Proof

**N. 9** Two categories of authorities are entitled to request proof of qualification:

**(a) Judicial authorities:** Any federal or cantonal judicial authority before which a lawyer providing services appears. This encompasses ordinary courts (civil courts, criminal courts, administrative courts of all instances) as well as the Federal Supreme Court, the Federal Administrative Court and the Federal Criminal Court. The term «judicial authorities» corresponds to that used in Art. 21(1) BGFA (BBl 1999 6063; Bohnet/Martenet, Droit de la profession d'avocat, para. 841).

**(b) Supervisory authorities over lawyers:** The cantonal supervisory authorities responsible for enforcing the professional rules pursuant to → Art. 14 BGFA. Their right to request proof under Art. 22 BGFA forms part of the general supervision over lawyers providing services (→ Art. 25 f. BGFA). The supervisory authority may require proof independently of any specific proceedings — for instance in the context of a disciplinary review (BBl 1999 6063).

**N. 10** Private individuals (parties, opposing parties) are not entitled to request proof. Art. 22 BGFA establishes exclusively a public-law duty to produce proof vis-à-vis authorities, not a private-law duty to provide information (Dreyer, CR LLCA, Art. 22 BGFA, N. 3).

#### 3.3 Content and Form of Proof

**N. 11** The Act does not prescribe the form of proof. The Dispatch cites by way of example «das Anwaltspatent, eine Bescheinigung über die Zulassung zum Anwaltsberuf im Herkunftsstaat usw.» [the licence to practise as a lawyer, a certificate of admission to the legal profession in the home State, etc.] (BBl 1999 6063 para. 234.22). What is decisive is that the proof establishes the actual and current admission to the legal profession in the home State. In practice, the following are accepted in particular:
- the licence to practise or the certificate of admission of the home State;
- a current certificate issued by the competent professional organisation or judicial authority of the home State confirming existing admission;
- an extract from the corresponding lawyers' register of the home State.

**N. 12** For registration in the EU/EFTA lawyers' register, Art. 28(2) BGFA expressly requires a certificate not more than three months old. This standard is applicable by analogy to Art. 22 BGFA: an outdated certificate does not sufficiently establish continued admission (to the same effect, **BGE 151 II 640** consid. 4.2.2, which expressly invokes the three-month standard in the context of Art. 28(2) BGFA as an expression of the currency requirement; Dreyer, CR LLCA, Art. 22 BGFA, N. 5). However, a strict three-month time limit does not apply to Art. 22 BGFA, since the Act contains no corresponding provision.

**N. 13** Proof may be produced in the language of the home State; a translation may be required by the requesting authority on a case-by-case basis if the language of the document is not intelligible to it. There is no general translation obligation (Dreyer, CR LLCA, Art. 22 BGFA, N. 5).

#### 3.4 Timing and Procedure for the Request

**N. 14** Art. 22 BGFA does not create a prior duty to produce proof. Proof is required only upon specific request by the judicial or supervisory authority; it may also be produced after the lawyer has commenced appearing before the authority (BBl 1999 6063; judgment **2A.536/2003** consid. 4.3). The authority is not obliged to request proof; it decides in the exercise of its discretion (Dreyer, CR LLCA, Art. 22 BGFA, N. 4).

**N. 15** The judicial authority may request proof upon the filing of a pleading or at first contact. The supervisory authority may require it in the context of disciplinary proceedings or a review without specific cause. No time window for production is fixed by statute; the time limit set by the authority applies, which must comply with the principle of proportionality.

### 4. Legal Consequences

#### 4.1 Consequences upon Production of Proof

**N. 16** Proof of qualification as a lawyer that has been duly produced has no constitutive effect on the right to represent parties — that right derives directly from Art. 21(1) BGFA. It does, however, confirm to the authority that the conditions for the provision of services are met, and provides the authority with no further grounds to restrict the lawyer's appearance (Fellmann, Anwaltsrecht, para. 179).

#### 4.2 Consequences of Failure to Produce Proof

**N. 17** If the required proof is not produced or is not produced within a reasonable period, the judicial authority may exclude the lawyer providing services from appearing in the specific proceedings. In procedural terms, the exclusion operates as a disregard of the power of attorney or as the rejection of the submitted procedural act. Whether and under what conditions pleadings already filed are attributed to the party (rather than to the lawyer) is governed by the applicable procedural law (CPC, CrimPC, APA; cf. Dreyer, CR LLCA, Art. 22 BGFA, N. 6).

**N. 18** Failure to produce proof before the supervisory authority may be treated as a breach of the duty to cooperate in supervisory proceedings and may entail disciplinary sanctions under → Art. 26 BGFA. In particular, a reprimand and a fine are possible. Since Art. 22 BGFA creates a professional obligation, its breach — together with other indications — may also be treated as a basis for examining whether the conditions of Art. 21(1) BGFA were actually met at the relevant time (Bohnet/Martenet, Droit de la profession d'avocat, para. 841).

**N. 19** Refusal to produce proof is not conduct relevant under criminal law, unless fraudulent misrepresentation regarding the qualification is also involved. The latter could, in certain circumstances, satisfy the elements of the offence of procuring a false document by deception (Art. 253 SCC) or fraudulent misuse of a data processing system (Art. 147 SCC), where the corresponding acts are present.

### 5. Contested Issues

#### 5.1 Relationship to the Assessment of Unreasonableness and Proportionality

**N. 20** In judgment **2A.536/2003** (consid. 4.3), the Federal Supreme Court expressly held that the proof of qualification as a lawyer provided for in Art. 22 BGFA and in Art. 7(1) of the Services Directive was «weder unzumutbar noch geschäftsschädigend» [neither unreasonable nor detrimental to business]. It had even been an accommodation to service providers from abroad that they were not required to obtain registration in the respective States in advance, but could immediately take on representation and produce proof only upon request. Fellmann (Anwaltsrecht, para. 179) and Kellerhals/Baumgartner (BSK BGFA, Introductory remarks to Art. 21–26, N. 4) share this assessment.

**N. 21** In contrast, Dreyer (CR LLCA, Art. 22 BGFA, N. 4) points out that the practice of certain judicial authorities of regularly requesting proof without any specific cause can impair the practical effectiveness of the freedom to provide services. A routine request without any specific grounds for suspicion — comparable to a de facto registration obligation — would contradict the purpose of Art. 22 BGFA and would be problematic under European law (cf. Art. 4(1) of Services Directive 77/249/EEC, which expressly excludes prior registration). The authority must exercise its discretion with restraint when requesting proof.

#### 5.2 Form and Currency of Proof

**N. 22** There is a disputed question as to what requirements are to be imposed on the currency of proof of qualification. Dreyer (CR LLCA, Art. 22 BGFA, N. 5) argues in favour of an analogous application of the three-month time limit under Art. 28(2) BGFA, since the need for currency is structurally comparable. Bohnet/Martenet (Droit de la profession d'avocat, para. 841) and Kellerhals/Baumgartner (BSK BGFA, Art. 28, N. 3), by contrast, consider the three-month rule to be specifically tailored to the registration procedure and reject its analogous application to Art. 22 BGFA: in the context of the freedom to provide services, the question of currency is to be assessed according to the circumstances of the individual case. The Act itself contains no time-limit provision for Art. 22 BGFA, which leaves the interpretive question open.

#### 5.3 Relationship between Art. 22 BGFA and Art. 28(2) BGFA, and Demarcation of the Regimes

**N. 23** The duty to produce proof upon request regulated in Art. 22 BGFA is also relevant in practice to the demarcation between the freedom to provide services and permanent practice. In judgment **2A.536/2003** (consid. 3.2.1 and 4.3) and in **BGE 151 II 640** (consid. 4.2.1), the Federal Supreme Court clarified that the proof of qualification under Art. 22 BGFA is limited to lawyers providing services, and that for EU/EFTA lawyers practising on a permanent basis the registration regime under Art. 27 f. BGFA applies exclusively. The verification practice of the supervisory authorities under Art. 22 BGFA indirectly reveals whether a lawyer was still operating under the freedom-to-provide-services regime at the time of the review, or whether the conditions triggering the obligation to register in the EU/EFTA lawyers' register had already been met (Dreyer, CR LLCA, Art. 22 BGFA, N. 2; Nater/Wipf, in: Bilaterale Verträge Schweiz-EG, 2002, pp. 258 f.).

### 6. Practical Notes

**N. 24** **Document preparation.** EU/EFTA lawyers appearing in Switzerland under the freedom to provide services should always carry — or be able to obtain promptly — a current certificate of their admission to the legal profession in their home State. As a benchmark — even though no statutory time limit applies — a certificate issued not more than three months ago by the competent professional organisation of the home State is recommended, since this standard corresponds to the statutory requirement for the registration procedure under Art. 28(2) BGFA (**BGE 151 II 640** consid. 4.2.2; BBl 1999 6063).

**N. 25** **Practice of the authorities.** Judicial authorities typically request proof at first contact when the lawyer's home-State professional title (→ Art. 24 BGFA) indicates a foreign State of admission. A routine, unprompted request upon every filing would not be compatible with the purpose of the provision (N. 21). Supervisory authorities tend to require proof in the context of disciplinary proceedings or complaints from parties to proceedings.

**N. 26** **Demarcation from the registration obligation.** Lawyers providing services who anticipate exceeding the threshold of 90 working days per calendar year (Art. 5 FZA [Agreement on the Free Movement of Persons]) or who intend to practise permanently in Switzerland must apply in good time for registration in the EU/EFTA lawyers' register (→ Art. 27 BGFA; → Art. 28 BGFA). From that point onwards, the constitutive certificate of registration replaces the occasion-specific proof under Art. 22 BGFA. Activity after the threshold has been exceeded without registration is inadmissible and is subject to disciplinary measures under → Art. 26 BGFA (**BGE 151 II 640** consid. 5.7.2; Weber-Stecher, in: Professional Legal Services: Vom Monopol zum Wettbewerb, 2000, p. 59).

**N. 27** **Effects on pending proceedings.** If proof is requested during pending court proceedings and is not produced within the time limit, exclusion from representation is at risk. Pleadings filed prior to the exclusion remain effective under the general principles of procedural law, since the power of attorney existed at the time of filing. In such a case, the party has the right to be represented by another authorised person (cf. Art. 68 CPC, Art. 128 CrimPC). Judicial authorities are well advised to set a short additional time limit before treating procedural acts as invalid.

**N. 28** **Relationship to cantonal implementing legislation.** Cantonal implementing legislation for the BGFA may specify the procedure for producing proof under Art. 22 BGFA (competences, time limits, communication channels). However, it may not impose any more far-reaching substantive requirements regarding qualification, since federal law is conclusive in this area (→ Art. 3 BGFA; Dreyer, CR LLCA, Introductory remarks to Art. 21–26 BGFA, N. 6; Bohnet/Martenet, Droit de la profession d'avocat, para. 306).
