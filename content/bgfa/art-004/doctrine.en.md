# Art. 4 BGFA — Principle of Inter-Cantonal Freedom of Movement

## Doctrine

### 1. Legislative History

**N. 1** Art. 4 BGFA is the central core provision of the Act. In a single sentence it expresses the regulatory purpose of the entire enactment: the inter-cantonal freedom of movement of lawyers. The Federal Council stated this objective unambiguously in its Message of 28 April 1999: the Lawyers Act is to facilitate «the inter-cantonal mobility of lawyers» and functions in this respect as a complement to the Federal Act on the Internal Market (BBl 1999 6020; **BGE 131 II 639** cons. 3.4.1).

**N. 2** Prior to the entry into force of the BGFA on 1 June 2002, the law governing the legal profession was regulated exclusively at cantonal level. Any person wishing to appear before a court in a canton other than the one in which they had obtained their licence was required to obtain a separate cantonal authorisation to practise. In its Message, the Federal Council described the resulting «fragmentation», which significantly impeded the free exercise of the profession (BBl 1999 6019 ff., para. 114). Art. 196 para. 5 FC (transitional provision relating to Art. 95 FC) had already obliged the cantons to mutually recognise educational qualifications, but a uniform implementation under federal law was lacking.

**N. 3** The Federal Council's consultation draft originally envisaged that lawyers would have to register in every canton in which they maintained a business address. The Federal Council departed from this concept in its Message: it opted for a single register-entry requirement as the basis for nation-wide admission (**BGE 131 II 639** cons. 3.3; BBl 1999 6046). The Chambers approved this concept without reservation (AB 1999 N 1553; AB 1999 S 1164).

**N. 4** The BGFA is based on Art. 95 paras. 1 and 2 FC, which confers on the Confederation the competence to enact provisions on private-sector gainful activity and to guarantee a unified Swiss economic area (judgment **2C_897/2015** of 25.5.2016 cons. 6.1). The choice of this constitutional basis means that the BGFA is directed primarily at admission to the profession in the form of register entry and does not reform the cantonal lawyers' licence as such (judgment **2C_897/2015** of 25.5.2016 cons. 6.2.3).

---

### 2. Systematic Classification

**N. 5** Art. 4 BGFA stands at the head of the second division («Freedom of Movement and Register», Arts. 4–11) and constitutes the foundational rule for the entire register system. Its relationship to the subsequent provisions is that of a general principle to implementing rules:

- → Art. 5 BGFA: obligation of the cantons to maintain a register as the institutional precondition for the freedom-of-movement effect conferred by Art. 4 BGFA
- → Art. 6 BGFA: registration procedure (application for entry, jurisdiction of the supervisory authority)
- → Art. 7 BGFA: professional requirements (cantonal lawyers' licence, licentiate or master's degree, traineeship and examination)
- → Art. 8 BGFA: personal requirements (legal capacity, no unsatisfied debt enforcement certificates, independence)
- → Art. 9 BGFA: deletion from the register as the mirror image of entry
- ↔ Art. 3 para. 2 BGFA: reservation for cantonal law permitting licence-holders to appear before their home-canton courts without register entry

**N. 6** Art. 4 BGFA establishes register entry as the sole requirement for nation-wide representation of parties before judicial authorities. It thereby operates as the gateway norm of the federal freedom-of-movement system. The Federal Tribunal established at an early stage that entry in the cantonal lawyers' register is exhaustively governed by federal law under Arts. 4 ff. BGFA (**BGE 131 II 639** cons. 2.1; **BGE 130 II 87** cons. 1; see also **BGE 130 II 270** cons. 3.1.1).

**N. 7** For EU/EFTA lawyers, the special provisions of Arts. 21–34a BGFA apply. Art. 4 BGFA is, however, also indirectly relevant for this group: lawyers from EU/EFTA states who are definitively entered in the cantonal register pursuant to Arts. 30–34 BGFA enjoy the same freedom of movement as domestic register members (**BGE 130 II 87** cons. 5.1.2).

**N. 8** In relation to procedural law, Art. 4 BGFA has a radiating effect. The lawyers' monopoly in civil and criminal matters (Art. 68 CPC, Art. 127 para. 5 CrimPC) presupposes that the persons entitled to act as representatives are «entitled under the BGFA», i.e. entered in a cantonal lawyers' register (**BGE 147 IV 379** cons. 1.2.2). ↔ Art. 68 CPC, Art. 127 para. 5 CrimPC, Art. 40 para. 1 SCA.

### 3. Constituent Elements / Content of the Norm

#### 3.1 Personal Scope: «Lawyers who are entered in a cantonal lawyers' register»

**N. 9** Art. 4 BGFA requires register entry as the sole substantive condition for freedom of movement. Any person entered in any cantonal lawyers' register — regardless of which canton — is entitled to represent parties before judicial authorities throughout Switzerland. The register in question must be one maintained pursuant to Art. 5 BGFA; cantonal admission systems outside the BGFA register do not suffice.

**N. 10** The BGFA applies, pursuant to Art. 2 para. 1 BGFA, to persons who hold a cantonal lawyers' licence and represent parties before judicial authorities within the scope of the lawyers' monopoly. Art. 4 BGFA gives concrete expression to this constituent requirement in the area of freedom of movement: the register entry alone is decisive, not the licence itself (**BGE 130 II 87** cons. 3). The cantonal lawyers' licence is merely a precondition for register entry (→ Art. 7 para. 1 BGFA), not for the authorisation to practise as such (judgment **2C_897/2015** of 25.5.2016 cons. 7.2.2).

**N. 11** Register entry is compulsorily confined to a single canton. The Federal Tribunal held definitively in **BGE 131 II 639** cons. 3 that simultaneous entry in several cantonal registers is excluded. Lawyers with multiple business addresses must register in the canton in which they predominantly practise (BBl 1999 6046; **BGE 131 II 639** cons. 3.5). See Staehelin/Oetiker, BSK BGFA, Art. 6 N. 12 (approvingly cited in **BGE 131 II 639** cons. 3.5).

**N. 12** Lawyers who are not registered — including holders of a cantonal lawyers' licence — cannot rely on Art. 4 BGFA. They retain the possibility, on the basis of Art. 3 para. 2 BGFA, of appearing before the courts of their home canton, provided that the canton in question makes use of this reservation (judgment **2C_897/2015** of 25.5.2016 cons. 3.1). Nater, BSK BGFA, Art. 3 N. 7 notes that the cantons have given different form to this competence.

#### 3.2 Material Scope: «Representation of parties before judicial authorities»

**N. 13** The material scope of Art. 4 BGFA covers the representation of parties in the monopoly area, i.e. forensic activity before judicial authorities in the technical sense. The Federal Tribunal has characterised the lawyers' monopoly as a «non-genuine monopoly in the legal sense», since it is structurally a classic administrative licence in the field of economic policing (**BGE 130 II 87** cons. 3; see **BGE 151 I 285** cons. 3.2).

**N. 14** Not covered by Art. 4 BGFA — and therefore not subject to a register-entry requirement — are pure legal advice and extra-judicial activities such as serving on a board of directors, asset management or commercial advisory services (**BGE 130 II 87** cons. 3). Such activities fall neither within the lawyers' monopoly nor within the scope of the BGFA as a whole (judgment **2C_897/2015** of 25.5.2016 cons. 7.2.1; Fellmann, Anwaltsrecht, 2nd ed. 2017, para. 808).

**N. 15** The concept of «judicial authorities» in Art. 4 BGFA must be understood broadly and encompasses all judicial authorities in civil, criminal and administrative proceedings at cantonal and federal level. In the area of criminal defence, the Federal Tribunal confirmed in **BGE 147 IV 379** cons. 1.2.2 that the lawyers entitled to conduct a defence under Art. 127 para. 5 CrimPC are those «who are entered in a cantonal lawyers' register (Art. 4 BGFA)».

**N. 16** Cantonal lawyers' monopolies in the area of administrative justice are consistent with federal law (**BGE 151 I 285** cons. 5.3.1). Several cantons restrict professional representation before their administrative courts to persons entered in a cantonal lawyers' register. The Federal Tribunal has, in long-standing case law, characterised this practice as a permissible restriction on the right to freely choose legal representation (Art. 29 para. 2 FC), justified by the protection of the public (**BGE 151 I 285** cons. 5.4; BGE 120 Ia 247 cons. 3b).

#### 3.3 Legal Consequence: «without any further authorisation»

**N. 17** The core content of Art. 4 BGFA lies in the negative formulation «without any further authorisation». This wording means that a single register entry in a single canton automatically and without any additional examination, registration or authorisation includes the right to represent parties in all other cantons and before federal authorities. Any additionally required cantonal admission condition for already registered lawyers violates this federal law provision. The BGFA exhaustively regulates the conditions for register entry (**BGE 130 II 270** cons. 3.1.1).

**N. 18** A further immediate consequence of register entry is the subjection to the federal regime of professional regulation and disciplinary supervision (→ Arts. 12–20 BGFA). The registered lawyer enjoys freedom of movement but is, in return, subject to the uniform professional duties and to the supervision of the competent cantonal authorities (→ Art. 14 BGFA).

### 4. Legal Consequences

**N. 19** Art. 4 BGFA produces three types of legal effects:

*(i) Positive entitlement:* Register entry confers the right to represent parties before all judicial authorities in Switzerland — both in the canton of entry and in all other cantons and before federal authorities. The entitlement is spatially unlimited and remains in force without time limit until any deletion (→ Art. 9 BGFA).

*(ii) Negative constraint on cantons:* Cantons may not impose additional authorisation requirements on registered lawyers. Registration in the canton of entry has, as the Federal Tribunal has stated, «conclusive effect» in the sense of a federal-law pre-emption of cantonal admission barriers (**BGE 130 II 270** cons. 3.1.1).

*(iii) Reference point for the monopoly area:* Art. 4 BGFA defines the category of persons to whom party representation within the lawyers' monopoly is reserved. Non-registered persons are excluded from the monopoly area, regardless of their professional qualifications (**BGE 147 IV 379** cons. 1.2.2; **BGE 151 I 285** cons. 3.2).

**N. 20** The freedom of movement enshrined in Art. 4 BGFA is subject to the reservation of continued fulfilment of the conditions for entry. If any of those conditions ceases to be met, the supervisory authority is required to order deletion (→ Art. 9 BGFA). Freedom of movement is accordingly not an acquired legal position in the sense of a vested right, but an entitlement conditional upon the continued subsistence of register entry (Fellmann, Anwaltsrecht, 2nd ed. 2017, para. 233).

**N. 21** Infringement of the monopoly established by Art. 4 BGFA may entail civil-law consequences (nullity of procedural acts), criminal-law consequences (unauthorised practice of the profession) and supervisory-law consequences. Cantonal law may sanction the unauthorised exercise of the legal profession as a regulatory offence (cf. Art. 37 AnwG/SG; judgment sg_gerichte_AW.2015.59 of 29.10.2015).

### 5. Disputed Issues

#### 5.1 Scope of the Monopoly Concept

**N. 22** In legal scholarship there is controversy as to what is to be understood by «representation of parties before judicial authorities» within the meaning of Art. 4 BGFA and the related lawyers' monopoly. Fellmann, Anwaltsrecht, 2nd ed. 2017, para. 808, defines the lawyers' monopoly both positively (which activities may only be carried out by registered lawyers) and negatively (which persons without a licence are excluded from representation). Diebold, AJP 2023, p. 1249, by contrast, emphasises that outside the monopoly area there is no federal regulation of legal advice and argues for a restrictive delimitation of the monopoly area in the interests of economic freedom.

**N. 23** For civil proceedings, the legislator has in Art. 68 para. 2 CPC expressly confined the monopoly area to *professional* party representation. In criminal proceedings, no corresponding restriction exists: the Federal Tribunal held in **BGE 147 IV 379** cons. 1.2.3 that Art. 127 para. 5 CrimPC establishes the lawyers' monopoly for both professional and non-professional defence. Bohnet/Martenet, Droit de la profession d'avocat, 2009, para. 973, and Nater, BSK BGFA, Art. 3 N. 6a, consider that the cantonal margin of manoeuvre in criminal law is thereby closely circumscribed by federal law.

#### 5.2 Cantonal Revocation of the Licence and its Relationship to Art. 4 BGFA

**N. 24** It was disputed whether cantons, on the basis of Art. 3 para. 1 BGFA, may revoke the lawyers' licence when the personal conditions for holding it cease to be met, even though the BGFA provides only for deletion from the register (→ Art. 9 BGFA) and not for revocation of the licence. Kettiger (Entzug des Anwaltspatents, Jusletter 28.9.2009, pp. 4 f.) answers in the negative: Art. 3 para. 1 BGFA relates only to professional requirements; the qualified silence of the federal legislator on licence revocation argues for its inadmissibility. The prevailing scholarship and the Federal Tribunal take the opposite view: since the cantons are permitted to determine the conditions for obtaining the licence, revocation when those conditions cease to be met is also possible (judgment **2C_897/2015** of 25.5.2016 cons. 6.3; Fellmann, Anwaltsrecht, 2nd ed. 2017, para. 686; Bohnet/Martenet, Droit de la profession d'avocat, 2009, paras. 576 ff.; Brunner/Henn/Kriesi, Anwaltsrecht, 2015, p. 12). The Federal Tribunal followed the position of Fellmann and made clear that licence revocation and deletion from the register are parallel but distinct legal instruments.

#### 5.3 Extension of the Cantonal Monopoly to Administrative Justice

**N. 25** Bernard, Monopole de la représentation en justice et liberté économique, in: Gegenwart und Zukunft des Anwaltsberufs, 2023, pp. 174 ff., and Bohnet/Othenin-Girard, CR BGFA, Art. 3 N. 18, support, with reference to cantonal autonomy in procedural law (Arts. 43, 46 para. 3, 47 para. 2 FC), the right of cantons to introduce a lawyers' monopoly also in administrative proceedings. The Federal Tribunal confirmed this view in **BGE 151 I 285** cons. 5.3.1. Hirsig-Vouilloz, CR VwVG, Art. 11 N. 14, and Nyffenegger, VwVG-Kommentar, Art. 11 N. 3, share this assessment. Isolated objections have been raised that the differing requirements for party representation in cantonal administrative proceedings on the one hand and before the Federal Tribunal on the other could give rise to friction with the principle of procedural unity (Art. 111 para. 1 SCA); the Federal Tribunal dismissed these concerns in **BGE 151 I 285** cons. 5.3.3, since Art. 111 para. 1 SCA relates to party representation and not to standing to appeal.

### 6. Practical Notes

**N. 26** *Single canton, universally effective:* Registered lawyers need not be registered in any further canton in order to represent parties before judicial authorities there. Freedom of movement is automatic and requires no additional notification or reporting obligation to the supervisory authority of the register of the canton in which the activity takes place.

**N. 27** *Choice of canton of registration:* Lawyers who practise in several cantons must register in the canton in which they *predominantly* practise (**BGE 131 II 639** cons. 3.5). If the primary focus of activity shifts, they are required to change their register canton. Multiple entries are excluded and would constitute an irregularity in the register to be corrected by the supervisory authority.

**N. 28** *Disciplinary supervision:* When a lawyer registered in Canton A appears before a court in Canton B, they are subject for that mandate to the supervision of both authorities. The supervisory authority of the canton of activity (Canton B) is required, in any disciplinary proceedings, to inform and hear the supervisory authority of the canton of registration (Canton A) (→ Art. 16 BGFA; **BGE 131 II 639** cons. 3.4.2; BBl 1999 6059).

**N. 29** *Void acts of representation:* Where a person not entitled under Art. 4 BGFA appears as a party representative, the procedural acts performed by that person are generally invalid. Courts are required to examine the entitlement to represent parties ex officio (Art. 68 CPC; Art. 127 para. 5 CrimPC). If the entitlement is lacking, the party must be given the opportunity, within a time limit to be set, to appoint an authorised representative.

**N. 30** *Cantonal reservations under Art. 3 para. 2 BGFA:* A canton may permit holders of its cantonal lawyers' licence — even without register entry — to appear before its own courts. This exception, however, relates only to the cantonal dimension; inter-cantonal freedom of movement under Art. 4 BGFA is available exclusively to registered lawyers. Nor may cantonal lawyers' monopolies in administrative justice restrict the right of audience more extensively than the BGFA prescribes for registered lawyers (**BGE 151 I 285** cons. 5.4).

**N. 31** *Lawyers in employment relationships:* Registration in the lawyers' register and thus freedom of movement under Art. 4 BGFA are in principle also available to employed lawyers. However, an employment relationship with an unregistered employer gives rise to a rebuttable presumption of a lack of independence (→ Art. 8 para. 1 lit. d BGFA; **BGE 130 II 87** cons. 5.1). In such cases, practice requires, for the purpose of register entry, a clear contractual and organisational separation between the employment relationship and the independent practice of the law (**BGE 130 II 87** cons. 6).
