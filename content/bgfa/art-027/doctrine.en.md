# Art. 27 BGFA — Principles (Permanent Practice by EU/EFTA Lawyers)

## Doctrine

### 1. Legislative History

**N. 1** The BGFA of 23 June 2000 (SR 935.61) implements, in Art. 21–34, the EU directives listed in Annex III of the Agreement on the Free Movement of Persons of 21 June 1999 (AFMP; SR 0.142.112.681) as regards the law governing the legal profession. Art. 27 BGFA is based primarily on Directive 98/5/EC of the European Parliament and of the Council of 16 February 1998 to facilitate practice of the profession of lawyer on a permanent basis in a Member State other than that in which the qualification was obtained (the «Establishment Directive»; OJ L 77 of 14 March 1998, p. 36). In addition, Directive 77/249/EEC (the «Services Directive») and Directive 89/48/EEC (the «Higher Education Diplomas Directive») are relevant; cf. BBl 1999 6061 para. 234.

**N. 2** In the Dispatch on the BGFA, the Federal Council stated that Art. 27 BGFA read in conjunction with Art. 28 BGFA applies to «stable and continuous» practice of the profession in Switzerland, thereby covering the freedom of establishment — as distinct from the merely temporary provision of services under Art. 21–26 BGFA (BBl 1999 6066 para. 234.32). For registration on the public list (→ Art. 28 BGFA), EU/EFTA lawyers are required to submit «solely» a certificate attesting to their registration with the competent authority of their home State (BBl 1999 6066). The Federal Council's draft of Art. 27 and Art. 28 BGFA was adopted in both chambers without debate (AB 1999 N 1551 ff., 1569; AB 1999 S 1173).

---

### 2. Systematic Classification

**N. 3** Art. 27 BGFA is situated in the 4th Section of the BGFA («Permanent Practice of the Profession of Lawyer under the Home-Country Professional Title», Art. 27–29) and forms the systematic bridge between the free movement of services (→ Art. 21–26 BGFA) and full integration into the cantonal lawyers' register (→ Art. 30–34 BGFA). The three-tier system of the BGFA for EU/EFTA lawyers — free movement of services, permanent practice under the home-country professional title, entry in the cantonal lawyers' register — follows the systematic structure of the Establishment Directive; as expressly stated in judgment 2A.536/2003 of 9 August 2004, consid. 3.2.

**N. 4** Art. 27 para. 2 BGFA declares Art. 23–25 BGFA (compulsory representation by a lawyer, professional title, supervision in the context of the provision of services) to be correspondingly applicable. This cross-reference ensures that lawyers registered on the EU/EFTA lawyers' list are subject to the same professional duties and the same disciplinary supervision as those practising within the framework of the free movement of services. The difference from the freedom to provide services lies principally in the obligation to register and in the requirement of a residence permit (→ Art. 4 AFMP read in conjunction with Art. 6 ff. and Art. 12 ff. Annex I AFMP).

**N. 5** Art. 27 BGFA has a close connection with Art. 30 para. 1 lit. b BGFA (↔ Art. 30 BGFA): registration on the EU/EFTA lawyers' list for at least three years, together with proof of effective and regular activity in Swiss law, opens the way to facilitated entry in the cantonal lawyers' register. Art. 27 BGFA is therefore not merely an expression of current practice of the profession, but also the starting point of a career path towards full equivalence with holders of a cantonal licence to practise law.

---

### 3. Elements of the Provision / Normative Content

**N. 6** Art. 27 para. 1 BGFA sets out three substantive requirements for permanent practice under the home-country professional title: (1) nationality of an EU or EFTA Member State; (2) authorisation to practise as a lawyer in the home State under one of the professional titles listed in the Annex to the BGFA; (3) registration with a cantonal supervisory authority. The modalities of registration are governed by → Art. 28 BGFA. Non-EU/EFTA nationals who have obtained a legal qualification in an EU State may also register, provided they are registered with the competent authority of that State; cf. judgment of the Administrative Court of the Canton of Zurich VB.2016.00490 of 8 December 2016.

**N. 7** The term «permanent» (French: «à titre permanent»; Italian: «permanentemente») is the key distinguishing concept vis-à-vis Art. 21 BGFA (free movement of services, maximum 90 working days per calendar year). In judgment 2A.536/2003 of 9 August 2004, consid. 3.2.2, the Federal Supreme Court held that characterisation as a «temporary service» is not excluded solely by the fact that the lawyer concerned has established an office in Switzerland. What is decisive is the frequency and duration of the activity (with reference to ECJ C-55/94, Gebhard).

**N. 8** In **BGE 151 II 640** of 26 February 2025 (judgment 2C_271/2024), the Federal Supreme Court substantially refined the requirements for the characteristic of «permanent» in the light of European law (→ N. 14–16): the entitlement to permanent practice of the profession is a consequence of registration and not a precondition for it (consid. 6.2). For the purposes of registration, the intention to operate a law firm in Switzerland on a permanent basis, combined with corresponding actual arrangements, is sufficient. A prior establishment of a primary economic focus in Switzerland may not be required (consid. 5.7.2).

**N. 9** Art. 27 para. 1 BGFA grants EU/EFTA lawyers the right to represent parties before judicial authorities, and not merely the right to provide legal advice. Use of the professional title of the home State in the relevant official language is mandatory (Art. 24 read in conjunction with Art. 27 para. 2 BGFA). In proceedings subject to compulsory representation, they must act in agreement with a lawyer registered in the cantonal lawyers' register (Art. 23 read in conjunction with Art. 27 para. 2 BGFA); cf. judgment 2A.536/2003, consid. 3.2.2.

**N. 10** The freedom of establishment for lawyers in several States is guaranteed: Art. 27 BGFA does not prohibit an EU/EFTA lawyer from simultaneously maintaining a secondary or tertiary law firm in another AFMP Member State. This is consistent with the case law of the ECJ (ECJ 107/83, Klopp, [1984] ECR 2971, para. 22; ECJ C-431/17, Monachos Eirinaios, para. 27; BGE 151 II 640, consid. 5.6.3 and 5.7.1). The Administrative Court of the Canton of Aargau had already affirmed this in WBE.2017.393 of 24 January 2018.

---

### 4. Legal Consequences

**N. 11** Registration on the EU/EFTA lawyers' list (→ Art. 28 BGFA) has several legal effects: (a) it entitles the lawyer to unlimited permanent representation of parties before judicial authorities in Switzerland under the home-country professional title; (b) it subjects the lawyer to cantonal supervision in accordance with the professional rules of the BGFA (→ Art. 12–17 BGFA via Art. 27 para. 2 read in conjunction with Art. 25 BGFA); (c) it triggers the running of the time limit for subsequent facilitated entry in the cantonal lawyers' register (→ Art. 30 para. 1 lit. b BGFA); (d) it may affect the immigration law status of the lawyer concerned (cf. Art. 12 ff. Annex I AFMP; BGE 151 II 640, consid. 5.4.2).

**N. 12** The law governing the legal profession and immigration law are to be assessed independently of each other: immigration status is not an obstacle to registration on the EU/EFTA lawyers' list under Art. 27/28 BGFA (**BGE 151 II 640**, consid. 6.3). This corrects the earlier practice, which treated a valid residence permit as a precondition for registration (as was still the case in judgment 2A.536/2003, consid. 4.1). Permanent practice of the profession may independently give rise to a claim under immigration law, but the sequence (first registration on the list, then residence) is now anchored in law.

**N. 13** Art. 27 BGFA is also relevant to admission to act as duty counsel: registered EU/EFTA lawyers may undertake duty defence work in Switzerland; the duty defence is not restricted to lawyers with a place of business in Switzerland (judgment of the Cantonal Court of St. Gallen AK.2014.361 of 3 February 2015 on Art. 132 para. 2 CrimPC read in conjunction with Art. 21 and 27 BGFA).

### 5. Contested Issues

**N. 14** **Contested issue: requirements for «permanent» activity as a condition for registration.** The Federal Supreme Court has substantially changed its practice on the interpretation of the characteristic «permanent» over the course of two decades. In judgment 2A.536/2003, consid. 4.1, it held that Art. 28 para. 2 BGFA — according to its wording — required only proof of the legal qualification, but that the «regulatory context» implied that permanent activity in Switzerland was a precondition for registration. Academic commentary shared this basic position: Kellerhals/Baumgartner, in: Fellmann/Zindel (eds.), Kommentar zum Anwaltsgesetz, 2nd ed. 2011, N. 2 f. on Art. 27 BGFA, required integration into the economy of the host State and activity exceeding 90 working days as an indicator of permanence.

**N. 15** In judgment 2C_694/2011 of 19 December 2011, consid. 4.4, the Federal Supreme Court tightened the requirements further: it required that the activity in Switzerland of a permanent nature form the «primary focus or centre of professional activity», going beyond the mere establishment of a secondary office. This corresponded to the literature, which demanded integration into the economy of the host State: Fellmann, Anwaltsrecht, 2nd ed. 2017, para. 153; Bohnet/Martenet, Droit de la profession d'avocat, 2009, para. 840; Weber, Niederlassung oder Dienstleistung, in: Schweizerisches Anwaltsrecht, 1998, pp. 574 ff. A dissenting view was expressed by Weber-Stecher, Internationale Freizügigkeit, in: Professional Legal Services, 2000, p. 59, who considered the requirements to be too high and incompatible with the Establishment Directive.

**N. 16** **BGE 151 II 640** (judgment 2C_271/2024 of 26 February 2025) fundamentally refined the case law. The Federal Supreme Court held that the earlier case law of judgment 2C_694/2011 was no longer compatible with the development of European law (consid. 5.5.3). Directive 98/5/EC effects «full harmonisation» of the registration requirements (ECJ C-431/17, Monachos Eirinaios, para. 26; ECJ C-58/13, Torresi, para. 38), with the result that the certificate of registration with the competent authority of the home State forms «in principle the sole requirement» for registration (consid. 5.6.3). A prior establishment of a primary focus in Switzerland, or proof that the Swiss activity outweighs the foreign activity, is henceforth inadmissible (consid. 5.7.2). The intention to operate a law firm in Switzerland on a permanent basis, combined with corresponding actual arrangements, is sufficient.

**N. 17** **Contested issue: relationship between the law governing the legal profession and immigration law.** Earlier case law (judgment 2A.536/2003, consid. 4.1) and legal commentary (Kellerhals/Baumgartner, op. cit., N. 4 on Art. 27 BGFA) required a residence permit as a substantive precondition for permanent practice. **BGE 151 II 640**, consid. 6.3, has clarified that the law governing the legal profession and immigration law «are to be assessed independently of each other»: immigration status does not constitute an obstacle to registration. Chappuis/Châtelain, in: Commentaire romand, Loi sur les avocats, 2nd ed. 2022, N. 7 on Art. 27 BGFA, and Nater/Wipf, Internationale Freizügigkeit, in: Bilaterale Verträge Schweiz-EG, 2002, pp. 256 f., had advocated a differentiated approach, which served as the starting point for the Federal Supreme Court's new ruling.

**N. 18** **Contested issue: obligation to register or merely a right?** Art. 3 para. 1 of Directive 98/5/EC formulates registration as an obligation («shall register … with»). Swiss law does not expressly adopt this formulation; Art. 27 para. 1 BGFA confers a right («may»). Judgment 2A.536/2003, consid. 4.2, held that registration serves primarily to enable supervision by the supervisory authority. Bohnet/Martenet, op. cit., para. 841, and Chappuis/Châtelain, op. cit., N. 4 on Art. 28 BGFA, consider the wording to be clear and registration to be a right of the lawyer, not an obligation. In practice, the distinction remains relevant to the question whether a lawyer who exceeds the 90-day threshold without being registered commits a breach of professional rules (→ Art. 17 BGFA).

### 6. Practical Notes

**N. 19** **Distinguishing free movement of services from establishment.** The distinction is made on the basis of the frequency and duration of the activity. The establishment of an infrastructure in Switzerland does not preclude characterisation as a temporary service. As a rule of thumb: an intended activity of more than 90 working days per calendar year indicates permanent activity, triggering an obligation to register under Art. 27/28 BGFA; where the duration is shorter or unclear, the freedom to provide services under Art. 21 BGFA applies (BBl 1999 6066; BGE 151 II 640, consid. 5.7.2).

**N. 20** **Registration procedure.** EU/EFTA lawyers register with the supervisory authority of the canton in which they have a business address. The certificate of registration from the home State must not be more than three months old (→ Art. 28 para. 2 BGFA). Bar associations may not impose any additional substantive requirements, in particular no proof of pre-existing economic activity in Switzerland (**BGE 151 II 640**, consid. 5.8). The intention to establish a practice may be evidenced by the opening of a law firm, a postal address, and corresponding declarations.

**N. 21** **No prior residence permit required.** The law governing the legal profession and immigration law are to be treated independently. Refusal of registration on grounds of the absence of a residence permit infringes Art. 27 BGFA (**BGE 151 II 640**, consid. 6.3). The immigration law authorisation is to be arranged in parallel, but separately.

**N. 22** **Multiple establishments.** EU/EFTA lawyers may practise simultaneously in several Member States. A secondary or tertiary law firm abroad is no impediment to registration on the Swiss EU/EFTA lawyers' list (WBE.2017.393 of 24 January 2018; **BGE 151 II 640**, consid. 5.7.1). The fact that the primary revenue focus lies outside Switzerland does not preclude registration.

**N. 23** **Relationship to Art. 30 para. 1 lit. b BGFA.** The three-year period for facilitated access to the cantonal lawyers' register runs from the date of registration on the EU/EFTA lawyers' list. The risk of abuse — registration merely to set the three-year period running — is low, since subsequent entry in the cantonal lawyers' register requires proof of effective and regular activity in Swiss law (**BGE 151 II 640**, consid. 5.7.3). In the absence of actual activity, facilitated access under Art. 30 para. 1 lit. b BGFA is in any event unavailable.

**N. 24** **Professional title and professional duties.** Registered EU/EFTA lawyers use exclusively their home-country professional title in Switzerland (Art. 24 read in conjunction with Art. 27 para. 2 BGFA). They are subject to the professional rules under Art. 12–17 BGFA via Art. 25 BGFA. Professional secrecy (→ Art. 13 BGFA) applies without restriction. Breach of the professional rules may result in disciplinary measures (→ Art. 17 BGFA).
