# Art. 14 BGFA — Cantonal Supervisory Authority for Lawyers

## Doctrine

### 1. Legislative History

**N. 1** Art. 14 BGFA originates from the Federal Council's Message of 28 April 1999 (BBl 1999 6013). The Federal Council noted therein that the supervision of lawyers was a matter for the cantons, because professional admission — the cantonal law licence — was traditionally anchored in cantonal law (BBl 1999 6058). At the same time, the legislature recognised that the harmonisation of the professional rules of conduct (→ Art. 12 BGFA) and of disciplinary law (→ Art. 17 BGFA) required a minimum institutional structure at the authority level: each canton must designate an authority that acts as a single point of contact for supervision. The Message expressly emphasised that the supervisory authority was obliged to exercise disciplinary supervision (BBl 1999 6058), without any right of initiative for private individuals being provided for.

**N. 2** The National Council and the Council of States inserted Art. 14 BGFA into the Act without material amendments. Unlike the professional rules of conduct (Art. 12 BGFA) and disciplinary law (Arts. 17–20 BGFA), which the BGFA regulates exhaustively (**BGE 129 II 297 E. 1.1**; **BGE 130 II 270 E. 3.1**), Art. 14 BGFA contains only a minimum structural requirement: the cantons determine the organisational form and composition of the supervisory authority themselves. The BGFA entered into force on 1 June 2002.

---

### 2. Systematic Classification

**N. 3** Art. 14 BGFA opens the Third Section of the BGFA («Professional Rules of Conduct and Disciplinary Supervision», Arts. 12–20). It forms the institutional foundation of disciplinary supervision: without an authority designated pursuant to Art. 14 BGFA, disciplinary law (→ Art. 17 BGFA) could not be enforced, and the professional rules of conduct (→ Arts. 12–13 BGFA) would remain without a competent enforcement authority. Art. 14 BGFA is accordingly in a reciprocal relationship with the other provisions of the Third Section: the authority is the body that imposes disciplinary measures pursuant to Art. 17 BGFA, must observe the limitation periods under Art. 19 BGFA, and receives notifications pursuant to Art. 15 BGFA. ↔ Art. 5 para. 3 BGFA provides that the same authority also maintains the cantonal register of lawyers; the supervisory and register functions are therefore institutionally linked.

**N. 4** The obligation to designate a supervisory authority is of a public-law nature and is directed exclusively at the cantons. It does not create subjective rights for third parties, and in particular confers no entitlement on a complainant to have disciplinary proceedings opened or a sanction imposed (**BGE 129 II 297 E. 3.1**; **BGE 132 II 250 E. 4.4**). Disciplinary supervision serves the general public interest in the proper exercise of the legal profession, not the protection of individual private interests.

---

### 3. Elements of the Provision / Normative Content

**N. 5** *Statutory text:* «Each canton shall designate an authority that supervises the lawyers who represent parties before judicial authorities within its territory.»

**N. 6** *Obligation to designate a single authority:* The wording («an authority») indicates that each canton must designate one — and in principle only one — competent authority. In practice, the cantons implement this through specialised supervisory commissions (e.g. the Supervisory Commission for Lawyers in the Canton of Zurich), through existing administrative authorities, or through mixed-composition bodies. The BGFA imposes no requirements as to the composition or legal form of the authority; in particular, it does not prescribe that it must be a judicial body. However, a right of appeal to a final-instance *judicial* authority must be available against decisions of the supervisory authority, so that an administrative law appeal to the Federal Court under the former OG (and today the appeal in public-law matters under Arts. 82 ff. BGG) is admissible (**BGE 129 II 297 E. 1.1**; **BGE 132 II 250 E. 3**). Poledna notes that the BGFA additionally leaves the cantons the possibility of conferring on the supervisory authority supervisory instruments beyond disciplinary law, such as the power to issue directions for the restoration of a lawful state of affairs (Poledna, in: Fellmann/Zindel [eds.], Kommentar zum Anwaltsgesetz, 1st ed. 2005, N. 9 on Art. 14 BGFA; confirmed in **BGE 132 II 250 E. 4.3.1**; Judgment 2C_665/2010 of 24.5.2011 E. 6.1).

**N. 7** *Material scope — «within its territory»:* The supervisory authority has subject-matter jurisdiction over all lawyers who represent parties before judicial authorities within the cantonal territory. The determining factor is not the lawyer's domicile but the actual representation activity on the cantonal territory. Jurisdiction is governed, pursuant to Art. 5 para. 1 BGFA, by the canton in which the lawyer has his or her business address and is entered in the register; that authority is therefore also generally competent for the lawyer's activities outside its canton (Judgment 2C_508/2007 of 27.5.2008 E. 2.2; Judgment 2C_661/2011 of 17.3.2012 E. 3.2).

**N. 8** *Personal scope:* Art. 14 BGFA covers only lawyers who are subject to the Act pursuant to Art. 2 para. 1 BGFA — that is, persons who hold a cantonal law licence and represent parties before judicial authorities within the framework of the lawyers' monopoly. Lawyers who are exclusively engaged in advisory activities, who are not entered in the register, and who are not engaged in litigation are not, as a matter of federal law, subject to supervision under Art. 14 BGFA (Judgment 2C_897/2015 of 25.5.2016 E. 5.2.1). The cantons may extend such supervision by cantonal law to lawyers who are not registered — a matter that is contested in legal scholarship (→ N. 13).

**N. 9** *Duty to notify as an ancillary mechanism:* Art. 15 para. 1 BGFA obliges cantonal judicial and administrative authorities to notify the supervisory authority immediately of incidents that could constitute a breach of the professional rules of conduct. The public prosecutor's office may, on the basis of this duty to notify, file a disciplinary complaint without thereby being regarded as biased — provided that the complaint appears objectively tenable (Judgment 1B_118/2021 of 13.7.2021 E. 4.1 and 4.2). Art. 14 BGFA and Art. 15 BGFA together form the basic framework of the cantonal supervisory structure.

---

### 4. Legal Consequences

**N. 10** *Obligation of the cantons:* Art. 14 BGFA establishes a federal-law obligation on each canton to designate a supervisory authority. This obligation is mandatory; a canton cannot waive it. The failure to designate an authority would violate federal law and could be asserted by way of appeal.

**N. 11** *Disciplinary supervision as a public function:* The supervisory authority is obliged to exercise disciplinary supervision. According to consistent Federal Court case law, the complainant has no enforceable right to have disciplinary proceedings opened or a disciplinary sanction imposed (**BGE 129 II 297 E. 3.1**; **BGE 132 II 250 E. 4.2–4.4**). The complainant has neither under Art. 103 lit. a of the former OG (now Art. 89 para. 1 BGG) nor under Art. 88 of the former OG (now Art. 89 para. 1 BGG) a legally protected interest of his or her own that would confer standing to challenge a negative disciplinary decision. This applies even where the alleged breach of professional rules is ongoing, because the supervisory authority cannot directly intervene in a continuing mandate (**BGE 132 II 250 E. 4.3.2**).

**N. 12** *Release from professional secrecy as an ancillary competence:* The supervisory authority is, under cantonal law (e.g. §§ 33 ff. AnwG/ZH), competent to release lawyers from professional secrecy (→ Art. 13 BGFA), in particular for the purpose of asserting fee claims. This competence arises — in the absence of a federal-law provision — from cantonal law, but is unobjectionable as a matter of federal law, because the authority designated under Art. 14 BGFA, as the cantonal supervisory instance, may also exercise corresponding ancillary competences (Judgment 2C_508/2007 of 27.5.2008 E. 2.2; Judgment 2C_661/2011 of 17.3.2012 E. 3.2). The release decision has no substantive legal effects but merely enables the lawyer to assert his or her fee claim without breaching professional secrecy.

### 5. Contested Issues

**N. 13** *Extension of supervision to unregistered lawyers:* It is contested whether the cantons may extend supervision under Art. 14 BGFA and the application of the professional rules of conduct under Arts. 12–13 BGFA by cantonal law to lawyers who are not entered in the register and are therefore not subject to the BGFA. The Canton of Lucerne has done so (§ 8 para. 2 AnwG/LU). Nater considers such extension problematic and doubts its conformity with federal law (Nater, in: Fellmann/Zindel [eds.], Kommentar zum Anwaltsgesetz, 2nd ed. 2011, N. 8b on Art. 3 BGFA). The Federal Court left the question open in Judgment 2C_897/2015 of 25.5.2016 E. 5.2.2, but held that the BGFA exhaustively governs disciplinary law and professional admission, while the cantons retain a margin of discretion only in the area of licence requirements.

**N. 14** *Additional supervisory instruments of the cantonal authority:* It is disputed whether cantonal supervisory authorities may employ additional supervisory instruments beyond the disciplinary sanctions exhaustively regulated in the BGFA (Art. 17), such as directions for the restoration of a lawful state of affairs (e.g. an order to remove certain names from a website). Poledna and Bauer/Bauer affirm a corresponding cantonal regulatory competence on the ground that such instruments are not professional rules of conduct or disciplinary sanctions but restorative supervisory measures (Poledna, N. 9 on Art. 14 BGFA; A. Bauer/Ph. Bauer, Commentaire Romand de la Loi sur les avocats, 2010, N. 11 on Art. 14 BGFA). The Federal Court expressly confirmed this position in Judgment 2C_665/2010 of 24.5.2011 E. 6.1: the Federal Act on the Legal Profession does not preclude a direction based on § 10 of the cantonal legal profession act, since the cantons possess the corresponding regulatory competence; this applies at least to measures aimed at the restoration of a lawful state of affairs rather than at disciplining.

**N. 15** *Revocation of the law licence by the supervisory authority:* A particular controversy exists as to whether cantonal law may authorise the supervisory authority to revoke the cantonal law licence. The Federal Court held in Judgment 2C_897/2015 of 25.5.2016 that revocation of the law licence does not constitute a disciplinary measure but an administrative measure, and that the cantons — insofar as they make the acquisition of the licence conditional on personal requirements (such as Art. 8 para. 1 lit. c BGFA: no certificate of unsatisfied claims) — are in principle empowered to revoke the licence when those requirements cease to be met (E. 6.3). Kettiger objects, arguing that the travaux préparatoires show that Art. 3 para. 1 BGFA leaves the cantons a margin of discretion only with respect to the *professional*, not the personal, requirements for the granting of a licence (Kettiger, Entzug des Anwaltspatents, Jusletter 28 September 2009, pp. 4 f.). The Federal Court did not follow this academic opinion (2C_897/2015 E. 6.2.3): when enacting the BGFA, the federal legislature did not intend to harmonise the entire law on cantonal law licences but only to regulate registration and intercantonal freedom of movement; with regard to the cantonal law licence itself, the cantons retain broader discretion.

### 6. Practical Notes

**N. 16** *Determining the competent supervisory authority:* In cases of dispute, the competent supervisory authority is always that of the canton in which the lawyer has his or her business address and is entered in the register (Art. 5 para. 1 in conjunction with Art. 14 BGFA). That authority is also competent for the release from professional secrecy, where the lawyer is registered in that canton (Judgment 2C_508/2007 E. 2.2; Judgment 2C_661/2011 E. 3.2). Cantonal differences in the specific organisation of the supervisory authority (composition, procedure) are compatible with the BGFA, provided that the minimum requirements — in particular the guarantee of access to a judicial instance — are observed.

**N. 17** *Appellate procedure:* Against decisions of the cantonal supervisory authority, an appeal first lies to a final-instance judicial authority of the canton (Art. 86 para. 2 BGG); only thereafter is an appeal in public-law matters to the Federal Court admissible (Art. 82 lit. a in conjunction with Art. 86 para. 1 lit. d BGG). Exhaustion of the cantonal remedies is mandatory even where the standing of the appellant appears doubtful (**BGE 132 II 250 E. 3**). Complainants do not, as a general rule, have standing to appeal before the Federal Court (→ N. 11), but may challenge a costs order made against them (**BGE 129 II 297 E. 2.2–2.3**).

**N. 18** *Cantonal freedom of organisation and independence:* The cantons enjoy broad discretion in the organisation of the supervisory authority. The mere fact that members of the supervisory authority also sit on the cantonal High Court, which acts as the appellate instance in respect of decisions of the supervisory authority, does not per se constitute a violation of the guarantee of a court established by law (Art. 30 FC) or the ECHR, provided that the adjudicating judges are not personally involved in the matter (Judgment 2C_665/2010 of 24.5.2011 E. 2.1–2.2). On the requirements regarding the independence of the supervisory authority itself, see Fellmann, Anwaltsrecht, 2nd ed. 2017, para. 661 ff.; Bohnet/Martenet, Droit de la profession d'avocat, 2009, para. 576 ff.

**N. 19** *Relationship to Art. 3 BGFA (cantonal reservation):* Art. 14 BGFA must be read in conjunction with Art. 3 BGFA (→ Art. 3 BGFA). The cantons are empowered to confer on the supervisory authority competences that go beyond what the BGFA provides — in particular in the field of administrative measures (revocation of the law licence, directions) — insofar as these do not affect the professional rules of conduct (Art. 12 BGFA) or disciplinary sanctions (Art. 17 BGFA) exhaustively regulated by the BGFA. The distinction between permissible cantonal implementation and impermissible encroachment on exhaustive federal law remains a practically important and legally sensitive issue.
