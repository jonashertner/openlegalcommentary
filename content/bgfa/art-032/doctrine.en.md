# Art. 32 BGFA — Interview to Assess Professional Qualifications

## Doctrine

### 1. Legislative History

**N. 1** Art. 32 BGFA was adopted together with the Act as a whole on 23 June 2000 and entered into force on 1 June 2002. The provision implements Art. 4 of Council Directive 89/48/EEC of 21 December 1988 on a general system for the recognition of higher-education diplomas, which is referred to in Annex III to the Agreement of 21 June 1999 on the Free Movement of Persons (AFMP; SR 0.142.112.681) between Switzerland and the EU (BBl 1999 6013, 6022 ff.). In doing so, the Federal Council was guided by the so-called Higher Education Diplomas Recognition Directive (Dir. 89/48/EEC), which permits the host State to choose between an adaptation period and an aptitude test where there are substantial differences between the training courses (BBl 1999 6069, item 234.42).

**N. 2** The interview under Art. 32 BGFA is a distinctive feature of the Swiss implementing legislation. Instead of a full aptitude test under Art. 31 BGFA, the Federal Council provided for a simplified examination format tailored to the specific situation of EU/EFTA lawyers who had already been practising Swiss law for several years (BBl 1999 6069). Parliament adopted the Federal Council's draft of Art. 30–32 BGFA without material amendments (AB 1999 N 1551 ff., 1569; AB 1999 S 1173); the three-tier access model for EU/EFTA lawyers was uncontested in Parliament.

---

### 2. Systematic Classification

**N. 3** Art. 32 BGFA is situated in the 5th Section of the BGFA («Registration in the cantonal register of lawyers», Art. 30–34a). Together with Art. 30 and Art. 31 BGFA, it forms the three-tier access system for EU/EFTA lawyers who wish to establish themselves permanently in Switzerland and to practise under the Swiss professional title:

- → **Art. 30 BGFA**: The fundamental provision on registration in the cantonal register of lawyers; choice between the aptitude test (let. a) and three-year registration with proof of practice in Swiss law (let. b).
- → **Art. 31 BGFA**: The aptitude test as the full examination format.
- **Art. 32 BGFA**: The interview as a simplified means of proof, subsidiary to the full aptitude test under Art. 31 BGFA.

**N. 4** The interview under Art. 32 BGFA applies exclusively in the context of Art. 30 para. 1 let. b BGFA: it is available only to those EU/EFTA lawyers who have been registered in the EU/EFTA register of lawyers under Art. 28 BGFA for at least three years but who cannot demonstrate effective and regular practice in Swiss law within the meaning of Art. 30 para. 1 let. b BGFA throughout the entire three-year period. The interview is therefore not an independent third access route, but a substitute means of proof within the framework of demonstrating practice in Swiss law (cf. judgment 2A.536/2003 of 9 August 2004, consid. 3.2.3; **BGE 151 II 640** consid. 4.2.3).

**N. 5** In contrast to the Swiss register of lawyers under Art. 5 ff. BGFA, Art. 32 BGFA is distinguished by the fact that Swiss patent holders are not subject to any qualification interview. Art. 32 BGFA is limited to EU/EFTA lawyers and thus constitutes a specific equalisation mechanism: registration under Art. 30 para. 2 BGFA results in full equality of treatment with domestically registered lawyers (↔ Art. 7 BGFA for professional requirements, ↔ Art. 8 BGFA for personal requirements). → Art. 33 BGFA governs the professional title following registration.

---

### 3. Content of the Provision

**N. 6** Art. 32 BGFA empowers the competent cantonal supervisory authority to invite EU/EFTA lawyers who apply for registration in the cantonal register of lawyers under Art. 30 para. 1 let. b BGFA to an interview regarding their professional qualifications. The interview takes the place of full proof of practice in Swiss law where such proof cannot be furnished for the entire three-year period.

**N. 7** **Conditions for the interview**: The interview requires the cumulative fulfilment of the following conditions: (1) registration in the EU/EFTA register of lawyers under Art. 28 BGFA for at least three years; (2) proof that the practice in Swiss law during that period was not «effective and regular» within the meaning of Art. 30 para. 1 let. b BGFA; (3) an application for registration in the cantonal register of lawyers. The interview is not convened at the request of the applicant but is ordered by the authority; it is an examination measure, not an advisory measure (Kellerhals/Baumgartner, BSK BGFA, Art. 32 N. 2 ff.).

**N. 8** **Subject matter of the interview**: The interview relates to «professional qualifications» in Swiss law. It is substantively narrower than the aptitude test under Art. 31 BGFA, which systematically covers all material differences between the training courses. The interview is intended to enable the applicant to demonstrate the knowledge of Swiss law acquired during the three-year period. According to the explanatory report, this concerns in particular procedural law and the principal areas of substantive Swiss law in which the lawyer in question has practised (BBl 1999 6069, item 234.42; Bohnet/Martenet, Droit de la profession d'avocat, 2009, N. 897 ff.).

**N. 9** **Competent authority**: Pursuant to Art. 30 para. 1 let. b BGFA in conjunction with Art. 32 BGFA, the cantonal supervisory authority under Art. 14 BGFA has jurisdiction. That authority determines both whether the practice in Swiss law was sufficient and whether an interview is to be ordered and how it is to be conducted. The specific design of the interview is governed by cantonal law within the federal minimum standard (→ Art. 3 BGFA; Fellmann, Anwaltsrecht, 2nd ed. 2017, N. 236 ff.).

**N. 10** **Relationship to the aptitude test (Art. 31 BGFA)**: The interview under Art. 32 BGFA and the aptitude test under Art. 31 BGFA are alternative access routes for different situations. The aptitude test is available to the EU/EFTA lawyer regardless of the length of any prior registration period (Art. 30 para. 1 let. a BGFA). The interview, by contrast, is limited to applicants who choose the three-year route under Art. 30 para. 1 let. b BGFA but are unable to furnish full proof of practice. The interview therefore presupposes a certain level of pre-existing knowledge, which the three-year registration is intended to secure (Dreyer, Commentaire romand LLCA, 2nd ed. 2022, preliminary remarks to Art. 21–26 N. 11).

---

### 4. Legal Consequences

**N. 11** **Where the interview is passed**: If the applicant demonstrates sufficient professional qualifications in the interview, registration in the cantonal register of lawyers under Art. 30 para. 1 let. b BGFA follows. Upon registration, the EU/EFTA lawyer is accorded the same rights and obligations as domestically qualified lawyers (Art. 30 para. 2 BGFA). From that point onwards, the lawyer is fully subject to the professional conduct rules and disciplinary law under Art. 12 ff. BGFA (→ Art. 12 BGFA; → Art. 17 BGFA).

**N. 12** **Where the interview is failed or not ordered**: If the applicant fails to demonstrate sufficient professional qualifications in the interview, registration in the cantonal register of lawyers via Art. 30 para. 1 let. b BGFA must be refused. The EU/EFTA lawyer retains the right to sit the aptitude test under Art. 31 BGFA (Art. 30 para. 1 let. a BGFA). In addition, the lawyer may continue to remain permanently registered in the EU/EFTA register of lawyers under the original professional title and represent parties (→ Art. 27 f. BGFA).

**N. 13** **Procedural aspects**: The decision regarding the ordering, conduct, and outcome of the interview is a challengeable administrative act. It is subject to cantonal administrative judicial review and — as the final instance — to an appeal in public-law matters to the Federal Supreme Court (Art. 82 let. a BGG). The Federal Supreme Court reviews compliance with federal law freely (Art. 95 BGG).

---

### 5. Disputed Questions

**N. 14** **Scope of the interview vs. aptitude test**: Legal scholarship disagrees on whether the interview under Art. 32 BGFA differs substantively from the aptitude test under Art. 31 BGFA or merely constitutes a formally simplified examination format. Kellerhals/Baumgartner (BSK BGFA, Art. 32 N. 4 f.) take the view that the interview is substantively a lesser form of the aptitude test: it should examine knowledge of Swiss law only in the areas of activity actually practised, rather than illuminating the entire difference between the training courses. Bohnet/Martenet (Droit de la profession d'avocat, 2009, N. 899), by contrast, emphasise that the difference lies primarily in the format (oral, dialogical) rather than in the substantive content examined; the knowledge to be tested is, in both forms, determined by the material differences between the Swiss and the foreign legal system. The case law has not conclusively resolved this question; however, it can be inferred from BGE 151 II 640 consid. 4.2.3 that the interview serves as proof of knowledge of Swiss law.

**N. 15** **«Shorter practice in Swiss law»**: Art. 30 para. 1 let. b BGFA requires a «shorter practice in Swiss law» during the three-year period as a precondition for the interview. It is unclear how short that practice may minimally be. Fellmann (Anwaltsrecht, 2nd ed. 2017, N. 237) takes the view that a complete absence of practice in Swiss law precludes the interview; otherwise the interview would de facto become an independent access route. Bohnet/Martenet (Droit de la profession d'avocat, 2009, N. 899), by contrast, consider minimal practice sufficient, provided that the applicant can demonstrate knowledge in the interview; the criterion of «shorter practice» should be read teleologically, namely as a demarcation from the full period of practice, not as a qualitative minimum requirement. The practical handling by supervisory authorities varies, and a supreme-court clarification of this question remains outstanding.

**N. 16** **Procedural guarantees in the interview**: There is disagreement as to which procedural guarantees apply in the interview under Art. 32 BGFA. Since the interview interferes with economic freedom (Art. 27 FC), the principle of proportionality requires adequate advance notice of the examination topics and criteria (Fellmann, Anwaltsrecht, 2nd ed. 2017, N. 238). Bohnet/Martenet (Droit de la profession d'avocat, 2009, N. 900) additionally emphasise the right to be heard (Art. 29 para. 2 FC) and call for a written statement of reasons in the event of a negative outcome. Cantonal law may provide for more extensive procedural guarantees; at the federal level, the principles of Art. 29 FC are required.

**N. 17** **Relationship to Art. 3 BGFA (cantonal margin of discretion)**: Since Art. 32 BGFA sets only the federal legal framework and leaves the specific design of the interview to cantonal law, the question arises as to how far cantons may impose stricter requirements. The prevailing view holds that cantons may regulate the modalities of the examination but may not introduce additional substantive conditions unknown to federal law; this corresponds to the general principle of exhaustive federal regulation of admission to the profession (cf. Federal Supreme Court judgment 2C_897/2015 of 25 May 2016, consid. 5.3; Nater, BSK BGFA, Art. 3 N. 8b).

---

### 6. Practical Notes

**N. 18** **For EU/EFTA lawyers**: Anyone seeking the simplified access route under Art. 30 para. 1 let. b BGFA should already document mandates in Swiss law actively during the three-year period in the EU/EFTA register of lawyers. The interview under Art. 32 BGFA is not a guaranteed right but an instrument of the competent authority; the applicant cannot invoke the interview, rather the authority orders it when it considers the proof of practice insufficient but regards proof in the interview as feasible.

**N. 19** **For supervisory authorities**: Before ordering an interview under Art. 32 BGFA, it must be examined whether the three-year registration in the EU/EFTA register of lawyers is established (→ Art. 28 BGFA), whether — even if short — some practice in Swiss law is made credible, and whether an interview is procedurally expedient. The interview is to be ordered only where there is a material gap in practice in Swiss law; where the proof of practice is fully satisfied, registration should be carried out directly.

**N. 20** **Demarcation from the provision of services**: The interview under Art. 32 BGFA concerns exclusively permanent registration. EU/EFTA lawyers who are active only in the context of freedom to provide services under Art. 21 ff. BGFA (up to 90 working days per calendar year) are subject neither to the interview nor to the aptitude test. The determining factors for the demarcation between the provision of services and permanent practice are the frequency and duration of the activity and the intention to establish oneself permanently (**BGE 151 II 640** consid. 5.7.1 f.; judgment 2A.536/2003 of 9 August 2004, consid. 3.2.2).

**N. 21** **EU law context**: When interpreting Art. 32 BGFA, the European law context must be taken into account. The Federal Supreme Court has clarified that, when interpreting Art. 27–30 BGFA, the case law of the CJEU issued after the date of signature of the AFMP (21 June 1999) is disregarded only for compelling reasons (**BGE 151 II 640** consid. 5.6.1, with reference to **BGE 144 II 113** consid. 4.1). According to the CJEU's case law on Art. 3 of the Establishment Directive 98/5/EC, that provision effects full harmonisation of the conditions for registration; additional national hurdles are in principle impermissible (CJEU, judgment of 7 May 2019, C-431/17, *Monachos Eirinaios*, paras. 26 f.). The interview under Art. 32 BGFA is compatible with this legal position because it does not constitute an additional barrier to market access but rather a facilitation compared with the aptitude test under Art. 31 BGFA.
