# Art. 8 BGFA — Personal Requirements

## Doctrine

### 1. Legislative History

**N. 1** Art. 8 BGFA originates from the Federal Council's draft of 28 April 1999 (BBl 1999 6013). The Federal Council conceived the personal requirements as minimum guarantees for the protection of the public seeking legal assistance: legal capacity (let. a), criminal integrity (let. b), and financial reliability (let. c) were adopted with little controversy. The independence requirement (let. d) alone was contentious.

**N. 2** In the draft version (Art. 7 let. e E-BGFA), the Federal Council had deliberately chosen an open formulation that would have allowed cantonal supervisory authorities and courts to determine the contours of independence on a case-by-case basis (BBl 1999 6038, s. 172.17, fn. 44). It did not wish to block developments in the legal market and deliberately refrained from exhaustively regulating the organisational forms of law firms (**BGE 138 II 440** at 8).

**N. 3** The parliamentary deliberations were intensive (AB 1999 N 1556–1566; AB 1999 S 1165 ff.; AB 2000 N 38 ff.; AB 2000 S 234 ff.). In the first reading, the Council of States adopted a broad formulation that would have denied independence in the event of any third-party influence; at the same time, it incorporated an express reservation of freedom of organisation (Art. 11 let. b E-BGFA). The National Council deleted this addition and agreed on a narrower, employment-related definition: the presumption of lack of independence attaches to an employment relationship with a non-registered person. The Council of States concurred with this solution. The current text of Art. 8 para. 1 let. d BGFA gives unambiguous expression to the will of the parliamentary majority to anchor the independence principle in structural-institutional terms (**BGE 130 II 87** at 5.1.1).

**N. 4** Art. 8 para. 2 BGFA (exception for non-profit organisations) was restricted in the parliamentary deliberations to «recognised non-profit organisations»; the broader term «non-profit-oriented organisations» was rejected, with the consequence that, for example, trade unions and tenants' associations do not fall under this exception (BBl 1999 6013; AB 1999 S 1165 ff.; **BGE 130 II 87** at 5.1.1; **BGE 139 III 249** at 1).

**N. 5** Art. 8 para. 1 let. b BGFA was amended by the Criminal Records Act of 17 June 2016 (SR 330). Since 23 January 2023, the ground for exclusion no longer refers to the «entry in the criminal records register» but to the «private extract pursuant to Art. 41 CRA» (AS 2022 600; BBl 2014 5713). The substantive scope of the provision remained essentially unchanged.

---

### 2. Systematic Classification

**N. 6** Art. 8 BGFA is situated in the first chapter of the Act (free movement and register, Arts. 1–11) and, together with Art. 7 BGFA (professional requirements), forms the conditions for registration under Art. 6 para. 2 BGFA. The supervisory authority registers a lawyer only after it has established that both sets of requirements are satisfied. → Art. 6 BGFA; → Art. 7 BGFA.

**N. 7** The personal requirements are at the same time ongoing obligations: if a requirement subsequently lapses, Art. 9 BGFA requires removal from the register. ↔ Art. 9 BGFA. This applies in particular to independence (Art. 8 para. 1 let. d BGFA), which also appears as a professional rule in Art. 12 let. b BGFA (**BGE 138 II 440** at 3). ↔ Art. 12 let. b BGFA.

**N. 8** «Institutional independence» (Art. 8 para. 1 let. d BGFA) and «mandate-related independence» (Art. 12 let. b and c BGFA) must be distinguished systematically: the former is a condition for admission; the latter is a professional rule whose breach triggers disciplinary measures under Art. 17 BGFA (Fellmann, Anwaltsrecht, 2nd ed. 2017, N. 110 ff.; Bohnet/Martenet, Droit de la profession d'avocat, 2009, N. 1328 f.; Staehelin/Oetiker, BSK BGFA, 2nd ed. 2011, Art. 8 N. 31). → Art. 17 BGFA.

**N. 9** For EU/EFTA lawyers who establish themselves permanently in Switzerland, Art. 30 para. 2 BGFA refers to the personal requirements of Art. 8 BGFA; institutional independence applies to them as well. → Art. 30 BGFA. The Federal Supreme Court has denied any reverse discrimination, since EU law recognises the same principle of national treatment with respect to the independence obligation (Art. 8 Directive 98/5/EC; **BGE 130 II 87** at 5.1.2).

### 3. Content of the Provision

#### 3.1 Art. 8 para. 1 let. a: Legal Capacity

**N. 10** The lawyer must have legal capacity (→ Art. 12 CC: majority and capacity of judgment). Lack of legal capacity as such is rarely a practical problem; the requirement becomes relevant primarily in connection with the question of whether a subsequent interdiction triggers removal from the register (→ Art. 9 BGFA). Staehelin/Oetiker, BSK BGFA, 2nd ed. 2011, Art. 8 N. 9 ff.

#### 3.2 Art. 8 para. 1 let. b: Criminal Integrity

**N. 11** A criminal conviction precludes registration if it was imposed for «acts incompatible with the legal profession» and still appears in the private extract pursuant to Art. 41 CRA. The term «incompatible» is an indeterminate legal concept; the relevant offences are those expressing dishonesty, unreliability, or professional misconduct (property offences, forgery of documents, offences against sexual integrity depending on context; see Meier/Reiser, CR LLCA, 2010, Art. 8 N. 15 ff.; Staehelin/Oetiker, BSK BGFA, 2nd ed. 2011, Art. 8 N. 16 ff.).

**N. 12** Expungement from the private extract (Art. 41 CRA) removes the bar to registration by operation of law. The supervisory authority — where the other requirements are met — must proceed with registration; it has no discretion to continue to treat an expunged entry as an obstacle.

#### 3.3 Art. 8 para. 1 let. c: Absence of Loss Certificates

**N. 13** The existence of loss certificates (Art. 149 DEBA) indicates financial unreliability incompatible with the trust-based nature of the legal profession. The provision aims to protect clients from the risk of misappropriation of client funds (Fellmann, Anwaltsrecht, 2nd ed. 2017, N. 246 f.). Even a single loss certificate suffices as a ground for exclusion; the supervisory authority has no margin of discretion. If loss certificates are subsequently cancelled, the obstacle lapses.

**N. 14** ↔ Art. 12 let. h BGFA: The duty to keep client assets separate from one's own assets corresponds to the requirement of an absence of loss certificates; a breach of Art. 12 let. h BGFA may lead to disciplinary measures and, where loss certificates subsequently arise, to removal from the register.

#### 3.4 Art. 8 para. 1 let. d: Institutional Independence

**N. 15** The independence requirement is the practically most significant and legally most difficult personal requirement. It aims to ensure the greatest possible freedom and objectivity of the lawyer in safeguarding the interests of the client and the court, and forms the basis of public trust in the legal profession (**BGE 130 II 87** at 4.1; **BGE 138 II 440** at 5).

**N. 16** Independence under Art. 8 para. 1 let. d BGFA is institutional in nature (*indépendance institutionnelle*): the practice of law must be organised in such a way as to preclude any structural influence by third parties (**BGE 138 II 440** at 3). It is distinct from mandate-related independence (Art. 12 let. b and c BGFA; *indépendance matérielle*), which must be maintained in each individual case.

**N. 17** **Wording and presumption**: According to settled case law, the wording of the provision («They may only be employed by persons who are themselves entered in a cantonal register») is not to be read literally. It establishes a *rebuttable* presumption of lack of independence for lawyers who are employed by a non-registered person (**BGE 130 II 87** at 5.1.1 and 5.2; **BGE 138 II 440** at 6 and 14).

**N. 18** **Law firms as legal entities**: In **BGE 138 II 440**, the Federal Supreme Court held that Art. 8 para. 1 let. d BGFA does not generally preclude law firms organised as legal entities (corporations, limited liability companies). Institutional independence is preserved where the entity is entirely controlled by registered lawyers (at 17). The guarantees may be secured through the articles of association, shareholders' agreements, and employment contracts (at 23). The question of multidisciplinary partnerships involving non-lawyers was left open.

**N. 19** **Employees of non-lawyers**: For lawyers employed by banks, insurance companies, fiduciary companies, or other profit-oriented enterprises, the presumption of lack of independence applies in principle without restriction; a general exclusion is proportionate (**BGE 130 II 87** at 4.3.3). However, part-time self-employed legal practice alongside an employment relationship is permissible, provided the lawyer establishes clear organisational arrangements, in particular maintains a separate business address, the employment relationship does not touch on the legal practice, and mandates clearly fall outside the employer's field of activity (**BGE 130 II 87** at 5.2, 6.1–6.3).

**N. 20** **Public-law employment**: For persons employed under public law (court clerks, civil servants), the independence requirement applies by analogy. The Federal Supreme Court has held that a blanket prohibition on part-time legal practice throughout an entire canton is disproportionate; restricting the prohibition to mandates within one's own area of competence suffices (judgment 2P.301/2005 of 23 June 2006 at 5; **BGE 138 II 440** at 6). The compatibility of legal practice with a part-time judicial office is in principle recognised (**BGE 133 I 1** at 6.4.1 f.).

**N. 21** **Proof of independence**: A lawyer who, as an employee, wishes to rebut the presumption of lack of independence bears the burden of proof. The lawyer must provide full disclosure of the employment relationship and establish the following: (a) the employer's knowledge and consent; (b) an express contractual exclusion of any right of instruction by the employer with respect to the legal practice; (c) a prohibition on representing the employer, its clients, and related companies; (d) maintenance of a separate business address; (e) separate file management and preservation of professional secrecy vis-à-vis the employer's officers and employees; (f) separation of assets (**BGE 130 II 87** at 6.1–6.3).

#### 3.5 Art. 8 para. 2: Exception for Non-Profit Organisations

**N. 22** As the sole exception to the principle in para. 1 let. d, Art. 8 para. 2 BGFA permits registration of lawyers employed by recognised non-profit organisations, provided the other personal requirements (let. a–c) are met and the representation of parties is strictly confined to mandates falling within the scope of the organisation's purpose.

**N. 23** The concept of «recognised non-profit organisation» is determined by reference to Art. 56 let. g FDTA (SR 642.11); the decisive criteria are unselfishness and the pursuit of the public interest (**BGE 135 I 1** at 7.4.1; **BGE 132 V 200** at 5.2.3). Trade unions and tenants' associations do not ordinarily satisfy this requirement (**BGE 130 II 87** at 5.1.1; Staehelin/Oetiker, BSK BGFA, 2nd ed. 2011, Art. 8 N. 57).

**N. 24** The restriction on mandates («strictly confined to mandates falling within the scope of the organisation's purpose») must be interpreted narrowly: a lawyer employed by an association may not represent association members before the Federal Supreme Court if the mandate was previously conducted in the capacity of an employee of the association (**BGE 139 III 249** at 1). The de facto assumption of a mandate previously held by an employer violates the independence requirement.

### 4. Legal Consequences

**N. 25** Where all personal requirements under Art. 8 BGFA together with the professional requirements under Art. 7 BGFA are satisfied, the applicant has a *legal entitlement* to registration in the cantonal register (→ Art. 6 para. 2 BGFA). The supervisory authority has no discretion («it shall register the lawyer once it has established»).

**N. 26** If a requirement is not met, registration must be refused. In the event of a subsequent lapse, removal under Art. 9 BGFA must be ordered. According to **BGE 130 II 87** at 7, the lawyer is obliged to notify the supervisory authority of any changes in circumstances that may be relevant to independence (→ Art. 12 let. a BGFA, duty of diligence).

**N. 27** The personal requirements are not «once-and-for-all» conditions: the lawyer must satisfy them throughout the entire period of registration. In cases of doubt, the supervisory authority may demand evidence at any time.

**N. 28** Transitional law (Art. 36 BGFA): The transitional provision grants an exemption from the *professional* requirements, but not from the *personal* requirements. A lawyer employed by a non-registered person who cannot demonstrate institutional independence has no entitlement to registration even on the basis of Art. 36 BGFA (**BGE 130 II 87** at 8).

**N. 29** Criminal law: Where registration is lacking, professional representation of parties before courts is inadmissible for want of authorisation. A non-registered lawyer may not effectively represent parties within the reserved domain; a power of attorney granted to such a person produces no procedural effect (**BGE 139 III 249** at 1 in conjunction with Art. 40 para. 1 SCA).

### 5. Contested Issues

**N. 30** **Rebuttability of the independence presumption for full-time employees**: Nater/Zindel took the view that lawyers who had been registered under the more liberal cantonal practice in force at the time were to be registered under the transitional law on the basis of Art. 36 BGFA, even if they did not satisfy the requirement of Art. 8 para. 1 let. d BGFA (Nater, SJZ 98/2002 p. 362, 364). Hess disagreed and considered transitional registration to be excluded (Hess, SJZ 98/2002 p. 485, 493 f.). The Federal Supreme Court followed the position of Hess (**BGE 130 II 87** at 8.2).

**N. 31** **Law firms as legal entities and institutional independence**: Prior to 2006, legal scholarship broadly took it for granted that corporate forms of organisation were incompatible with professional independence (see e.g. Sanwald, Festschrift Bernischer Notare 2003, p. 242). A change of opinion set in as the legal market evolved; Bohnet/Martenet, Fellmann, Schiller, Meier/Reiser, and Zindel support the admissibility of law firms as legal entities, provided that only registered lawyers control the entity (Bohnet/Martenet, Droit de la profession d'avocat, 2009, N. 2401; Fellmann, Anwaltsrecht, 2nd ed. 2017, N. 1593 ff.; Schiller, Schweizerisches Anwaltsrecht, 2009, N. 1248 ff.; Meier/Reiser, CR LLCA, 2010, Art. 8 N. 56; Zindel, SJZ 108/2012 p. 249, 259). The Federal Supreme Court confirmed this view in **BGE 138 II 440**; the admissibility of multidisciplinary partnerships involving non-lawyers remains open.

**N. 32** **Interpretation of «non-profit» in Art. 8 para. 2 BGFA**: Legal scholarship is unanimous that trade unions and tenants' associations are not non-profit organisations within the meaning of Art. 56 let. g FDTA (Staehelin/Oetiker, BSK BGFA, 2nd ed. 2011, Art. 8 N. 57; Meier/Reiser, CR LLCA, 2010, Art. 8 N. 69; Bohnet/Martenet, Droit de la profession d'avocat, 2009, p. 277 f., N. 625). The Federal Supreme Court has confirmed this view in **BGE 139 III 249** at 1 and **BGE 132 V 200** at 5.2.3. Whether organisations such as legal advice centres for persons in need fall under this provision depends on the criteria of unselfishness, absence of membership contributions, and a specific social-law orientation (**BGE 135 I 1** at 7.4.1).

**N. 33** **Independence requirement as a core element or proportionate restriction**: Schiller (Schweizerisches Anwaltsrecht, 2009, N. 1146) takes the view that institutional independence does not belong to the rule-of-law core of the law of the legal profession and must be interpreted narrowly. The Federal Supreme Court shares this restraint to the extent that the requirements must not be set excessively high and that qualified lawyers may be denied the right to represent parties only to the extent necessary to achieve the legislative objective (**BGE 138 II 440** at 3 and 4; **BGE 130 II 87** at 3).

### 6. Practical Notes

**N. 34** **Application for registration**: Pursuant to Art. 5 para. 1 let. c BGFA, the lawyer must attach to the application all certificates demonstrating that the requirements of Art. 8 BGFA are satisfied. Where the applicant is employed, full transparency regarding the employment relationship is mandatory; incomplete information leads to rejection of the application (**BGE 130 II 87** at 7).

**N. 35** **Checklist for employed lawyers**: A person seeking registration as an employee (of a non-lawyer) must cumulatively demonstrate: (1) a separate business address outside the employer's premises; (2) a written agreement with the employer providing for instruction-free legal practice; (3) a prohibition on representing the employer, its clients, and related companies; (4) no duty to disclose information to the employer concerning legal mandates; (5) separate file management; (6) segregation of client funds from the employer's assets (**BGE 130 II 87** at 6.3.1–6.3.2).

**N. 36** **Law firm as joint-stock company**: Where a law firm is to be operated as a corporation (*Aktiengesellschaft*, AG) or limited liability company (*Gesellschaft mit beschränkter Haftung*, GmbH), the articles of association, shareholders' agreement, and internal regulations must be structured so that only registered lawyers hold voting control, the board of directors, and management. Share transfer restrictions in favour of registered lawyers and a prohibition on acquisition by non-lawyers are indispensable (**BGE 138 II 440** at 23).

**N. 37** **Loss certificates**: Lawyers in financial difficulty must act immediately, since even a single loss certificate leads to removal from the register (→ Art. 9 BGFA). The duty to notify the supervisory authority derives from Art. 12 let. a BGFA.

**N. 38** **Non-profit organisations**: Before employing a lawyer, a non-profit organisation should verify that it has been granted tax-exempt status pursuant to Art. 56 let. g FDTA. If this status is lacking, registration under Art. 8 para. 2 BGFA is not possible; the lawyer would need to establish independence separately under Art. 8 para. 1 let. d BGFA.

**N. 39** **Interaction with professional rules**: Institutional independence (Art. 8 para. 1 let. d BGFA) does not dispense with the mandate-related assessment in each individual case (Art. 12 let. b and c BGFA). Even a duly registered lawyer must examine conflicts of interest at the time of accepting each mandate. ↔ Art. 12 let. b and c BGFA; ↔ Art. 13 BGFA.
