# Art. 1 BGFA — Subject Matter

## Doctrine

### 1. Legislative History

**N. 1** Art. 1 BGFA summarises the subject matter of the Act in a single sentence: «This Act guarantees the freedom of movement of lawyers and lays down the principles governing the practice of the legal profession in Switzerland.» The provision is programmatic in nature; it does not confer independent rights or impose independent obligations, but describes the regulatory programme of the Act as a whole.

**N. 2** The BGFA was the result of decades of efforts to unify Swiss lawyers' law. The starting point — already at the beginning of the 20th century — had been the creation of a federal lawyers' patent; that ambition failed at an early stage (BBl 1999 6019 item 114). Before the BGFA entered into force, the practice of the legal profession was regulated exclusively by cantonal law; inter-cantonal activity required a separate authorisation from the canton of destination (**BGE 125 II 56 E. 3**).

**N. 3** The immediate occasion for the BGFA was the Federal Act on the Internal Market (BGBM, SR 943.02) of 1995, which established the free exercise of a profession throughout the entire national territory as a political objective. The Federal Council recognised that the sector-specific law on lawyers required a separate federal-law framework that went beyond the general internal market regime (BBl 1999 6020). At the same time, the Agreement on the Free Movement of Persons with the EU (AFMP, SR 0.142.112.681) of 1999 created the need to regulate the activities of EU/EFTA lawyers on a treaty basis.

**N. 4** The Federal Council's Message of 28 April 1999 on the Lawyers Act (BBl 1999 6013 ff.) identifies three main objectives: first, guaranteeing inter-cantonal freedom of movement through a simplified admission procedure (registration instead of a separate authorisation); second, harmonising substantive professional rules at the federal level; third, implementing the relevant EU directives on cross-border legal practice (BBl 1999 6021 f.). The Federal Council expressly emphasised that the Act was «primarily a freedom-of-movement statute» (**BGE 130 II 270 E. 3.1**).

**N. 5** Parliament adopted Art. 1 of the draft without substantive amendments. The parliamentary debates focused on the design of the register entry (Art. 5 ff.) and the catalogue of professional rules (Art. 12). Art. 1 itself was uncontested (AB 1999 N 1551 ff.; AB 1999 S 1158 ff.). The BGFA entered into force on 1 June 2002.

---

### 2. Systematic Classification

**N. 6** Art. 1 stands at the beginning of the first chapter («Freedom of Movement and Register», Art. 1–11) and fulfils the function of an introductory provision. It is not a competence rule and does not confer directly applicable subjective rights. As a teleological guideline it serves the interpretation of all subsequent provisions.

**N. 7** The Act is divided into three functional parts: (i) inter-cantonal freedom of movement (Art. 1–11), (ii) professional rules and disciplinary supervision (Art. 12–20), and (iii) rules for EU/EFTA lawyers (Art. 21–34a). Art. 1 expressly applies to all three parts, as confirmed by **BGE 151 II 640 E. 4.1**: «The BGFA guarantees the freedom of movement of EU/EFTA lawyers and lays down the principles governing the practice of the legal profession in Switzerland (Art. 1 BGFA).»

**N. 8** Art. 1 stands in a close relationship of mutual dependence with the key articles of the Act:
- ↔ Art. 2 BGFA: defines the personal scope of application;
- → Art. 3 BGFA: reservation of cantonal law (cantonal requirements for obtaining a lawyers' patent);
- → Art. 4 BGFA: guarantee of inter-cantonal freedom of movement through the register entry;
- → Art. 12 BGFA: concretisation of the «principles governing the practice of the legal profession»;
- → Art. 17 BGFA: disciplinary consequences for breach of those principles;
- → Art. 21–34a BGFA: implementation of the AFMP for EU/EFTA lawyers.

**N. 9** The constitutional basis of the BGFA is Art. 95 para. 1 FC (federal competence to regulate gainful private activity) in conjunction with Art. 95 para. 2 FC (unified economic area). This constitutes a concurrent legislative competence between the Confederation and the cantons; areas not conclusively regulated by federal law remain with the cantons (**Judgment 2C_897/2015 of 25.5.2016 E. 6.1**; **Judgment 2C_113/2024 of 3.12.2024 E. 8.2**).

### 3. Content of the Provision

#### 3.1 Dual Regulatory Purpose

**N. 10** Art. 1 identifies two separable regulatory objectives: first, *guaranteeing freedom of movement*, and second, *laying down principles governing the practice of the legal profession*. This duality shapes the overall structure of the Act: the first part (Art. 4–11) serves freedom of movement; the second part (Art. 12–20) concretises the principles of professional practice.

**N. 11** *Freedom of movement* refers primarily to inter-cantonal mobility: a lawyer entered in a cantonal register may appear before judicial authorities throughout Switzerland without any further authorisation (Art. 4 BGFA). The instrument of freedom of movement is the register entry — not the cantonal lawyers' patent. The patent is a precondition for the entry, but is not itself the freedom-of-movement authorisation (Nater, BSK BGFA, Art. 1 N. 12c; **Judgment 2C_505/2019 of 13.9.2019 E. 5.1**).

**N. 12** The *principles of professional practice* are concretised in Art. 12 BGFA (professional rules) and Art. 13 BGFA (professional secrecy). The Federal Supreme Court has clarified that these professional rules regulate professional practice *exhaustively*: «The provision of Art. 12 BGFA exhaustively governs the professional duties of lawyers» (**BGE 131 I 223 E. 3.4**; **BGE 130 II 270 E. 3.1.1**). There is no room for diverging cantonal professional rules; their basis is Art. 1 in conjunction with Art. 12 BGFA (BBl 1999 6039).

**N. 13** The harmonisation of professional rules at the federal level itself serves freedom of movement: a lawyer practising in several cantons need only comply with a single uniform body of rules, not with the varying cantonal professional codes of different cantons. There is thus a functional unity between the two regulatory objectives of Art. 1 (Fellmann, Anwaltsrecht, 2nd ed. 2017, para. 1 ff.; Bohnet/Martenet, Droit de la profession d'avocat, 2009, para. 300).

#### 3.2 Relationship to Cantonal Law

**N. 14** Art. 1 does not entirely displace cantonal lawyers' law; rather, it establishes a federal framework. Art. 3 BGFA preserves the reservation of cantonal law with respect to the acquisition of the lawyers' patent. The federal legislator had deliberately refrained from harmonising the entirety of lawyers' law — its focus was on the register entry and the freedom of movement associated with it, not on the cantonal patent (**Judgment 2C_897/2015 of 25.5.2016 E. 6.2.3**).

**N. 15** Areas not regulated by federal law include in particular: the professional and personal requirements for obtaining a lawyers' patent (Art. 3 para. 1 BGFA); the cantonal examination system (Art. 7 para. 1 lit. b BGFA leaves the substantive design to the cantons; **Judgment 2C_505/2019 of 13.9.2019 E. 5.1**); notarial law; and cantonal administrative and court procedure law. Cantonal lawyers' procedural law (e.g. an obligation to conduct procedural acts electronically) is permissible provided it pursues an objective other than that of the federal professional rules (**Judgment 2C_113/2024 of 3.12.2024 E. 8.4**).

#### 3.3 Scope of Freedom of Movement

**N. 16** The freedom of movement under Art. 1 encompasses three regulatory levels, which the BGFA implements accordingly:
1. **Inter-cantonal freedom of movement** (Art. 4–11): Swiss lawyers with a register entry;
2. **EU/EFTA freedom to provide services** (Art. 21–26): temporary activity of up to 90 working days;
3. **EU/EFTA freedom of establishment** (Art. 27–34a): permanent activity following entry in the EU/EFTA lawyers' list.

**N. 17** The EU/EFTA chapters of the BGFA implement the relevant EU directives — in particular the Services Directive (77/249/EEC), the Establishment Directive (98/5/EC) and the Diploma Recognition Directive (89/48/EEC) — within the framework of the AFMP (Annex III) (**BGE 151 II 640 E. 4.3**). When interpreting these provisions, the case law of the CJEU — in particular, from the date of signature of the AFMP (21 June 1999) onwards for compelling reasons — must be taken into account (**BGE 151 II 640 E. 5.6.1**).

### 4. Legal Consequences

**N. 18** Art. 1 does not produce independent legal consequences. As a programmatic provision, it is the benchmark for the interpretation of all other BGFA provisions and for the question of whether cantonal law is compatible with federal law (supremacy of federal law, Art. 49 para. 1 FC).

**N. 19** Art. 1 acquires practical significance in three constellations:
- **Interpretive benchmark**: Federal-law provisions of the BGFA must always be interpreted teleologically in light of the two objectives — freedom of movement and harmonisation of professional practice (**BGE 130 II 270 E. 3.1**).
- **Derogatory force**: Cantonal provisions that conflict with the objectives of Art. 1 are contrary to federal law. The professional duties under Art. 12 BGFA are exhaustive; cantonal supplements are permissible only where they pursue an objective distinct from that of the BGFA (**BGE 131 I 223 E. 3.4**; **Judgment 2C_113/2024 of 3.12.2024 E. 8.3 f.**).
- **Fundamental rights limitation**: The practice of the legal profession falls within the scope of economic freedom (Art. 27 FC); restrictions require a legal basis, a public interest, and must be proportionate (**BGE 130 II 87 E. 3**). Art. 1 concretises the legitimate public interest in a qualified and uniformly regulated legal profession.

### 5. Contested Issues

#### 5.1 Relationship Between the Freedom-of-Movement Purpose and Substantive Professional Law

**N. 20** It was disputed whether the BGFA is primarily a freedom-of-movement statute or equally a professional law statute. The Federal Supreme Court emphasises the dual nature: the Act «effects a harmonisation of substantive lawyers' law in significant areas», yet the promotion of freedom of movement remains the primary objective (**BGE 130 II 270 E. 3.1**). Nater (BSK BGFA, Art. 1 N. 12c) and Bohnet/Martenet (Droit de la profession d'avocat, 2009, para. 300) agree with this assessment, but stress that the harmonisation of professional rules fulfils an independent function, not merely an accessory one.

#### 5.2 Personal Requirements for the Lawyers' Patent

**N. 21** It is substantially disputed whether Art. 3 para. 1 BGFA permits cantons to make the acquisition of the lawyers' patent conditional upon *personal* (and not merely professional) requirements. The prevailing academic opinion and the Federal Supreme Court answer this in the affirmative: cantons have traditionally also been entitled to impose personal requirements; when enacting the BGFA, the focus was on the register entry, not on the cantonal patent (**Judgment 2C_897/2015 of 25.5.2016 E. 6.2.3**; Fellmann, Anwaltsrecht, 2nd ed. 2017, para. 684 f.; Bohnet/Martenet, Droit de la profession d'avocat, 2009, para. 576 ff.; Bohnet/Othenin-Girard/Schweizer, CR CO, Loi sur les avocats, Art. 3 N. 16).

**N. 22** A minority view denies this, arguing that the parliamentary debate on Art. 3 para. 1 BGFA was exclusively concerned with professional requirements; moreover, the concept of the lawyers' patent in the Act is linked only to professional requirements (Art. 7 BGFA), not to personal requirements (Art. 8 BGFA) (Kettiger, Entzug des Anwaltspatents: Zur Frage der Rechtmässigkeit kantonaler Regelungen des Patententzugs, Jusletter 28 September 2009, p. 4 f.). The Federal Supreme Court did not follow this position; it carries no persuasive force de lege lata (**Judgment 2C_897/2015 of 25.5.2016 E. 6.2.3**).

#### 5.3 Exhaustive Effect of the Professional Rules

**N. 23** The exhaustive effect of Art. 12 BGFA is settled case law. What remains open, however, is how far cantonal norms may reach that *in practice* concern the legal profession but pursue an objective other than that of the professional rules. The Federal Supreme Court developed a two-part test in **Judgment 2C_113/2024 of 3.12.2024 E. 8.4**: a cantonal provision is compatible with Art. 1 in conjunction with Art. 12 BGFA if (i) it pursues an objective distinct from that of the federal professional rules and (ii) it does not conflict with the spirit and purpose of those rules. The canton of Zurich's obligation to digitalise procedural acts passed this test, as it served procedural economy rather than police-law protection objectives.

#### 5.4 Unified Federal Lawyers' Patent

**N. 24** Academic literature has long called for the BGFA to be replaced by a comprehensive professional statute introducing a federal patent and consistently implementing a clear separation between the certificate of professional competence and the licence to practise. A corresponding motion (Karl Vogler, 3 May 2012, business number 12.3372) is pending before the Federal Council. The Federal Supreme Court has found that the current legal situation — with its split between the cantonal patent and the federal register entry — leads to fragmentation that the federal legislature, not the courts, would need to remedy (**Judgment 2C_897/2015 of 25.5.2016 E. 8**). Amberg (Das neue Anwaltsgesetz, ZBJV 2015, p. 635 and 640) and Keller/Kruettli (Anmerkungen von Unternehmensjuristen zum Entwurf für ein Schweizerisches Anwaltsgesetz, ZBJV 2016, p. 63) argue in favour of a clear separation.

### 6. Practical Notes

**N. 25** Art. 1 BGFA has the following consequences for legal practice:

**Freedom of movement**: A lawyer entered in the cantonal register is admitted to represent parties before judicial authorities throughout Switzerland without any further authorisation (Art. 4 BGFA). The register entry — not the cantonal patent — is the decisive instrument. A person who holds a cantonal patent but has not (yet) obtained a register entry does not yet enjoy inter-cantonal freedom of movement.

**N. 26** **Exhaustive professional rules**: Since Art. 12 BGFA exhaustively governs professional duties, cantonal supervisory authorities must adjudicate disciplinary matters by applying a uniform federal-law standard. Cantonal professional codes of conduct may only be consulted insofar as they express a view that is accepted throughout the country (**BGE 130 II 270 E. 3.1.1**). Association guidelines — including those of the FSA — do not have the character of legal norms.

**N. 27** **EU/EFTA lawyers**: Foreign lawyers from EU/EFTA states may practise in Switzerland by three routes (Art. 21–34a BGFA). Since **BGE 151 II 640** (2025), the requirements for entry in the EU/EFTA lawyers' list are to be interpreted as setting a low threshold: it is sufficient to demonstrate lawyers' qualifications and the intention to operate a permanent office in Switzerland. A prior establishment of an economic focus of activity in Switzerland may not be required.

**N. 28** **Cantonal procedural law**: Cantonal obligations applicable in the course of professional practice (e.g. an obligation to communicate electronically) are not per se contrary to federal law. The decisive question is whether they pursue a regulatory objective distinct from that of the BGFA (**Judgment 2C_113/2024 of 3.12.2024 E. 8.4**). Lawyers must be familiar with and comply with the procedural rules that vary from canton to canton.

**N. 29** **Admission requirements**: The content of the cantonal lawyers' examination falls within the competence of the cantons; the BGFA merely requires that a traineeship of at least one year concluded by an examination be completed (Art. 7 para. 1 lit. b BGFA). Examining candidates in public and administrative law — notwithstanding the absence of a cantonal lawyers' monopoly in administrative proceedings — is permissible (**Judgment 2C_505/2019 of 13.9.2019 E. 5.2.2**).
