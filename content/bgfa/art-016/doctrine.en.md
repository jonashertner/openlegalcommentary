# Art. 16 BGFA — Disciplinary Proceedings in Another Canton

## Doctrine

### 1. Legislative History

**N. 1** Art. 16 BGFA is based on the Federal Council's dispatch of 28 April 1999 (BBl 1999 6013). The Federal Council recognised that the intercantonal freedom of movement guaranteed by the BGFA (→ Art. 4 BGFA) necessarily presupposes coordinated disciplinary supervision. Lawyers may appear before courts outside their canton of registration; breaches of professional duties committed there must not give rise to a gap in legal protection. The Federal Council stated that Art. 16 BGFA aims «to promote cooperation among supervisory authorities and to accelerate the development of a (as far as possible) uniform practice» (BBl 1999 6059).

**N. 2** In the dispatch, the Federal Council expressly proceeded on the basis that a single supervisory authority «primarily competent» — that of the canton of registration — was to be consulted (BBl 1999 6059). This concept of bilateral coordination between exactly two authorities was a deliberate choice: multiple entries in various cantonal registers were rejected because they would have caused «undesirable complications» in disciplinary matters (**BGE 131 II 639** at 3.4.2, with express reference to Art. 16 BGFA). The Federal Chambers approved the draft without reservation (AB 1999 N 1553; S 1164).

**N. 3** The provision was not substantively amended during the parliamentary process. It is directly linked to the legislator's decision to permit only a single register entry per lawyer (Art. 6(1) BGFA), which in turn makes the clear allocation of jurisdiction in Art. 16 BGFA possible.

---

### 2. Systematic Classification

**N. 4** Art. 16 BGFA falls within Section 3 of the BGFA («Supervision», Arts. 14–20) and serves as the connecting provision between the power to open proceedings under → Art. 14 BGFA and the substantive sanctioning under → Art. 17 BGFA. It exclusively governs *intercantonal* procedural coordination; intra-cantonal supervision (in relation to lawyers registered in the authority's own register) is governed solely by Art. 14 BGFA.

**N. 5** The provision implements the principle of a single registration (Art. 6(1) BGFA, confirmed in **BGE 131 II 639** at 3.3) within the supervisory framework: because a lawyer is always assigned to exactly one supervisory authority — that of their canton of registration — as the «home authority», Art. 16 BGFA is able to establish a clear cooperative structure without conflicts of jurisdiction. The disciplinary law of the BGFA is exhaustive according to the settled case law of the Federal Supreme Court (**BGE 150 II 308** at 7.4; **BGE 132 II 250** at 4.3.1; **BGE 130 II 270** at 1.1); Art. 16 BGFA is therefore to be understood as part of this comprehensive federal regulation.

**N. 6** Art. 16 BGFA is closely connected to:
- → Art. 14 BGFA: supervisory jurisdiction as the foundational provision
- → Art. 15 BGFA: duty to notify by judicial and administrative authorities (the trigger for Art. 16 BGFA)
- → Art. 17 BGFA: catalogue of measures delimiting the sanctioning phase (para. 2)
- → Art. 18 BGFA: duty to notify following a ban on practising, which supplements Art. 16(3)
- → Art. 20 BGFA: deletion periods for disciplinary measures recorded in the register of the canton of registration

---

### 3. Content of the Provision

#### 3.1 Para. 1 — Duty to Inform upon Opening of Proceedings

**N. 7** Where a supervisory authority («the conducting authority») opens disciplinary proceedings against a lawyer who is *not* registered in its canton, it immediately incurs a duty to inform the supervisory authority of the canton of registration. This duty arises upon the formal opening of proceedings, not only upon a subsequent substantive decision.

**N. 8** The ratio legis of immediate notification is to give the supervisory authority of the canton of registration — which is best informed about the professional life of the lawyer concerned and any prior disciplinary measures — the earliest possible opportunity to assess the situation on its part. This also prevents parallel investigations from being conducted in different cantons without knowledge of one another (Poledna, in: Fellmann/Zindel [eds.], BSK BGFA, 2nd ed. 2019, Art. 16 N. 2 f.).

**N. 9** The recipient of the notification is always the supervisory authority of the *canton of registration*, regardless of whether the misconduct occurred in the canton of registration or elsewhere. This duty to inform does not confer on the authority of the canton of registration any procedural rights of its own against the lawyer concerned in the ongoing out-of-canton proceedings.

#### 3.2 Para. 2 — Right to Submit Observations before Sanctioning

**N. 10** Where the conducting authority intends to order a disciplinary measure, it must afford the supervisory authority of the canton of registration the opportunity to submit observations. This consultation is mandatory; it must be obtained before the sanctioning decision is taken. The Federal Supreme Court has expressly held that the investigation cannot be considered concluded at that point (judgment 2C_699/2007 of 30 April 2008 at 2.2).

**N. 11** The subject matter of the consultation is limited to the *outcome of the investigation*. The authority of the canton of registration submits observations on the factual and legal assessment of the established facts and, where relevant, on aspects of the professional background of the person concerned. It may refer to prior disciplinary measures even if these have already been deleted from the register (**BGE 150 II 308** at 5.9 and 5.10); however, the weight of such information diminishes as more time elapses.

**N. 12** The right to submit observations does not confer formal party status on the supervisory authority of the canton of registration in the out-of-canton proceedings. It is not entitled to make formal submissions or to lodge appeals. The observations have the character of a cooperative contribution by an authority; the conducting authority retains sole responsibility for the decision and the sanction (Bohnet/Martenet, Droit de la profession d'avocat, 2009, p. 628 f.; Fellmann, Anwaltsrecht, 2nd ed. 2017, para. 740).

**N. 13** Breach of the duty to consult under para. 2 constitutes a formal procedural defect. The Federal Supreme Court held in judgment 2C_699/2007 of 30 April 2008 at 2.2 that the right to be heard of the lawyer concerned may be violated where the cantonal authority declares the investigation closed even though the consultation under Art. 16(2) BGFA had not yet been obtained. The Administrative Court of the Canton of Aargau confirmed the scope of application of Art. 16(2) BGFA in its judgment WBE.2023.357 of 17 February 2025.

#### 3.3 Para. 3 — Notification of the Outcome

**N. 14** The outcome of the disciplinary proceedings must be communicated to the supervisory authority of the canton of registration, regardless of the result (acquittal, discontinuation, or sanction). This final notification serves to ensure that the «home authority» is fully informed and enables it to keep the register entry up to date (→ Art. 5(2)(e) BGFA: the register contains all disciplinary measures that have not been deleted). Art. 10(1)(c) BGFA additionally ensures that supervisory authorities of other cantons have access to the register, so as to guarantee adequate information about disciplinary measures in the intercantonal context (**BGE 150 II 308** at 5.7).

---

### 4. Legal Consequences

**N. 15** Art. 16 BGFA imposes on the conducting authority three successive duties: (1) immediate notification upon opening of proceedings (para. 1); (2) granting of the right to submit observations before sanctioning (para. 2); (3) notification of the outcome of the proceedings (para. 3). These duties are addressed to the supervisory authority as the holder of sovereign powers; the subjective rights of the lawyer concerned to compliance with intercantonal coordination are protected only indirectly.

**N. 16** For the lawyer concerned, breach of Art. 16(2) BGFA has procedural consequences: if the observations of the canton of registration are not obtained, a mandatory procedural prerequisite for the sanctioning decision is absent. This constitutes a ground for annulment in cantonal administrative appeal proceedings or in proceedings before the Federal Supreme Court.

**N. 17** The notification under para. 3 is the basis on which the canton of registration enters any disciplinary measures imposed into the register. If the notification is absent, there is a risk of a breach of the duty to maintain the register under Art. 5(2)(e) BGFA. The Federal Supreme Court has affirmed this notification duty as part of intercantonal coordination (**BGE 150 II 308** at 5.9).

**N. 18** Cantonal law may not provide for additional sanctions (such as publication in the official gazette) beyond the exhaustive catalogue set out in Art. 17 BGFA (**BGE 150 II 308** at 7.4 and 7.9). This exhaustiveness also protects the lawyer concerned in the context of intercantonal proceedings.

### 5. Disputed Questions

#### 5.1 Legal Nature of the Observations and Binding Effect

**N. 19** It is disputed what binding effect the observations of the authority of the canton of registration under para. 2 carry. The prevailing view in the literature holds that they are non-binding: the conducting authority is free to depart from the observations; the coordination duty is exhausted by the right to be heard (Poledna, BSK BGFA, Art. 16 N. 8; Bohnet/Martenet, Droit de la profession d'avocat, 2009, p. 629). The Federal Supreme Court has not expressly addressed the binding effect; from the systematic structure of the legislation and a teleological interpretation it follows that no binding effect exists, since the conducting authority leads the proceedings and takes the substantive decision.

**N. 20** A minority view considers that the authority of the canton of registration possesses privileged knowledge (prior proceedings, professional background) and that its observations should therefore carry de facto greater weight. In **BGE 150 II 308** at 5.9, the Federal Supreme Court underlined the practical significance of the right to submit observations by noting that out-of-canton supervisory authorities thereby obtain the opportunity to learn of prior disciplinary measures already deleted from the lawyers' register. This underscores a strong informal significance, but not a formal binding effect.

#### 5.2 Taking into Account Deleted Disciplinary Measures in the Consultation Procedure

**N. 21** Prior to **BGE 150 II 308**, it was disputed whether deleted disciplinary measures may be communicated and taken into account in the context of Art. 16(2) BGFA. Brunner/Henn/Kriesi (Anwaltsrecht, 2015, p. 251) took the view that measures no longer visible in the register should be disregarded. The Federal Supreme Court, by contrast, clarified in **BGE 150 II 308** at 5.9 f. that supervisory authorities may take prior misconduct — including sanctions deleted from the register — into account in their assessment. The significance of past disciplinary measures generally diminishes as more time elapses.

#### 5.3 Relationship to the Procedural Rights of the Lawyer Concerned

**N. 22** It is disputed whether the lawyer concerned has the right to be informed of the content of the observations submitted by the authority of the canton of registration and to respond thereto. The Federal Supreme Court held in judgment 2C_699/2007 of 30 April 2008 at 2.2 that the initiation of the consultation under Art. 16(2) BGFA means that the proceedings have not yet concluded; the person concerned must therefore still have the opportunity to make submissions. Constitutional law (Art. 29(2) FC, right to be heard) requires that the lawyer concerned be informed of the content of the observations and afforded an opportunity to respond. Cantonal procedural law must ensure this requirement is met.

#### 5.4 Supervisory Jurisdiction in Cases of Breaches of Professional Duties Spanning Multiple Cantons

**N. 23** Where a lawyer commits breaches of professional duties in several cantons outside the canton of registration, each of the affected supervisory authorities may conduct its own disciplinary proceedings pursuant to Art. 14 in conjunction with Art. 16 BGFA. The question arises whether, in such constellations, the efficiency objective of Art. 16 BGFA — which is designed for bilateral coordination — should be achieved through concentration of proceedings in the canton of registration. Neither the Federal Supreme Court nor cantonal courts have yet conclusively addressed this question. Poledna (BSK BGFA, Art. 16 N. 5) points out that the clear wording of Art. 14 BGFA links supervisory authority to the place of representation before a court, and that considerations of procedural economy do not justify departing from this statutory allocation of jurisdiction. The question of concentration of proceedings in cases of parallel out-of-canton proceedings thus remains open de lege lata and awaits clarification by the courts.

### 6. Practical Notes

**N. 24** **For supervisory authorities:** The duty to inform under para. 1 must be fulfilled immediately upon opening of proceedings. It is advisable to document the notification in writing with a file note. The consultation under para. 2 must be obtained before the sanctioning decision is taken; this must also be ensured in terms of timing. The final notification under para. 3 must be given in every case — including in the event of discontinuation or acquittal.

**N. 25** **For lawyers concerned:** The consultation of the authority of the canton of registration is a mandatory procedural step. If it is omitted, a formal defect exists which may serve as a ground of appeal. The lawyer concerned is likewise entitled to be informed of the content of the observations and to have the right to respond (Art. 29(2) FC; judgment 2C_699/2007 at 2.2). Lawyers who practise outside their canton of registration should be aware that prior disciplinary measures, including deleted ones, may be raised in the consultation procedure (**BGE 150 II 308** at 5.9 f.).

**N. 26** **On the relationship between the canton of registration and the conducting authority:** The canton of registration holds the complete picture of the professional background (Art. 5(2)(e) BGFA) and has access under Art. 10(1)(c) BGFA to the registers of other cantons. The conducting authority would do well to actively seek information from the canton of registration, even if this goes beyond the minimum requirements of Art. 16(2) BGFA. The invitation to submit observations should explicitly ask about prior disciplinary measures — including deleted ones — as these may be relevant to the determination of the sanction.

**N. 27** **On publication in the official gazette:** Cantonal law may not provide for publication of a disciplinary measure in the official gazette or any other public medium, since the BGFA exhaustively governs the catalogue of measures (**BGE 150 II 308** at 7.4 and 7.9). Corresponding cantonal provisions (such as § 23(1)(d) EG BGFA/ZG) are contrary to federal law and may be challenged by way of an appeal in public-law matters.

**N. 28** **On the register entry following conclusion of proceedings:** The notification under para. 3 is a prerequisite for the canton of registration to be able to keep the register content up to date pursuant to Art. 5(2)(e) BGFA. If the notification is absent, the lawyer concerned may demand that the canton of registration request the notification from the conducting authority.
