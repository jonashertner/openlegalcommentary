# Contested: Open Methodology and the BGFA

## The Question

Can a Swiss law firm publish its working methodology — litigation strategies, negotiation frameworks, analytical templates, procedural playbooks — as open-source materials, without violating the professional secret under Art. 13 BGFA or the professional rules under Art. 12 BGFA?

This is not an academic question. It is the legal foundation for the open-source law firm model explored at [regenerative.law](https://regenerative.law): a practice built on radical transparency of method, while maintaining absolute confidentiality of client information.

## Position A: Maximalist Reading — Art. 13 Prohibits Publication

### The Argument

Under this reading, Art. 13 BGFA protects *all* knowledge that a lawyer acquires "infolge ihres Berufes" — not merely information directly communicated by clients, but also the professional methodology, strategic insights, and pattern recognition developed through the cumulative experience of client work.

The textual basis is the phrase "infolge ihres Berufes" (in the course of their profession). This is read broadly: anything the lawyer knows *because* they practise law is knowledge gained "infolge ihres Berufes." Since methodology is refined through case work, and case work occurs in the context of client mandates, the methodology is — however indirectly — derived from client-entrusted information.

### Supporting Arguments

1. **Derivation**: Even if no single client entrusted the methodology, it was developed through the aggregate of client relationships. Publish the methodology, and you reveal something about the cases that shaped it.

2. **Mosaic risk**: Individual pieces of methodology may be innocuous, but a complete playbook could allow inferences about the types of cases a firm handles, the industries it serves, or the strategies it favours — information that, combined with other publicly available data, could identify specific clients or matters.

3. **Professional culture**: The tradition of the Swiss bar is discretion. Publishing methodology departs from professional norms in a way that, even if technically permissible, invites scrutiny from disciplinary authorities.

4. **Client expectations**: Clients may reasonably expect that not only their specific information, but also the general strategic approach developed in their matter, will remain confidential.

### Weaknesses

This position proves too much. If all professional knowledge is "anvertraut," then:
- Every CLE presentation by a practising lawyer violates Art. 13
- Every law review article drawing on practice experience violates Art. 13
- Informal knowledge-sharing among colleagues violates Art. 13
- The entire tradition of practitioner commentary (*Praxiskommentare*) is unlawful

These are absurd consequences that no one — including proponents of a broad reading — actually endorses. The position is internally incoherent: it must admit exceptions for "legitimate" scholarship, but it provides no principled basis for distinguishing legitimate scholarship from impermissible methodology-sharing.

## Position B: Minimalist Reading — Art. 13 Protects Only Client-Specific Information

### The Argument

Under this reading, "anvertraut" is the operative term, and it means *entrusted by the client*. Only information that a specific client communicated to the lawyer, or that the lawyer obtained on a specific client's behalf, falls within Art. 13. General methodology — strategies, frameworks, templates — is the lawyer's own intellectual property, developed through professional experience, and is no more "entrusted" by clients than a surgeon's technique is "entrusted" by patients.

### Supporting Arguments

1. **Textual**: The statute says "von ihrer Klientschaft anvertraut" — entrusted *by their clients*. Methodology is not entrusted by anyone; it is developed by the lawyer. The preposition "von" and the verb "anvertraut" both point to a directional relationship: information flows *from* client *to* lawyer. Methodology flows in no such direction.

2. **BGE 112 Ib 606**: The Bundesgericht drew a clear distinction between information received from the client and information the lawyer develops independently. This supports limiting Art. 13 to client-communicated information.

3. **Systematic**: Art. 13 sits within the BGFA's professional rules (3. Abschnitt), alongside Art. 12. The professional rules regulate the lawyer-client relationship, not the lawyer's participation in the broader legal community. Reading Art. 13 to prohibit scholarship or knowledge-sharing would be systematically incongruent.

4. **Purposive**: The purpose of Art. 13 is to enable frank lawyer-client communication. Publishing general methodology does not impair that purpose. Clients are no less likely to confide in their lawyer because the lawyer has published a general guide to litigation strategy.

5. **Constitutional (Art. 27 BV)**: Where a statutory provision admits of multiple readings, the reading more compatible with fundamental rights is to be preferred (*verfassungskonforme Auslegung*). The minimalist reading preserves the lawyer's economic freedom (Art. 27 BV) and freedom of expression (Art. 16 BV) without compromising client protection.

6. **Existing practice**: Lawyers routinely publish practitioner commentaries, litigation guides, strategy articles, and negotiation manuals without anyone suggesting that this violates Art. 13. The proposed reading merely makes explicit and systematic what the profession already does selectively.

### Weaknesses

The minimalist reading requires a clean separation between client-specific information and general methodology. In practice, the boundary is not always sharp. A strategy developed for a specific case may be generalisable, but the act of generalisation is itself a judgment call. How much abstraction is "enough"?

Additionally, there may be niche situations where the methodology *itself* reveals client information — for example, if a firm develops a highly specialised approach to a rare type of transaction, and only one client could plausibly have required it.

## Position C: Middle Ground — Publish If Sufficiently Abstracted

### The Argument

This position accepts that general methodology is not, in principle, protected by Art. 13, but introduces a functional test: methodology may be published only if it has been **sufficiently abstracted** such that:

1. No specific client can be identified from the published material,
2. No specific mandate can be reconstructed,
3. The methodology is presented at a level of generality that is useful to the profession as a whole, not merely a disguised case report, and
4. A reasonable, informed reader — including persons with knowledge of the firm's client base — could not infer client-specific information from the published material.

### The Test

The middle ground proposes an **identification test** analogous to data protection principles: the question is not whether the information was technically "entrusted" by a client, but whether its publication could functionally compromise client confidentiality.

This is a pragmatic, risk-based approach. It does not require a binary classification of information as either "client-specific" or "general." Instead, it asks: would publication of this material, in this form, at this level of detail, risk identifying a client or a matter?

### Supporting Arguments

1. **Practical**: This approach mirrors what careful lawyers already do when writing about their practice. They abstract, they generalise, they omit identifying details. The middle ground simply makes this practice explicit and gives it a doctrinal foundation.

2. **Proportionate**: It respects both Art. 13 (by maintaining protection against identification) and Art. 27 BV (by permitting publication where no client interest is at stake).

3. **Workable**: Unlike the maximalist position, it does not require heroic exceptions for existing scholarship. Unlike the pure minimalist position, it acknowledges that the boundary between "client-specific" and "general" is sometimes blurry and requires contextual judgment.

### Weaknesses

The identification test introduces uncertainty. What counts as "sufficient" abstraction is a judgment call, and different disciplinary authorities might reach different conclusions. The approach provides guidance, not a bright line.

There is also a timing dimension: a methodology that is innocuously general today might become identifying tomorrow, if context changes (e.g., a case becomes public, a client relationship becomes known).

## Analysis: Which Position Is Most Defensible?

**Position C (middle ground) is the most defensible**, for the following reasons:

1. **Textual fidelity**: It respects the statutory language of Art. 13 ("anvertraut" by the client) while acknowledging that the boundary between categories is functional, not formal.

2. **Doctrinal coherence**: It is consistent with BGE 112 Ib 606 (distinguishing client-entrusted and independently developed information) and BGE 130 II 193 (recognising that even circumstantial information can be sensitive if it permits identification).

3. **Constitutional compatibility**: It satisfies the Art. 27 BV proportionality analysis. The restriction on publication is limited to cases where client identification is genuinely at risk — a narrowly tailored means to achieve the legitimate end of client protection.

4. **Practical workability**: It aligns with what the profession already does and provides a framework for responsible innovation.

5. **Avoidance of absurdity**: Unlike Position A, it does not criminalise existing legal scholarship. Unlike Position B in its purest form, it does not ignore the rare cases where general methodology could reveal client-specific information.

Position B (minimalist) is substantially correct in principle — the professional secret protects client-entrusted information, not professional knowledge as such — but requires the pragmatic qualification of Position C to handle edge cases responsibly.

Position A (maximalist) is untenable. It is inconsistent with existing practice, it proves too much, and it fails the proportionality analysis under Art. 27 BV.

## Implications for the Open Source Law Firm

If Position C is adopted, an open-source law firm is **legally viable** under the BGFA, subject to the following operational constraints:

1. **Rigorous abstraction**: All published materials must be reviewed through the identification test before publication. No material that could allow a reasonable reader to identify a specific client or matter may be published.

2. **Structural separation**: The firm must maintain a clear organisational boundary between client work (confidential) and published methodology (open). This includes separate workflows, review processes, and — ideally — a time lag between case experience and publication of derived methodology.

3. **Review protocol**: A designated reviewer (ideally the firm's compliance partner or an external peer) should assess each publication for identification risk before release.

4. **Client communication**: Engagement letters should disclose the firm's open-source practice model, explaining that general methodology may be published while all client-specific information remains strictly confidential. This is not a waiver of Art. 13 — it is transparency about a practice that does not, on the better reading, engage Art. 13 at all.

5. **Disciplinary risk management**: The firm should be prepared to defend its model before a cantonal *Aufsichtsbehörde*. The legal analysis is strong, but it is novel, and first-mover risk exists. A well-documented compliance framework strengthens the defence.

## What Remains Unresolved

1. **No direct authority**: There is no BGE or published cantonal decision directly addressing the question of whether publishing general methodology violates Art. 13 BGFA. The analysis above is constructed from general principles and analogous authority. Until a court or disciplinary authority rules on the specific question, legal certainty is limited.

2. **Cantonal variation**: Disciplinary authorities are cantonal (*Aufsichtsbehörden*). Different cantons may interpret Art. 13 differently. A model that is accepted in one canton might be challenged in another.

3. **The mosaic problem**: Even if individual publications are safely abstracted, the *aggregate* of a firm's publications over time might create a mosaic that permits inferences about its client base. This cumulative risk is difficult to assess ex ante.

4. **Digital context**: Publishing methodology online makes it searchable, combinable, and persistent in ways that traditional scholarship (law review articles, CLE presentations) is not. Whether this changes the identification risk analysis is an open question.

5. **Client consent**: While the analysis suggests that consent is not strictly necessary for publishing general methodology, obtaining it (via engagement letter disclosure) provides an additional layer of protection. The limits and mechanics of such consent — particularly for former clients who entered the relationship before the open-source model was adopted — remain unclear.

6. **The BGFA revision**: The BGFA is periodically discussed for revision. Any future amendment to Art. 13 could resolve or complicate the question. The current analysis is based on the statute as it stands.
